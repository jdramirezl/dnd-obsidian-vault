# Introduction - Specific Beats General

## Original Text

This book contains rules, especially in parts 2 and 3, that govern how the game plays. That said, many racial traits, class features, spells, magic items, monster abilities, and other game elements break the general rules in some way, creating an exception to how the rest of the game works. Remember this: If a specific rule contradicts a general rule, the specific rule wins.

Exceptions to the rules are often minor. For instance, many adventurers don't have proficiency with longbows, but every wood elf does because of a racial trait. That trait creates a minor exception in the game. Other examples of rule-breaking are more conspicuous. For instance, an adventurer can't normally pass through walls, but some spells make that possible. Magic accounts for most of the major exceptions to the rules.

---

## Condensed Version

**The Golden Rule**: When a specific rule contradicts a general rule, the **specific rule wins**.

**Why This Matters**: Many game elements create exceptions to general rules:
- Racial traits
- Class features
- Spells
- Magic items
- Monster abilities

**Minor Exceptions Example**:
- **General rule**: Most characters aren't proficient with longbows
- **Specific exception**: Wood elves have longbow proficiency (racial trait)
- The specific trait overrides the general rule

**Major Exceptions Example**:
- **General rule**: Characters can't pass through walls
- **Specific exception**: Certain spells allow this
- Magic creates most major rule exceptions

**Key Concept**: D&D has general rules that apply broadly, but specific abilities and features can override them. Always apply the more specific rule when conflicts arise.
