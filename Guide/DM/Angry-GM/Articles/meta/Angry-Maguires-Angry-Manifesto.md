# Angry Maguire's Angry Manifesto

## Summary

Published July 30, 2018. Angry's "Jerry Maguire" moment — a sweeping manifesto laying out everything he believes is wrong with the RPG industry and why he's building his own RPG from scratch rather than creating content for D&D. After a successful Kickstarter for his book (Game Angry) and incorporating Angry Games, Inc., he announces plans for a ground-up RPG designed to fix systemic industry problems. Key critiques:

- **Rulebooks, not toolboxes:** RPGs present rules as procedures to follow rather than tools for the GM to use. The GM's role should be taught *before* the rules, not after.
- **The GM as a necessary evil:** The industry fears GMs and tries to constrain them with legalistic rules instead of empowering them. This creates GMs terrified of making judgment calls.
- **Players don't read rulebooks:** Most players only care about character creation. Rules should be written for GMs; players need a brief, focused document.
- **Four RPG markets exist, but companies only sell to two:** The industry packages products the same way TSR did 50 years ago — giant textbooks, separate monster manuals, individual modules — without questioning whether that model still works.
- **RPGs are failing new players:** The "older cousin model" (you only get into RPGs when someone who already plays brings you in) limits growth to a slow, linear rate. The industry has no strategy for making RPGs discoverable or approachable to newcomers.
- **Monster stats should be in adventures:** If you only run published adventures, you shouldn't need to buy a Monster Manual. The fact that you do means monster stats are designed badly.

He emphasizes he's not trying to "fix D&D" — D&D 5E and Pathfinder are fine games. He's building something new from scratch, designed around these principles, with a 2-year development timeline.

> [!tip] Context
> This is a meta/announcement article, not actionable GM advice. However, it's one of the most important articles for understanding Angry's design philosophy and why his advice takes the form it does. The critique of "rulebooks vs. toolboxes" and "the GM as the core mechanic" are foundational to his entire body of work.
