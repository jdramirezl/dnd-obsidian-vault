# The Angry Guide to Kickass Combats (Part 2): Battlefields and BattleFEELS

## Summary

Part 2 of the combat design series introduces the remaining two of three components that define every great combat: the **Battlefield** and the **Ambiance** (the "B" and "A" of the ABCs). The battlefield is not just a map — it's an active participant in the fight that shapes tactics, movement, and decisions. Ambiance is the intangible feel of a battle, largely established by how the fight opens and the relative power positions of the combatants. Angry also argues forcefully against graph paper, advocating for sketching terrain as functional blobs before finalizing maps.

## Key Advice

### The ABCs of Combat Design

Every battle has three major components:

| Letter | Component | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Ambiance | The feel/character of the battle — how it opens, relative power, mood |
| **B** | Battlefield | The terrain and environment — an active participant, not just a backdrop |
| **C** | Creatures | The enemy force — goals, strategies, and tactics (covered in Part 1) |

### Terrain Categories

Angry breaks terrain into functional categories based on what they *do*, not what they look like:

| Terrain Type | Function | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| **Walls/Obstructions** | Block movement AND line of sight | Outnumbered parties; ranged fighters love corners |
| **Chokepoints/Funnels** | Force all traffic through a small area | Outnumbered parties; strong melee fighters |
| **Obscurement** | Blocks/impedes line of sight but not movement | Melee combatants (harder for ranged to target them) |
| **Cover** | Small obstructions (pillars, trees, boulders) | Functionally small walls |
| **Ground Obstacles** | Impede/prevent movement but not line of sight (pits, rivers, arrow slits) | Ranged combatants (evil twin of obscurement) |
| **Hazards** | Punish anyone who moves through (fire, lava, acid) | Function as soft obstructions with a risk/cost bypass |
| **Booby Traps** | Invisible terrain that creates a new feature when triggered | Good traps create lasting terrain; bad traps are one-shot screwjobs |
| **Impeding/Difficult Terrain** | Slows movement only (rubble, mud, underbrush) | Ranged combatants (creates distance without changing range) |
| **Props** | Variable objects (tables, barrels, chandeliers) | Anyone creative enough to use them |

### The Forgotten Terrain Features

- **Distance** — Not just squares; what matters is how many *actions* (moves) it takes to cover. Climbing a 30-foot ladder is "far" even if the scaffold is 10 feet away. Difficult terrain creates distance without adding literal distance.
- **Open Space** — Serves the side with numerical advantage (more room to outflank, surround, spread out). Tight spaces favor the outnumbered side.
- **Vectors of Approach** — The number of directions from which trouble can come. A four-way intersection = four vectors. A doorway = one vector.

### Mapping Without Graph Paper

- **Ditch graph paper.** Think in functional blobs: "this area is open," "this blob is hazardous," "here's a chokepoint."
- **Think in moves, not squares.** Most D&D creatures move 5–6 squares per move. What matters is: does it take 1 move, 2 moves, or 3 moves to get there?
- **Finalize later.** Replace blobs with actual features that fulfill the function. "The hazard is boiling mud. The chokepoint is a narrow cave passage."
- **Recognize functional equivalents.** A clearing with a pond, a cave with a ravine and bridge, and a temple with a reflecting pool can all be the *same battlefield* functionally.

### Ambiance (Battle Character)

- Ambiance is how the combat **feels** — established primarily by how the battle starts.
- The core question: **Are the PCs starting at an advantage, disadvantage, or neutral?** Answer in one sentence with the reason why.
- Factors that shape ambiance: starting positions, relative numbers, power levels, surprise, formation, distance, who has advantageous terrain.
- **Conditional ambiance** is powerful: "If the PCs enter via the balcony, they have advantage. If they enter by the lower door, they don't."
- Ambiance is what players will *remember* the battle for. If a battle won't be remembered, it isn't worth playing.

### Caveats for Combat Design

1. **It's hard work** — but gets faster with practice. Eventually you can crank out a good battle on a napkin.
2. **Know your game** — You need system mastery: monster capabilities, PC abilities, tactical options, rules.
3. **Want the right things** — Combats should be exciting, challenging, and dangerous. PCs should earn their victories.
