# The Angry Guide to A$$kicking Combats (Part 1): Picking Your Enemies

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-angry-guide-to-akicking-combats-part-1-picking-your-enemies/)
**Date:** September 9, 2014

---

Part 1 of a multi-part series on combat design. This installment covers choosing and assembling groups of foes — the WHO and WHY of combat. Part 2 covers battlefields and terrain (the WHERE and HOW).

## Feedback Loops in Design

Encounter design, adventure design, and campaign design all feed into each other. You're never JUST building an encounter — decisions at one level affect all others. A goblin guard post leads to spider pets, which leads to a breeding pit, which changes the map, which changes the story to spider-worshiping goblins, which connects to a drow plotline.

**The same applies within encounter design:** choosing foes and designing battlefields feed each other. Sometimes you start with the battlefield and populate it; sometimes you start with the monsters and build the battlefield. Always be willing to change one to suit the other.

## Encounter Budgets Are Just a Starting Point

Challenge ratings, XP budgets, and encounter math are a baseline, not gospel. Difficulty depends on:
- Monster synergies and team composition
- Terrain and battlefield design
- Player skill and tactical acumen
- DM skill at running monsters

The rules are a knob you can twist. As you learn your players, twist it. Your well-designed combats will be more dangerous than average — be willing to dial difficulty down. Weak encounters are fine — they let players feel powerful. Don't be a slave to the math.

## Numbers and Variety

The two most important principles for building monster teams:

### More Monsters Are Better Than One

Solo monsters fail because of:
- **Pacing problems:** No back-and-forth, no ebb and flow between turns
- **Action economy:** Even with lair actions, a solo functions at ~60% compared to a five-person party
- **No progress indicators:** Nothing changes as PCs win — no sense of gradual progress
- **Easy to pin down:** Party surrounds and controls a single foe easily

**Ideal:** About as many monsters as PCs, with multiple different types.

### Variety Creates Interesting Decisions

Different monster types force different tactical responses. Players must decide which threats to eliminate first. Each kill changes the monsters' options, keeping the fight dynamic.

**Don't go overboard:** 5 unique monsters is a lot of bookkeeping. Most fights should have 2–3 types with 3–5 total creatures.

## Goals, Strategies, and Tactics: What a Monster Really Is

When you add a monster to a fight, you're not adding a stat block — you're adding a strategy. Understanding monsters requires three concepts:

### Monster Goals

What the bad guys want to accomplish. Defined by the monster's nature AND its role in the encounter.

**Team goals vs. individual goals:**
- Hobgoblin guards: (a) raise alarm, (b) hold the gate. They'll die for these goals.
- Goblin archers at the same post: same team goals, plus (c) stay out of harm's way — they're cowardly and selfish.
- Ghouls: team goal is to eat heroes, but each individual ghoul wants to drag away and eat ONE hero.

### Monster Tactics

Individual, moment-to-moment decisions. "What do you do this round?" Defined by the stat block and the DM's creativity. A hobgoblin forming a shield wall, a goblin archer targeting the wizard, a ghoul using claws instead of bite — these are all tactics.

### Monster Strategies

The overall plan — how the team works toward its goal and how each member contributes. Like positions on a hockey team.

**Hobgoblin strategy analysis:**
- High AC, decent HP → can take a beating
- Bonus damage near allies → stay close together
- Can two-hand longsword (drop shield) → more damage but less defense
- Has longbow → ranged option but loses defense

**Strategy for gate guards:** Form a defensive wall, hold position, deal bonus damage from proximity. Don't use bows (unless PCs stay far away). Don't two-hand swords (need defense and staying power).

**Same hobgoblins, different strategies:**
- Archer hobgoblins behind the main line → rain arrows from defensive position
- Flanking hobgoblins → two-hand longswords for maximum damage on engaged foes

**Goblin strategy:** Stay far from danger, under cover, pepper foes with arrows. If in melee, disengage and run — this serves both self-preservation AND the delay goal (heroes waste time chasing goblins).

**Ghoul strategy:** Spread out among heroes. Claw to paralyze, then drag away disabled victims. Each ghoul works independently.

**Wolf strategy:** Pack tactics — focus on one target at a time, knock prone, gain advantage, savage on the ground. Opposite of ghouls despite similar predator goals.

**Strategic synergies:** Wolves + hobgoblins = nasty double teams. Knock prone → advantage → bonus damage from proximity.

## Strategies Change Mid-Fight

Goals and strategies are big-picture, but they adapt:
- Hobgoblins facing an AoE wizard might break the shield wall and spread into small teams
- When the gate is lost, goblins flee while hobgoblins make a fighting withdrawal or die buying time
- Reinforcements change the calculus — hobgoblins might go offensive

This is where encounter design and adventure design enter a feedback loop — the next line of defense must be designed alongside the first.

## Six Takeaways

1. **Build an encounter, then build a combat.** Know WHY the fight is happening and what each side wants before placing monsters.
2. **Add more monsters and more variety.** Multiple creatures of 2–3 types. Don't group initiative.
3. **Encounter budgets are a starting point.** Futz and fiddle. Weak fights are fine. Strong fights are fine.
4. **Think in Goals, Strategy, and Tactics.** Every monster needs a strategy — a position on the team. Analyze stat blocks for what the monster CAN do and how it BEHAVES.
5. **Everything feeds everything else.** Encounters ↔ adventures ↔ campaigns. Mechanics ↔ story. Foes ↔ battlefields.
6. **Everything is reversible.** Good synergy = harder fight. Poor synergy = easier fight. Monsters out of their element = easier. Use this as a difficulty dial.

## Bonus: The Element List

When starting a site-based adventure, make a short list (≤5) of game elements that suit the adventure: monster types, terrain features, environmental hazards. Draw from this list when building individual encounters, combining 2–3 elements at a time. Add new elements as you discover them during design. This ties the adventure together thematically and makes encounter building easier.

You can fill a ten-encounter dungeon using only 3–5 elements and still have every encounter feel fresh — if you know what you're doing.
