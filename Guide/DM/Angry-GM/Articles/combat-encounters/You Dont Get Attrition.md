# Maybe You Just Don't GET Attrition

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Maybe You Just Don't GET Attrition](https://theangrygm.com/you-dont-get-attrition/)
**Date:** May 16, 2023
**Series:** Random Bullshit (foundational game design theory)

---

## Core Concept

**Attrition** is a brilliant Macrochallenge baked into D&D's design. Individual combat encounters (Microchallenges) are supposed to be nearly unfailable — the real test of player skill is **how efficiently** they handle each one, managing resources across an entire adventuring day. Stripping Attrition out of D&D breaks the game's core challenge structure.

---

## Challenge vs. Difficulty — They Are Not the Same

### Challenge
> The extent to which a game's outcome relies on **player skill**.

- A Challenging game tests your skills — strategy, resource management, perception, risk tolerance
- *Chess* = wholly Challenging (outcome = skill). *Candy Land* = Nonchallenging (outcome = random)
- A game with **no Challenge** is not a game — it's a hobby or diversion
- Challenge is where **Agency** and **Progress** live

### Difficulty
> The level of player skill required to achieve a **good outcome**.

- A game can be highly Challenging but not very Difficult (skill matters a lot, but the bar is low)
- A game can be very Difficult but not very Challenging (the bar is high, but luck matters more than skill)
- You **cannot** have Difficult Nonchallenges

### The Three Factors in Game Outcomes
1. **Player Skill** — strategy, decisions, resource management, risk assessment
2. **Random Chance** — dice rolls, card draws
3. **Avatar Strength** — character stats, level, gear, optimization

A Challenging game is one where Player Skill correlates more strongly with outcomes than the other factors.

---

## Challenge Rating and Encounter Difficulty Are Misleading

In D&D:
- **Challenge Rating** measures the balance between Avatar Strength and monster stats, weighted for Random Chance — it measures everything **except** Challenge
- **Encounter Difficulty** (Easy/Medium/Hard/Deadly) measures the gap that Player Skill has to fill
- In theory, an Easy encounter is winnable regardless of skill; a Hard encounter requires leaning on Player Skill

**But that's not how it actually works** — because D&D doesn't really do Challenge and Difficulty at the individual encounter level. CR and Encounter Difficulty are actually about **rates of Attrition**.

---

## Microchallenge vs. Macrochallenge

### The Play-Perfect-Forever Problem
If every encounter is pass/fail and failure = game over, then the more encounters you add, the more likely players eventually fail one. This creates a game where players must play perfectly forever — terrible for a persistent, ongoing RPG.

### The Solution: Macrochallenge
Design scenarios where individual encounters (Microchallenges) don't determine the overall outcome. Instead, an overarching Macrochallenge provides the real test.

**Examples of Macrochallenges:**
- **Time pressure** — kill three groups of orcs within a strict time limit (individual kills are easy; doing it fast enough is the test)
- **Mystery** — individual clue-gathering encounters are straightforward; deducing the answer is the real challenge
- **Attrition** — individual encounters are nearly unfailable; the test is how efficiently you handle them across the whole day

---

## The Attrition Macrochallenge

### How It Works
1. Make individual encounters **very hard to fail** — only a complete idiot loses a single fight
2. Build in **expendable resources** — HP, spell slots, abilities, consumables
3. The scenario's outcome hinges on **efficiency** — how many resources players spend per encounter
4. Players can win every fight but still end up with a bad outcome if they waste too many resources

### Why It's Brilliant
- Solves the Play-Perfect-Forever problem elegantly
- Doesn't require Scenario Designers to be design virtuosos — it's baked into the system
- Fits naturally with D&D's existing play experience
- The outcome isn't binary win/lose — it's a **spectrum of efficiency**
- Easy to layer other Macrochallenges on top:
  - **Timed adventures** = Attrition + hard cap on inefficiency
  - **Random encounters** = punishment for wasting time
  - **Event-based encounters** = forced resource expenditure (no rest allowed)
  - **Optional side areas** = rewards for risking inefficiency

### Why People Hate It (Wrongly)
- They think individual encounters are "too easy" — **they're supposed to be**
- They think the game is all-or-nothing win/lose — **efficiency is the state between victory and defeat**
- They fixate on Microchallenges and miss the Macrochallenge entirely
- They strip out resource management and then complain the game has no challenge

### Why WotC Is Making It Worse
- "Most players only have one combat per in-game day" → stripping out rest/resource mechanics
- This removes the Attrition Macrochallenge without replacing it with anything
- The Attrition Macrochallenge **cannot be removed — it must be replaced**
- If you don't like it, you need to do serious heavy lifting to build an alternative Macrochallenge

---

## Microchallenges Are Supposed to Be Nearly Unfailable

This is the most misunderstood aspect of D&D's design:

- Individual encounters should be **borderline impossible to lose** for a competent party
- The dice and combat mechanics exist to determine **how much it costs** to win, not whether you win
- You roll dice because the **resource cost** matters for the Macrochallenge
- A "Deadly" encounter in the DMG means "might knock one PC unconscious" — not "might TPK"
- Calling a battle early because "they're going to win anyway" misses the point — the question is **what the win costs**

---

## The Role of Scenario Design

**System Designers** build the game engine and toolkit. **Scenario Designers** (adventure writers, homebrewing GMs) build actual playable games from those tools.

The Attrition Macrochallenge was codified in the **3rd Edition DMG** as the core Scenario Design pattern. It ensures that even amateur Scenario Designers can create a minimally fun gameplay experience without understanding advanced design theory.

Good roleplaying game systems **hardcode good Scenario Design** into the engine so amateurs don't have to reinvent it. The Attrition Macrochallenge is D&D's primary example of this.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Challenge** = how much player skill matters. **Difficulty** = how much skill is needed. They're different.
- D&D's CR and Encounter Difficulty measure **Attrition rates**, not Challenge or Difficulty
- Individual encounters are **supposed to be nearly unfailable** — the real test is efficiency across the day
- **Attrition** is the Macrochallenge that makes D&D's encounter-based gameplay work
- Removing Attrition without replacing it **breaks the game's challenge structure**
- If you want to replace Attrition, you need to build your own Macrochallenge — and that's hard
- Stop complaining D&D is too easy while simultaneously refusing to track resources
