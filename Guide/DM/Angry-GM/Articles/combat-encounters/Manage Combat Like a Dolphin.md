# How to Manage Combat Like a Dolphin

**Source:** [The Angry GM – How to Manage Combat Like a Motherf$&%ing Dolphin](https://theangrygm.com/manage-combat-like-a-dolphin/)
**Date:** November 18, 2015
**Series:** Run Better Battles

---

## Core Concept

Combat pacing follows a **dolphin pattern** — narrative plunges underwater (momentum) and mechanics leap above the surface (breathing). Every turn cycles through narrative transitions and mechanical resolution, keeping combat fast, tense, and exciting.

---

## The Five-Step Turn Cycle

Every PC turn in combat follows this process:

1. **GM Transitions Into the Player's Turn** — brief narration setting the scene for that character
2. **Player Asks a Question or Declares an Action**
3. **Action is Resolved** — dice, mechanics
4. **GM Describes and Applies Results**
5. **GM Transitions Out of the Player's Turn** — narration that flows into the next character's turn

Steps 1 and 5 are the **narrative transitions** most GMs skip. They are the most important part of pacing.

---

## Begin and End with Narration

The transitions between turns should meld together. The resolution of one action sets the scene for the next:

> "You charge the goblin and smash it with your mace, bringing it to a stop. Its allies are hesitating. Bob, you've got an opening…"

This is both **resolution for Alice** and **scene-setting for Bob**. The dolphin leaps into mechanics and plunges back into narrative seamlessly.

---

## Initiative Tracking: The Legal Pad Method

Don't overcomplicate initiative. Use a piece of scratch paper:

1. Ask each player their initiative in order around the table
2. Write each name/initial and number on the page — high numbers near the top, low near the bottom
3. Leave space between entries for monsters
4. Roll for monsters and slot them in
5. Add monster HP next to their entries
6. Track damage, conditions, and notes right on the same sheet
7. Draw a line and start the next combat below

This sheet becomes the **combat dashboard** — initiative order, HP, conditions, everything in one place. Never delegate initiative tracking to a player; you need it for narrative transitions.

---

## Urgency: Making Combat Feel Like an Emergency

At the **start of each round**, briefly recap the battlefield situation — emphasize the emergencies:

> "Alice is battling the massive ogre in melee, both looking pretty hurt. One goblin sniper remains raining arrows on Bob. Another goblin is standing over Carol as she lies dying. Dave, you're still behind the tree."

At the **start of each player's turn**, point out the most pressing emergency near them:

> "Bob, you've got a goblin dancing around your attacks and that sniper won't leave you alone. What do you do?"

This **kickstarts the player's brain** by framing the situation in terms of urgency rather than mechanics.

---

## Exigency: The Ticking Clock of Each Turn

Players must begin speaking **immediately** when their turn starts. If they don't:

- Prompt them: "What do you do? You need to decide or you'll lose the turn to indecision."
- If they still can't decide, they take the **Dodge action** (lost turn to indecision)
- This almost never actually happens more than **once** — the threat alone keeps players focused

**Zero seconds** is the baseline for experienced players. After "what do you do," they should start talking immediately. They've been watching the battle unfold and should have been formulating plans.

Urgency and exigency work together — the scene-setting jumpstarts the brain, and the time pressure keeps them moving.

---

## Avoiding Speedbumps

### Assume the Best of Characters
- If a safe path exists between start and destination, **assume the character takes it**
- Don't force players to show exact square-by-square movement hoping to spring opportunity attacks
- Eyeball distances and lines of sight
- When a safe path is impossible, offer **simple choices**: "You can get there if you take an OA from the ogre, OR avoid it with a double move"

### Only the GM Opens Rulebooks
- Players should know their characters' abilities or defer to the GM
- If a player doesn't know a rule, the GM makes a ruling
- Looking up rules during combat kills the dolphin — make a judgment call and move on
- Players with heads in rulebooks get the book taken away

---

## The Adjustment Period

Introducing urgency and exigency takes **1–3 sessions** to settle in. Players will:
- Make decisions more quickly
- Be more attentive during others' turns
- Make better overall decisions

Start doing it and don't let up. It becomes second nature for everyone.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Narrative transitions** between turns matter more than flowery action descriptions
- The GM must **control the pace** — never delegate initiative tracking
- **Scene-setting** at the start of every turn creates urgency and focus
- **Time pressure** (exigency) prevents analysis paralysis and keeps players engaged
- Combat should feel **frantic and tense** — boredom is a luxury you don't allow
- Keep your combat dashboard simple: one piece of paper with everything on it
