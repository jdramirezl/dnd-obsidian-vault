# Dungeons and Dragons and Dismemberment

## Summary

Angry builds a simple called shot and dismemberment system for D&D 5E that allows players to target specific body parts of large or monstrous creatures. The system uses disadvantage as the universal cost, assigns separate HP pools to body parts, and ties creature abilities to those parts. He provides two complete monster examples — the Octomammoth (a tentacled cave beast whose limbs ARE its HP) and a Paragon Manticore (whose wings and tail can be destroyed while it keeps fighting). The system is designed to be simple enough to use ad hoc on any creature.

## Key Advice

### The Basic Called Shot Rule

- Any melee or ranged **attack roll** can target a specific body part by taking **disadvantage**.
- If the attack hits, the target is struck in that location.
- For normal humanoids, this has **no extra effect** — the damage roll determines whether you grazed an ear or blew a head off.
- Called shots cannot be made when already attacking with disadvantage.
- Only attack rolls qualify (not saving throw spells, not area effects, not magic missile).

### The Multipart Monstrosity System

Body parts get their own HP pools with these rules:
- Damage to a body part **also damages the creature** (spillover).
- When a body part hits 0 HP, it's destroyed and the creature loses associated abilities.
- Healing can be applied to body parts (if not destroyed).
- Body parts share the creature's stats except where noted.
- Body parts can have their own resistances/immunities (e.g., tentacles need slashing to sever, are immune to psychic).

### Two HP Distribution Methods

1. **Creature IS its parts** (Octomammoth): Divide total HP evenly among components. Destroying all parts kills the creature. Used for tentacled beasts, swarm-like creatures.
2. **Parts are breakable extras** (Manticore): Large parts = ¼ creature HP, small parts = ⅛ creature HP. Destroying parts removes abilities but leaves the creature with plenty of fight. Used for wings, tails, stingers.

### Why Players Don't Dismember

In modern D&D, dismemberment rules are only written into specific monsters (like the hydra). Since players can't dismember anything else, they never think to try it — even on the hydra. Introducing a general called shot rule teaches players that targeting body parts is always an option, making them more creative combatants.

### Ad Hoc Dismemberment

Once players learn the system, they'll try it on creatures you didn't plan for. Handle it on the fly:
- Apply disadvantage to the attack.
- Determine the effect (loses fly speed, loses sting attack, etc.).
- Estimate HP using the ¼/⅛ rule or the "parts equal the whole" rule.
- Rule resistances logically (slashing for limbs, no psychic for appendages).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Introduce the called shot rule to your table**: Explain it as a house rule at the start of a session. It's simple: disadvantage on the attack roll to target a specific body part.
2. **Start with a showcase monster**: Use a creature with obvious targetable parts (tentacles, wings, tail) for the first encounter so players learn the system naturally.
3. **Assign body part HP using the simple rules**: ¼ of creature HP for large parts (wings), ⅛ for small parts (tail, stinger). Or divide evenly if the creature IS its parts.
4. **Tie abilities to body parts**: Fly speed → wings. Sting attack → tail. Grapple → tentacles. When the part is destroyed, the ability is lost.
5. **Remember spillover**: Damage to a body part always also damages the creature. This prevents called shots from being a pure waste of damage output.
6. **Don't allow called shots on humanoids for extra effect**: A humanoid dies from losing any major body part, so the HP required to sever a limb equals the HP to kill the creature. No benefit, just disadvantage.
7. **Be ready to improvise**: Once players learn they can dismember, they'll try it on everything. Use the ¼/⅛ rule and common sense to adjudicate on the fly.
8. **Combine with Paragon rules**: The Multipart Monstrosity system works independently of Paragon monster rules and can be layered on top for boss fights.

> [!tip] Teach Through Play
> Don't just announce the called shot rule — demonstrate it with a creature that *begs* to be dismembered. A flying monster harassing the party from unreachable ledges teaches players to clip wings. A tentacled horror dragging allies underwater teaches them to sever limbs. Once players learn that targeting body parts is a viable tactic, they'll start thinking creatively about every monster they face — and that's exactly the kind of tactical thinking that makes combat memorable.
