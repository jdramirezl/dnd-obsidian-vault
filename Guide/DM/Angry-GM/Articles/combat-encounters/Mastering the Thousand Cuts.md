# Mastering the Thousand Cuts

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Mastering the Thousand Cuts](https://theangrygm.com/mastering-the-thousand-cuts/)
**Date:** September 30, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

## Core Concept

The **Art of the Cutaway** is how a GM shifts focus between characters, actions, scenes, and timelines. Mastering it means understanding that you can target *anyone* with your next GM Move regardless of where or when they are in the game world. A "Cutaway" isn't a special technique — it's just choosing your next move deliberately.

---

## The Three GM Moves

Every action a GM takes during play is one of three moves:

### 1. Queue an Action
Invite a player to declare what their character does. Add it to the Action Queue.
> "Adam, what does Ardrick do? Beth, while Ardrick gets ready, what is Beryllia doing?"

### 2. Resolve an Action
Pick an action from the Queue, determine the outcome (brain or dice), describe the result.
> "Roll Ardrick's attack. Nice! Solid hit! How much damage?"

### 3. Take a World Action
Something in the world acts — an NPC, a monster, a trap, an environmental event.
> "The orc swings! Crit! 17 damage to Ardrick's kidney-spleen."

**That's it.** All GM narration — transitions, scene-setting, cutaways — is just making these three moves happen with good timing and good targets.

---

## Pacing = Picking the Right Move and Target

After resolving any action, the GM chooses:
- Stick with this character and Queue their next Action?
- Resolve another character's Queued Action in this scene?
- Take a World Action here?
- Cut to a completely different scene, character, or time?

In combat, the turn order makes this easy. Outside combat, it's the GM's art.

---

## Keeping Track of Reality

**You must always know:**
- Where and when every PC is
- What they're doing
- The last thing they did

**Always have paper and pen.** No typing, no memory — physical scratch notes. End every session with a page covered in shorthand like:
> A inn landlord
> B ~~shopping~~ rumors 4 hr
> C t gild

This is your Action Queue — tracking who's doing what, where, and when.

---

## Scene Setting and Re-Setting

### Every Cutaway requires Scene Setting
The deeper the cut, the richer the scene-setting needed.

### Cutting Back requires Scene Re-Setting
When you return to a scene you left, remind players of:
- **Who's involved**
- **Where they are**
- **Who's present**
- **What's happening**
- **The scene's tone**
- **The last action taken** (especially for Threaded Actions)

> "Duke Dunderfloss has just revealed that Lady Ostentatia had been feeding him information. He sneered, 'I've been one step ahead of you this entire time.' Adam, how does Ardrick respond?"

The farther the teleportation and the longer you've been away, the more detailed the Re-Set.

---

## Temporal Management

When characters are doing things at different time scales (a 15-minute fight vs. a 4-hour research session), you must:

1. **Keep track of the clock** for each thread
2. **Signal time jumps** to players when cutting back in time
3. **Use standard temporal resolutions:** quick (seconds–1 min), medium (minutes–quarter hour), long (hours–full day)
4. **Catch everyone up** once each character fills a standard time chunk

> "At noon, Beryllia and Cabe return to the inn. Hours prior, though, as the sun started its morning climb, Danae entered the Temple."

**Avoid Time Paradoxes:** When characters are close enough to affect each other, keep them on the same clock with frequent cuts.

## When to Cut (and When Not To)

### Consider Cognitive Load
- Know your limits and your players' limits
- Start with **two threads**, avoid deep cuts, limit time travel
- Gradually push boundaries as you get comfortable

### Pacing and Engagement
- Cut to **vary speed, tension, and tone** — action scene to drama to comedy
- Cut to **re-engage** a player who's been sidelined too long
- **Don't cut** if everyone is enraptured by what's happening — spotlight time isn't a problem until a player says it is

### Threaded Actions
Strings of naturally flowing actions (conversations, searching a room) — generally **don't cut these**. But you can if:
- You cut at a point where you can easily Cut Back
- You Re-Set the Scene thoroughly when returning

## Dramatic Cutaway Techniques

### Dramatic Tension
Cut to create uncertainty about what happens next:
- **Cliffhanger** — reveal a problem, then cut before anyone can react
- **Pre-reveal cut** — cut just before opening the chest or revealing the answer
- **Post-reveal cut** — cut right after a bomb drops to let it breathe

### Dramatic Irony
Players know things their characters don't. Use this deliberately:
- Let players watch a conversation that reveals the Duke is onto them — then cut to the burglar who doesn't know
- Show the time running out in one thread while the other thread's player sweats

### Metanarrative Chicanery (The Fifth Element Technique)
Use the dissonance between player knowledge and character knowledge for maximum tension:

> The Duke dismisses Danae. Cut to: "Meanwhile, an hour ago, Cabe entered the study." Now Chris knows the clock is ticking but can't see it clearly — and you can narrate "searching the desk took ten minutes… now what?" while he does the math in his head.

Or the ultimate move — resolve the burglar's success, then cut back to the Duke revealing he knew all along:
> "I don't trust anything truly valuable to locks and chests; I keep my valuables on my person."

**Watch *The Fifth Element* (1997)** for the gold standard of this editing technique.

### Recontextualization
After a major revelation, a Cutaway gives players time to **re-examine everything that came before** in a new light. This "aha" feeling is intrinsically rewarding and certain player types can't get enough of it.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Cutaways are just **choosing your next GM Move deliberately** — they're not a special technique
- You only have **three moves**: Queue an Action, Resolve an Action, Take a World Action
- **Always track** where/when every PC is and what they last did — paper and pen, always
- **Re-Set the Scene** every time you cut back — who, where, what, tone, last action
- Start slow: **two threads, no deep cuts, no time travel** — then gradually expand
- Use cuts for **pacing, engagement, dramatic tension, and dramatic irony**
- The Art of the Cutaway is how you **edit the film of your game** — it's your biggest, most invisible mark on the experience
