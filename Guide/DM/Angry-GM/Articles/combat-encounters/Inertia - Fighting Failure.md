# Inertia — Fighting Failure

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Inertia: Fighting Failure](https://theangrygm.com/inertia-fighting-failure/)
**Date:** January 29, 2025
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Core Concept

**Inertia** is a game design concept — a Play Dynamic, not a Game Mechanic — that describes a game's constant pull toward a loss or its resistance to being won. Without Inertia, scenarios lack tension and urgency. It is one of the key concepts that separates True Scenario Designers from Mere Adventure Builders.

---

## Two Definitions of Inertia

### Strong Inertia
> A force that constantly pushes a game toward a loss.

The game is actively dragging the players toward failure. Think of a time bomb or a countdown — if the players do nothing, they lose.

### Weak Inertia
> A game's resistance to being won.

The game undoes or counters the players' progress. Even when players act, the game pushes back, making it hard to gain ground.

### Elastic Inertia
The longer you play and the harder you're winning, the more the game resists your efforts. Like the AI in *Mario Kart* — the better you do, the harder the game pushes back.

---

## Play Dynamics vs. Game Mechanics

**This distinction is critical.** Inertia is not a mechanic you can point to in a rulebook. It's a **Play Dynamic** — an emergent feeling that arises from the interaction between players and game mechanics.

- **Game Mechanics:** Hit Points, damage, spell slots, resting rules, Infection Decks, Player Decks
- **Play Dynamics:** Attrition, Inertia, Momentum — the feelings and pressures that emerge from mechanics rubbing against players

You cannot design "Inertia Mechanics." You design mechanics that, together, give rise to an Inertia Dynamic. Good mechanics do many things simultaneously — they can't be reduced to serving a single dynamic.

---

## Inertia Is Not Challenge

**Challenges** are individual elements players overcome (or fail to overcome) on the way to a Goal — combat encounters, puzzles, social interactions, entire adventures.

**Inertia** cannot be resolved. It's always there. It's a constant force, not an obstacle.

Analogy: An obstacle course with an elastic band tying you to the start line.
- The obstacles = **Challenges**
- The elastic band = **Weak Inertia** (resists your progress, pulls you back if you stop)
- A *Battle Royale* collar with a time bomb = **Strong Inertia** (you lose if you don't hustle)

## *Pandemic* as a Masterclass in Inertia

The board game *Pandemic* (Matt Leacock, 2008) demonstrates all three types of Inertia:

### Strong Inertia — The Player Deck
- Players draw from the Player Deck every turn for resources
- When the deck runs out, the game ends — it's a **time bomb / turn counter**
- But the deck also provides resources, so it's not purely an "Inertia Mechanic"

### Weak Inertia — The Infection Deck
- Every turn, new Disease Cubes appear on the board
- Players can spend all their actions removing cubes and barely break even
- The game **undoes player progress** every single turn
- The balance between actions available and cubes placed ensures players can rarely do more than tread water

### Elastic Inertia — Outbreaks
- Cities can only hold 3 Disease Cubes; overflow causes Outbreaks in adjacent cities
- More cubes on the board = more likely Outbreaks cascade
- The longer the game goes, the **harder it gets** — Inertia strengthens over time
- 8 total Outbreaks = game over (another Strong Inertia element)

### The Epidemic Cards — Surging Inertia
- Hidden in the Player Deck; when drawn, players get no resources
- Force more Disease Cubes onto the board
- Shuffle discarded Infection Cards back on top — same cities get re-infected
- Increase the infection rate permanently
- Create a **surge of short-term disaster** plus **long-term acceleration**
- But also have a subtle benefit: the "Localizing Effect" keeps cubes near players

## System Designers vs. Scenario Designers

Inertia is closer to the **Scenario Designer's** responsibility than the System Designer's. But good system designers should build mechanics that help Scenario Designers create Inertia Dynamics.

**D&D's problem:** It has very few system-level tools for advanced Scenario Design. It has a decent adventure-building toolset, but almost nothing to help with Macrochallenges, Inertia, or Momentum. This is why truly great scenario design in D&D is rare — the system doesn't help you.

### Attrition and Inertia
Attrition *sounds* like it should be Inertia, but in modern D&D, **you can't count on Attrition to serve as Inertia anymore**. The resource recovery mechanics (long rests restoring everything) have eroded Attrition's ability to function as a meaningful Inertia Dynamic.

## Key Takeaways

- **Inertia** = the game's constant pull toward loss or resistance to victory
- Comes in **Strong** (active push toward loss), **Weak** (undoes progress), and **Elastic** (gets harder over time) varieties
- It's a **Play Dynamic**, not a mechanic — it emerges from mechanics interacting with players
- It is **not** the same as Challenge — Challenges can be resolved; Inertia is always present
- Good Scenario Designers deliberately create Inertia Dynamics in their adventures
- D&D provides almost no system-level support for this — you have to build it yourself
- *Pandemic* is the gold standard example of masterful Inertia design
- Companion concept: **Momentum** (covered in the next article in the series)
