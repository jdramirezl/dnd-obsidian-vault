# Momentum: Building Victory

## Summary

Momentum is a gameplay dynamic — not a mechanic — whereby once players start winning, they become more likely to keep winning. It's the complement to Inertia (which pulls toward failure). Together, Momentum and Inertia create the push-pull tension that makes games feel engaging. Angry stresses that Momentum is distinct from mere Progress: clearing rooms in a dungeon is Progress, but finding a scroll of ice knife that helps against the lava dragon boss is Momentum. Scenario designers must deliberately build Momentum into their adventures; it rarely emerges on its own.

## Key Advice

### Momentum ≠ Progress

- **Progress** is getting closer to the finish line (killing minions, crossing miles).
- **Momentum** is how your progress empowers you to handle the scenario's major challenge.
- Merely overcoming obstacles doesn't count as Momentum. The designer must create ways that making progress also makes future challenges easier.

### Momentum as a Gameplay Dynamic

- Momentum is not a game mechanic. There's no "Momentum score."
- Sometimes a single mechanic creates a single dynamic (e.g., 4E Action Points), but this is the exception.
- Ideally, Momentum emerges organically from play rather than being bolted on as an abstract system.

### System-Level Momentum Tools

| Tool | System | How It Works | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Magical Items** | D&D (all editions) | Finding items mid-adventure arms players for future challenges | 4E made identification instant during short rests — a deliberate Momentum fix |
| **Action Points** | D&D 4E | Earn an AP every 2 encounters; spend for extra actions; reset on long rest | Affected both Momentum AND Inertia — created a Push Your Luck dynamic |
| **Bennies** | Savage Worlds | Start full, spend down through play | Works *backward* from Momentum — starts strong, dwindles |
| **Escalation Die** | 13th Age | Cumulative combat bonus each round | More of a Reversal/Turnaround mechanic than pure Momentum |

### Designing for Momentum

- Keep Momentum and Inertia in mind from the start of scenario design.
- Organic Momentum (finding a useful item on a dead monster) feels better than abstract Momentum (earning "Momentum Points").
- If you have to bolt on a mechanic late in design to introduce a missing dynamic, you messed up earlier.
- Optional side paths that reward exploration with tools for the main challenge are a great Momentum vehicle.
- Balance Momentum against Inertia based on the desired playfeel — *Pandemic* deliberately has stronger Inertia than Momentum to feel tense.

### Pandemic Examples

- **Research Centers**: Building more centers around the board reduces wasted movement actions, increasing odds of victory.
- **Curing diseases**: Once cured, you can remove all cubes of that color in a city with one action instead of one cube — each cure compounds your ability to manage outbreaks.

## Key Takeaways

1. Momentum is a compounding force of success — every good move makes the next one more likely to succeed.
2. Progress is not Momentum. You must design how progress empowers players against the core challenge.
3. System designers rarely provide good Momentum tools; it's on the scenario designer.
4. Organic Momentum (items, knowledge, allies) feels better than mechanical Momentum (abstract points).
5. The interplay between Momentum and Inertia is what makes games feel dynamic — balance them to your desired playfeel.
