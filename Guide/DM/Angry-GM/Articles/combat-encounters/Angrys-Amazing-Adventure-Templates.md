# Angry's Amazing Adventure Templates

## Summary

Angry presents a structural template system for planning D&D adventures based on two overlapping structures: the adventure structure (narrative arc) and the session structure (real-world play sessions). He introduces a budget system using XP "awards" as a unit of measurement that doubles as both an XP distribution method and a measure of relative table time. The result is four adventure templates (side trek, short, medium, long) that tell you exactly how many encounters, objectives, and climaxes to include.

## Key Advice

### Two Structures in Every Adventure

1. **Adventure Structure**: The narrative arc — motivation → obstacles → climax → resolution. Every adventure needs a major objective and a climactic encounter.
2. **Session Structure**: The real-world play session. Each session should ideally have its own mini-climax and sense of accomplishment, even if it's part of a longer adventure.

### Three Types of Encounters

Encounters are classified by *feel*, not difficulty:
- **Minor Encounters** (0.5 awards): Speed bumps. A couple of die rolls. No battlemat needed. Quick to resolve.
- **Major Encounters** (1 award): Standard encounters. Multiple rolls, action passes. Battlemat-worthy.
- **Climactic Encounters** (1.5 awards): Boss fights, set pieces, turning points. Same table time as major, but feel bigger and more important.

Difficulty ratings (easy/medium/hard/deadly) don't matter much — players rarely notice the difference. What matters is *scale and scope*.

### Two Types of Objectives

- **Minor Objectives** (0.5 awards): Intermediate goals, side pursuits, optional discoveries.
- **Major Objectives** (1 award): The main goal that resolves the adventure.

### The Four Adventure Templates

| Template | Sessions | Total Awards | Major Obj | Minor Obj | Climactic Enc | Remaining Awards |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Side Trek** | ½–1 | 4 | — | 1 | 1 | 1–2 |
| **Short** | 1–2 | 8 | 1 | — | 1 | 5 |
| **Medium** | 2–3 | 12 | 1 | 1 | 2 | 8 |
| **Long** | 3–4 | 18 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 11 |

Remaining awards are spent "buying" minor encounters (0.5 each) and major encounters (1 each).

### XP Award Values

One XP award = the amount of XP one PC earns for one medium-difficulty encounter at their level (DMG p. 82).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Choose your adventure length**: Decide if you're building a side trek, short, medium, or long adventure based on how many sessions you want it to fill.
2. **Lock in required elements**: Every adventure needs its climactic encounter(s) and objectives as specified by the template. Place these first.
3. **Spend your remaining budget**: Use the leftover awards to "buy" minor and major encounters. Mix combat, social, and obstacle encounters.
4. **Distribute climaxes across sessions**: For multi-session adventures, ensure each session has at least one climactic encounter and one minor objective so every session feels complete.
5. **Classify encounters by feel, not math**: Decide whether an encounter is minor, major, or climactic based on how it will *feel* at the table — not its CR or difficulty rating.
6. **Use the grid as a differentiator**: Minor encounters don't use the battlemat. Major and climactic encounters do. If you're using the grid, add features that make it worth using.
7. **Adjust on the fly**: If a session is running long, drop a minor encounter. If it's running short, add one. The template gives you the structure to see what's expendable.
8. **Don't follow templates religiously**: They're guidelines for seeing the structure of your adventure and making informed decisions, not rigid rules.

> [!tip] Budget Your Adventures
> Think of adventure design like a budget. Every adventure has a fixed number of "awards" to spend on encounters and objectives. Climactic encounters and objectives are mandatory line items — lock those in first. Then spend the remaining budget on a mix of minor and major encounters. This ensures your adventure has proper pacing, narrative structure, and a predictable session count without over- or under-preparing content.
