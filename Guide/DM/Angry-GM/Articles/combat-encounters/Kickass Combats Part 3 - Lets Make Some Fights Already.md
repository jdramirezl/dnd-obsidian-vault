# The Angry Guide to Kickass Combats (Part 3): Let's Make Some F'ing Fights Already!

## Summary

The capstone of the combat design series. Angry opens his process and walks through building four complete combat encounters from scratch, each starting from a different "seed" (creature, battlefield, ambiance, or full encounter concept). He introduces the **Platonic Party** — a conceptual, non-specific adventuring party used to mentally simulate encounters during design — and lays out a **10-step encounter building process** centered on imagining how the fight plays out, then deliberately disrupting the party's first-order strategy to force interesting decisions.

## Key Advice

### The Platonic Party

A conceptual understanding of the "standard adventuring party" — not your specific PCs, but archetypes:

| Archetype | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **Ragnar (Fighter)** | High survivability, powerful melee, controls a zone, wants to take hits and control enemy access |
| **Jozan (Cleric)** | Moderate survivability, support/healing, versatile firefighter, some ranged capability |
| **Lidda (Rogue)** | Mobile but moderate survivability, heavy damage when free to choose engagement, hamstrung when pinned |
| **Soveliss (Ranger)** | Mobile, higher survivability than rogue, versatile at both close and long range |
| **Mialee (Wizard)** | Glass cannon, devastating ranged/area damage, extremely vulnerable, loses versatility when allies are in melee |

**Why not use your actual PCs?** Building encounters against specific PCs is brutally unfair — it punishes them for their choices and means unusual strategies are never rewarded. Adjust the Platonic Party's *size* to match your group, but keep it generic.

### The 10-Step Encounter Building Process

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Seed** | Start with whatever you have: a creature, a battlefield, an ambiance, or a full encounter concept |
| **2. Creature** | Establish the Platonic Reality (strategy) of the creature — what it wants and how it fights |
| **3. Put Aside** | Set aside extra ideas and creatures; don't overload the concept yet |
| **4. Battle Concept** | Place the creature in Purgatory (empty void) or your battlefield; sketch vague terrain blobs |
| **5. Fight (1st Pass)** | Mentally simulate how the Platonic Party vs. the creature plays out |
| **6. Block** | Identify and disrupt the party's **first-order strategy** — force them to think and adapt |
| **7. Tweak** | Re-simulate; adjust difficulty up or down; facilitate hamstrung PCs if needed |
| **8. Numbers** | Run the XP budget math; adjust creature count to fit |
| **9. Map** | Convert concept blobs into actual terrain features; add props and flavor |
| **10. Start** | Determine starting positions and conditional openings based on PC actions |

### First-Order vs. Second-Order Strategy

- **First-Order Strategy** — The party's "go-to" script. If everything aligns perfectly, they execute it and win without thinking. This is boring.
- **Second-Order Strategy** — What the party does when their default plan is disrupted. This is where interesting decisions happen.
- **Your job:** Disrupt the first-order strategy so the party HAS to think.
- **NEVER block the second-order strategy.** This can make fights brutally, unfairly difficult. Even strong tactical players may take a round too long to adapt and get killed.

### Build Fair, Play to Win

- After building a fair encounter, **play the monsters to win**. Implement their ideal strategies. D&D is built not to let the DM win, so it's safe to try.
- If you don't play to win, all your careful encounter design is wasted.

### Four Worked Examples

#### 1. Hobgoblins at the Gate (Seed: Full Encounter)
- **Concept:** Hobgoblin defensive outpost; PCs must break through or stop an alarm.
- **Creature strategy:** Hobgoblins form defensive clusters, strong in melee when grouped, decent ranged but lose shield defense.
- **Block:** Added goblin archers on a rampart to threaten ranged PCs; created a chokepoint to prevent easy flanking; added rolling tower shields for hobgoblin cover.
- **Result:** Melee PCs advance under fire, ranged PCs engage goblins, rogue must choose between flanking hobgoblins or eliminating archers. Alarm gong within one move of a goblin — hard but not impossible to prevent.

#### 2. Ghoul Patrol (Seed: Creature)
- **Concept:** Ghouls ambush the party in a sewer.
- **Creature strategy:** Individualistic cannibals; each tries to paralyze someone and drag them off to eat.
- **Block:** Ghouls attack from multiple directions (surround), start close (within one move), emerge from partially boarded sewer pipes.
- **Result:** Party forced into a defensive circle; paralyzed PCs get dragged into pipes; allies must crawl after them or hack through boards. Terrifying.

#### 3. The Rescue (Seed: Ambiance)
- **Concept:** PCs on a ridge see an NPC being chased by gnolls; must intervene quickly.
- **Creature strategy:** Gnolls are melee bruisers with bonus movement; get extra attacks when they drop someone to 0 HP.
- **Block:** Removed gnoll ranged weapons (except one archer); placed PCs on a ridge requiring a skill check to descend quickly; NPC two moves from everyone.
- **Result:** PCs must decide: rush down the hill (risk tumbling) or stay on the ridge with ranged attacks while the NPC gets overwhelmed. Gnoll bonus-move-on-kill creates cascading danger.

#### 4. The Tree Root Cave (Seed: Battlefield)
- **Concept:** Vertical cave under a giant tree; root bridges, hanging roots, ankle-deep water pool below.
- **Creature strategy:** Troll regenerates; hides underwater to avoid fire/acid and heal. Stirges in the ceiling harass ranged PCs.
- **Block:** Troll retreats into deep water pits (immune to fire/acid there); stirges descend on wizard and ranger, forcing melee PCs to choose between pressuring the troll or rescuing allies.
- **Result:** Multi-axis fight — melee PCs chase troll through water while stirges create action-denial on ranged PCs above.

## Connections

- Completes the ABCs framework from Parts 1 and 2 (Ambiance, Battlefield, Creatures)
- The Platonic Party concept connects to the broader principle of designing for archetypes, not specific characters
- "Build fair, play to win" is a foundational DM philosophy that recurs across Angry's work
- The worked examples demonstrate that the same 10-step process works regardless of which seed you start with
