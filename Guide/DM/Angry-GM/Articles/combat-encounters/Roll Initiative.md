# Roll Initiative!

## Summary

Initiative is not synonymous with combat, not the same as turn order, and not something you're "in." It's simply a mechanical tool for determining which action resolves first when multiple creatures are racing to act simultaneously. Combat should start *before* you roll initiative — the GM freezes time at the moment someone communicates willingness to fight, builds an Action Queue to get player commitments, and only then calls for initiative rolls. Non-combatants (fleeing characters, negotiators) don't need initiative at all — their actions happen around the combat. This reframing opens up possibilities that strict turn-order rules normally cut off: covering retreats, forestalling fights, and handling mixed combat/non-combat situations.

## Key Advice

### When to Use Combat Resolution Tools

Combat occurs when all four conditions are met:

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Conflict** | Opposing forces have opposing goals |
| **Violence** | Both sides willing to injure/kill AND risk injury/death |
| **Roughly Equal** | Similar scale and power (not a massacre or colossus-climbing) |
| **Aware** | Combatants know they're in a fight and can defend themselves |

If any condition is missing, don't use combat rules. A rogue shanking a sleeping guard isn't combat. 15th-level heroes vs. 1st-level goblins isn't combat. Spartans holding a narrow pass against an army isn't combat (it's a single dramatic question resolved with a roll or two).

### What Initiative Actually Is

- Initiative models **reaction time** — how long it takes a character to go from "we're fighting now" to completing their first action.
- It's a tie-breaker for simultaneous actions, not a combat-start button.
- Initiative and Turn Order are separate concepts. Initiative determines order; Turn Order is the structure that results.
- You can use Initiative outside combat whenever simultaneous actions need ordering (two characters diving for the same gun, etc.).

### Starting Fights Right (The Action Queue Method)

1. **Someone initiates violence** — a goblin screams "Kill them!" or Ardrick yells "CHARGE!"
2. **Freeze time** at that moment. Describe the inception of the action.
3. **Build an Action Queue** — go around the table, invite each player to declare their response.
4. **Confirm combat is happening** — once it's clear from declarations that a fight is on.
5. **Call for Initiative rolls** — drop all the queued action bits after the frozen moment.
6. **Resolve the first round** — starting with whoever won initiative.

Example flow:
> **GM:** The goblin screams "Kill them!" and the goblins start forward. Ardrick is front and center — Adam, what does he do?
> **Adam:** Ardrick readies his sword to receive them.
> **GM:** Cabe is beside him. Chris?
> **Chris:** I palm my daggers and start throwing.
> **GM:** Beth, Beryllia is behind them?
> **Beth:** I'll cast protective ward on Cabe.
> **GM:** Let's roll initiative, everyone.

### Why This Matters

- **Investment**: Building the Action Queue before initiative creates player buy-in.
- **Last chance to avoid combat**: Players can try to forestall the fight (throw food at hungry monsters, offer treasure, flee).
- **Clever plays**: A player might interrupt the escalation with a creative action that prevents combat entirely.
- **Risk**: Trying to forestall a fight can backfire — the GM judges what's possible given timing and distance.

### Non-Combatants and the Action Queue

Characters who aren't fighting don't roll initiative. Their actions happen *around* the combat:

> **Adam:** Ardrick fights.
> **Beth:** Beryllia yells "Stop, we have treasure!"
> **Chris:** Cabe runs away.

Resolution: Cabe flees and Beryllia shouts (both happen around the fight). Only Ardrick and the goblins roll initiative. Beryllia can join later if she decides to fight. Cabe's flight can be resolved after combat or cut to separately.

### Key Principles

- **No one is just waiting for their turn** — everything happens nearly simultaneously. Defensive scores (AC, Dex saves) assume active dodging and deflecting.
- **Ambushes bypass initiative** — if one side is unaware, resolve the opening salvo first, THEN roll initiative once everyone's aware.
- **You can't Ready actions outside combat** — being prepared doesn't bypass reaction time. That's what initiative models.
- **Per-round initiative rolling** is a strong argument from this framework — each round is a new race to act first.
- **HP and AC are combat tools** — outside combat, use ability checks with contextual DCs instead of attack rolls against AC.

### Willing-Minded and Able-Bodied Only

- Creatures that don't know they're in a fight can't fight → resolve actions against them without combat rules.
- Creatures unwilling to fight (cowering, surrendering) aren't combatants → don't lock them into turn order.
- Creatures vastly outmatched aren't in "combat" → resolve the situation narratively or with a single dramatic roll.
- Most creatures don't actually want to fight — predators want easy kills, guards want to scare intruders away, soldiers prefer surrender. Only PCs are kill-or-be-killed idiots.

## Key Takeaways

1. Initiative is a reaction-time tie-breaker, not a combat-start button.
2. Build an Action Queue before calling for initiative — freeze time, get commitments, then roll.
3. Only aware, willing, able combatants in a roughly equal violent conflict need initiative and turn order.
4. Non-combatants' actions happen around the combat, not within the turn order.
5. This method gives players a last chance to avoid or forestall combat with clever play.
6. HP and AC are combat-only tools — don't use attack rolls to resolve non-combat violence.
7. The real secret: Initiative isn't a combat rule. It's just a way of determining which simultaneous action happens first.
