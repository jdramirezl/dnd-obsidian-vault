# Between Jobs

## Summary

Angry lays the conceptual groundwork for a downtime system by defining what downtime *should* be in a fantasy adventure RPG. He argues that downtime activities must serve the adventure cycle — they must be fed by adventures and feed into future adventures. Activities that don't impact gameplay (like D&D's carousing rules) are worthless. Meaningful downtime requires constraints, opportunity costs, hidden information, and real choices — not just random tables and gold sinks.

## Key Advice

### Downtime Must Serve the Adventure Cycle

Non-adventure activities are only valuable if they create a **feedback loop** with adventures:
- What players do during adventures should impact what they can do between adventures.
- What they do between adventures should affect how their next adventure goes.

**Good downtime activities**: Crafting items, buying equipment, doing research, gaining temporary boons, resting, treating illnesses, lifting curses, replenishing supplies, repairing equipment, selling treasure, spending XP on advancement.

**Bad downtime activities**: Carousing (random table, no choices, no adventure impact), running businesses (no mechanical hooks to gameplay), building strongholds (no feedback into adventures without GM invention).

### The Card Game Analogy

Think of downtime like the draw/discard/refresh phases in a card game. The "main phase" is the adventure. The phases before and after involve drawing cards, readying resources, and making strategic choices that affect the main phase. Downtime is the prep and cleanup phase — not the game itself, but strategically important.

### Meaningful Choices Require Constraints

For downtime to involve real choices, it needs:
- **Time pressure**: Players should never have quite enough time to do everything they want. Unpredictable downtime duration is a feature, not a bug.
- **Opportunity costs**: Doing one thing means not doing another. Crafting potions means not doing research.
- **Risks and surprises**: Random outcomes and hidden information make choices hard to optimize.
- **Real costs**: Money and time must actually matter. D&D's wealth scaling makes monetary costs meaningless, and time has no inherent value unless it can't be spent on something else.

### Time Is Just an Exchange Rate

Time's value comes from the fact that you can't do two things at once. The key question: "If you only have three days in town, what are your priorities?" That's a meaningful choice. "You have unlimited time, what do you do?" is not.

### Downtime Must Be Streamlined

Downtime isn't the game — it's the prep/cleanup phase. It must be:
- Easy to manage and adjudicate
- Quick to present to players
- Predictable enough for the GM to prep consequences between sessions

## Actionable Steps

1. **Create a feedback loop**: Ensure every downtime activity either prepares for the next adventure or cleans up from the last one. If an activity doesn't connect to adventuring, cut it or redesign it.
2. **Impose time constraints**: Never give players unlimited downtime. Use unpredictable durations — "the ship leaves in 1d4+1 days" — to force prioritization.
3. **Make downtime choices meaningful**: Present more activities than players have time for. Healing, crafting, research, shopping, reputation management — they can't do it all.
4. **Avoid random-table downtime**: Carousing tables and business event tables involve no player choice. Replace them with activities that require decisions and have adventure-relevant consequences.
5. **Build mechanical hooks**: If you want strongholds, businesses, or contacts to matter, create mechanical systems that connect them to adventure gameplay (e.g., a contact provides intel, a business generates a specific resource).
6. **Keep it fast**: Downtime should take minutes of real time, not hours. If setup and breakdown take longer than the game itself, players won't engage.
7. **Let the GM prep consequences**: Design downtime so the GM knows in advance what complications might arise, allowing them to weave downtime consequences into the next adventure rather than improvising on the spot.

> [!tip] Never Quite Enough Time
> The secret to meaningful downtime is scarcity. Give players a list of useful things they *could* do between adventures, then give them less time than they need to do all of them. When the bard needs two days to perform at the college, the wizard needs three days to craft a potion, and the ship leaves in two days — *that's* when downtime becomes a game of real choices and real trade-offs.
