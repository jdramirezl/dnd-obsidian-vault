# Shallow Kombat! Why D&D Combat is Shallow

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/shallow-kombat/)
**Date:** December 8, 2023

---

D&D combat is shallow by design. Many GMs sense something is wrong — fights devolve into everyone dishing out maximum damage until one side drops — but can't articulate why. The system's combat engine is detailed and mechanically complex, yet the actual fights are mediocre, bland, and lack depth. And there's no saving them without gutting the whole system.

## The "Race to Zero Hit Points" Problem

Combat is violent conflict resolution. Once two parties decide to risk injury or death, the conflict is about who dies first. Each side always has two goals: remove the other side as an obstacle, and don't get removed yourself. Routs, surrenders, and disables are outcomes, not goals — you can't force them.

"Alternative combat goals" won't fix this. If you start a fight, you've decided injuring and killing is the way to accomplish your goal. Even morale and nonlethal options are usually keyed to damage.

## Why the System Produces Shallow Combat

D&D's attrition-based macrochallenge ensures that as long as everyone dishes out at least average damage every round, the players win in 3–5 rounds. Even if a character goes down, they're unlikely to die before the fight ends. The players just need to click "Attack" every round.

**Key systemic factors:**

- **Offense-only design.** Most character options are offensive. There's no meaningful way to allocate your turn to defense. The Dodge/Parry options just make you fall behind in the damage race.
- **No readable opponents.** Players can't predict when a monster's powerful attack is coming, so they can't allocate resources to defense. Some monsters reward turtling then counterattacking, but players never recognize those opportunities.
- **Passive defenses.** Attackers test against static defense stats. Your only defense is to stand there and tank the hit.
- **Constraints removed over editions.** Opportunity attacks, cover rules, resistances, immunities — elements that restricted tactics and forced creative play — have been sanded down. Anyone can safely stand anywhere and attack anyone with anything.
- **Advantage/disadvantage ceiling.** Once you have advantage, there's nothing more to gain from stacking favorable positions. No incentive for layered tactical play.

## The 4E Lesson

In 3E, undead resisted almost everything — no sneak attacks, no crits, no poison. Players hated it because they had to work at it. WotC's response for 4E: "Every character must be able to use their signature abilities on every foe all the time." In effect: every character must have an "Attack" button they can spam in any fight. Every edition since has continued removing constraints that added depth.

## Turn-Based Combat's Inherent Challenge

Turn-based combat is hard to do well. Joseph Anderson's critique: bare-bones turn-based combat "can be summed up in one sentence: 'Make sure you spend some turns healing damage or else you die.'" D&D doesn't even require that — the math ensures players win the damage race by default.

## Why Players Don't Notice

Players have lots of options for attacking, buffing, and debuffing. Those choices feel meaningful and exciting. The lack of stakes doesn't matter — there's enough spectacle to satisfy. It feels like a tactical challenge even though it isn't one. Only the GM behind the curtain can see the illusion.

Some players do get bored — warlock players sighing "I guess I'll eldritch blast again" — but by and large, players like D&D combat as-is.

## The Real Question

Maybe the problem is you, the GM. If it's fun and feels challenging and exciting, aren't you giving your players a good game? Maybe ask yourself: "Why do I think D&D's Shallow Kombat is a problem in need of a fix?"

Angry's secret: he thinks it IS a problem worth addressing. But he wants GMs to understand WHY before demanding fixes. The combat is shallow by design and stays that way because it keeps players happy. It'll take more than tweaks, hacks, or alternative goals to add depth.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Don't expect alternative combat goals to fix the problem.** Combat is inherently a kill-or-be-killed race.
- **Upping difficulty alone doesn't help.** It just makes the damage race longer or shorter — tactics don't change.
- **The system rewards offense over everything.** Until defense becomes a meaningful choice, players will always spam attacks.
- **Consider whether this is actually a problem at your table.** If your players are having fun, the shallow combat may be working as intended.
- **If you want depth, you may need a different system.** D&D's combat engine is fundamentally designed for spectacle over strategy.
