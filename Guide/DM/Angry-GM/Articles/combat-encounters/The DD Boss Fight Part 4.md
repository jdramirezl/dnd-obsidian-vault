## Key Advice

- **A Boss is a Solo monster split into three Stages.** Each Stage is a separate stat block with one-third of the Solo's total HP. All three Stages form a single encounter worth the full Solo XP.
- **No Stage has a bloodied state—except Stage 3.** Stage 3 is always considered bloodied, letting players use abilities that key off that condition.
- **Stage Transitions are triggered "no action" abilities** that fire when a Stage hits 0 HP. The old creature is completely removed and replaced by the next Stage as an entirely new creature. Nothing carries over.
- **Each Stage gets one action point.**
- **Don't standardize condition resistance or action economy.** Every Boss should address stun, dominate, and daze (plus ideally immobilize, restrained, prone), but do it in flavorful, monster-specific ways rather than slapping on a generic "Boss Resilience" power.
- **The action economy problem has many solutions.** Double turns, mobility powers, threatening reach, minor-action attacks, triggered actions, auras, and minion summoning can all fill the gap. Pick what fits the creature.
- **Stage Transitions should telegraph the new strategy** when possible, changing how the monster engages the party and involving different PCs.
- **One Stage can be replaced with equivalent-XP monsters or a skill challenge.** A boss can break into smaller creatures, retreat behind guards, or trigger a trap gauntlet—as long as the replacement fills one-third of the encounter's XP and damage output.
- **Skill challenge replacements must deal damage on failures** (directly to HP, not surges) to maintain encounter pressure. Success/failure should affect the next stage narratively, not mechanically (don't subtract HP from the next stage).
- **Reality Check your Boss with two tests.** The Output Check: imagine the boss fighting an average party—is it making 4–5 attacks per round, spread across multiple PCs? The "What Do I Do Now" Check: is every PC engaged in at least two of the three stages?

## Summary

This article codifies the final Boss Fight system for D&D 4E. A Boss Monster is a Solo split into three sequential Stages, each with its own stat block and one-third of the Solo's HP. When a Stage drops to 0 HP, a Stage Transition removes it entirely and introduces the next Stage as a fresh creature—nothing carries over. Stage 3 is always bloodied. Rather than standardizing condition resistance and extra actions, Angry argues each Boss should handle these problems in unique, flavorful ways (a dragon resists differently than an ogre or mind flayer). The system supports replacing any Stage with equivalent-XP standard monsters or a skill challenge, enabling creative mid-fight shifts like a villain fleeing through traps or splitting into smaller creatures. Two reality checks—Output (enough attacks spread across PCs?) and "What Do I Do Now" (every PC engaged?)—validate the design. The article includes a Young Red Dragon as a worked example with Draconic Alacrity and Draconic Resilience as dragon-specific solutions to the action economy and condition problems.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Split your Solo into three Stages** with separate stat blocks, each at one-third total HP and one action point. No bloodied state on Stages 1–2; Stage 3 is always bloodied.
2. **Design a Stage Transition for each Stage** as a "no action" triggered ability at 0 HP that removes the old creature and introduces the next. Use it to telegraph the new fighting style.
3. **Address conditions per-monster.** Decide how this specific creature handles stun, dominate, daze, immobilize, restrained, and prone. Ignoring one should be a deliberate design choice.
4. **Ensure 4–5 attacks per round** through some combination of multiple attacks, triggered actions, opportunity attacks, minor-action attacks, or auras.
5. **Make each Stage feel different.** Change the monster's engagement style so different PCs shine in different stages.
6. **Consider replacing one Stage** with equivalent-XP standard/elite monsters or a damage-dealing skill challenge for variety.
7. **Run the Output Check and the "What Do I Do Now" Check** before bringing the Boss to the table.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> The Boss Fight system turns a single boring Solo slog into a three-act dramatic encounter. Split the monster into three Stages, make each Stage fight differently, handle conditions and action economy with creature-specific flavor, and validate with two simple reality checks. The modularity means nothing carries between Stages, keeping the design clean and the encounter dynamic.
