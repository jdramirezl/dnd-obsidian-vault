# A Wandering and a Wondering

## Summary

Angry dissects what makes exploration actually *feel* like exploration in TTRPGs, arguing that D&D's claim of exploration as a "fundamental pillar" is hollow because the system provides zero mechanical support for it. He identifies four keys to the "gamefeel" of exploration and explains why most homebrew and published solutions fail — they simulate exploration without creating the feeling of it. The article lays groundwork for a future system to make towns and civilized areas genuinely explorable.

## Key Advice

### The Four Keys to the Gamefeel of Exploration

1. **Satisfying Curiosity** — Exploration starts when players have a question and seek the answer. Questions can be spatial ("what's behind that door?"), historical ("who built these ruins?"), social ("why is the innkeeper troubled?"), or mechanical ("what does this lever do?").

2. **Intellectual Conquest** — Exploration is a way of gaining power through knowledge. Every answered question gives the players more mastery over the game world. Revealing the map, understanding NPC motives, learning how areas connect — all of this is power.

3. **Self-Direction** — Exploration must be player-driven. The GM can provide opportunities and pose interesting questions, but the players must choose to pursue them. Assigned "explore 90% of rooms" tasks kill the feeling. Goals are almost anathema to exploration.

4. **Varied Discoveries as Payoffs** — Without payoffs, players stop exploring. Discoveries must vary in *kind* (lore, upgrades, shortcuts, challenges, items, abilities), *scope*, and *scale*. Information-only rewards have diminishing returns. The best explorable worlds (e.g., Hollow Knight) offer many different *types* of things to find.

### Why Exploration Fails in TTRPGs

- **System-game separation**: TTRPG systems are toolkits; they rely on GMs to build exploration, but provide no tools for it.
- **Verbal description problem**: Players can't notice things on their own — the GM describes what's important and omits what's not, so everything described feels like a Chekhov's Gun.
- **Lack of reward variety**: D&D's reward structure is mostly gold and magic items. There's no built-in system for discoverable abilities, shortcuts, boons, or information rewards.

### Exploration Is Not Travel Logistics

Encumbrance, overland travel, supply management, starvation rules — none of this is exploration. These are *costs* of exploring, not exploration itself. A cost for a thing is not the thing.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Design around curiosity**: Seed your world with things that provoke questions — anomalies, mysteries, inconsistencies, and hints of deeper stories.
2. **Vary your discovery types**: Don't just scatter gold and magic items. Include useful information, temporary boons, shortcuts, hidden areas, optional challenges, NPC storylines, and environmental beauty/dread.
3. **Make exploration self-directed**: Present opportunities to explore but let players choose. Don't assign exploration as a task or checklist.
4. **Reward knowledge with power**: Information players discover through exploration should give them tangible advantages — weaknesses of upcoming enemies, safer routes, better negotiating positions.
5. **Consider adding monster/trap lore**: Create discoverable tidbits about creatures and hazards that players can find through research, divination, or environmental clues before encountering them.
6. **Don't front-load all information**: Keep some character options, world details, and mechanical rewards as things to be discovered through play rather than read in a book.

> [!tip] Wonder and Wander
> Exploration boils down to two words: **wonder** and **wander**. Players must wonder about something ("I wonder what's over there"), then wander off to satisfy that curiosity. If there's nothing wonderful to find, they'll stop wandering. If they keep stumbling over wonderful things, they'll start wandering even without wondering. Design your world to provoke wonder and reward wandering with varied, meaningful discoveries.
