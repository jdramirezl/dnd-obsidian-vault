# An Angry Shower Scene

## Summary

Angry walks through his "mental predesign" process — the thinking that happens before any mechanical design work. Using the third scene of his example adventure ("Kobolds Stole the Thing; Get the Thing!"), he demonstrates how to design a scene as a complete mini-game with its own win condition, loss condition, player agency, and information management. The key insight: every scene needs a way to lose that isn't just TPK, and players need enough information to play the right game.

## Key Advice

### Design Scenes as Complete Games

Every scene should have:
- **A goal**: What the players are trying to accomplish.
- **A conflict**: What prevents them from just accomplishing it.
- **A win condition**: How they succeed.
- **A loss condition (not just death)**: How they can fail without dying.
- **Player agency**: Ways players can affect their odds of winning or losing.
- **Information requirements**: What players need to know to play the scene properly.

### The Information Gap

The difference between what players know and what they need to know to play a scene properly is the **information gap**. The GM must either:
- **Close the gap directly** (feed information unambiguously) — makes the adventure easier.
- **Plant clues and let players close it** — adds a mental challenge.

If players don't know the real rules of the game they're playing, they'll default to the wrong strategy (e.g., methodically searching every room when they should be moving fast and quiet).

### Mental Predesign ("Shower Time Design")

Before touching mechanics, maps, or stat blocks, think through:
1. How is this scene played?
2. How do players win?
3. How do players lose (besides dying)?
4. How can players affect their odds?
5. What do players know vs. what do they need to know?
6. How does the villain/opposition behave logically?

This process is fast once practiced — Angry did it in one shower. It produces better adventures than jumping straight to maps and encounters.

### The "Click" Moment

As you predesign, your brain will spontaneously connect disparate elements of your adventure. Backstory, narrative details, and mechanical implications will suddenly snap together. You don't need a backstory before you start designing — you can fill it in as the design reveals what's needed.

### Alert Tracker Example

For the kobold mine scene, Angry designed a three-state alert system:
1. **Unaware**: Kobolds are distracted, packing to leave. Easy encounters.
2. **Alerted**: Some kobolds shift to defense; patrols appear. Harder encounters.
3. **Overrun**: Full panic. Dragonborn flees with the MacGuffin. Players must intercept or lose.

Each encounter has three versions based on alert state. The dragonborn's escape route uses branching, looping tunnels.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start with the final scene**: Design the climax first so you know what information and resources earlier scenes need to provide.
2. **Define win and loss conditions for every scene**: Death should almost never be the only way to lose. Create loss conditions tied to the adventure's stakes (villain escapes, artifact is destroyed, time runs out).
3. **Identify the information gap**: List what players know at the start of each scene vs. what they need to know. Decide whether to close the gap directly or through clues.
4. **Plant clues in earlier scenes**: Use the first scene to seed information needed for the final scene. Reward players who protect NPCs or explore thoroughly with useful intel.
5. **Think through villain logic**: Ask what the villain would realistically do. Their behavior creates the mechanical framework (alert states, escape triggers, defensive priorities).
6. **Build simple trackers, not simulations**: A three-state alert tracker is enough. Don't simulate individual kobold movements — players will never know the difference.
7. **Practice mental predesign**: Before every session, spend idle time (shower, walk, commute) thinking through how scenes play out as games. It gets fast with practice.

> [!tip] Design Scenes Backwards
> Build your adventure's final scene first. Once you know the win condition, loss condition, and information requirements of the climax, you can design earlier scenes to feed into it — planting clues, establishing stakes, and giving players the tools they need. The scene structure lets you break an adventure into interconnected mini-games whose outcomes cascade into each other.
