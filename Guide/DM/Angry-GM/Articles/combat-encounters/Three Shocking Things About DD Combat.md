## Key Advice

- **There is no such thing as a "combat encounter."** Combat is conflict resolution through violence—it's something that happens *within* an encounter, not the encounter itself. An encounter has a goal, an endpoint, and sources of conflict. Combat is just one way to resolve that conflict.
- **Thinking of combat as the encounter causes two problems.** First, you close off non-combat solutions (negotiation, fleeing, sneaking, surrendering). Second, you refuse to end the encounter until the combat ends, dragging out boring mop-up phases.
- **Combat rules are an optional overlay, not a mode switch.** Initiative, action economy, and attack rolls don't override the core game loop (describe situation → player decides → resolve action → repeat). Everything possible outside combat is still possible inside combat. Dungeon World proves this by having no combat chapter at all.
- **The "Combat Swoosh" problem:** Rolling initiative signals a mode switch like a JRPG battle screen. But the underlying game hasn't actually changed. Skills, ability checks, creative actions—all still work.
- **Avoid using combat rules whenever possible.** They only work well for pitched battles between roughly equal forces. Zombie hordes, colossal giants, chases, and retreats all break down under standard combat rules. Run those narratively instead.
- **"Include alternate goals in combat" is bad advice.** Combat doesn't have goals—encounters do. If you know why the conflict is happening and what both sides want, alternate goals handle themselves.
- **"Build a way out of combat" is also bad advice** as typically framed. Battles end in exactly three ways: one side can't continue, the other side can't continue, or both sides are unwilling to continue. That's it.
- **Every round, decide if each creature is still willing to fight.** This is the most important decision DMs never make. Don't use morale dice—you already decide tactics, targets, and movement. Deciding whether to keep fighting is just another decision based on the creature's nature and situation.
- **Delay initiative as long as possible. Drop out of combat rules as soon as you can.** Give players a moment before hostilities begin. End the encounter when the dramatic question is answered, not when the last HP drops.

## Summary

Three foundational truths about D&D combat that most DMs get wrong. First: there's no such thing as a "combat encounter"—combat is conflict resolution through violence that happens within encounters, not the encounter itself. Treating combat as the encounter closes off non-violent solutions and forces boring mop-up phases. Second: combat rules are unnecessary in the strict sense—they're an overlay on the core game loop, not a replacement for it. Dungeon World demonstrates this by running fights with no combat chapter at all. Everything possible outside combat (skills, creative actions, ability checks) remains possible inside it. Third: combat rules should be avoided when they don't fit. They work for pitched battles between roughly equal forces but break down for zombie hordes, colossal monsters, chases, and retreats. In those cases, run things narratively. Practical takeaways include: always know why a fight is happening and what both sides want; decide every round whether each creature is willing to keep fighting (no morale dice needed); delay rolling initiative as long as possible; and drop out of combat structure the moment the dramatic question is answered.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Before any potential combat, define the encounter's dramatic question.** What does each side want? Why is there conflict? Combat is just one possible resolution.
2. **Give players a beat before hostilities.** Instead of "the goblins charge, roll initiative," try "the goblins grab their weapons and snarl—what do you do?" Let players forestall the fight if they choose.
3. **Every round, ask yourself: "Is this creature still willing to fight?"** Consider its nature, intelligence, self-preservation instinct, and current situation. Hobgoblins hold the line; goblins scatter; animals flee when wounded; undead never stop.
4. **End encounters when the dramatic question is answered.** If the party has clearly won, narrate the cleanup: "You dispatch the remaining two zombies and move on." Don't waste rounds on foregone conclusions.
5. **Don't use combat rules for mismatched fights.** Zombie hordes, colossal creatures, and chases should be run narratively—shotgun the turn order, let hits auto-kill mooks, and go with your gut.
6. **Stop thinking about "combat outs."** Instead, understand the three ways battles end (one side unable, other side unable, both sides unwilling) and play the monsters accordingly.
7. **Remember that all non-combat rules still apply in combat.** Skill checks, ability checks, creative problem-solving—none of it goes away when initiative is rolled.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Combat is not an encounter—it's one way conflicts get resolved within encounters. The combat rules are an optional structure that works best for balanced, pitched battles. For everything else—hordes, giants, chases, retreats—drop the rules and run narratively. Always know why the fight is happening, decide each round if creatures keep fighting, and end the encounter when the dramatic question is answered, not when the last HP hits zero.
