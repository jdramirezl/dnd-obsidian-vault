# Tactical Infiltration Action

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/tactical-infiltration-action/)
**Date:** December 18, 2023

---

A supplemental lesson to the True Game Mastery course on running stealth and infiltration scenes. The core argument: if you already know how to run a game like a True Game Master (Declare-Determine-Describe cycle, action adjudication, handling simultaneous actions), stealth scenes work themselves out. The key ingredient is **dynamism** — a back-and-forth game of "this is what you know; now how do you react?"

## Stealth Ain't Sneaking

**Stealth** is a skill/trait — what makes a character good at sneaking. **Sneaking** is an action — an attempt to avoid notice. Stealth is NOT a mode you toggle on. Characters take actions, and some of those actions benefit from the Stealth skill.

Players must declare specific sneaking actions:
- "I dart from shrub to shrub when the guard looks away"
- "I throw a rock to draw the guard's attention, then dash under the watchtower"

If there's a lookout watching an open courtyard with no cover, no amount of Stealth gets you through unseen. **Stealth doesn't replace gameplay** — it provides bonuses on sneaky actions, but players still have to plan their path, use cover, and create opportunities.

## Perception and Awareness

### Levels of Awareness

Don't think of Perception in binary terms. There are levels:

| State | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Inattentive/Relaxed** | Less likely to notice things | Guards dicing in barracks, navigators, sleepers |
| **Alert** (default) | Normal chance to notice | Guard on duty, PC on watch |
| **Aware** | Knows/suspects something is present, but no details | Heard a rustling, noticed an unusual floor tile |

**Alert is the default state** — use unmodified Passive Perception. Inattentive characters get penalties. Aware characters need to interact with the world to learn more.

### Key Principle

When players successfully notice something, it should lead to MORE gameplay — interaction and investigation — not less. A successful check raises more questions than it answers. You don't "spot a secret door," you "notice a seam in the brickwork."

## Awareness at Distance

Older editions provided crucial sensory range information that modern games lack. Rules of thumb:

### Sight Distances (Clear Conditions, Open Line of Sight)

| Distance | What You Can See |
|---|---|
| **1 mile / 5,000 ft** | Movement of person-sized things |
| **2,500 ft** | Person-sized thing exists (no details) |
| **1,500 ft** | Gross features: shape, size, color. Human vs. orc |
| **300 ft** | Identify individuals, coats of arms, armor, large weapons |
| **30 ft** | Every tiny detail including facial cues and actions |

### Sound Distances (Quiet Conditions)

| Distance | What You Can Hear |
|---|---|
| **500 ft** | Sounds of combat |
| **250 ft** | Normal conversation (audible, not intelligible) |
| **150 ft** | Armored person's movements |
| **100 ft** | Unarmored person's movements; reliable speech comprehension |
| **30 ft** | Loud back-and-forth conversation |
| **10–20 ft** | Normal conversation |

**These are the human maximums** — the farthest ranges at which to consider checking for Awareness.

### Why Distance Matters

Most GMs don't tell players what they can see/hear beyond spitting distance. If players don't know guards are behind a door until they're standing in it, there's no sneaking past. **Give players sensory information at appropriate distances** so they can make meaningful decisions.

## The Emergent Cat and Mouse Game

Good stealth scenes aren't scripted — they emerge from proper action resolution with appropriate information:

1. The lookout hears a sound 100 feet away. They don't know it's a goblin — just a rustling.
2. "What do you do?" Does the lookout investigate? Call out? Ready a weapon?
3. Does the goblin see the lookout's reaction? Does it freeze? Retreat? Ready a bow?
4. Does the lookout see what the goblin does? Or does the rustling just stop?
5. And so on — one action after another, each providing just enough information.

The same dynamic applies to party infiltrations: crossing a guarded courtyard, hearing a patrol approaching, passing a barracks door. And when things go wrong: can they silence the guards before the alarm? Can they hide? What happens when someone comes nosing around?

## Armored Characters and Stealth

The complaint that only stealthy characters get to play in stealth scenes is a feature, not a bug. The party must choose their risk:

- **Sneaks go alone** — but if caught, they're dead without backup
- **Toughs leave armor behind** — but fight unarmored if discovered
- **Toughs keep armor and accept penalties** — hope the party can contain individual failures
- **Party finds creative mitigations** — this IS the gameplay

Adventuring is at its best when characters have to do things they're not good at, or find ways to mitigate weaknesses, or do damage control when something fails.

## Practical Takeaways

- **Stealth is not a mode.** Players declare specific sneaking actions; Stealth provides bonuses.
- **Use distance and sensory ranges.** Give players information at appropriate distances so they can plan.
- **Think in levels of awareness:** Inattentive → Alert → Aware. Each level leads to more gameplay.
- **Successful perception checks should raise questions, not answer them.** "You hear rustling" not "there's a goblin."
- **Let stealth scenes emerge dynamically** from proper action-by-action resolution. Don't script them.
- **Armored characters struggling with stealth is part of the game.** The party choosing how to handle that IS the interesting decision.
