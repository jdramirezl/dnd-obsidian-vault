## Key Advice

You should be able to run a good game — including combat — with nothing but your voice. The grid is a crutch that tracks relationships between battlefield elements, but most of that data is irrelevant at any given moment. By staying "zoomed out," keeping details blurry until forced to pin them down, and thinking in terms of intentions and bargains rather than positions and distances, you can run fast, dynamic combats without a map.

## Summary

Angry argues that every GM should be able to run combat without a grid, tokens, or visual aids. Not because maps are bad, but because relying on them atrophies your core GMing skills. He calls this approach "narrative combat" (deliberately avoiding the pretentious "theater of the mind" label) and provides a framework for how to think about it.

**What the grid actually does:**
- Tracks exact positions and distances between every element on the battlefield.
- Shows relationships: who can interact with whom, and the state of paths between them.
- Most of this data is useless at any given moment — the fighter only cares who's within 5 feet; the ranger only cares about line of sight.

**The zoom principle:**
- Always watch combat from the highest altitude possible. See clumps of activity, not individual squares.
- A one-sentence battlefield description ("two groups 60 feet apart with a pond between them") can resolve the entire first round.
- Only zoom in when a specific question demands precision. Zoom back out as soon as possible.

**The blur principle:**
- Anything you haven't pinned down to an exact location could be anywhere. Don't define details until forced.
- Once pinned, a detail is fact — but it can become blurry again when creatures move or the situation changes.
- If something is vague, the first person to assume something about it is right (player or GM).
- Terrain works the same way: "trees scattered around" means cover is available everywhere until someone claims a specific spot.

**Intentions over actions:**
- Describe monster actions in terms of *why*, not *where*: "The hobgoblins spread out to avoid another area spell" rather than "Hobgoblin 1 moves 30 feet left."
- Players naturally follow this lead, declaring intentions ("I take cover from the archers") rather than grid movements.
- This trains better action declarations even when you return to gridded combat.

**Bargains and costs:**
- Think in terms of what actions cost, not exact distances. "The enemies are 50 feet away" means "melee starts in round two."
- A hazard in the middle means "pay a toll or trade your action for a Dash to go around."
- Area spells should hit 2 targets by default (the game's design assumption). Hitting 3 requires working for it or taking a risk.
- Free movement (30 feet) is assumed for everyone. Cover is cheap — give me your move and we'll call it even.

**Key design insight:** The grid shows relationships, not positions. If you think about combat as a list of relationships (who can reach whom, who has cover from whom), you can manage it in your head. Most fights follow a predictable pattern: movement in early rounds, then established skirmishes, then cleanup — so the data you need shrinks quickly.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start with a one-sentence battlefield description** — two groups, a distance, one terrain feature. That covers round one.
2. **Stay zoomed out** — track clumps, not individuals. Only zoom in when a specific action demands it.
3. **Keep details blurry** — don't assign positions until someone forces you to. Once assigned, let them blur again when creatures move.
4. **Narrate intentions, not coordinates** — "They spread out to avoid spells" beats "Goblin 1 moves to E7."
5. **Think in action costs** — 30 feet = free move. 60 feet = costs your action (Dash). Hazard = pay a toll or go around.
6. **Default area spells to 2 targets** — let players work or risk for a third.
7. **Practice regularly** — run at least a few combats per session without a grid to keep your narrative skills sharp.
