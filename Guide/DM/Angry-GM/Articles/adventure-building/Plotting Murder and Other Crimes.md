# Plotting Murder and Other Crimes

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/plotting-murder-and-other-crimes/)
**Date:** December 1, 2021
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

This article is about mysteries — how mystery puzzles work and how people solve them. Not about building a mystery adventure. But if you don't get this, you can't build a mystery adventure. You can't run a dragon-slaying adventure if you can't design a good dragon fight.

The mystery in a mystery adventure is like the dragon in a dragon-slaying adventure. What makes a mystery adventure a mystery adventure is that there's a puzzle to solve. Unlike dragon-slaying adventures, mystery adventures are usually all about solving the mystery.

---

## Demystifying Mysteries

A mystery is just a logic puzzle. Specifically, a **Whodunnit**: something bad's been done and the investigators have to figure out who did it. There's a crime, a perpetrator whose identity is unknown, and protagonists whose job is to deduce the perpetrator's identity.

---

## Howdunnit

A Whodunnit is usually solved in phases:

1. **Phase 1:** Build an initial suspect list
2. **Phase 2:** Qualify or eliminate suspects until there's a manageable shortlist of prime suspects
3. **Phase 3:** Gather enough information on prime suspects to pronounce one guilty
4. **Troubleshooting Phase:** Realize there are no prime suspects and go back over everything

There are three specific bits of information that establish someone as a suspect. Investigators focus on **qualifying** each suspect (finding those three bits) or **eliminating** them (countering one of the three).

---

## The Three Ms: Motive, Means, and Opportunity

These are the three necessary pieces of information that show someone *could* have committed a crime. They don't prove guilt — just possibility.

### Motive
A reason to commit a crime. People don't act without reasons. Committing a crime is an extreme act requiring a strong, usually emotional reason. Motives are a good way to populate an initial suspect list — cops always ask whether a victim had enemies and look into who had anything to gain.

### Means
The ability to commit a crime. If the crime involved a weapon, the perpetrator must own or have access to it. If it involved strength, the perpetrator has to be strong. If it involved planning, they have to be smart. If it involved esoteric knowledge, they have to know it.

### Opportunity
Having the chance to commit a crime. Usually a matter of actually being there when the crime happened or having access to the scene. That's why cops try to "put the suspect at the scene."

### How They Work in Practice
Investigators usually put someone on the initial suspect list based on just one of the three, then try to fill in the blanks or eliminate them.

---

## Leads and Clues and Other Essential Mystery Crap

A mystery is one part logic puzzle to one part scavenger hunt.

### Clues and Evidence
Any information that suggests or disproves motive, means, or opportunity is a **clue**. Big clues (smoking guns) establish these by themselves. Small clues merely hint, sometimes only in combination with others.

**Evidence** is the specific thing that demonstrates the information. The information that a woman was seen leaving the bakery is a clue. The witness statement saying so is the evidence.

### Leads
A **lead** doesn't help solve the mystery directly — it provides a direction. Something to check out to turn up a clue, evidence, or another suspect. In the dungeon analogy: clues are the treasure; leads are the hallways.

### Red Herrings, False Leads, and Dead Ends
Sometimes clues aren't clues at all. Just because someone had a motive doesn't mean they're guilty. Sometimes clues are lies. These make mysteries harder — they give wrong answers, distract from right answers, and waste resources. In the dungeon analogy: traps and cursed items.

---

## Building a Case

Real mysteries don't usually end with a eureka moment and a confession. The investigators might never know if they got the right person. They look at the clues and evidence they have and decide which suspect they've got the strongest case against, and whether that case is strong enough.

> The GM's job is NOT to make a mystery the players can't fail to solve. A mystery with a definitive answer that reveals itself if the PCs just stick it out long enough is just another kind of railroad.

---

## Mystery Puzzle Types

### The Simple Whodunnit
Small list of suspects, only one had motive, means, and opportunity. Investigate to establish the perpetrator and eliminate the others.

### The Advanced Whodunnit
Multiple suspects, not all can be eliminated. Investigators must decide who's most likely by building a case against each. If they can't build a strong enough case, they lose.

### The Impossible Crime
Crimes without suspects. Something about the crime eliminates all possible suspects — the classic locked room, the beloved victim with no enemies. Something's not as it seems. The twist either points to the suspect or the suspect points to the twist.

### The Frame Job
A prime suspect with strong motive, means, and opportunity — but they're innocent. The perpetrator covered their tracks or intentionally fabricated phony clues. Sometimes the frame job IS the real crime.

### Serial Crimes
Multiple crimes with no obvious connection. The motive is usually psychological. The trick is finding the pattern. Often the first crime involves a victim the perpetrator had a connection to; subsequent crimes target victims with something in common with the first. Tremendously complex — not great for RPGs unless you have skilled, crime-drama-loving players.
