# Good Ingredients Make Good Adventures

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/good-ingredients-good-adventures/)
**Date:** May 15, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Minimum Necessary Adventure

An adventure is a chunk of role-playing game that is, in and of itself, a complete game. It has a beginning, a middle, and an end. As long as you have a goal, a beginning, a middle, and an end, you've got a full adventure.

Two caveats:
1. Just because something is a required ingredient doesn't mean you have to plan it in detail beforehand — any ingredient could be Hard Planned, Soft Planned, or No Planned
2. You don't have to come up with ingredients in any specific order

## The Goal

The single most important ingredient. For something to work as a game, the players have to have something they are trying to achieve.

The goal transcends the parts of the adventure — it ties the whole thing together:
- **Beginning:** Provides the reason for setting out and the direction
- **Middle:** Pushes heroes forward; lets them measure progress
- **End:** Defines where, when, and how the adventure ends
- **Tension:** The entire adventure answers "will the heroes accomplish the goal or fail?"

Goals can be simple ("rescue the dragon"), complex ("capture the bounty or kill them"), or shifting (changing during the adventure as events unfold).

**The one rule:** There must ALWAYS be a goal, and the players must ALWAYS know what it is.

### Side Quests and Sub Goals

- **Sub-goals:** Optional extras connected to the main goal — completing them means "winning even more"
- **Side quests:** Optional things not connected to the main goal

## The Beginning

### The Background

The story of how the adventure came to pass. By definition, the background is the parts not interesting enough to be part of the adventure itself. Most backgrounds can be limited to a couple of sentences. It serves to provide guidance for decisions and anchor the adventure in the world.

### The Motivation

The reason the heroes pursue the goal. Comes in two parts:
1. **Character motivation:** Why the characters want to pursue the goal
2. **Player motivation:** Why the players want to pursue the goal

## The Middle

### The Structure

How scenes and encounters fit together. Three basic types:
- **Linear:** Scenes follow one after another
- **Branching:** Players choose routes through constrained options
- **Open:** Players can go from any scene to any scene

Complex structures are combinations of these three. Plan a **critical path** — the most direct route through the adventure.

### The Pace

Who controls the flow and whether the adventure changes:
- **Static vs. Dynamic:** Unchanging structures vs. structures that appear, disappear, and change
- **Player-driven vs. GM-driven:** Players decide movement vs. GM pushes them along

### The Scenes and Encounters

All encounters are scenes, but not all scenes are encounters. Encounters are scenes with conflicts.

#### Plot-Point Scenes vs. Filler Scenes

- **Plot-point scenes:** Scenes around which the adventure turns — stepping stones between beginning and end
- **Filler scenes:** Not vital but add depth, context, challenge, or interest

#### The Hook

Usually the first scene. Players find out the goal, their motivations, and get sent on their way.

#### The Climax

Usually the last ENCOUNTER. The point of highest tension and the point after which the adventure should be resolved. Every adventure will have a climax — if you don't design it, something will fill the role anyway. Plan for it.

## The End

### The Resolution

What impact the end has on the world and heroes. What changes? What are the consequences? Why did any of this matter?

**Critical:** The resolution must account for BOTH success AND failure. GMs who don't consider failure write broken adventures where:
- Failure is impossible (railroading)
- Failure is undefined (no one knows what failure looks like)
