# It's a Trap!

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/its-a-trap/)
**Date:** June 19, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

Part of the ongoing *True Game Mastery* course. This lesson covers managing the action when players' characters encounter traps and hazards.

## What Are Traps and Hazards?

Harmful but inanimate elements of the game world. Traps are usually crafted by sentient beings; hazards usually comprise natural conditions, forces, and events. Some cause harm or deplete resources, others create obstacles, others incite undesirable consequences.

**Resources** include: hit points, hit dice, spell slots, money, torches, inventory space, favors, reputation, time (both in-game and real-life playtime). Anything you've got and can expend, use up, or lose.

## How Most GMs Get Traps Wrong

Most GMs handle traps as **Roll-to-Detect-Now-Roll-to-Disarm**. This leads to bad misconceptions:

### "Passive Scores Take the Gameplay Away"
When GMs see encounters with traps as two dice rolls, they can't fathom why comparing Passive Perception to a static DC would be a good idea.

### "No Rogues Means No Traps"
Boiling traps down to Detect-and-Disarm leaves GMs feeling it's unfair to throw a trap at a party without a trap specialist.

### "You Must Be This Hurt to Level Up"
GMs who don't understand Challenges and Resources can't stomach it when players circumvent hazards through clever play or blind luck.

## Get Your Mental Shit Together

- If mechanical tools include Passive Scores, use them—they're a good tool
- If players surmise something terrible will spray from the chest and open it from behind, they've circumvented the trap
- If they walk around the pressure plate, they did something and deserve a prize
- Fantasy adventure games are about spending as few resources as possible
- **If the players get through your deathtrap dungeon without a scratch, they didn't cheat—they won. Don't be a sore loser.**

## Know Thy Hazards

Traps and hazards can't be abstracted into stats and dice alone. A trap is a cause-and-effect thing: a triggering **If** and a terrible **Then**.

True Game Masters know what their traps look like, how they work, and can describe the interactive bits. D&D's DMG describes each trap narratively (pp. 120–123). Pathfinder 2E Core Rulebook explains trigger mechanisms and trap effects (p. 520). The 3.5 DMG has an excellent trap design discussion (p. 67).

**Your job:** understand traps well enough to describe their presence and action in the world. If the adventure doesn't provide details, make them up.

## Running Trap Encounters

### The Moment of Awareness

Encounters with traps hang on the initial moment of awareness. It's very different depending on whether you notice it before or after it's triggered.

**Being Aware** means nothing more than a suspicion that there's something worth investigating. Present the first significant detail they perceive and invite players to act—even if those choices are to investigate further, evade, or wait.

### What Might Players Notice?

Four things that might make characters aware of a trap:

1. **The trigger** — pressure plate, tripwire, hidden catch, magical rune, weakened planks, depression in snow
2. **The delivery mechanism** — acid nozzle, blade slot, dangling stone block, alarm chimes, hanging snowdrift
3. **Signs of danger in the environment** — bones of previous victims, scorch marks, shattered arrows, scratches in the floor, owner's reminder marks
4. **Deduction** — the space just screams trap (elaborate tile floors in plain dungeons, gem-encrusted idols on pedestals, every chest in a kobold workshop)

### When Might They Notice?

Characters shouldn't perceive fine details identifying traps unless they're within about a dozen feet. Provide sparse details and reveal more only as characters enter and move about the space. Don't describe entire rooms of detail from the doorway.

**Example of good trap presentation:**
> GM describes a 20-foot square chamber with flagstone floor, masonry walls, and an iron-banded chest on a stone platform in the center. Player enters slowly, looking carefully. GM checks Passive Perception and reveals a depression in the dust on one flagstone. Player investigates with a knife—finds a hole with a metal spike point below. Player asks about more holes. GM reveals dozens of bored holes surrounding the chest.

### When Things Go Undetected

You must know what happens when characters blunder into dangers. This is more situational than you'd think. Knowing how the trap works in-world AND knowing exactly where the characters are and what they're doing when it goes off are far more important than knowing its stat block.

## Telegraphing as a Difficulty Knob

The more noticeable the trap, the more likely players will notice and investigate it. The more they learn by investigating, the more likely they circumvent it.

Of the four awareness aspects, two are about Telegraphing and Foreshadowing. Want easier traps? Lay it on thick. Want deadlier traps? Scale back. You're hitting the mark when the party correctly guesses a trap is present and disables it without the rogue pulling out tools.

## Mechanical Sabotage Skills

Trap-disabling skills are **mechanical sabotage**, not a magical "Get Out of Trap Free" button:

- Decide in advance what a talented mechanic could do to screw up the trap
- Be ready to tell the player what's possible: "Sure, you can jam the pressure plate" or "No, it's a dirt-covered blanket over a pit"
- These skills are circumstantial, situational options—not panaceas
- They're usually not the most efficient solution
- If you can avoid triggering a trap or prevent it from hurting you, that's way more efficient than finagling its guts

## Angry's House Rules: Click! and Whoops!

### Click!
When a character triggers a trap, freeze the action at the exact moment they become aware they stepped wrong—just before something terrible happens. The character feels the pressure plate sink underfoot. They get to decide what to do next: freeze, dive forward, jump backward, drop prone, raise shield. Then adjudicate accordingly, granting bonuses or penalties to saving throws.

### Whoops!
Whenever a character is futzing around with a trap or poking around looking for it, there are even-ish odds (50%) that a failure will set it off. Same for mechanical sabotage—if you're trying to unhook the latch inside the chest and fail, odds are even you'll accidentally trigger it.

## Summary

Running trap encounters well means:
- Determine in advance what details traps present in the world (triggers, mechanisms, environmental cues)
- Present the smallest details when characters should become aware of them
- Dole out information on a drip feed as characters explore
- Track characters' positions, actions, and movements at all times
- Adjudicate traps based on how they work and what the characters are doing

**The best trap is one the players know exists and avoid through clever play or lucky guesswork. If your players bypass your traps without ever rolling dice, they're winning, not losing. Celebrate that.**
