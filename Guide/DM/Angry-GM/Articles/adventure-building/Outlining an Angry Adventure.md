# Mr. Angry's Wild Ride: Outlining an Angry Adventure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/outlining-an-angry-adventure/)
**Date:** May 15, 2020
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

Welcome aboard! It's time for some practical adventure building with Angry. In the spirit of the Amazing Adventure Building Checklist that preceded this – and fully cognizant of the fact that adventure design is enough of a chore that I don't want to prolong it – let's skip the Long, Rambling Introduction and just make us some adventure, okay?

## Please Keep Your Hands and Arms Inside the Website

Some of you wanted me to actually follow the steps and build an adventure so you could see how it was done. The Angry Way. And that's what I'm going to do. I'm going into Turbo Angry Adventure Outlining Mode for this.

Important safety precautions:

- Make sure you've read the Amazing Adventure Building Checklist and also the last section of my article about Building Adventures the Angry Way.
- At this stage of the process, it's important not to worry about HOW you'll do things within the game's rules, mechanics, and systems. Assume you can do anything and be ready to break the rules or write new ones when you have to.
- I'm starting this process with nothing. I want to come up with a fairly simple adventure because this is meant to serve as an example.

Making an adventure the Angry Way is a two-part process:

**The first part is the design part.** The outline part. That's when you run through the first five steps of the Amazing Checklist. Once you're done with those steps, you're done designing and you'll have a nice overview of the whole adventure.

**The second part is the build part.** That's when you actually do all the mechanical stuff – draw maps, plan encounters, come up with rules, and fill in details. That's all covered by step six.

During the design part, your goal is just to get ideas on paper. Don't worry about how to make things work until you're actually trying to make things work. The design part just produces an outline. The basic shape of the adventure.

## Step 1: The Goal

The goal is the thing the players have to do to win the adventure. It's how they know they're done.

> Kobolds stole a thing from a town; get the thing back.

Notice how simple and straightforward that goal is. It's one sentence. That's how you know it's a good one. Complicated goals don't make deep adventures. Adventure depth comes from all the crap that happens during the adventure. But simple goals make it easy for players to start moving in the right direction.

## Step 2: Boring, Gamey Details

The boring, gamey details provide constraints that you have to work within. They tell you how you're going to build your encounters and how many you need.

> This adventure is for three to five, 3rd- to 5th-level characters and should take three to four sessions to complete.

We're building an adventure the Angry Way. Angry balances his adventures around three-level tiers of play, not around precise levels. And not around precise numbers of players either. I'll be using numbers that make a good adventure for four 4th-level PCs, but I'll also be letting everything wobble a little.

## Step 3: The Central Conflict

The central conflict describes what's creating the problem the heroes have to solve. It's the one thing the players absolutely have to deal with by adventure's end. It also determines what the climax will look like.

> A dragonborn thrall of Tiamat sent the kobolds to steal the thing he wanted because he can do evil with it; the party must defeat him to get it back.

In the Angryverse, kobolds are a slave race made by Tiamat to serve chromatic dragons. Left to their own devices, they don't pull off big heists or make complicated plans. So, the kobolds need a leader. An evil dragonborn fits the bill.

Why does the dragonborn want the thing? What even is the thing? We can figure that out later.

## Step 4: Plot Points

Now's when you outline the major steps in the adventure. Each plot point will become a scene and each scene usually takes between one-half and two sessions to play, so it's a good idea to have roughly one plot point per session.

> **Plot Point 1:** Kobolds attack the town, causing chaos and destruction. While the townsfolk and the heroes protect the town and minimize the chaos, wily kobold thieves sneak into a specific place and steal a thing.

> **Plot Point 2:** It's revealed that the kobolds came from an abandoned mine in the rugged hills. The heroes will have to travel through the dangerous wilderness to reach the kobolds' lair.

> **Plot Point 3:** The kobolds have built their lair deep in the abandoned mine. The heroes have to explore the mine, deal with the kobolds, confront the dragonborn, and recover the thing.

Having decided in step two that my adventure spans three or four sessions, I need a minimum of three plot points. These ones add variety to the adventure and they'll make good examples of how to build different scenes.

## Step 5: Scenes and Scene Details

Each plot point represents a scene. And each scene needs specific scene details.

A scene is a segment of continuous action during which the heroes try to accomplish a specific thing in a specific place during a specific time period. As the scene plays out, the heroes deal with a series of encounters that add up to the plot point in question.

### Scene One: The Kobold Attack

**Setting:** The town.

**Central Conflict:** Chaos and destruction spread through the town (rampaging drake). The kobolds unleash a rampaging drake in the town. Confronting and defeating the drake will serve as a good climax.

**Stakes:** If the party handles the kobold attack well, they'll receive resources and information that will help them defeat the kobolds. If they handle the attack poorly, townsfolk will die and buildings will be destroyed. There will be fewer resources available for them and the information they receive will be limited. The success of the whole adventure doesn't hinge on whether or not the party defends the town well.

**Structure:** The scene is structured like a game of whack-a-mole where people scream and die whenever a mole escapes getting whacked. The heroes will have to run around and try to deal with emergencies as they pop up.

**Length:** One or two sessions of play.

### Scene Two: Into the Hills

**Setting:** The hills.

**Central Conflict:** Nature hates adventurers (a difficult climb down to the mine). In the final encounter, the players have to make a difficult descent into the steep gorge at whose bottom lies the abandoned mine.

**Stakes:** If the party handles the scene poorly, they will find themselves injured and exhausted and their resources depleted when they reach the abandoned mine. They will have to deal with the final scene in a weakened state. It's good to vary the stakes – make some scenes about earning a benefit and make others about avoiding problems.

**Structure:** Mostly just a linear series of obstacles with maybe a branch or two. And random complications.

**Length:** One-half to one session.

### Scene Three: Exploring the Mine

**Setting:** The abandoned mine.

**Central Conflict:** The evil dragonborn stole the thing to do evil and doesn't want to give it back. (The central conflict in the final scene of an adventure is the adventure's central conflict.)

**Stakes:** If the party defeats the dragonborn and gets the thing, they can return it to the town and win. But if the dragonborn escapes or defeats the party, he gets away with the thing and uses it to do evil.

**Structure:** Dungeon.

**Length:** One or two sessions. Probably two.

## Onward to Step Six…

Now you know what it looks like to outline an adventure the Angry Way. Next, I'm going to start building the scenes and encounters themselves. I'm going to build it backward – starting with the last scene because it'll be the easiest and most straightforward. It's just a dungeon. And then we can work our way back to the first scene.

It'll be easier to build the stakes in the first two scenes if we know what's coming in the third scene.
