# Let's Build a Pretty Good Dungeon: Introduction

## Summary

This is the kickoff article for the "Pretty Good Dungeon" project series. Angry introduces a practical dungeon-building project called *Reclaim the Place* — a dungeon adventure where players must clear three distinct infestations from an underground complex so a faction can reclaim a ruined place. The series aims to bridge the gap between casual homebrew adventure design and formal scenario design by applying core game design principles to dungeon mapping. Rather than just theorizing, the series will walk through actually designing and mapping a dungeon step by step.

## Key Advice

### The Three Core Design Principles

Every design decision in the series flows from these three rules:

1. **The path of least resistance must lead to the best gameplay experience** — The route players are most likely to take should also be the most fun, well-paced, and properly challenging route.
2. **Players can't choose to ruin the experience** — Players must have freedom to explore and deviate, but no choice should lead to a frustratingly unplayable screwjob. Going left instead of right might make things harder, but never broken.
3. **The system must be respected** — D&D (or whatever system you use) is built around assumptions like the adventuring day (4–6 encounters before rest). You can deviate, but you must account for it — ramp difficulty for fewer encounters, provide recovery for more.

### Order Matters

The sequence in which players experience events drastically affects gameplay quality. Good order empowers players to make meaningful choices. Bad order can disempower them or create unfair screwjobs. Order also affects:

- **Pacing** — Tension should bounce between high and low but generally rise.
- **Narrative** — Players need proper context before conflicts to understand motivations and stakes.
- **Non-mechanical difficulty** — Showing the lock before the key makes puzzles easier; showing the key first makes them harder. You control difficulty through sequencing.

### Design vs. Player Knowledge

The adventure's design premise (three wings, three infestations, three specific tasks) is the *designer's* framework. Players don't need to know the full structure — they just need enough information to start and enough to figure out how to win through play.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Define your adventure premise** — Identify the goal, the obstacles, and the structure (e.g., three wings with three infestations to clear).
2. **Internalize the three design principles** — Before mapping anything, commit to: best path = most likely path, no path ruins the game, and the system's mechanical needs are respected.
3. **Think about event order** — Before placing encounters, consider what sequence of discoveries, challenges, and revelations will feel best for the players.
4. **Separate designer knowledge from player knowledge** — Design the full structure, but only reveal to players what they need to begin and what they can discover through play.
5. **Respect the adventuring day** — Know how many encounters your system expects between rests and plan around that number.

> [!tip] The Premise Template
> Use Angry's simple adventure structure as a starting template: *"There's a [place] that [faction] wants to reclaim. The [underspace] has [N] distinct [infestations] that must be cleared by completing a specific [task] in each."* This gives you a clear goal, natural branching structure, and built-in completion criteria — all before you draw a single room.
