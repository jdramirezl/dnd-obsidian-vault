# Two Other Things You Never Heard of to Make Encounters Not Suck

## Summary

Sequel to "You Can Quit Encounters Anytime You Want." GMs overdesign encounters with subsystems (skill challenges, encounter traps, hauntings) that mask problems rather than fix them. The real fix is two GM skills: **Visualization** and **Dynamic Conflict**. These are running skills, not design skills — they empower the GM to handle anything without mechanical crutches.

## Adventure Designer vs. Game Master

Two separate roles even when they're the same person:

**Adventure Designer:** Design a complete game experience. Pass the GM everything needed — and nothing more. Design the racecourse, hand the GM a map, get off the course.

**Game Master:** Present the situation, point toward the goal, ask "what do you do?" Determine how actions work out. React to players — don't steer them. Follow wherever they go.

**Critical:** The designer passes minimum necessary information. Not solutions to problems (that's the players' job). Not scripts for every possibility.

## Visualization

Visualization is not just imagining something. It's constructing something in your mind that you can **examine and manipulate**. Different from merely picturing, different from linguistic description.

### Why It Matters

If players fire flaming arrows at a village: thatch roofs catch fire, terracotta tiles don't. You can answer this from visualization without anyone having written it down. **Visualization lets you answer questions about details no one invented.**

### Key Distinctions

- Visualization ≠ Description (brain stores visuals differently from language)
- Visualization ≠ Flavor text (description, not construction)
- Visualization ≠ A map (just detailed flavor text)

### The GM's Job Sequence

1. **Visualize** the scene
2. **Narrate** what you see
3. **Adjudicate** player actions

### Practicing

- Close eyes, three deep breaths, conjure a thing, study it
- Open eyes, close again — make sure it's still there
- Ask yourself questions about it
- Eventually won't need deep breaths or closed eyes
- Delivering narration with eyes closed is a good habit

> "A GM who provides scene-setting narration with his eyes closed will probably never, ever railroad you."

## Dynamic Conflict

### The Cliff vs. The Combat

Both can be encounters. Combat has far more depth because of **dynamic conflict** — bad guys actively try to make heroes lose. The situation changes. Players can't just execute a plan.

A cliff is **static conflict**. Assess, plan, roll dice. After the third roll, it stops being interesting. TTRPGs have no execution challenges — just decisions and random outcomes.

### What Dynamic Conflict Means

The source of conflict is **fighting back** — actively trying to make players lose. Requires:
1. Identifying the source of conflict
2. Knowing what it means to lose (stakes)
3. **Personifying the conflict source** — giving it agency

### Personifying Nature: The Cliff Example

What could nature do to push players toward defeat?

- **Dislodge a loose rock** — climber now dangling by one arm, grip failing. Situation changed, what do you do?
- **Unleash a cauldron of bats** from a hidden cleft
- **Leave bat guano** on rockface making subsequent climbs harder
- **Bloodhawk nest** on a rest ledge — mama attacks on a three-inch ledge
- **Thorny venomous vine** snags and entangles — hanging one-handed
- **It starts to rain**

Each changes the situation and forces new decisions. Passive Perception still applies (spotting bat caves = spotting hidden goblins). Players can actively scan the rockface before climbing.

### Why This Is the GM's Job

The designer provides a couple of actions nature might take and stats them up, plus guidance on frequency (difficulty). But the GM must understand dynamic conflict and personification to run it — same as how a stat block provides attacks but the GM decides tactics.

### The Broader Implication

> "How many social encounters boil down to 'convince the guy to do what you want before he gets tired of you'? All of them."

Dynamic conflict applies everywhere.

## Design Principles

1. **Visualize before you narrate** — construct the scene as an examinable object
2. **Personify everything** — treat every conflict source as an active agent
3. **Dynamic > Static** — encounters need things that change the situation
4. **Saving throws aren't actions** — conflict sources must take actions that change situations
5. **Don't overdesign** — visualization and dynamic conflict replace elaborate subsystems
6. **Keep personification within the encounter** — don't conjure new encounters; identify the actual conflict source first
