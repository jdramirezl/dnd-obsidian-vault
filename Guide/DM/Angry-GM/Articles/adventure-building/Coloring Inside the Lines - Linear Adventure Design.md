# Coloring Inside the Lines: Linear Adventure Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/coloring-inside-the-lines-linear-adventure-design/)
**Date:** December 16, 2015
**Category:** Adventure Building (Theoretical)

---

## Linear Structure

In a **linear adventure**, players don't choose which scenes to transition to. The adventure begins, a scene plays out, there's a transition, another scene plays out, and so on. Players don't choose to go left or right, investigate the tower or the mansion, or approach the thieves' guild or the town watch.

The simplest example is a linear dungeon obstacle course — the party enters, encounters a series of obstacles, and eventually reaches their goal.

**Linear adventures can be great fun.** They are easy to run and manage — great for new GMs. But writing a linear adventure that isn't crap takes finesse.

---

## The Dreaded Railroad

**Railroading** is a verb — it refers to a GM designing or running a game so that players have no feeling of **agency** (the feeling that you have some control over the outcome).

Example of no agency: Walking across a meadow, a chunk of iron falls from the sky and kills you. You couldn't avoid it, couldn't take precautions, couldn't see it coming. Even with a saving throw, your life or death is decided by a coin toss.

**Agency is all about perception.** Players only see what actually happens in the game. They don't know about treasures they didn't find, paths they didn't take, monsters they didn't encounter, or clever plans they didn't think of. It's impossible for players to know how much freedom they actually had.

**A linear structure is NOT automatically railroading.** Agency is created when players make choices that affect the outcome — but those choices don't always have to be structural.

### Example: The Ogre

Step 2 is always ogre. Step 3 is always goblins. But:
- If the party attacks, the ogre fights to the death
- If the party holds back, they can negotiate — the ogre lets them pass
- If they offer gifts or manipulate/intimidate, the ogre joins them as an ally
- With an ogre ally, the goblins in the next room might be afraid to fight and the goblin chief offers to surrender

**There are different types of agency.** It isn't always about choosing left or right. Agency is also about the freedom to engage with scenes in different ways and to have choices follow you through the adventure.

## Agency in Linear Adventures

Two ways to convey agency in linear adventures:

### 1. Agency of Approach

Players are free to decide HOW to engage with a particular obstacle. The ogre can be attacked, negotiated with, snuck past, or tricked.

**Key:** Set the scene in a way that creates options. If the ogre raises his club and bellows a threat, it looks like a fight. Instead, call attention to the ogre holding his ground, hefting his club, staring at the party unsure of what to do — that creates options.

### 2. Agency of Consequence

Choices in one scene affect how other scenes play out. Befriending the ogre gives you an ogre ally for future scenes.

Combat naturally carries both types of agency — players make many choices during combat, and how well they win (resources remaining) affects future encounters. If the party gets beaten up by the ogre, the handful of goblins in the next room becomes exponentially more dangerous.

**This is why action-heavy linear adventures rarely feel like railroads.** Combat automatically carries both Agency of Approach and Agency of Consequence.

### How to Ruin Combat's Agency

When consequences of battle don't follow the party. The standard "no more than one encounter per day" wilderness travel pattern:
- Random encounters are random — players have no say in what they encounter
- Most are just random combats — no say in how to deal with them
- Never more than one per day — any encounter that doesn't kill a PC has consequences completely erased by the next encounter

**Better approach:** Let the party do things to avoid random encounters or take risks that increase the chance. Make random wilderness encounters afford more choices for approach (e.g., goblins on the road don't really want to fight either).

## Planning Scenes in a Linear Adventure

When placing scenes along the path, constantly ask:
- "How are the players free to engage with this scene?"
- "How does this scene affect the next?"
- "Am I accidentally erasing the consequences of scenes?"

**Resting:** If the PCs can freely rest and recover without consequences, that's not really a choice — they'd be stupid NOT to rest. One of the first things to ask: "What are the consequences of retreating and resting?"

**Not every scene needs to drown in agency.** It's okay to have the occasional simple scene ("scale this wall, no way around it, just climb"). Pacing requires varying both the speed and complexity of scenes.

As you map out the structure, ask where the agency is:
- Is there a scene with an obstacle the party can deal with in multiple ways?
- Is there a scene that changes how a future scene plays out?
- Are there scenes that do both?
- Can the players easily escape consequences?

If you can point to a few spots for agency, you're fine.

---

## Failure in Linear Adventures

Linear adventures are very sensitive to failure states. Because there's only one path, if the PCs mess up at any point, they usually have no other way to get to the end.

**There is absolutely nothing wrong with the adventure failing one scene in.** A linear adventure is, by definition, a bottleneck. It's fine — as long as you are okay with it. Most GMs are not, and that's understandable.

As you drop scenes along the path, consider:
- What happens if a given scene is a failure?
- Does all forward progress stop? Is the adventure over?
- Or do the players come out dragging a consequence into another scene?
- If the adventure IS over, how do the players know?

**The most obvious failure state is everyone dies.** That's fine. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise.

But if your adventure isn't just a life-or-death gauntlet, decide which scenes end the adventure and which offer a way to keep moving forward. Your adventure failure state helps figure out how to deal with bad outcomes:
- Tracking a foe and taking too long → quarry gets away, trail goes cold
- Time limit expires → princess sacrificed, temple sinks into desert
- Evading an enemy → enemy catches up

---

## Summary

- Linear adventures can be great fun — they are tricky to write but easy to run.
- **Railroading** is about perception of agency, not about structure.
- A linear structure is NOT automatically a railroad.
- Build **Agency of Approach** (multiple ways to engage with scenes) and **Agency of Consequence** (choices carry forward) into your scenes.
- Combat naturally provides both types of agency — don't erase it by removing consequences.
- Plan for **failure states** — know which scenes end the adventure and which allow forward progress despite failure.
- Vary pacing by mixing slow/fast and complex/simple scenes.
