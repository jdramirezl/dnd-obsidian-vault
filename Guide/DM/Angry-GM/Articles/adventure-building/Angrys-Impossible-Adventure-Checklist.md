# Angry's Double Impossible Adventure Building Checklist

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/angrys-impossible-adventure-checklist/) | **Category**: Adventure Building

---

## Key Advice

- **Every adventure needs a goal.** The goal is the through-line that ties everything together and gives context for every decision — both the players' and yours as the designer.
- **One plot point per one or two sessions.** This keeps short adventures from being overcomplicated and ensures long adventures have enough lesser climaxes and reversals to feel satisfying.
- **A scene is a continuous chunk of action with a specific sub-goal, set in one location, over one time period.** Think of scenes as chapters, not movie scenes. They last 1-2 sessions each.
- **Every scene needs its own central conflict, stakes, and structure.** The scene's central conflict is the biggest thing between the players and the scene's goal. The last scene's central conflict IS the adventure's central conflict.
- **Build scenes as you go.** For long adventures, you don't need to do all the work upfront. Build each scene as the party approaches it, tailoring based on how the adventure has played out.

## Summary

This article delivers a complete, step-by-step adventure building process in under 3,000 words — something Angry claims is impossible but does anyway. He provides three caveats: he doesn't actually follow this process rigidly (but would if he had to); you can do the steps in any order; and this framework produces functional, fun adventures but not masterpieces (though masterpieces are complex variations on this same theme).

**Step 1: The Goal.** Define what the heroes are trying to accomplish. Without a goal, players can't make useful choices and you can't build coherent content. Even sandbox games have goals — the players just pick which ones to pursue.

**Step 2: Boring Gamey Details.** Decide length (in sessions), level, player count. Sessions determine how many scenes and conflicts you need.

**Step 3: The Central Conflict.** What's the biggest thing between the heroes and the goal? This defines the climax — once resolved, nothing else stands in the way. If the conflict is nebulous (like "no one knows where the treasure is"), use a proxy to represent it (a rival NPC, a final puzzle, a raging river).

**Step 4: Plot Points.** List the major steps players must take to win. One plot point per 1-2 sessions. These are sub-goals, not individual encounters.

**Step 5: Scenes.** Assign each plot point (and the main goal) to a scene. Each scene gets: a Setting (can be large or small), a Central Conflict (tied to the adventure's conflict if possible), Stakes (what happens on success/failure and how it affects future scenes), Structure (gauntlet, branching path, open exploration, timed events), and Length (1-2 sessions, with wiggle room for half-session or three-session scenes).

**Step 6: Map and Populate.** For each scene, design encounters according to its structure. The climactic encounter resolves the scene's central conflict. Add 1-2 major encounters (significant obstacles) and 2-4 minor encounters (incidental challenges). Build scenes as needed rather than all upfront.

**Step 7: The Hook and Resolution.** The hook presents the goal and gives players a reason to care. The resolution provides the payoff. These bookend the adventure.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start with the goal.** Write one sentence: "The heroes are trying to ___." Everything else flows from this.
2. **Decide session count.** This determines how many plot points and scenes you need. One plot point per 1-2 sessions.
3. **Define the central conflict and its proxy.** What's the biggest obstacle? If it's abstract, pick a concrete proxy (NPC, monster, puzzle) to represent it in the climax.
4. **List plot points as sub-goals.** Write them as a simple list or prose description. These are the major milestones, not individual encounters.
5. **Build scenes around plot points.** Each scene gets a setting, central conflict, stakes, structure (linear/branching/open), and target length.
6. **Populate each scene with encounters.** One climactic encounter (resolves the scene's conflict), 1-2 major encounters, 2-4 minor encounters. Vary scene structures for pacing.
7. **Write the hook first, resolution last.** The hook must make players care about the goal. The resolution must pay off the adventure's promise.
8. **Mix scene structures.** Alternate between linear, branching, and open scenes to add variety and improve pacing across the adventure.
