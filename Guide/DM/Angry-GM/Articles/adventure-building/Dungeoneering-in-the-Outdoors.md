# Fanservice BS: Dungeoneering in the Great Outdoors

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dungeoneering-in-the-outdoors/)
**Date:** June 6, 2018

---

## The Core Answer

How do you make exploring a wilderness area feel like exploring a dungeon? **You design it like a dungeon.** Structure, encounters, pacing — all the same.

## It's Not About Walls

Dungeons don't really have walls in the way that matters. You describe the **spaces between the walls** and the **paths between those spaces**. You can break down any space into a series of encounters and the paths between those encounters. Literally.

## Natural "Dungeon" Structures

Dense, overgrown forests are shot through with:
- **Game trails** — animals choose the easiest paths, wearing down brush over time
- **Seasonal stream beds** — cut by streams that flow in spring and dry out in summer
- **Clearings** — open spaces caused by hilltops, ponds, fires, floods, mudslides, collapsed buildings, or standing structures

You can literally map a section of dense forest as a dungeon — "rooms" (clearings/points of interest) connected by "halls" (trails/paths). The only real difference is paths can be much longer (half-hour instead of minutes).

### Other Terrain That Works
- **Mountains/craggy hills** — trails, passes, canyons, gullies, cliffside paths, rope bridges, caves
- **Swamps** — raised land islands connected by narrow land bridges
- **Corn fields** — trampled paths with poor visibility and difficult terrain

## Getting Players to Buy In

Present it as a dungeon. Stop narrating hours of marching and suddenly describe a trail spilling into a broad clearing with a strange statue, a pond, dense underbrush, and paths branching off. Players are **primed** for room-by-room gameplay because the whole game is based around it. Video games have also primed them (Lost Woods in Zelda, Darkroot Garden in Dark Souls).

### The Transition Is Key

The outdoor dungeon must be **special and distinct** from surrounding wilderness. Mark the transition:
1. Establish it beforehand — the adventure is ABOUT finding something in this specific area
2. Provide a descriptive transition — "now you're at the entrance to the adventure"
3. Present the obvious paths

This shifts players' brains from overland travel to room-by-room gameplay.

## "But What If Players Walk Off the Paths?"

**They probably won't.** Players generally play the easiest game you let them play. The game's natural structure primes them to accept transitions. In 30 years of wilderness dungeons, it almost never happens.

### If They Actually Do

Resolve it like any other action:
- Moving off-trail is **slow, hard going** — make sure they know it
- **Where do they end up?** If they pass beyond the dungeon area, the underbrush thins and they find themselves in the larger wilderness
- Use navigation rules for getting lost
- They've dug themselves out of the fun adventure

### Two Key GM Mental Defects

1. **GMs assume players are always out to break the game.** They aren't — they're looking for the easiest way to play and win. Handle off-path actions fairly. Resistance followed by punishment is spite.

2. **GMs assume bypassing encounters ruins the game.** If players skip things or kill the boss without rolling initiative, look at them — they're proud, elated, high-fiving. Proper challenge is about how players feel, not running every encounter as planned.

## Summary

Build and present an outdoor dungeon exactly like an indoor dungeon. Map it, transition into it, design encounters. 99% of the time, players will fall in line. If they don't, resolve it and learn for next time.
