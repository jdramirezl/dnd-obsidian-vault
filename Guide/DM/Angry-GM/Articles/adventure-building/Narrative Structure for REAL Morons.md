# Narrative Structure for REAL Morons

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/narrative-structure-for-morons-2/)
**Date:** May 30, 2018
**Series:** Narrative Structure for Morons (Part 2 of 3)

---

## It Ain't About the Acts, It's About the Transitions

There are no such things as Acts. Acts aren't a thing. A room is just a space defined by four walls, a floor, and a ceiling. If you take away the walls and floors and ceiling, the room ceases to exist. "Room" is descriptive, not prescriptive. You can't learn how to build a room. You can only learn how to build a floor, walls, and a ceiling.

Acts are the spaces between plot-points. Act I is called "the setup" because it describes everything that comes BEFORE the first major turning point. The only way to see where the acts begin and end is when you see the plot-points.

## The Screenplay

In 1979, Syd Field wrote *Screenplay: The Foundations of Screenwriting*. He focused heavily on certain plot points and turning points:

1. **Setup** – the character in their normal life, shown as sympathetic
2. **New Opportunity** (~10 min in) – hero is presented with an opportunity to change their situation
3. **Change of Plans** (~30 min mark) – the incitement; hero is confronted with a problem and resolves to solve it
4. **Point of No Return** (~60 min mark) – makes the story personal; hero is personally committed. It's succeed or die time.
5. **Major Setback** (~75% through) – hero suffers a catastrophic loss
6. **Final Push** – hero resolves to make a last push toward victory
7. **Climax** – hero faces the biggest obstacle and resolves their outer motivation

The Point of No Return occurs almost exactly at the sixty-minute mark, no matter how long the film is. In *Die Hard*, at sixty minutes, John McClane says "yippee-kai-yay." In *Raiders of the Lost Ark*, at sixty minutes exactly, someone yells "we found it" – the Ark of the Covenant.

## Dealing with Agency

The problem with narrative structure in RPGs: you don't have the same control over the heroes that an author does. Plot-points dependent on the outcome of an event – like a setback or climax – don't work because you can't guarantee outcomes. You have to be ready for turning points to go either way.

This means our narrative structure model becomes less of a framework and more of a **toolbox**.

## The Minimum: Turning Points That Must Exist

### The Call to Adventure

The first turning point that every story needs. It establishes the conflict and gives the heroes a reason to care. It can take any form – a treasure map, a stranger who hires them, a sudden attack. It doesn't even have to move the heroes toward the REAL conflict. It just has to get them moving.

This is usually the first scene of an adventure. Any scenes that precede it should be setting up the motivation. If you don't have a specific reason to set up emotional blackmail, just Call the heroes to Adventure and be done with it. Because this crap ISN'T adventure.

### The Climax

Every adventure has a climax. Ideally, it pays off whatever was initially set up by the Call to Adventure. The climax doesn't have to be a boss fight – it can be a chase scene, a tense negotiation, a rousing speech. But it has to be exciting.

The Climax ends the adventure. Anything after it ISN'T adventure. Insert scenes after the Climax sparingly. If something was at stake – established in the Call to Adventure or during another turning point – that needs to be paid off.

## The Gravy: Turning Points That Make Any Story Better

### A New Life (Before the Call to Adventure)

Before the Call to Adventure, the heroes might find themselves in a new situation or facing a new opportunity. They might arrive in a new city, be hired by a new patron, or come together and agree to adventure. This makes a great turning point for the start of a new campaign.

Scenes before A New Life are boring – they show the heroes in their old situation. They can be totally skipped and usually should be.

### It's Personal (Somewhere in the Middle)

Something happens to make an adventure really personal. Before this point, the heroes could live with failure. After it, they can't. They can't walk away. If they die trying, they've thrown their life away.

Common forms: the villain strikes at the heroes personally (a loved one is threatened, an important NPC kidnapped, an important object stolen). Or a greater threat is revealed. But to be effective, the heroes have to see the threat on a personal level. It has to affect them.

It isn't just about raising the stakes – it's about making the heroes go from "we'll do our best" to "we HAVE to win, there is NO other option for us, personally."

This turning point generally works close to the middle of an adventure and makes a great session-ending scene.

### The Big Screw / The Final Test (Before the Climax)

The story itself turns against the heroes. Something they trusted or were counting on fails them. A major NPC betrays them. The villagers give up hope. Unlike Field's Major Setback, the Big Screw is more open-ended:

- If the outcome is bad → heroes are at their lowest point and have to resolve to fight anyway
- If the outcome is good → heroes have been tested and found worthy of facing the climax

For the Big Screw to have power, it has to be emotional. A traitor doesn't hurt unless you genuinely cared about them. This turning point feels much more substantial if there was an It's Personal moment earlier.

After the Big Screw, there shouldn't be any other challenges before the Climax, but there might be a scene or two for the players to react, recommit, resolve personal issues, and plan their assault.

## The Complete Model

> **(Optional) A New Life:** The heroes' lives start a new course, one that will bring them into contact with adventure.
> **The Call to Adventure:** Something happens to make the heroes aware of a major conflict that they have to resolve and a good reason to want to resolve it.
> **(Optional) It's Personal:** An event occurs to make the upcoming conflict personal and to make it impossible for the heroes to accept failure. Whether they succeed or fail against this challenge, they can't go back. They have to go forward.
> **(Optional) The Big Screw/The Final Test:** Something major – really, the story itself – turns against the heroes and they have to deal with it. They either get through the test and move on to face the final conflict or else they end up at their lowest point and resolve to end the conflict once and for all.
> **The Climax:** The heroes face whatever conflict has been building from the beginning, or face something that will allow them to resolve that conflict once or fail. If they succeed, they've won and the adventure is over. If they fail, they've lost and the adventure is still over. And maybe they are dead. Sometimes, that's how it goes.
