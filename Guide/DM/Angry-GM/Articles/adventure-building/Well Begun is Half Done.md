# Well Begun is Half Done

## Summary

How you start your session is critically important. A proper startup establishes inertia, draws players into the game, and transitions brains from social mode to visualization-and-narration mode. This article presents Angry's Session Startup Script: a six-step process from "shut up, it's game time" to "what do you do?" It builds on older recap advice with new emphasis on mental transitions, player reintroductions, and the "bigger to smaller" narrative pattern.

## Why Starting Right Matters

Your brain struggles to change gears. RPGs require visualization, description, and translating language into sensory impressions. This is mental yoga — easier if you stretch and ease into it. Your job as GM is to ease yourself AND your players into this state.

## The Session Startup Script

### 1. Call to Order

Clearly state the game has begun. Use a consistent phrase every session. The clock starts the game, not "whenever you're ready."

> "All right, shut up y'all! It's game time."

Transitions from *hangout* to *club gathering*.

### 2. Table Business

Scheduling, rules clarifications, conflict resolutions. If nothing official, **invent something** — generic club-sounding business transitions brains from social to club mode.

> "Remember we're off next two weeks. Next session Friday October 7th. I fixed your encumbrance numbers."

### 3. Recap the Campaign

Follow the **bigger-to-smaller** pattern: broadest overarching stuff first, then increasingly specific.

**3a. Current Goal and Motivation** — What's the major goal? Why? What's at stake?

> "You're escorting Ananda into Shadowpine Forest to check on an herbalist. By helping her, you hope to establish a relationship with a useful healer and get a potion discount."

**3b. Subgoals and Motivations** — Special conditions, side objectives, optional goals.

> "You've befriended the Aster's crew and become their de facto bodyguards. Try to keep them alive."

**3c. Major Plot Points from Sessions Past** — Everything between adventure start and last session. **One paragraph max.** Only major plot points.

**3d. Most Recent Previous Session** — A little more detailed but not much. Skip minor encounters, shopping, side conversations. Should take no more time than recapping all previous sessions.

**3e. Reiterate Important Information** — Clues, rumors, weaknesses, hints. The info matters even if how they learned it doesn't.

> "You learned Shadowpine Forest has been strange — shadowy watchers, a sense of unwelcome. Also, Olek left for Frostwind over a week ago and hasn't returned from a day trip."

### 4. Reintroduce the Party

**4a. Players Reintroduce Their PCs** — Every session. Race, physical appearance, equipment, mannerisms. Plus **one thing the party learned about their character recently**.

This is NOT a backstory monologue. It IS:
- What you'd see watching a movie about the character
- A visualization exercise loading the character into active memory
- Practice at describing and visualizing

The "one recent thing" keeps it from becoming rote and forces a mini personal-story recap. Players will find it tedious — make them do it anyway. Over weeks/months they'll get good at visualizing and describing.

**4b. Reintroduce NPC Companions** — GM describes any NPC party members.

### 5. Describe the Current Situation

**5a. Recall the Current Plan of Action** — What were they planning when last session ended?

> "Glamdrig intended to visit the blacksmith. Thogrinn and Rozen were heading to the furrier. Quintus wanted to visit the village priest."

**5b. Describe Current Locations and Activities** — Where are characters right now? What are they doing? Introduces the scene you're about to set.

> "You've just finished early dinner in the common room of 'Neath the Pines."

### 6. Start the Game

**6a. Visualize and Set the Scene** — Describe what characters see, hear, feel.

**6b. Invite the Principle Character to Act** — Direct the opening action to a specific character.

> "The captain looks to Aquila. 'What say you...' Well, Bob, what says Aquila?"

## Key Principles

1. **Transitions matter** — each step transitions: social → club → game → character → action
2. **Bigger to smaller** — general to specific, past to present
3. **Remind, don't regurgitate** — important points only
4. **Player reintroductions are visualization practice** — the real purpose
5. **Consistency builds habit** — same structure every session
6. **The clock starts the game** — not "whenever ready"
