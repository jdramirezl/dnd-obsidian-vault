# Flight to Elturel: Designing Part 2 (Part 3)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/flight-to-elturel-part-3/)
**Date:** October 9, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Moving Parts Adventures

A **moving parts adventure** involves few planned encounters. Instead, the GM sets up several game elements moving around as the players move, and resolves collisions when they happen.

### Example: Ghoul in the Sewers
Heroes in a sewer maze playing cat and mouse with a ghoul. As players moved, the ghoul moved too — ambushing, making quick attacks, fleeing. The ghoul exploited the party getting drawn into fights with oozes or rats to paralyze a PC and drag them away. Eventually players realized they had to do something proactive to trap the ghoul.

**The essence:** not bouncing from encounter to encounter, but actively dealing with a problem that keeps cropping up and getting worse.

### Requirements for Building One
- The ghoul needed good stealth and dash/disengage/hide abilities
- The map needed dense passages with few dead ends and lots of escape routes
- Individual encounters had to be weak nuisances (not the focus)
- Had to think about how the ghoul interacted with other sewer monsters

## Systems Make It Work

The most important thing: it works **logically or systematically**. Players must understand how parts move and interact so they can plan.

**A system is any tool that helps you resolve something logically and consistently, setting up patterns players can see.** Players don't have to see the systems — they have to see the patterns.

### Ghoul Movement System (~5 sentences):
- Player moves one room/intersection = one turn; resolving an encounter = one turn
- Each player turn, the ghoul takes a turn (moves one room/intersection)
- Ghoul runs into an encounter → immediately goes back the way it came
- Party not concealing itself → ghoul knows where they are within two moves
- Party gets into an encounter → ghoul knows from anywhere in the dungeon

## Flight to Elturel Scene 2: The Key Choice

The party must travel from Baldur's Gate to Elturel with Stedd, pursued by Zhentarim. As written naively, this sucks — the party just decides to travel fast and the GM determines if the Zhents catch up.

### Two Routes (present explicitly with map):

1. **River Chionthar** — fast, direct, can't get lost. But Zhentarim follow easily; must rely on speed.
2. **Fields of the Dead** — longer, slower, trackless. Risk of getting lost, more dangerous. But Zhentarim must track the party and may lose them.

The party can change routes at any time.

## Travel and Tension

Used the **Tension Pool** for travel. Party decides every four hours: pace (slow/average/fast) and whether to travel into dangerous twilight (extra 4 hours, more complications).

### Preventing Repetitive Decisions
If conditions don't change, the same choice every day isn't a choice. Needed disrupting factors:
- **Complications** — not just fights but resource drains and delays
- **Zhent advance force** — small group 8 hours ahead of main force; "almost caught" creates two decision points of warning
- **Food scarcity** — party didn't shop before escaping; 10 days food for 10-day trip but extra mouth (Stedd had none)
- **River complications:** robbery, spoiled food, hungry pilgrims, tributary crossings, monsters
- **Wilderness complications:** scarce water, drought, standing boggy water causing sickness, monsters

## The Pursuing Zhents

Kept it simple:
- Same speed as party; tracked how much ground both covered daily
- **River:** Zhents always fast pace, always travel into evening
- **Wilderness:** Zhents average pace (tracking); if party confounds them, slow pace for 8 hours
- Party gets one-day head start

**Designed to barely succeed:** River route — party stays one step ahead if perfect. Wilderness — party never sees the Zhents if they confound them at every turn.

## Humanity and Conflict: Stedd's Mood

Stedd has a **morale meter** (ten-point scale):
- Loses points from: fights, being endangered/hurt, going without food, forced marching into dark evening
- Gains points from: party successfully building rapport
- Loses points from: party trying to connect and failing
- At bottom: panics, casts *thunderwave* accidentally, flees — party must chase him or lose him

### Why "Failure Costs a Point" Works
1. Failure is assured if you do nothing — Stedd's mood can't survive the trip without bolstering
2. Emotional engagement overrides optimization — when a teenager is flinging spells and screaming everyone would be better off if he was dead, players stop optimizing

## The Climax: The Wererat

Even if Zhents never caught up, needed a climax. Created a **second bounty hunter** — a wererat who:
- Spied on the party (appearing as a rat in flavor text over multiple sessions)
- Tricked the party into trusting her (found "naked in the wilderness after being robbed")
- Sabotaged Stedd's mood by saying wrong things at wrong times, looking accidental
- Planned to get Stedd to abandon the party so she could grab him alone
- Powerful enough to not die in one hit but had a specific weakness

The party figured out she was deliberately messing with Stedd, then figured out she was a wererat before she transformed, bit one of them, and fled.

## Summary

The adventure layers multiple interacting systems:
1. **Route choice** — speed vs. evasion strategy
2. **Travel management** — pace, resources, complications via Tension Pool
3. **Zhent pursuit** — ticking clock responding to party's strategy
4. **Stedd's mood** — human drama demanding attention
5. **The wererat** — secondary antagonist targeting the human element
