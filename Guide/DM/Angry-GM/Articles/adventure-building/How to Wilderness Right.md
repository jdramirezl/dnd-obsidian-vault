# How to Wilderness Right

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-wilderness-right/)
**Date:** October 13, 2021
**Series:** GMing Basically

---

Angry's step-by-step wilderness travel adjudication system. 90% RAW D&D 5E, with the 10% difference from the Time Pool, the Arbitrary Game Turn, interpretation of unclear rules, and willingness to fudge toward good enough.

## Knowing the World

You need a **good GM map** with:
- Accurate scale
- Terrain at every point (D&D 5E: arctic, coast, desert, forest, grassland, mountain, swamp)
- Important destinations
- Prominent territories (dangerous regions controlled by non-civilized entities with special Complication lists)

Only mark stuff you know exists. Only plot the big stuff. The map doesn't have to be pretty—no one but you sees it.

### Territories

When the party enters a territory, there's a chance they notice it (Intelligence checks, Perception, or it's obvious like orc skulls on pikes). Anything the party does in a territory—travel, camp, search—is **Reckless**.

### Map Scale: Six Miles Per Hex

The one correct scale. Why:
- 6 is half of 12, easily divisible by 2, 3, and 4
- Easy travel time math for PCs traveling 2–4 mph
- Easy to cut speeds for rugged terrain
- On a clear day, the horizon is ~3 miles away—a PC in a clear hex can see to its edges

## What the Players Know

Players get their own map—prettier but less accurate. Use **leagues** (~3 miles, the distance a person hikes in one hour over clear terrain). The information mismatch is part of what makes travel challenging and fun.

## Navigating the World

Heroes can't click-to-travel. They can:
- Strike out in a cardinal/ordinal direction and try to keep true
- Follow a big obvious feature (road, river, shoreline)
- Follow vague directions and landmarks

## The Wilderness Travel Turn

Each turn = an attempt to reach a destination or complete one leg of a complex trip.

### Step 1: Plan the Trip
Players must declare: **direction**, **pace** (fast/normal/slow), **formation**, and **travel tasks** (navigate, forage, keep watch, track). If not following a visible feature, someone must navigate. Also establish expected travel time and supplies.

### Step 2: Plot the Projected Route
On your secret map, figure out what a successful trip looks like. Draw a line. Don't share with players.

### Step 3: Can the Trip Fail?
Trips fail by **getting lost** (failed navigation) or **missing the destination** (failed to spot it).

Can't get lost if: following a big feature, ranger in favored terrain, outlander between visited locations.
Can't miss if: heading for a big obvious thing in clear terrain.

If multiple checks needed, break into multiple Travel Turns.

### Step 4: Determine Success
Roll navigation/tracking checks **secretly**. Don't tell players yet.

**Lost:** Party travels 1d6 hours in the wrong direction (one hex side off), then gets a Survival check to realize it. If they fail, another 1d6 hours wrong, another check. Turn doesn't end until they realize they're lost.

**Missed Destination:** Party walks right past it with no awareness. They keep going until the players themselves realize something's wrong.

### Step 5: Determine What Happens Along the Way
Roll Time Pool once/day for Complications. Roll wandering monsters. Foraging checks. Territory awareness. Note planned encounters. Track supplies and projected time. Don't tell players yet—just take notes.

### Step 6: Narrate from One Interruption to the Next
If nothing interesting happens, summarize with flavor text. Narrate terrain transitions and the first couple days. At each interruption (Complication, encounter, territory boundary, low supplies, projected time reached), stop and play it out.

## Searching the Wilderness

Party can search an area for a vaguely-known location. Get them to define search parameters. ~1 hour per 6-mile hex (open) or ~2 hours (rugged). Roll the check; determine search time based on distance.

## Is Travel an Adventure?

Resolve big trips in the fewest Arbitrary Game Turns possible. This makes travel significant because players' choices matter, they can fail, and failures cost resources.

But making travel **exciting** takes more than resolution mechanics. When the party is just hiking between towns, that's a transition. When they're searching for an ancient shrine in dangerous territory, that's part of the adventure. **Plan encounters for the journey the same way you'd plan them for a dungeon.**

> Don't rely on random travel rules to carry the adventure. You're not putting encounters in hexes. You're putting encounters in an adventure.
