# Hashtag Adventure Goals

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/hashtag-adventure-goals/)
**Date:** June 28, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Setting Goals

Every adventure must have: Goal, Background, Motivation, Structure, Pace, Scenes, Hook, Climax, and Resolution. The goal is so important that it's hard to do anything else without it. It's part of the basic nature of games themselves — all games must have a goal. TTRPGs don't have one built in; adventures provide it.

**You can't make a decision if you don't know what you're trying to accomplish.**

The goal gives players information to decide how to act, provides the narrative endpoint, and delivers dopamine that keeps them coming back. It ties every other element together: motivation, hook, scenes, encounters, conflicts, climax, and resolution.

## KISS — Keep It Simple, Stupid

A goal is a single sentence: **"Verb" the "noun."** Save the dragon. Kill the princess. Recover the treasure. Escape the labyrinth.

**Simple goals are almost always the best goals.** Goals don't add depth — depth comes from structure, scenes, and conflicts. Goals only add complexity.

## Subgoals

Goals that must be accomplished before the main goal can be reached. They serve to:

1. **Provide incremental victories** — bigger than scenes but smaller than the main goal. Brains get desensitized to the same stimuli; varied victories keep players engaged.
2. **Provide breadcrumbs** — especially for big, seemingly impossible goals. "Kill a god" becomes achievable through steps.
3. **Stretch adventures without multiple goals** — you can't have multiple goals (one will end up being THE goal). Subgoals fake multiple goals by nesting them inside a bigger one.

## Side Goals

Like subgoals but completely optional. For something to be truly optional:
- The party has to **choose** to do it
- They have to be able to choose **not** to do it
- The choice has to be **deliberate**

A good side goal either (a) requires deliberate choice and wouldn't be pursued without incentive, or (b) costs the party something to do.

**Example:** "Don't kill any ogres — they're innocent thralls." The ogres will try to kill the PCs, so sparing them takes extra risk and effort.

Side goals add depth, challenge, and choice — especially when they place conditions on the main goal or require expending extra resources.

## What's in a Goal

For a goal to have meaning, players must know what it is. Goals are usually stated clearly during the hook scene.

Sub goals and side goals can be spelled out during the hook, have their own hook scenes, be revealed through gameplay, or be discoverable through clues.

### The Victory Must Be Acknowledged

To make a goal FEEL like a goal, acknowledge the victory. **Experience points are victory.** Assign each goal an XP total commensurate with its size and tell the players exactly why they earned it.

## Obfuscating Goals

Sometimes it's possible and desirable to hide goals. But heroes must always be working toward SOME goal.

### Shifting Goals

Something invalidates one goal and sets up another. **Never take away the goal without explicitly establishing a new one.** Players can NEVER have no goal.

### Excuse Goals

A subset of shifting goals — provides an excuse to get the party moving toward something that will establish the real goal.

### Discoverable Goals

Goals revealed during gameplay through clues. If the party never discovers the goal, don't reward them for accidentally achieving it.

### Surprise Goals

Side goals the party doesn't know about until they accomplish them. The key: they involve players interacting with the world beyond the scope of simply winning — showing they see the world as a world, not just quest objectives.

Surprise goals are worth a reward when they show players paid attention and made logical connections. Use carefully to avoid training players to act at random.
