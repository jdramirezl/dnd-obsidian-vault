# Let's Start at the Very End

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/lets-start-at-the-very-end/)
**Date:** August 4, 2015
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## Core Thesis

When building an adventure, **start with the ending** — the resolution. Not the hook, not the first scene. The resolution describes the ways the adventure can end and tells you when the adventure is over.

## Key Concepts

### Resolution = The WHAT

An adventure breaks down into three parts:

- **Resolution (WHAT):** The ways the adventure can end — what the heroes are trying to accomplish.
- **Motivation (WHY):** The forces pushing heroes to accomplish the goal.
- **Structure (HOW):** The obstacles and events heroes deal with to accomplish their goals.

The resolution is not a specific ending — it's the shape of how you know the adventure is done. That's why it's called a *resolution*, not an *ending*.

### The Paradox of GMing

Two competing truths:

1. **A well-prepared GM runs a better game** — combat is more interesting when well-built, stories need proper pacing, cause and effect must make sense.
2. **It's impossible to fully plan an RPG** — players expect freedom of choice, and you never know what they'll do.

The two worst solutions:
- **Plan everything, force players to follow** — sacrifices freedom.
- **Plan nothing, improvise everything** — sacrifices narrative quality.

Smart GMs find a **happy medium**: come prepared with the bits and pieces you'll likely need, but be ready to deviate, improvise, and throw away unused prep.

### Planning for Failure

A good resolution includes **both success and failure states**.

If you only plan for success, you risk the **"endless adventure"** — where PCs have failed but don't realize it, so they keep trying to succeed even though they can't.

Build in an **unambiguous failure state** — something that tells the players the adventure is over and they messed up.

## Running Examples

### Example 1: Dragon Terrorizing a Village

**Success resolution:** The dragon is killed, driven off, or some agreement is brokered — the dragon is no longer a threat to the village.

**Failure resolution:** The heroes attack the dragon and don't win. The vengeful dragon launches an assault on the village out of spite, burns it to the ground, swallows a few people, and flies off to find a new village.

### Example 2: Smuggling Ring (Urban Intrigue)

**Success resolution:** Discover HOW the smugglers are getting goods into the city (even without destroying the ring, the police can handle it from there). Better outcome: discover the method AND destroy the ring.

**Failure resolution (layered):**
1. The police force pulls the party off the case — the investigation is taking too long with no results.
2. A city official (possibly corrupt) resents the PCs taking the law into their own hands once the case is closed, creating active trouble for them.

## Important Principles

- **Resolutions aren't set in stone.** Adventure building is organic. As you figure out motivations, you might tweak resolutions. As you work through structure, motivations might need adjusting. The three parts feed each other.
- **Leave resolutions as open as possible.** Players like to be creative and make choices. Don't lock into a single path to victory.
- **You always start with the very end.** Then write the beginning, then fill in the middle.
