# How to Present Your Module, Part 1: Fixing Crappy Character Sheets

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/fixing-crappy-character-sheets/)
**Date:** October 14, 2020

---

I'm still sequestered away on my writer's retreat, working hard on the learn-to-play module — *The Fall of Silverpine Watch*. Writing this module has been an interesting experience. It's been slow going. Not because of the writing itself, but because I realized I didn't want it to suck.

## Presenting My Learn-to-Play Module

There's a huge difference between designing an adventure and writing a module. Especially if you give any sort of a f$&% about writing a good one.

The difference between a homebrew adventure you plan to use yourself and an adventure module you plan to share with the world is that, when it comes to a homebrew adventure, you're done when the design's done. Once you've sketched out the maps and flowcharts and have a goal and a plot and some encounters, there's nothing else to do.

With a module, you can't stop when the adventure is designed. Because **you have to present the design to someone else in a way that lets them run it at their table with their players without you there to help.** You have to explain the adventure to someone else. It's a whole extra step. And that step involves putting a lot of words to paper.

The problem was that explaining everything and putting words to paper made a sucky module.

## How Adventure Modules Miss the Point

Every adventure module published by WotC these days has a problem. So does every adventure published by Paizo. While they provide all the information GMs need to run their adventures and spell it all out in exhaustive detail, they do it in pretty much the s$&%iest way possible.

### The Problem: Walls of Prose

Taking *Ghosts of Saltmarsh* as an example — a single encounter is described in a column-and-a-half of text. There's some read-aloud text highlighted in green, but the rest is just paragraphs of normal text with a few boldfaced phrases. Things aren't grouped together logically. The prose just sort of flows from one topic to the next. Like a novel. **You have to read literally every word of text before you can start running the encounter properly.**

When you're running an encounter at the table, the information you need has to be **accessible**. It has to be easy to spot at a glance. You're concentrating on a lot of s$&% when you're running a game. You can't stop the action to read six paragraphs of text to figure out what happens when the PCs try to trick the guards.

### The Problem: Page Flipping

When a fight breaks out, you need monster stats from multiple sources — the module's appendix, the Monster Manual, possibly different pages of each. If reinforcements arrive, you need to flip to other encounter descriptions to find out what shows up. You're supposed to run a tense, fast, furious combat while flipping pages in two different books to keep track of four different stat blocks.

Electronic formats don't fully solve this either. You end up with lots of tabs open, lots of tab-juggling and bookmark-tapping.

### The Problem: Prep Burden

You might argue that GMs should expect to do some prep work. But there's a lot of details to remember. And they're hard to find in a pinch. The more open-ended the module, the less you can expect a GM to keep in his head because there's just too many possibilities to be ready for.

**The module's designer knows exactly what the module's for.** They know who's using it for what and how. The format WotC has chosen — and the one everyone imitates — is a format that ignores all the issues for all the formats. It's a format that would only be chosen by someone who didn't know what the product was going to be used for or who didn't give a f$&% how it was going to be used.

## Speaking of the End User…

I found myself struggling because I knew that, while I was totally in line with every product WotC and everyone else had ever published for D&D 5E, I was presenting things the worst way I possibly could.

But my goal with this module isn't just to write a better module than anything WotC has ever produced. My goal is to write a module that **teaches people how to run modules**. This module is supposed to teach an inexperienced GM how to run a game for the first time and prepare them to run more games with more modules.

### The Audience Dilemma

Can I assume that only inexperienced GMs who've read my book will be running the module? No — a lot of readers are experienced GMs who might still want to run it. These assumptions matter for two reasons:

1. **General design concerns** — If I design a solid but simple tutorial adventure, I'll disappoint experienced readers who want something they can use too.
2. **Teaching concerns** — If I present my module in a radically different format from every other module that exists, I'm not really teaching newbies how to run games. They'll have a hard time moving to other published modules afterward.

So I can't change the world with this project. But I can clean it up a little.

## Presenting: The Learn-to-Play Characters

The pregenerated characters for *The Fall of Silverpine Watch* were designed with six goals:

### 1. Everything Needed on the Sheet
Players should have everything they need to understand how to play their characters right in front of them. No cracking books. Since the GM is probably learning too, they shouldn't have to answer hard questions or crack books either.

### 2. No Extraneous Crap
Background features are pretty useless in most games. Inspiration? Personality? Traits? Flaws? Alignment? Crap. Players don't need that s$&% and GMs don't use it. For a one-shot dungeon-crawler tutorial game, they won't matter.

### 3. Don't Dump Flavor
These character sheets might be the first tangible thing these players ever see of D&D or RPGs. If they have the souls of tax forms, they're not going to sell anyone on the experience. So background and world lore stayed in — even if the details were just simple little stories the players could use or ignore.

### 4. Setting Details for Fans
The module is set in the Angryverse (the dull, generic, default fantasy world for Angry's home games) and includes details about the setting in the fluff text on the character sheets.

### 5. Minimize Flipping and Shuffling
Every character had to fit on a single page. Both sides could be used, but only if the players only needed to look at one side during play. The **front** has everything needed during play. The **back** has only background information needed before play or to clarify things in an emergency.

### 6. Look Like the Official Sheet
The sheet had to look as much like the official D&D sheet as possible. Things could be cleaned up, elements removed or expanded, but things had to stay in mostly the same places. Anyone who learned on these sheets shouldn't end up completely lost when they make their own character on a real sheet later.

### The Result

Everything the cleric might do during play can be fully resolved just by looking at the front of the sheet. Even the spells and class features. Stuff is mostly where you'd find it on a real sheet. Everything can be tracked right on the sheet — check bubbles for hit dice and spell slots, fillable forms for hit points and character details.

Everything's explained in more detail on the back with background information. Each character's class, race, and background are written to hint at details about how the world works — from the polytheistic nature of religion to the detachment commoners feel from the feudal politics of the warring kingdoms of the Sunderlands.

## A Plea to Homebrew Creators

To all readers and fans who make their own homebrew content to share with others — to all the folks who distribute adventures and encounters and monsters:

**Stop imitating WotC.** They aren't good at presenting this s$&%. They don't think about the people at the table. The ones who have to use this s$&%. The GMs in the trenches. They've forgotten that their job is to empower GMs to create awesome player experiences. That it's the GMs — NOT WotC — that make the experience great.

The next time you put together a module you want to sell or share, **allocate twice as much time to the project as you normally do** and spend that extra time thinking about the person running your game and whether there's a better way to get your information into their brain.

### The Irony

We homebrewers are the ones who write the gaming content for others to use. And we have no experience running games from modules. Because we're homebrewers. So, we present our s$&% the way WotC presents their s$&%. We assume they know what they're doing. But they don't seem to.

We homebrewers-cum-module-writers are basically creating stuff we'd never use ourselves at our own tables. And that gives us a big blind spot. We can't trust WotC to fill it in for us anymore.
