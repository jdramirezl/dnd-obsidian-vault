# How to Present Your Module, Part 2: Reorganizing Encounters

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/reorganizing-encounters/)
**Date:** October 28, 2020

---

## What is an Adventure Module?

An **adventure** is a single, self-contained D&D game with a goal, beginning, challenges, endings, settings, plot, and characters.

An **adventure module** is a product that lets a GM run that adventure. It delivers the info a GM needs and provides useful tools to make that adventure happen at their table.

Shaded flavor-text boxes help the GM set the scene, but GMs are supposed to be able to do that themselves. GMs don't need the narration spelled out—they need to know which details are too important to leave out.

## Reading, Skimming, and Referencing

Most GMs know they're supposed to read every word in a module before running it. But they get bored once they hit individual encounters and monster stats. They start to skim. Module writers should decide what needs to be read thoroughly and what can just be skimmed, and present accordingly.

## Form vs. Function

Printed books care about page count above all. Electronic documents can add pages trivially but page-flipping and skimming are chores. Most products must satisfy both formats.

### The Stat Block Problem

In-line stat blocks would be better for GMs, but most modules reuse the same monsters. In D&D, stat blocks take up a lot of real estate. In-line stat blocks eat page space repeating the same content. The ideal solution is separate designs for print, electronic, and print-friendly formats—but that costs more time and money.

## Information Hierarchies in Module Design

GMs want to know what they must read vs. what they can skim-and-reference during play. Information must be segregated accordingly:

1. **Introductory information** — background/context, must be read in advance (prose walls of text are fine)
2. **Game material** — encounter/scene/location info used during play (needs to be skimmable with short prose read-in-advance sections clearly marked)
3. **Reference materials** — stat blocks, maps, handouts (useful at a glance)

The body of the adventure needs to be more skimmable than an encyclopedia. It should look more like an instruction manual—short chunks organized so you can find what you need even after looking away.

### Summaries

Individual adventure parts and even individual encounters benefit from short summaries. Useful to read in advance AND to refer to during play.

## Hierarchical Encounters

What does a GM do when running an encounter?

1. **Set the scene**
2. **Present something urgent** that invites the players to act
3. **Resolve those actions**
4. **Transition** to the next scene

The action resolution section is the hard part. How do you organize it so the GM can find what they need?

**Answer:** When players take actions, they're either interacting with a specific thing or reacting to a specific event. All relevant resolution information can be grouped with the things and events to which they relate.

### Draft Examples from The Fall of Silverpine Watch

Angry showed rough drafts of two encounters:

- **B6** — The simplest encounter in the module. Players take a long rest, GM explains how long rests work, an NPC imparts useful foreshadowing information.
- **B7** — The most complex encounter. An open-ended investigative scene where players examine a scene for clues. GM needs background information close at hand.

Key design elements in the drafts:
- Summary with certain words emphasized in a specific order
- Clear separation between scene-setting, incitement, action resolution, and transitions
- Action resolution grouped by things and events players interact with
- Background information kept close at hand for investigative scenes
