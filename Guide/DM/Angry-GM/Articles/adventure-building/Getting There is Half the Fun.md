# Getting There is Half the Fun

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/getting-there-is-half-the-fun/)
**Date:** December 15, 2016
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## The Problem

Heroes spend a lot of time traveling between points A and B. From a story perspective, travel should be exciting and dangerous. From a gameplay perspective, travel doesn't make for interesting gameplay — "ludocognitive dissonance."

## The Two Good Solutions

### Solution 1: Skip It

Narrate the travel. "After several days of travel through the wilderness, you arrive." This is a perfectly good solution.

### Solution 2: Make Travel Interesting

Make travel interesting enough to be a fun part of the game.

## The Crappy Solution Everyone Uses

1. **The Well-Intentioned GM:** Rolls random encounters every in-game day. One random combat every few days — not serious threats.
2. **The Genauein Encounter Solution:** Whenever the party travels, they have ONE AND PRECISELY ONE encounter.

Both are evolutionary steps — the GM should either narrate travel as a montage or commit to making it genuinely interesting.

## A Case for Interesting Travel

If travel is hard and daunting, PCs far from home won't retreat whenever the going gets tough. They'll want to get stuff done quickly. Site-based adventures gain three parts: there, adventure, and back again.

This creates campaign progression: at low levels, the party can't travel far. As they gain means (mounts, bags of holding, teleportation), the world opens up.

## The Five Factors of Travel

### 1. Time

The biggest factor. More time = more danger and more supplies needed. Affected by distance, terrain, and chosen route. In 5E, the party can choose pace (Slow, Medium, Fast) with tradeoffs.

### 2. Danger

Chance of encountering hazards or monsters. Varies by region:
- Civilized regions: safest
- Forests, flatlands, hills, wetlands: most dangerous
- Barren regions: somewhat safer
- Intelligent creatures claim territory, increasing danger
- Fantasy elements modify danger

Players SHOULD know about many dangers — it empowers informed decisions.

### 3. Navigation

Without magical means, navigating is harder than people realize. If you're one foot off in ten feet, you'll be miles off target after days. Navigation relies on landmarks and line of sight. Getting lost means traveling the wrong direction AND not being able to find the way back.

### 4. Resources

Food and water availability varies by terrain:
- Lush wilderness: possible to forage day-to-day
- Barren wilderness: foraging much harder
- Deserts/wastelands: yield almost nothing
- Civilized roads: inns and homesteads for coin

### 5. Discoveries

The pull-you-off-the-road-by-curiosity factor. The fantasy world is old — there's always some half-collapsed tower or magical waterfall over the next rise. The party should spot something weird and debate checking it out.

## How to Actually Handle Travel

### Mapping

You don't need detailed maps. Blobby, vague maps work. The key is the choices the map creates. Track travel in DAYS, not miles.

### The Travel Day

Party chooses pace at the start of each day:

| Pace | Speed | Perception | Stealth | Foraging | Navigation |
|------|-------|------------|---------|----------|------------|
| **Slow** | 2/3 normal | Advantage/+4 | Enemies Disadv/-4 | Normal | Advantage/+4 |
| **Medium** | Normal | Normal | Normal | Disadv/-4 | Normal |
| **Fast** | 4/3 normal | Disadv/-4 | Enemies Adv/+4 | Cannot forage | Disadv/-4 |

**Roll 6d6** (Morning, Afternoon, Evening, Dusk, Midnight, Predawn). Each die showing the Danger number or less = a potential encounter.

**Roll 1d6** for discoveries — showing Discovery number or less = something interesting off the road.

### Making Camp (End of Day)

1. **Navigation check** (secret): Best navigator vs. Navigation DC. Failure = lost.
2. **Log progress:** Based on pace. Subtract 1/3 if they investigated a discovery.
3. **Forage checks:** Each character rolls vs. Forage DC. Failures require consuming rations.

### Being Lost

Log zero progress. Navigator rolls secretly each day — success means they recognize they're lost the next morning. Once aware, the party needs a new plan: head toward a known feature, find a landmark, or backtrack.

### Random Encounters

Not just combat. Include hostile creatures, animals defending territory, hazards (quicksand, flash floods), obstacles (rockfalls, washed-out bridges), and social encounters. Skew combat toward the hard end of the difficulty curve.

### Discoveries

One or two per trip. Encounters that bait the party into checking them out. Most should offer some reward. Have a pile ready.

## Tweaks for 5E

- Use **variant encumbrance rules** (PHB 176)
- Drop "activity while traveling" — disguises nonchoices as choices
- Tone down 5E's generous foraging
- **Outlander (Wanderer):** Replace with mechanical bonuses to Navigation and Foraging
- **Ranger (Natural Explorer):** Replace with Advantage on Navigation, Advantage on Perception while traveling, foraging yields enough for a second person
