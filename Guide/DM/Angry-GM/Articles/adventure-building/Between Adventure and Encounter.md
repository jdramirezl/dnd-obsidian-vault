# Between Adventure and Encounter

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/between-adventure-and-encounter/)
**Date:** August 15, 2019
**Category:** Adventure Building (Practical)

---

## The Problem: A Missing Layer

D&D has certain structures built into the game system:

- **The Action** — Player declares, GM resolves using rules, GM describes results.
- **The Encounter** — GM describes scene, players act, GM acts as opponents, continues until one side wins.
- **The Adventure** — GM presents a goal, a string of Encounters happens, players eventually win.

**But there's a big gap.** The part of the game inside the Adventure but outside the Encounter — the space some call "Exploration" — has no name, description, advice, guidelines, mechanics, or structures.

Examples of this undefined state:
- Wandering the dungeon room to room between Encounters
- Traveling across wilderness from point A to point B
- Exploring a town, getting to know locals
- Following leads during a murder mystery

Because this state is vague "inbetween" space, very few people think to do anything with it beyond letting players wander until an encounter happens. The adventure becomes a **featureless ocean** — players sail from island to island, all interesting things happen on the islands, and the best mechanic for the ocean is rolling randomly for another island (random encounter).

### Why This Matters

- A lot of time is spent on that ocean — wasted time
- Many GMs struggle with running those parts
- It's the perfect place to manage **winning, losing, and setbacks**
- D&D's only structure is "the adventure day" — get six fights before the party sleeps

---

## Redefining Scenes

The word **Scene** is reclaimed for this missing layer between Adventure and Encounter.

**Scenes** are broad chunks of adventure — mostly location-based or based on a subgoal or chunk of time — that contain multiple Encounters plus the sea of stuff between them.

### Example: The Sheep Mystery

Villagers of Knoware hire PCs to figure out why they have fewer sheep. A monster in the Hills of Sumplays Els is stealing and eating them.

**Scene 1: The Village Investigation**
- PCs wander the village gathering information, checking crime scenes
- The Hunters' Guild leader tries to discredit the adventurers
- More encounters screwed up → more popular opinion turns against them
- Screw up too much → villagers run them out of town
- Do particularly well → gain extra information about the monster

**Scene 2: The Wilderness Search**
- PCs track the monster to its lair in the Hills
- Longer they wander → more supplies wasted, more random encounters
- Longer they take → more likely the monster returns to town and kills more sheep or a villager

**Scene 3: The Dungeon**
- The monster's lair with slimes, giant spiders, and dungeon vermin

### Overarching Mechanics for Scenes

These broader chunks can have overarching mechanics that play into the encounters:

- Village mood affects the investigation
- Locations have wandering monsters or consequences of exploring
- A haunted house takes periodic actions against the party regardless of location
- Strahd teleports around the castle when heroes attract attention
- A burning building has mechanics for fire spread, smoke inhalation, or a timer

Good adventures do this — they tie stuff to particular locations. But there's no format, no precedent in the DMG, no tools or advice for it.

---

## The Key Insight

Structure Adventures not just as a goal with a string of Encounters, but **break the Adventure into Scenes first:**

- What are the smaller goals in each section?
- Can you build overarching conflicts or progressions?
- What are the win and loss conditions?
- Can you give stats or mechanics to the map/flowchart itself — treating IT as a game mechanic?

The closest the books get is telling you to draw a dungeon map or encounter flowchart. Very rarely is the GM advised to give mechanics to the map itself.

### Practical Example: A Tournament Adventure

- **Scene 1: Arriving at Town** — Secure lodgings, register for tournament, introductory Encounters to meet townsfolk and foreshadow events.
- **Scene 2: The Tournament** — Two days of events and Encounters. A mechanic tracks each PC's popularity to determine who gets invited to the dance by whom — leading to other Encounters and determining where everyone is when disaster strikes at the dance.

---

## Summary

- There is a **missing layer** in the game design hierarchy between Adventures and Encounters — **Scenes**.
- Scenes are broad chunks of adventure (location-based or subgoal-based) containing multiple Encounters plus the "sea of stuff" between them.
- Scenes can have their own **overarching mechanics, win/loss conditions, and progressions**.
- Breaking adventures into Scenes first, then populating with Encounters, produces better-structured adventures.
- **Synch Scenes with Sessions** so you can end every Session on a Climax, Reversal, or Cliffhanger.
