# Four Things You've Never Heard of That Make Encounters Not Suck

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/four-things-youve-never-heard-of-that-make-encounters-not-suck/)
**Date:** May 7, 2013
**Series:** Encounter Building Basics

---

## What is an Encounter?

An encounter is a sequence of actions that answer a **dramatic question** by resolving one or more **conflicts**.

Structural hierarchy: Action → Encounter → (Act) → Adventure → (Arc) → Campaign

When wearing the Fedora of Game Runnery, you work with individual actions fitting into the overall structure. When wearing the Green Eyeshade of Game Plannery, you work with higher-level structures: Encounters, Adventures, Campaigns.

---

## 1: Dramatic Questions

The dramatic question is the alpha and omega of every encounter. Every encounter begins by posing one and ends when the players have an answer.

A dramatic question is the heroes' current objective rephrased as a yes-or-no question:
- "Does Indiana Jones escape from the collapsing temple?"
- "Can Indiana Jones escape from the horde of warriors?"
- "Does Indiana Jones recover the Ark from the Nazi convoy?"

It tells us what is at stake, why the scene is important, and when it's over.

### Why Your Combat Encounters Suck (Part 1)

A giant spider drops from the ceiling. Without a dramatic question, the default becomes "can the party kill the spider?" But the real question might be "can the party safely pass through the cave?" — which opens up more solutions than just fighting to the death. When the cleric immobilizes the spider and the party flees past, the encounter is over.

### Posing the Dramatic Question

State the question in your head at the start of every encounter. **You are not allowed to run an encounter without stating the dramatic question.** If you can't state it, you can't explain why players should care or how the scene should end.

If a scene comes out of nowhere and you aren't sure what the players want, **ask them.**

---

## 2: Conflicts

A conflict occurs when the heroes are prevented from achieving their objectives. Sources of conflict include monsters, NPCs, traps, hazards, obstacles, capricious gods, nature, states of mind, or party disagreements.

**A spider in a room is not a conflict.** It's not even a potential conflict by itself. Conflict occurs between two forces.

### Why Your Combat Encounters Suck (Part 2)

"The spider jealously guards its lair, attacking anyone who sets foot in its cave" — THAT is a source of conflict. The party wants to walk through; the spider wants to protect its home. Neither goal can live while the other survives.

**DMs don't create conflicts — they create sources of conflict.** A good encounter is a trainwreck, but DMs don't wreck the trains. They put trains on the same tracks so that once the party starts driving, they crash.

### The Motives of Brainless Things

Personify inanimate things:
- A **locked door** is a physical manifestation of someone's desire to keep everyone without the key out
- A **trap** is a manifestation of a desire to hinder, injure, kill, or warn
- A **river** will not bend its course for anyone — it represents the force of nature, which cannot be controlled and kills those who don't respect it

### Death and Other Ways to End Conflict

Once desires are no longer in opposition, or one side loses the ability to oppose the other, the conflict is resolved. It doesn't matter how.

### Multiple Conflicts

You can throw multiple sources of conflict into a scene: orcs vs. heroes, rain vs. archers, fire vs. everyone. Think about how sources of conflict come into conflict with each other.

### Internal Conflicts

Any given thing might have more than one desire. The orc's desire to kill vs. its desire to survive. The paladin choosing between justice and mercy. **Never dabble in the heads of player-characters** — players resolve their own internal conflicts. But plant internal conflicts in NPCs.

## 3: Decision Points

Decision points are moments when you ask "what do you do now?" Without them, there are no actions and no RPG.

### Keeping Encounters from Overstaying Their Welcome

When the dramatic question is answered or all conflicts are resolved, **end the encounter.** No matter what. Narrate a wrap-up, assume the next hit kills the monster, let monsters flee. **It is always better to end an encounter too early than let it become boring.**

Never let the players keep an encounter open after you decide it's over. If they want to continue, start a new encounter with a new dramatic question.

### True Decision Points

A decision point only occurs when **something changes**. If you could say "the situation is the same, now what do you do?" — that's not a decision point. Players act in response to stimuli.

Things that are NOT decision points:
- Reacting with a specific saving throw (player didn't decide anything)
- Choosing whether to continue doing something or stop (non-decision)
- Locks requiring three lockpicking checks (same action repeated)

When the majority of heroes have run out of decision points, you have **three turns** to resolve the encounter before it sucks.

### The Time Limit

EVERY encounter has a time limit created by decision points. Like golf — as you get closer to the hole, the number of useful places to aim shrinks until there's only one: the hole itself. All encounters work this way. You can fight it by adding new stimuli, but too much makes encounters frustratingly long.

## 4: Structures and Scoreboards

You need to know when the dramatic question has been answered and when conflicts have been resolved. Sometimes it's easy (party leaves the cave, dragon is dead). Sometimes it's extremely difficult.

### No Universal Framework

You cannot build a single universal framework for all encounters. Trying to cram encounters into universal frameworks ruins good encounters.

### When You Need Structure

Ask yourself if you really need one. Can you wing it? If not, look for obvious indicators (party captured the flag, crossed the finish line).

### The Ten-Point Scale (Quick Structure)

Figure out what's important, give it a score from 1 to 10, don't start at 1 or 10:
- Orcs begin with ferocity 8 and fear 2. Things that scare them increase fear; things that enrage them increase ferocity. If fear equals ferocity, they flee.
- Party has 7 distance units head start over an ogre. Ogre reduces distance by 1 each round. Party actions to slow the ogre increase distance by 1. At 0, caught. At 10, ogre gives up.

You can create new scores in response to player actions. If someone intimidates an orc, create a "fear" score and tell the party the orcs look nervous.

### The Dreaded "I Win" Button

If a player finds a way to win the encounter in a single action — even without a die roll — **they won the encounter.** If you can't stomach that, you can't stomach being a DM. The only reason to deny a properly declared action that answers the dramatic question is because you care more about your scorekeeping than running a role-playing game.

## Running an Encounter Like a Boss

1. **Identify the dramatic question** (party's actual objectives as a yes-or-no question)
2. **Identify sources of conflict** (why things will prevent the heroes from answering the question)
3. **Ask if you need a structure/scoreboard** (if you can wing it, do so)
4. **Narrate the opening** — describe what's happening, mention the dramatic question's subject, describe obvious sources of conflict
5. **Ask players what they do** — adjudicate each action (intention, approach, outcome, consequences)
6. **After each action, check:** Should players now know the answer? Are all conflicts resolved? If yes, end the encounter. If no, ask for another action.
7. **Watch for players running out of decision points** — three turns max before wrapping up

**End the encounter. That is how you run an encounter like a boss.**
