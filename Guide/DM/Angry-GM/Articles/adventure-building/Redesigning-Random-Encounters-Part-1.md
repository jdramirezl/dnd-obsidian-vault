# Always Tell Me the Odds: Redesigning Random Encounters (Part 1)

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/redesigning-random-encounters-1/) | **Category**: Adventure Building

---

## Key Advice

- **Random encounters are the salt in the cake.** They serve subtle but critical functions — tension, pacing, player agency — that most GMs don't notice until they remove them. Don't rip them out; fix them.
- **The randomness is for the players, not the GM.** What matters is that players can *see* the causal link between their choices (wasting time, being loud) and the chance of something bad happening. The actual odds behind the screen don't matter.
- **Random encounters don't need to advance the plot to enhance the story.** They create tension, embody player agency, and remind players the world is dynamic and beyond the GM's total control.
- **Players who see visibly random encounters take threats more seriously.** In a carefully balanced game, players can feel invincible. Random encounters break that certainty because they're outside the planned balance.
- **The random encounter *table* is the least important part.** The important element is the *chance* of an encounter happening, not what the encounter is. A d6 roll every 10 minutes (encounter on a 1) is all you need mechanically.

## Summary

This article is Part 1 of a two-part series on redesigning random encounters for modern D&D and Pathfinder. Angry argues that most GMs who dismiss random encounters are making the same mistake as a baker who leaves salt out of a cake — they can't see what it does, so they assume it does nothing. The result is games that lack tension, where players feel invincible, and where time management and resource conservation have no teeth.

The article traces how random encounters devolved from "chance meetings between parties of unknown motives" (with reaction rolls, parley, evasion, and trade) into "lists of random combats that jump out and attack." This happened as D&D editions stripped away reaction tables, pursuit rules, and the broader definition of "monster" as any creature of unknown intentions. The video game model of combat-swoosh random encounters took over.

Angry identifies what random encounters actually do well: they create a visible, causal connection between player choices and risk. Spending time searching every room, being noisy, failing to stop a fleeing enemy — all of these increase the chance of a random encounter. This makes player decisions meaningful in ways that disappear without random encounters. Why *don't* PCs take 20 on every roll? Why *don't* they search every square? Because random encounters are the cost of wasting time.

The article then introduces the Tension Pool mechanic: every 10 minutes of in-game time, drop a d6 into a visible bowl. Roll the pool when it hits 6 dice, or when players do something reckless. A 1 on any die triggers an encounter. The visible pool connects player actions to risk in a tangible, dread-building way. It also caps encounter frequency (max one per hour normally) and tracks time passage. The key design principle: figure out the normal play rate, set odds for 1-2 random encounters per 4-6 planned encounters, and make the causal relationship between player actions and encounter chance visible.

Part 2 (not included here) covers how to design the encounters themselves.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Don't remove random encounters — redesign them.** Use them for a while, identify what they add (tension, agency, pacing), then cut the crap and polish what remains.
2. **Make the randomness visible.** Players should see you roll. Use a visible dice pool or roll openly so they understand the causal link between their choices and the risk.
3. **Use the Tension Pool.** Drop a d6 into a visible bowl every 10 minutes of game time. Roll the pool at 6 dice or when players are reckless/loud. A 1 triggers an encounter.
4. **Cap encounter frequency.** Only roll the pool once per hour by default. Extra rolls come from player actions (noise, alarms, fleeing enemies). This prevents unlucky streaks from wrecking the session.
5. **Cut unnecessary complexity.** Don't model area dangerousness with different target numbers or modifiers — players can't see those anyway. One simple mechanic is enough.
6. **Tie consequences to the pool.** A fleeing goblin screaming for help? Roll the pool. An alarm trap triggered? Roll the pool. This teaches players that their tactical choices have downstream effects.
7. **Set your baseline rate.** Aim for roughly 1 random encounter per 2-3 planned encounters. For a standard session with encounters every 30-60 minutes, that's about a 1-in-6 chance per 10-minute increment.
