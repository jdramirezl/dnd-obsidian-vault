# Fanservice BS: A Thing About Mimics or Whatever

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/mimics-or-whatever/)
**Date:** November 30, 2017

---

## False Appearance in 5E

False Appearance exists solely to tell the GM that "this thing will automatically get the drop on the players." Until the mimic glues itself to the rogue and starts beating it to death, there is NO WAY to distinguish it from normal furniture. Perception won't do it. Investigation won't do it. Indistinguishable means "cannot by any means be differentiated from something else."

## Mimics as an Arms Race

Trap monsters were part of an arms race in early D&D. Mimics confounded overly cautious parties in two ways:

1. **They weren't monsters.** No way to hear them listening at the door, see them through a keyhole, or spot them by sending a scout ahead. The party couldn't get the jump on them.
2. **They weren't traps either.** Parties obsessed with checking every door and chest for traps had no chance to detect them. Magical trap detection didn't work. The carnivorous variety attacked once you got close enough.

There was also a friendly variety of mimic in AD&D 1E that would be nice if you offered it food. If you whacked every chest with a sword, you might stab a friendly, helpful monster and lose important information. And mimics are covered with fast-dry super-strength glue — if you used a sword as a mimic detector, when you found one, now the mimic had your sword.

## Nothing in D&D is Anything — Just Rules and Descriptions

From a purely mechanical standpoint, nothing in D&D is anything. There are just rules and an overlay of description. A snake is a game construct that gets a turn in combat, can move, and can take actions. The "snake" comes from descriptions applied while rules are resolved.

A well-designed monster has abilities and traits that align with the description. If the monster is a known creature, it should be possible to guess what it is from the stat block alone. **This is why reskinning is problematic** — the description and mechanical representation should be hard to separate.

### You Can Model Anything as a Monster

You could model fire as a monster: immune to all damage except cold, immune to all conditions. On its turn, it attacks anyone adjacent with fire damage. It can move 5 feet into flammable spaces and leaves behind another fire when it moves. Each instance doubles itself every time it spreads.

## Inheritance and Game Design

Everything comes down to **inheritance** (a programming concept). You define a type of thing with certain properties ("monster" or "trap"), then define sub-types ("goblin" or "pit trap"). The goblin inherits all monster properties plus its own unique ones, and can modify or remove inherited properties.

**Example from D&D 3.5:** All creatures got one standard + one move action (or one full-round action) per turn. Zombies inherited this but had a trait limiting them to only a single standard OR move action. Never both.

## The Problem with D&D 5E's Design

There are really only three types of things in 5E: Player-Characters, Monsters, and Everything Else.

The traps section of the DMG puts very little effort into defining properties of traps as a whole. Each trap is paragraphs of prose with numbers buried inside, not even in the same order. This makes traps muddy, confusing, and difficult to customize. (Xanathar's Guide makes up for this.)

**What stat blocks represent is a thought process:** "This is a class of thing. Before inventing things, figure out what makes all of these things what they are and define the rules. Then present details and exceptions clearly." That thought process should apply to everything: traps, magic items, vehicles, gods, planes of existence.

## Mimics Are Not Actually Trap Monsters

Classifying a mimic as a "trap monster" is a gross misstatement. Mimics have all monster properties. But being completely undetectable and unable to be disarmed or bypassed means they have none of the properties that make traps traps. They only behave like traps **descriptively**, not **mechanically**. They are just monsters that always have the element of surprise.

If mimics WERE designed as true trap monsters (using classification and inheritance):
- Could not be disarmed or sabotaged by usual means (stated explicitly)
- Until "sprung," would be **detectable** — careful inspection would reveal its true nature
- Might be possible to spot clues that something is amiss at a distance
- Would still likely attack the rogue the moment they got close

The hilarity would come from gameplay rather than from "there's no way to see this coming and now you're glued to a violent treasure chest eating your face."
