# Hide and Seek: Optional Content in TTRPGs

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/hide-and-seek-quest-optional/)
**Date:** July 31, 2017
**Series:** Random Bulls$&%

---

## Optional vs. Hidden Content

Most content in an RPG is optional by accident — it's part of the nature of the design. GMs create worlds, fill them with stuff, and accept players will pick their own path. But **hidden content** has deliberateness about it. It's not just that the party might skip room 17 — it's that room 17 is hidden behind a secret door.

## Why Video Games Include Hidden Content

1. **Replay factor** — players play again to find what they missed (less relevant for TTRPGs)
2. **Water-cooler talk** — different players have different experiences, making for great conversation
3. **Player engagement choice** — players decide how deeply to engage (e.g., Dark Souls' entire story is optional)
4. **Sense of discovery** — games focused on exploration and discovery are loaded with hidden content because there has to be actual stuff to discover

## Why Hidden Content Matters in TTRPGs

Two strong reasons:
1. **Sense of discovery** — only possible if a given discovery could be overlooked. If there was no way to miss something, not missing it is not an accomplishment.
2. **Freedom and agency** — your choices and actions don't matter if the outcome is a foregone conclusion. Hidden content is a big neon sign that flashes "freedom here!"

## The Freedom to Miss Stuff

A sense of discovery is only possible if a given discovery could be overlooked. Players know this, even unconsciously. The trophy for completing the tutorial doesn't feel as good as the trophy for finding all collectibles. One is a participation award; one rewards actual accomplishment.

Even if no player in your group cares about discovery specifically, the ability to miss stuff emphasizes that **players' actions and choices change the game in a fundamental way.** If you choose to explore a side path and discover a rare treasure, that treasure is yours because of the choice you made.

## Hidden vs. Merely Optional

The distinction is psychological and critical:

- **Optional content:** The party comes to an intersection; the boss is to the left; everything to the right is optional. The party simply chooses whether to go left or right. This isn't special — most RPG content is optional.
- **Hidden content:** The stuff behind the hidden door. The party had to do something deliberate and clever to find it. When they find a secret door leading to a magical sword, they know they could have missed it. That feels special.

There's a difference between choosing not to engage with something and missing it completely. Merely having options isn't exciting in a game that is literally about nothing but having options. Discovering something you may have overlooked? That's special — it drives home that the game isn't designed around your character; it's a world that exists even if you don't see it.

## Flagging Hidden Content

Hidden stuff has to LOOK hidden so that when the party finds it, they can say "wow, we would have missed that if we weren't so awesome." Every adventure should have some hidden stuff.

Plan deliberately hidden content that is obviously hidden:
- **Some easy to find** — obvious clues that invite investigation with high likelihood of success
- **Some more difficult** — only vague clues
- **Proper execution is key** — if the party never discovers ANY hidden content, it isn't doing anything for your game

## The Value Proposition

To a GM, hidden content is extra work the party might never see. GMs already design enough content that never sees the light of day. But hidden content creates:
- A sense of discovery
- A sense of freedom and agency
- The feeling that the world is real and exists beyond the quest objectives
- Player excitement when they find something obviously hidden

Every adventure should include some deliberately hidden content — not willy-nilly, but planned with obvious "hidden" flags so players feel clever when they find it.
