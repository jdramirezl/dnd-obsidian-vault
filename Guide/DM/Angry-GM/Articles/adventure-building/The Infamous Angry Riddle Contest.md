# The Infamous Angry Riddle Contest

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-infamous-angry-riddle-contest/)
**Date:** April 18, 2017
**Series:** Angry Table Tales

---

## Overview

A reposted puzzle contest from Angry's early site history. Presented as a scene from a fictional game, the party attempts to recover the Crown of Kings from a "prove your worth" dungeon. The final test is a logic puzzle involving three treasure chests. One player attempts to solve it, gets it wrong, and dies. The reader is challenged to identify what the player overlooked and what the party should have done.

## The Puzzle Setup

The party enters a room with three treasure chests bound in iron, copper, and bronze, plus a statue. A booming voice delivers the conditions:

> You have proven your might, but you have not yet proven yourself worthy of the Crown of Kings. A true ruler must temper his might with wisdom. Two of the chests will destroy anyone who touches them. The third is safe and contains the Crown. If you would claim the crown, lay your hand upon the statue and listen to its words. If you are not willing to risk death, you may leave. A ruler must take great risks and make great sacrifices.

The room has countermeasures preventing cheating (magical senses blocked, traps undetectable, no circumvention possible).

## The Statue's Four Statements

After touching the statue, it speaks:

1. "I speak only one true sentence."
2. "The copper chest is deadly to touch."
3. "The bronze chest is deadly to touch."
4. "The copper chest is safe to touch and contains the Crown."

## New Guy's (Wrong) Logic

New Guy reasoned: The two copper chest statements contradict each other — one must be true, one false. Since the statue says only one statement is true, the bronze chest statement must be false. Therefore the bronze chest is safe.

**He opened the bronze chest and was killed instantly.**

## The Two Questions

1. What did the player overlook?
2. What should the party have done (or do now) to find the Crown?

## Context and Design Notes

- The puzzle was originally part of a fictional "Angry Table Tales" series — not a real game session.
- The original contest generated ~200 answers; only about a dozen were correct.
- Some respondents sent death threats and stalked Angry across social media.
- The puzzle was specifically designed to trap people who are good at logic puzzles by exploiting their assumptions.
- The solution was posted the following week in a companion article.

## Key Design Principles

- **Countermeasures must be pre-planned and logical.** The dungeon designers anticipated bypass attempts and built countermeasures. Angry had notes prepared showing all countermeasures were designed beforehand.
- **Fairness means the GM hasn't misrepresented conditions** and the logic is sound — not that there are no consequences or that die rolls are involved.
- **The booming voice (trustworthy narrator) vs. the statue (suspect source)** — the separation between instructions and clues is central to the puzzle's design.
