# All Aboard the Plot Train

## Key Advice

Railroading isn't really a useful concept. The real problem is the **power struggle** between GM and players over control of the narrative. Whether the GM starts it (forcing plot developments) or the players start it (being contrarian), the resulting slap fight is what ruins the game. Don't fear railroading — fear the power struggle, and address it out-of-game when it happens.

## Summary

Angry argues that "railroading" has been diluted into meaninglessness — it now just means "I don't like what you did, GM." He traces the original definition (GM forcing players through predetermined events with no deviation allowed) and then systematically dismantles it:

- **Planning isn't railroading.** Even improvisation is just planning and executing in the same step. GMs must plan and nudge to create good stories and good games.
- **Unavoidable elements aren't railroading.** Every adventure with a goal has things the players must encounter. A boss at the end of a dungeon isn't a railroad.
- **The real issue is the power struggle.** When players feel their agency is diminished, they push back. When the GM feels players are being contrarian, they push harder. This escalation — not the initial act — is what destroys games.
- **It's an out-of-game problem.** Never solve out-of-game problems (perceived railroading, player contrariness) with in-game solutions (punishing alignment shifts, making the forest empty).

His practical advice: Don't worry about accidentally railroading. Well-intentioned GMs naturally course-correct. Don't paralyze yourself into planning nothing out of fear. The mysterious ally who betrays the party is a great plot device — don't throw away your hammer because it can break skulls.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop worrying about railroading** — recognize your capacity for it, but don't let fear of it paralyze your planning.
2. **Plan great moments** — plot twists, betrayals, dramatic reveals. These aren't railroads; they're good adventure design. They only become problems if you force them when players resist.
3. **Watch for the power struggle** — the moment you find yourself fighting players for control of the narrative (or vice versa), that's the real problem.
4. **Address it out-of-game** — if players feel railroaded, stop the game and talk. If players are being contrarian, stop the game and talk. Never solve these problems in-game.
5. **Be willing to let go** — if players see through your setup and veer away, let them. A well-intentioned GM adapts rather than escalating into a power struggle.
6. **Distinguish contrariness from agency** — players asserting meaningful choices is healthy; players killing helpful NPCs for fun is a social contract issue, not a railroading issue.

> [!tip] The Hammer Principle
> "It's a terrible carpenter who throws away his hammer just because he knows it can be used to break skulls as easily as it can be used to pound in nails." Don't abandon powerful GM tools (planned plot twists, narrative setups, dramatic betrayals) out of fear of railroading. Use them confidently, but be ready to adapt if players push back — and handle any conflict at the table, not in the game.
