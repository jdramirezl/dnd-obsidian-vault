# The Road to Elturel: An Object Lesson in Adventure Design (Part 1)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-road-to-elturel-part-1/)
**Date:** July 24, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Context

Angry started a new online D&D campaign after burnout. Chose the Sword Coast / Forgotten Realms with published setting lore to reduce front-loaded prep. Intended as beer-and-pretzels adventure-of-the-week play — but once the pressure dropped, creativity flowed and he ended up writing his own starting adventure anyway.

**Lesson:** The cure for GM burnout is not to stop running games. Don't take a break — make a change. Lowering pressure and giving yourself permission to engage at whatever level you want can restart the creative engine.

---

## Adventure Design Is a Two-Part Process

1. **Anchor the endpoints** — figure out the squishy bits (goal, motivation, backstory, hook, resolution)
2. **Figure out the path between them** — the fiddly bits (scenes, encounters, structure, pacing, decisions)

The squishy bits and fiddly bits feed each other. You bounce between them, revising as you go.

---

## A Spark to Dry Tinder

The hardest part is the first idea. You rarely design in order. You come up with *something*, then build other things from it, discover problems, rework, and iterate until things firm up enough to design scenes and encounters.

Angry's spark: **a stealth escort scene** — the party sneaking an NPC out of a city while guards search for them.

Additional known constraints:
- Start in **Baldur's Gate** (port city, near Candlekeep, has nobility — fits all four PCs' backgrounds)
- Immediately **leave** Baldur's Gate (the campaign is about exploration, not urban intrigue)

**Adventure design starts with figuring out how to get an actual game out of gibberish conditions and half-formed ideas.**

---

## Setting a Goal

The most important part of every adventure is the goal: **what the players are supposed to do to win.**

Given the constraints (smuggle NPC out + leave city + go somewhere), the goal writes itself:

> **Escort the NPC from the city to their destination.**

Goals are usually the simplest element. "Verb the noun" with optional prepositional phrases.

---

## Backstory and Motivation: Asking "Why"

### The NPC: A Runaway Kid

First idea: the NPC is a teenage runaway. Work with it — ideas are too valuable to reject. It's often easier to massage a crappy idea into a good one than to generate a brand-new good idea.

### Building Sympathy and Curiosity

Two hooks that pull players in: **sympathy** and **curiosity**.

- **Sympathy:** An abused teenager. Good-aligned characters (paladin of Helm, swashbuckler of Selune) will jump to help.
- **Curiosity:** Something mysterious and magical about the kid. Appeals to the cleric of Mystra's interests and the warlock's brain-snake patron.

### The Kid's Story (Iterative Development)

1. Kid is a magician's apprentice fleeing an abusive teacher → but why does the teacher want him back?
2. Kid is actually a **wild magic sorcerer** — failed as a wizard's apprentice because he had no learned magical talent, but has innate magic in his blood that's just manifesting
3. The wizard knew from the start — detected the wild magic potential, bought the kid, and waited for it to manifest so he could use the kid's **sorcerer blood for dark rituals**
4. The kid's mother died in childbirth. His father secretly resented him and sold him to the wizard for money.

**Result:** A sympathetic, abused kid with mysterious uncontrollable powers, hunted by agents of his evil master. If the players let this kid die or get captured, they'll feel guilty. That's the goal.

### On Detail and Explanation

- You need enough backstory for the kid to answer player questions and generate sympathy during days of travel
- You do NOT need to explain how wizards detect magical talent or what sorcerer blood is good for — these are classic tropes players will accept
- The players never interact with the wizard directly, so only enough detail to justify why he'd pay mercenaries

---

## Resolution: How Can This End?

Resolutions must be **bright lines** — they must unambiguously resolve the adventure.

### Failure States
- Kid is captured and returned to the wizard (could lead to a rescue adventure — failure ends *this* adventure, not the campaign)
- Kid is killed
- Entire party dies

### Success State
The kid must be **permanently, unambiguously safe**. Dumping him at an orphanage isn't enough — the wizard could send more goons.

**Solution:** The kid has an **uncle in Elturel** — his mother's brother, a paladin/knight who protects the city. The uncle once offered to adopt the kid, but the father refused and sold him to the wizard instead. The kid remembers the uncle saying "if you ever get into trouble, you've always got a place here."

Elturel itself is warded from evil by a golden light, protected by paladins and knights — a definitively safe destination.

> **The adventure ends when the party safely delivers the kid to his uncle in Elturel, or when the kid is captured or killed.**

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Don't reject simple or cliché ideas.** Depth comes from the number of ideas and how they interact, not from complexity.
- **Motivate the players first, characters second.** If the players are sufficiently motivated, they'll find a way to drag their characters along.
- **Backstory and motivation develop together.** You bounce between them, each informing the other.
- **Resolutions must be bright lines.** The adventure must have clear success and failure conditions.
- **The squishy bits (foundation) and fiddly bits (encounters/scenes) feed each other.** Expect to revise the foundation as you design the middle.
