# Tip: Interact with NPCs to Receive Quests

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/tip-interact-with-npcs-to-receive-quests/)
**Date:** August 25, 2016
**Series:** Give Non Players Character (NPCs)

---

## The Problem with Quest Givers

In MMOs and published modules, quest givers are just text boxes with a human mask — click, read exposition, go kill things. Players stop reading the text. Strip away the story buried in text boxes and all you have is "kill X of creature Y and collect component Z."

**If your NPC Quest Giver is just boxed text delivered through a mouthpiece, you're ruining the first scene of the game** — the one that sets the tone and provides drive for the whole adventure.

---

## Quest Givers and Patrons: What They Are

Both serve the same purpose: they set the players on the adventure. Think of every adventure like a dungeon — the Quest Giver is the first room.

A Quest Giver touches **two** of the three essential adventure components:
1. **Motivation** — provides the reason for the adventure
2. **Structure** — is part of the entry-point scene

### Why Use an NPC to Start an Adventure?

Different adventure starts set different tones:
- **Assassination scene** → promises urgent, desperate, action-packed intrigue
- **Treasure map** → promises puzzles and obstacles with a prize at the end
- **NPC Quest Giver** → adds a **human face** to the game, humanizes it, adds dramatic weight

---

## How to Create a Good Quest Giver

### Step 1: Figure Out Who Wants the Goal Accomplished and Why

Farmers want orcs stopped (they'll starve). The king wants a trade deal (invasion threat). The merchant wants stolen goods back. Whoever wants the thing done — that's your Quest Giver.

### Step 2: Figure Out Why the PCs Specifically

Maybe they're great heroes. Maybe there are elf PCs who'd grease diplomatic wheels. Maybe the quest giver doesn't trust his own court. Maybe there's something illegal hidden in the shipment.

### Step 3: Play the Scene, Don't Read Box Text

You understand what the NPC wants and why it's important. Now **be the NPC** and try to convince the PCs and players to play the adventure. This makes the adventure human and makes players feel their interactions matter.

---

## When Motives Align (80% of Adventures)

Most of the time, the NPC's motives and the PCs' motives are close enough that the scene plays smoothly. The GM plays the NPC, players play PCs, quick conversation, heroes go adventuring. Even this simple version is better than box text because it draws players into the world.

---

## When Motives Don't Align (Powerful but Rare)

Sometimes the NPC wants things the PCs don't care about. The opening scene becomes a **reverse encounter** — the GM/NPC has a goal and the PCs' objections are obstacles to overcome.

### Tools for Convincing PCs
- **Bribes** — money, equipment, discounts, contacts
- **Emotional appeals** — "the bandits steal from everyone," "think of the orphaned puppies"
- **Information leverage** — blackmail, secrets
- **Lies** — and the PCs might try to catch them ("You're paying 200 gold to recover 20 gold worth of grain?")

### The Smuggler Example

A merchant is secretly smuggling necromancy components in grain sacks. Bandits steal the shipment. He hires the PCs to retrieve it without involving authorities.

- **If PCs identify the contents:** They can confront him, take a bribe, go to authorities, or destroy the shipment
- **If they don't:** Straightforward adventure. But three adventures later, they find grain sacks with his merchant mark at a necromancy cult hideout — and suddenly realize they were used

Misaligned motives create deeper opening scenes, more resolution possibilities, and future complications.

### Why Use Sparingly

1. **Risk of no adventure** — PCs might walk away if unconvinced
2. **Trust issues** — too many lying/scheming NPCs makes players paranoid about ALL NPCs, breaking the game

---

## What the Quest Giver Knows

The other half of the job: providing an **entry point into the adventure structure**.

- **Volunteer vital information** — don't rely on players to ask the right questions. If they NEED to know where the dungeon is, tell them outright.
- **Point to starting scenes** — directions to the dungeon door, three investigation leads, etc.
- **Hold back extras** — context, background, fun facts can wait until players ask. Rewards them for interacting.

---

## Patrons: Recurring Quest Givers

### Accidental Patrons
A Quest Giver who keeps coming back with more work. Not a formal employer — just someone who returns because the PCs did a good job. Over time, the opening scene streamlines naturally: "Your old friend Bob shows up." "Okay Bob, what's the job?" This abbreviation comes from familiarity, not laziness — it feels natural.

### Employer Patrons
A true employer — the PCs are regularly assigned missions by a specific NPC or organization. Provides structure and purpose to an entire campaign. Often part of the campaign premise from the start.

### Patron Rules

**Screen time = detail required.** The more an NPC appears, the more developed they need to be — personality, goals, hopes, fears, nuance. You can add details over time (like a player learning their PC), but be mostly consistent.

**Trust is fragile.** Patrons build enormous trust over time. If that trust is broken by a sudden betrayal reveal, players may never trust any NPC again. Gradual divergence of motives is fine and creates interesting story arcs. Sudden "I was evil all along" reveals are devastating.

- **Accidental Patrons:** One can turn traitor without breaking the game (usually)
- **Employer Patrons:** Betrayal can permanently damage the campaign

---

## Key Takeaways

- Quest Givers are NOT just exposition vehicles — they're the **first scene** and set the tone
- **Play the NPC**, don't read box text
- Figure out: who wants the goal done, why, why the PCs specifically, what resources they have to convince, what info they'll volunteer
- Misaligned motives create depth but use sparingly — trust issues and risk of no adventure
- **Patrons** (recurring quest givers) streamline adventure starts and deepen the world
- Screen time demands detail; trust demands careful handling
