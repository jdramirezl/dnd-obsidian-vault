# Scenario Design That Makes a Statement

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/scenario-design-that-makes-a-statement/)
**Date:** October 28, 2024
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Overview

This lesson demonstrates how a True Scenario Designer uses foundational game design concepts (Goals, Challenges, Outcomes) to develop a **Design Statement** — the document that spells out the criteria and constraints for a scenario before actual design begins.

---

## Context: What Is the Scenario *For*?

You can't design a scenario without context. Every scenario has a purpose — a *for*. It can be very specific or very general.

### The Example Context

- Mid-level party traveling between two towns
- Last session was a low-key Town Session; next session will also be a Town Session
- Need something adventurous in between to break up the pacing
- A **self-contained, diversionary Side Trek** — a site-based dungeon

> **Sessiononical Thinking:** Angry classifies sessions as pacing tools — Town Sessions, Travel Sessions, Adventure Sessions — and plans adventures in terms of how many sessions they'll fill.

---

## The Design Statement

A complete Design Statement establishes:

1. **Goal**
2. **Starting Point**
3. **Likely Outcomes** (Good, Bad, and Indifferent — *plural*)
4. **Major Element of Challenge**

These don't follow a fixed order or format. Everything is provisional until you step back and say "that's the complete picture."

---

## Goal

The Goal is a **Win Condition** — something the players can actually *do* and *finish*. It's not a motivation.

- **Wrong:** "Explore, loot, and level up" (these are motivations)
- **Right:** "Fully explore every room of an interesting adventure site and plunder every valuable thing"

> **Key Insight:** Restating motivations as firm, finishable goals is the game-changing thing that revolutionizes adventure design.

### Intrinsically and Extrinsically Rewarding Goals

The best Goals lead to adventures that are both:
- **Extrinsically rewarding:** Useful items, valuable loot, character improvement
- **Intrinsically rewarding:** Satisfying curiosity, sense of discovery, intellectual conquest

---

## Starting Point

A Starting Point must:

1. **Reveal** there's a Goal to accomplish
2. **Convey reasons** why that Goal is worth accomplishing
3. **Point the players** in a direction to get started

### The Example

- Party encounters an ambushed merchant cart on the road
- Smoke from a campfire rises over a nearby ridge
- Following the smoke leads to bandits camping in front of the dungeon entrance
- The bandits have **nothing to do with the adventure** — this is important

> Leading players to the site through an incident (rather than just placing it on the road) psychologically makes them more likely to explore it.

---

## Likely Outcomes (Good, Bad, and Indifferent — Plural)

If you've only got one outcome, you've built a railroad. This adventure operates on a **completion percentage** (1%–100%):

1. **Full completion:** Explore every room and plunder every valuable thing
2. **Partial completion:** Explore and loot some portion of the site
3. **Rejection:** Ignore the site and continue the journey

### Important Principles

- **Death is NOT a Lose Condition.** Death is a Game Over Condition. Lose Conditions exist at the scenario design level.
- Only enumerate **likely** outcomes worth planning for
- Examine each outcome — make sure your campaign can continue given any of them

---

## Major Challenge

The Major Challenge must follow **directly** from the Goal and the Likely Outcomes.

- **Mere Adventure Builder:** "Survive all the encounters" or "defeat a boss monster"
- **True Scenario Designer:** The challenge must make it hard to *fully explore the site and loot every treasure* — directly tied to the Goal

### The Solution: Navigation Puzzle (Water Dungeon)

An Ancient Cistern with flooded chambers and waterlogged passages:
- Floodgates, spillways, and drains let players reconfigure the dungeon
- Opening access to some spaces restricts access to others
- Players must reason out the proper configuration for complete access
- Inspired by classic *Legend of Zelda* dungeon design

> This is the fundamental difference between a **Major Challenge** (navigation/configuration puzzle) and **Minor/Incidental Challenges** (individual encounters, traps, etc.).

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Design Statements are assignments, not designs** — criteria and constraints, not the actual design
2. **The Major Challenge must follow directly from the Goal**
3. **Play lots of games** (especially video games and board games) to build a library of design solutions
4. **Any idea can make a great or terrible scenario** — it's all down to the design
5. **Death isn't a Lose Condition** — it's a Game Over Condition
6. **Lose Conditions exist at the scenario design level**, not the system level
