# A Scenario Design Interlude: Why Are We Here?

## Key Advice

Designing great RPG adventures is **game design**, not just storytelling. Approach scenario creation like a board game or video game designer first — get the goals, challenges, and outcomes right — then layer the roleplaying veneer on top. The "roleplaying" part is the easy part; the hard part is making the underlying game feel good to play.

## Summary

This is a mid-course check-in within Angry's **True Scenario Designery** series. He reflects on why he became a GM (he loves *making* games for people to play) and argues that most published adventures fall into only two flavors:

1. **The Maze** — boxes and lines with challenges in the boxes and a prize at the end (dungeon crawls, flowchart adventures).
2. **The "And Then…"** — a series of happenings and responses that lead somewhere, driven by improvisation or simulation.

Both are fine, but neither reaches its full potential without deliberate game design thinking.

He introduces two critical mindsets:
- **The Quest Mindset**: Break everything — campaigns, adventures, scenes, encounters — into discrete chunks, each with a goal, starting point, outcomes, and a major challenge. Like WoW quests, but hidden under organic narrative flow.
- **Don't Sweat the Roleplaying Thing**: Design the game mechanics and structure first (like designing *Pandemic*), then add the RP layer. A well-designed scenario works even if nobody speaks in character.

He previews upcoming lessons on **Winning and Losing** in RPG scenarios, and concepts like **Momentum** and **Inertia**.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Adopt the Quest Mindset** — for every adventure, scene, or encounter, explicitly define: the goal, the starting point, the possible outcomes, and the major challenge.
2. **Design the board game first** — before adding narrative flavor, ask: "Would this be fun as a board game with no roleplaying?" If not, fix the structure.
3. **Break campaigns into discrete chunks** — even if the players experience smooth, continuous play, your design should have clear boundaries between quests/scenarios.
4. **Study non-RPG games** — play or learn *Pandemic* and *Magic: the Gathering* to internalize how good game designers handle goals, challenge, momentum, and losing.
5. **Stop conflating storytelling with game design** — narrative is the veneer; the underlying game structure is what makes an adventure feel satisfying to play.

> [!tip] The Pandemic Test
> Before finalizing any adventure, ask yourself: "If I stripped out all the narrative and roleplaying, would the underlying structure of goals, challenges, and decisions still be a fun game to play?" If the answer is no, your adventure has a game design problem, not a story problem. Fix the game first, then re-dress it with narrative.
