# Traps Suck

## Summary

Traps are iconic but deeply flawed in D&D. The standard trap process — detect, disarm, or suffer — is almost entirely devoid of meaningful player decisions. This article breaks down why traps suck, then offers techniques to make them work: deliberate placement with telegraphed patterns, consequences for searching everywhere, limiting trap types per location, and the CLICK! Rule that gives players a split-second reaction when a trap triggers.

## What Is a Trap?

An automated attack triggered by a target doing a particular thing. Components:
- **Trigger** — when someone does a thing, the trap fires
- **Effect** — what happens when it fires
- **Countermeasures** — how it can be detected, disarmed, or avoided

## Why Traps Suck in D&D

The standard process is broken at every stage:

1. **Detection is passive or random.** Passive Perception either auto-spots traps (boring) or the DC is set too high to matter. Active searching is blind guessing or "search everything" — both non-decisions.
2. **Disarming is a non-decision.** If you have a rogue, you use the rogue. Like using keys to open your front door.
3. **Triggering is devoid of agency.** Trap fires, dice roll, someone gets hurt. No player choices.
4. **RPGs lack execution challenges.** Unlike video games where you physically react, TTRPGs reduce everything to decisions and random outcomes.

## How Dark Souls Does Traps Right

- Pressure plates and arrow holes are **visible if you pay attention** — rewards attentive play
- The **"click" moment** — a brief window to react even if you missed the trap
- No disarming — you either avoid the trigger or mitigate the effect

## Making Traps Not Suck

### Telegraph Your Traps

Always have a **pattern** to trap placement. Reward attentive players with two layers:
- **Layer 1:** A detail warning there *could* be a trap (flavor text level)
- **Layer 2:** A more specific detail players must actively examine to confirm

This makes logical sense — trap builders need a way around their own traps.

**Example:** Statues throughout a dungeon. Traps always between pairs of statues. Trapped statues have swords and shields reversed (left-handed). Players who examine statues learn the pattern.

**Example:** Kobold tomb robbers set traps only in ruined sections where damage conceals mechanisms. First trap is right inside the door — a teaching moment.

### Make Searching Costly

Searching everywhere should be boring and time-consuming:
- **Describe the search process every time** before rolling — two sentences of tedious narration discourages reflexive searching
- **Use wandering monsters** or time pressure
- Make searching a real choice, not a default action

### Limit Trap Types Per Location

One or two types per adventure/location:
- Allows effective telegraphing
- Lets players learn the pattern
- Creates a game: within established rules, find increasingly interesting deployments
- First two traps straightforward; then ramp up difficulty and creativity

**Example:** Combine trap types for dread — gas pockets (flammable + poisonous) and flame-spewing statues. Then the party enters a statue room that smells like gas.

### Situation-Changing Traps

The best traps don't directly attack — they change the situation:
- Collapsing ceiling splits the party
- Floor collapse blocks escape
- Room seals and fills with water

These don't need telegraphing because they create decision points rather than just dealing damage.

### Encounter Traps

Puzzle-room traps (crushing walls, filling rooms) are really encounters. Design them as encounters: dramatic question, sources of conflict, decision points.

## The Mental Trap About Traps

GMs feel players detecting/disarming traps is "cheating." This is wrong.

**A trap is a challenge the party can overcome with 100% efficiency.** Smart play = all XP, no resource cost. Traps are like Daily Jeopardy — double your money or lose, but no one steals from you. You MUST have this mentality.

## The CLICK! Rule

A homebrew mechanic adding player agency when traps trigger:

1. Trap triggers — describe only the trigger sensation ("you feel a click under your foot")
2. Ask the player: **"What do you do?"**
3. Evaluate their blind reaction:
   - **Exceptionally good** (diving prone vs. scything blade) → trap rolls with disadvantage
   - **Normal** (raising shield, rolling sideways) → resolve normally (already in AC/saves)
   - **Exceptionally bad** (going prone vs. collapsing floor) → disadvantage on the save

Combined with telegraphing, players make informed guesses — drawing conclusions and acting on them.

## Design Principles

1. **Be deliberate** — every trap planned like any other encounter
2. **Telegraph with patterns** — reward attentive players
3. **Make searching costly** — prevent "search everything" as default
4. **Limit trap types** — one or two per location, escalate creatively
5. **Don't add traps to combat** until players have dealt with them outside combat first
6. **Use the CLICK! Rule** — give players a reaction moment
7. **Accept 100% efficiency** — traps are challenges players can ace
