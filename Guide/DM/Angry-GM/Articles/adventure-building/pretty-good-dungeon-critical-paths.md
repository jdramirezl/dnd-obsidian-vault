# Let's Build a Pretty Good Dungeon: Critical Paths

## Summary

This article distinguishes between two commonly conflated concepts: the **critical path** and the **golden path**. The critical path is the minimum necessary route through an adventure — every required element the players must encounter to win. The golden path is the optimized route that provides the best possible gameplay experience, which necessarily includes the critical path but also weaves in non-required elements that enhance pacing, narrative, and challenge. Angry also introduces a framework for categorizing all adventure content as Required, Expected, or Optional, and explains why more optional content isn't automatically better.

## Key Advice

### Critical Path vs. Golden Path

- **Critical Path** — The shortest route through all mandatory elements. If there's a gate and a lever, the critical path is: Entrance → Lever → Gate → Treasure. It's the minimum to win.
- **Golden Path** — The route that feels best to play. It includes the critical path but adds elements that improve the experience. Example: Entrance → Puzzle (see the lock) → Poem (find the key) → Puzzle (solve it) → Treasure. Backtracking and sidetracking enhance agency and satisfaction.

### The Three Content Buckets

Every element in your adventure falls into one of three categories:

| Category | Definition | Path Placement | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Required** | Players must encounter this to win | On the critical path | Gate the goal behind it; make it impossible to skip |
| **Expected** | Not required, but skipping it significantly diminishes the experience | On the golden path | Build challenge assuming players have this; skipping makes things frustratingly hard |
| **Optional** | Skipping doesn't significantly diminish the experience | Off the golden path | Build challenge assuming players DON'T have this; finding it makes things easier |

### The Icebrand Sword Example

The same item can be Required, Expected, or Optional depending on your design choice:

- **Required**: Dragon is impossible without the sword → sword is on the critical path
- **Expected**: Dragon is properly challenging WITH the sword, frustratingly hard without → sword is on the golden path
- **Optional**: Dragon is properly challenging WITHOUT the sword, easier with it → sword is off-path bonus

### Optional Content: More Isn't Better

Optional enhancements (lore, bonus treasure, alternative experiences) appeal to specific player types and cost table time and resources to acquire. Groups that chase too much optional content tip past the peak experience. The *presence* of optional content matters more than its actual effect — it creates the feeling of a rich, explorable world even if players skip most of it.

### Branching and Bottlenecking

- Golden paths can fork (assault vs. infiltration) — design each branch to provide an equally good experience.
- Players may complete tasks in any order — design each chunk around its own golden path.
- **Bottlenecking** squeezes divergent paths back through a single event/encounter, making multiple golden paths more manageable to design.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Map your critical path first** — List every element the players MUST encounter to win. Draw the shortest route through them.
2. **Classify every adventure element** — Go through every room, encounter, treasure, and piece of information. Label each as Required, Expected, or Optional.
3. **Design the golden path** — Starting from the critical path, weave in Expected elements in the order that produces the best pacing, narrative, and challenge feel.
4. **Build challenge levels around the golden path** — Assume players following the golden path have the Expected elements. Don't assume they have Optional ones.
5. **Use bottlenecks** — When paths branch, plan convergence points where they merge back together. This keeps design manageable.
6. **Audit your optional content** — Ask: does this enhance the experience for most players, or just some? Does acquiring it cost more table time than the enhancement is worth?

> [!tip] Lock Before Key
> The "show the lock before the key" principle is your most powerful sequencing tool. When players see a puzzle, obstacle, or locked door *before* finding the solution, their brains are primed to recognize the solution when they find it. This makes challenges feel fair and solvable. Reverse the order (key before lock) to increase difficulty. You control non-mechanical difficulty through sequencing alone.
