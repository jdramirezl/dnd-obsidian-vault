# How to Skin a Dungeon in Four Easy Steps

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-skin-a-dungeon/)
**Date:** September 16, 2020
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Recap

Part of the [Double-Impossible Adventure Building Checklist](https://theangrygm.com/angrys-impossible-adventure-checklist/) series. Building the dungeon-crawling part of a three-part adventure. Heroes tracked kobold raiders to an abandoned mine to recover a stolen item from a dragonborn. If the heroes raise a ruckus, the dragonborn flees.

## A Dungeon With a Twist

A self-contained environment the heroes can explore at their own pace—but they can't win if they treat it like a normal dungeon. They must move carefully and quietly:

- **First alarm:** encounters get harder, monsters change behavior
- **Second alarm:** the dragonborn starts heading for the exit
- If heroes don't intercept him, they lose him forever

## Two Scales of Every Dungeon

- **Macro-scale (layout):** how spaces join up, where everything is relative to everything else
- **Micro-scale (encounter):** precise room layouts, terrain features, monster locations, furniture, grid

You don't have to design both together. You can use a flowchart for layout and separate encounter maps for rooms with fights.

## Three Ways to Design a Dungeon

### 1. Haphazard Encounter Arrangement
Design encounter areas first, then fit them together. Rooms scattered willy nilly, long hallways. Gamiest approach. Example: Fane of the Night Serpent (*Tomb of Annihilation*).

### 2. Dungeon That Looks Like What It's Supposed to Look Like
Maps that make logical sense as real structures. Example: Haunted House from *Saltmarsh*. Problem: encounter spaces tend to be bland, cluttered, or too small for their battles.

### 3. Gameboard Dungeon
Gamey on the layout scale. The structure itself provides part of the challenge—like a Metroidvania dungeon or a cat-and-mouse chase. Hardest to design and most likely to break during gameplay.

**Key insight:** The best designs START with one approach but incorporate elements of all three. Every dungeon has to end up looking like SOMETHING.

## Laying Out a Gameboard Dungeon

### Pre-Thought: The Game Design

For a cat-and-mouse chase:
- **Single entrance/exit** — if the boss has a secret exit, players can't head him off
- **Circuitous route for the boss** — something must slow him down so players can react
- **Multiple paths** — enough for a chase, not so many the cat runs randomly
- **Balance inertia and catch-up** (skewed toward players):
  - Alarm raised close to boss → victory should be a sure thing
  - Alarm raised at the door → still winnable, just harder
  - In between → victory feels like it's slipping but still in grasp
- **Momentum** — beginning and end move faster than the middle; paths converge near the exit

### Step 1: Sketch a Layout

Circular maze-type pattern with entrance in the middle. Three rings deep, two paths between each ring. Dragonborn on the outside with an extra arc for two escape directions. Bridging paths between outer and next ring placed far apart.

### Step 2: Place Encounters and Empty Rooms

Place encounters so heroes hit a minimum of three on the most efficient route (two minor, one major). Each extra encounter increases odds of a screwup.

Place **empty rooms** for non-encounter content, environmental storytelling, treasure, or tension-building. At least as many empty rooms as minor encounters.

### Step 3: Re-Sketch to Look Like Something

The initial layout always looks artificial. The circular shape isn't the important part—what matters is how paths connect encounters and how they get longer farther from the exit. Resketch it any way you want as long as connections and distances stay the same.

For this mine: natural caves with some straighter mine tunnels. Winding tunnels also prevent shooting fleeing kobolds in the back.

### Step 4: Draw a Beautiful Map

Draw the final map over your sketch. Widen lines into halls/tunnels, draw rooms with proper shapes. Adjust as needed to make the dungeon look like what it's supposed to be.

**Result:** If you hadn't seen the concentric circle sketch, you'd never know it was there. The most efficient route is hidden from players.

## Scaling Down

Options for transitioning from layout to encounter scale:
1. **Freehand** — "close enough is good enough"
2. **Graph paper from the start** — keep a close eye on scale
3. **Scan and add a grid** — slap a grid over the scanned map
4. **Computer from the start** — easy to resize and add scale
5. **Scan and trace in mapping software** — e.g., ProFantasy's Campaign Cartographer 3+ with Dungeon Designer

Know your tools and what you can do with them. Find tools, learn them, get good with them.
