# Order Matters: Guided Nonlinearity and Closed Openness

## Key Advice

The **order** in which players encounter things in an adventure drastically changes the gameplay experience — from challenge balance to narrative impact to puzzle solvability. Open-ended design is essential to RPGs, but uncontrolled freedom lets players stumble into things in the wrong order and ruin their own experience. The solution is **Guided Nonlinearity** and **Slightly-Closed Open Worldness**: design your adventures so the **path of least resistance leads to the best gameplay experience**.

## Summary

Angry argues that sequence matters enormously in RPGs and introduces two video game design concepts that GMs should adopt.

**Order changes everything:**
- **Challenge balance** — Fighting a rock troll solo first (Moderate), then with orcs (Hard) is a fair escalation. Reverse the order and the first fight becomes a screwjob because players can't anticipate the troll's unusual attacks.
- **Puzzle solvability** — Human brains are wired so that finding the key before the lock makes puzzles dramatically harder. *Functional fixedness* and the *Zeigarnik effect* mean players literally won't think to check their inventory for the solution if they found it before encountering the problem. "Show the lock first, then give them the key" is a 30-year-old game design rule that works because of how brains are wired.
- **Narrative impact** — The same events in different order tell completely different stories. An asshole hit by a meteor before his redemption is karma; a redeemed man hit by a meteor is tragedy.
- **Fairness** — A monster that disintegrates weapons is a fair, interesting challenge *if* players found the warning journal first. Without that information, it's a punishing screwjob.

**The Paradox of Open-Ended Gaming:**
- RPGs promise freedom and meaningful player choices. But freedom means players can encounter things in the wrong order.
- You can't fix this with quantum ogres (secretly rearranging content regardless of player choice) — that destroys real agency.
- The answer is compromise: design so that freedom and good ordering coexist.

**Guided Nonlinearity (from Metroidvania games):**
- Build open-ended spaces with invisible, careful design decisions that make most players naturally find locks before keys and face easy challenges before hard ones.
- Players have real freedom to explore, but the design subtly keeps them on track. When they go off track, they're never so far off as to ruin the experience.
- Works best for constrained adventures: dungeon maps, mystery flowcharts, adventure paths.

**Slightly-Closed Open Worldness (from open-world games like Breath of the Wild):**
- In truly open worlds, design the environment so the most attractive choices lead to the best gameplay experience.
- Breath of the Wild lets you go anywhere from the start, but Nintendo's data showed nearly every player naturally went northeast first, completing the ideal introductory sequence — not because they were forced, but because the design made it the most appealing path.
- Fallout: New Vegas uses the same principle.

**The Core Principle:**
> "The path of least resistance should always lead to the best gameplay experience."

Most players take the path of least resistance most of the time. Design so that path is also the best-ordered, most satisfying experience. Players who deliberately go off the beaten path get a harder but still viable experience.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Design with order in mind** — when placing encounters, information, and treasures, think about what sequence produces the best experience.
2. **Show the lock before the key** — present problems before solutions. Players who find the *potion of flame avoidance* before the Flamewracked Hall will forget they have it.
3. **Introduce monsters in isolation before combining them** — let players learn a creature's unusual abilities in a manageable fight before throwing it into a complex encounter.
4. **Provide critical information before the encounter that needs it** — warnings, journals, NPC hints should be on the natural path *before* the challenge they inform.
5. **Make the best path the easiest path** — use environmental cues, NPC guidance, and map design to make the ideal sequence feel like the natural choice.
6. **Don't use quantum ogres** — real agency matters. Guide players through design, not by secretly rearranging content behind the scenes.
7. **Gate optional/hard content behind less obvious paths** — the main path should be well-ordered; side paths can be harder and less guided as a reward for exploration.
8. **Study video game level design** — Metroidvania games and open-world games like Breath of the Wild and Fallout: New Vegas are masterclasses in these principles.

> [!tip] The Path of Least Resistance
> "The path of least resistance should always lead to the best gameplay experience." Don't fight player tendencies — design around them. Most players will naturally take the easiest, most obvious route. Make that route the one that produces the best-ordered, most satisfying adventure.
