# Momentous and Inertial Adventure Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/momentous-and-inertial-adventure-design/)
**Date:** May 19, 2025
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Core Thesis

Great adventures create a **tug-of-war** between the players trying to win and the game resisting them. This is achieved through two gameplay dynamics: **momentum** (successes compound, making future success more likely) and **inertia** (the game fights back harder the longer the players struggle). Pre-designing these dynamics — before mapping or statting anything — is what separates True Scenario Design from Mere Adventure Building.

## Key Concepts

### Pre-Design

Before you build an adventure (map it, stat it, write encounters), you **pre-design** it:
- Lay out parameters, criteria, and constraints
- Outline the scenario structure
- Break it into scenes, acts, chapters
- Figure out what bits and pieces you'll need

Every adventure goes through pre-production now. No more wiping your ass across graph paper and calling it an adventure.

### The X-Factor

The **x-factor** is the one broad thing the players will mostly be doing in the adventure. Identify it early in pre-design. Keep your goals, major challenges, outcomes, momentum, and inertia dynamics all aligned with it.

Examples of x-factors: exploring, investigating, interacting, acquiring, overcoming, killing.

*End the Goblins* is a **Kill Something** (Extermination) adventure. The players win by killing enough goblins.

### Nothing Is True Until the Players Know It

**There is no actual goblin population.** The number does not exist. The players will never know how many goblins lived in the cave. As long as the cave looks like it could support at least as many goblins as the players kill, they'll never question it.

There are as many goblins as you need to build the challenge you're building. Goblins are tokens in a board game's supply — there's always one more if you need one.

## The Example: *End the Goblins*

### Premise
A tribe of goblins in a waterfall cave near a village. Too many to kill in one go. The party must make several sorties over multiple adventuring days. Win condition: build up enough of a body count to demoralize the survivors into fleeing.

### Why the "Mere Adventure Builder" Approach Fails

**Static dungeon approach:** Map 15–18 encounters, clear them one by one. Adequate but boring — just emptying rooms.

**Population tracking approach:** Roster of goblins, repopulate after each sortie, track kills. More dynamic but:
- Players just fight the same basic encounters over and over
- No real sense of progress except counting empty rooms
- No push-and-pull — encounters are roughly the same difficulty each time
- Lots of bookkeeping between sessions

Neither approach creates the feeling of fighting an overwhelming infestation that's simultaneously getting fiercer AND being worn down.

### Scenario Structure: Three Paths

Map the dungeon as **three critical paths**:
- Each starts at/near the entrance
- Each comprises 4–5 encounters
- Each culminates in a major encounter (boss/miniboss)
- Add side rooms and cuts between paths to hide the structure and add exploration

Players can clear one path per adventuring day. Three well-played days = victory.

### Building Inertia (The Game Fights Back)

**Every time the players retreat to rest, the dungeon gets harder on their next visit.** Pre-design five different encounter populations of increasing difficulty:

| Visit | Difficulty Mix | Feel |
|-------|---------------|------|
| 1st | Mostly easy encounters, random-seeming groups | Goblins living their lives, caught off guard |
| 2nd | Easy + average, deliberate defense | Guard posts, chokepoints, patrols |
| 3rd | Normal mix (easy/average/hard) | Standard well-designed goblin dungeon |
| 4th | Average + hard only | Nothing's easy anymore |
| 5th+ | Even mix average/hard + deadly encounters | Desperate, dug-in, vicious |

**The dungeon is always fully stocked.** No depletion. To the players, it looks like the goblins have a huge population and keep moving up reinforcements. This is what goblins *feel like* — an overwhelming infestation.

This is NOT the GM repopulating between sessions. This is the designer creating five pre-built encounter sets as part of the adventure design.

### Building Momentum (Successes Compound)

Three critical path bosses. Killing each **permanently removes an element from ALL future encounters**:

1. **Goblin Kennelmaster** — abusive and cruel. When killed, wolves go berserk. Goblins put some down, rest flee. → **Cross off all wolves from all encounters forever.**

2. **Goblin Warchief** — when killed, power vacuum and infighting. → **Cross off the most powerful goblin from every encounter forever.**

3. **Goblin Shaman** — provides divine protection. Every goblin stat block includes a trait reflecting their god's protection. → **Cross off that trait from all goblins forever.**

### The Push-and-Pull

Momentum and inertia are **separate systems** that work against each other:
- Inertia: encounters get harder each visit (escalating difficulty tiers)
- Momentum: encounters get easier as bosses die (elements permanently removed)
- The **net result** determines whether players are winning or losing
- Players can see both dynamics happening simultaneously

### Optional Extras

Side rooms (the red bubbles on the map) can contain optional advantages:
- Poison mushroom room near the bloodwine cellar → drug the goblins' supply, debuff them for one visit
- A prisoner who provides useful information or joins the party
- A secret side entrance to bypass early rooms on future visits

### Outcomes / Failure State

While players assault the lair, goblin raiding parties hit the village. Each day the adventure drags on, villagers suffer more. After five days of increasingly vicious raids, the villagers abandon the village. Adventure lost.

## Key Principles

- **Build inertia and momentum as separate systems.** They should be visible to players and pull against each other.
- **Tie dynamics to the x-factor.** This is a Kill Something adventure, so killing drives momentum.
- **Minimize GM bookkeeping.** Track only "how many times have players attacked" and "cross off thing." Five pre-built encounter lists on five pages.
- **Don't sweat balance on the momentum side.** Removing 1–2 creatures from encounters of 4–6 won't ruin anything, especially if players earned it.
- **The undefined population principle.** Don't simulate a world. Build a game. There are as many goblins as the game needs.
- **Design begets design.** Good solutions generate more ideas. The hardest part becomes trimming down to keep things simple.
- **Gamefeel is everything.** None of this matters if the players don't notice or feel it. (Managing gamefeel is the next lesson's topic.)
