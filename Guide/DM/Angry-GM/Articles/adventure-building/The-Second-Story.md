# The Second Story

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-second-story/)
**Date:** September 29, 2024
**Series:** Random Bullshit / Worldbuilding

---

## The Core Problem

GMs constantly ask: "How do I create a living, breathing world?" and "Why do my players act like murderhobos?"

**Answer:** Players act with a conscience when they care emotionally about the world and its inhabitants. A *living, breathing world* is one the players care about — they say "I love," "I hate," "I'm afraid for" the world and its people. They think about how their characters' actions affect things and generally want a positive impact.

---

## Why It's Hard to Teach

Creating a living, breathing world is:
- **Holistic and emergent** — not down to any single action, behavior, checklist, or process
- **In everything you do** — narration, presentation, adventure design, how events play out, how things build on other things
- **Not primarily in worldbuilding** — Angry's worlds are pedestrian, generic heroic fantasy. He's lazy about front-end worldbuilding. Details pop out during play.

## Take Notes (Even If You Think You Don't Need To)

### The Rhesa Example

A PC named Rhesa — a peasant girl who stole her dead uncle's adventuring sword and ran away from her fishing village to chase adventure. During a throwaway roleplaying scene, she told a minstrel too much about her backstory (but held back key details).

A real-life year later, Angry found detailed notes about exactly what Rhesa told the minstrel. He used them: a *different* minstrel performed "The Hero of Redshore" — a wildly embellished song based on the gossip chain. The song revealed details Rhesa's companions didn't know, filled gaps with creative license, and caused Rhesa to panic and flee the inn. The intraparty fallout became the session's centerpiece.

**None of that would have happened without notes.**

### Lessons from the Example

- **Track player choices so you can visit consequences on them later** — including minor, butterfly-effect actions
- **Track actions, not planned consequences** — write down what happened, not how you'll use it. The use will emerge naturally.
- **The world keeps turning off-camera** — minstrels wander, merchants gossip, rumors travel. Don't over-systematize how fast news spreads; just note what could travel and use it when it makes sense.

## The Second Story (The Main Technique)

There is **always** at least one background plot running in Angry's campaigns — separate from whatever the PCs are involved in. This is **The Second Story**.

### What It Is

An overarching background story that affects the world but isn't something the PCs can directly impact or resolve. It just *is*. It affects their adventures indirectly — sometimes distracting them, sometimes just coloring the world around them.

### Example: Chain of Stars Campaign

Two Second Stories running simultaneously:

1. **Bad weather / drought** — a hot dry summer, poor harvest, long cold winter, dry spring. No one's starving yet, but everyone's anxious and watching their food stores.
2. **The king's armies are away at war** — several lords marched west against orcish warbands. Roads are under-patrolled, inns are sparse, most people around are young, old, disabled, or women doing extra work.

One of these is tied to the campaign's main plot. The other is just a thing that's happening. The players think it's all connected — they're half right.

### Why It Works: Two Key Benefits

**1. Demonstrates the world doesn't revolve around the PCs.**
While they're killing demons and solving puzzles, the people in town have their own problems.

**2. Simplifies NPC improvisation enormously.**
Instead of tracking hundreds of individual NPC stories, you track **one story**. Every time an NPC appears on camera, ask: *"How is The Second Story affecting this person?"*

- Innkeepers apologize about poor fare
- Patrons complain about no meat in the stew
- Markets are sparse, so the merchant is clingy and desperate
- Armor repair takes extra time because there's only one elderly smith and a green apprentice

### The NPC Decision Tree

When encountering any NPC, consider three things in descending priority:

1. **Do I already know something about this NPC?** (pre-written or recurring character with notes)
2. **Are they connected to or affected by the PCs' adventures?** (usually no for random NPCs)
3. **How are they affected by The Second Story?** (the fallback — always available)

## Additional Insights

- **Tone is a choice.** Angry leaned optimistic — most people reach out rather than turning selfish. About one-third of NPCs are dickheads. Many GMs over-populate their worlds with selfish, corrupt people.
- **Scale varies.** Sometimes one Second Story for the whole campaign, sometimes one per city, sometimes two or three simultaneously. Always at least one.
- **Lucky bonus:** Sometimes The Second Story naturally drives a PC backstory without being forced.

---

## Key Takeaways

- A living, breathing world is one players **care emotionally about**
- **Take notes** — especially about minor player actions and throwaway scenes
- Track **actions**, not planned consequences
- Always have a **Second Story** — a background plot that colors the world independently of the PCs
- Use The Second Story as a **cheat code for NPC improvisation**: when you have nothing else for an NPC, show how the background situation affects them
- Most people in the world should be trying to be good people
