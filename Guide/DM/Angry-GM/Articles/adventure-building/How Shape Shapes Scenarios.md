# How Shape Shapes Scenarios

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-shape-shapes-scenarios/)
**Date:** September 9, 2025
**Series:** [True Scenario Designery](https://theangrygm.com/true-scenario-designery/)

---

*This feature is part of the ongoing course in advanced adventure, encounter, and campaign design, True Scenario Designery. This lesson continues a section about scenario structure.*

## The Shape of Adventure

*Shape* is one of the essential components of gameplay structure. When you have to design a scenario, you usually start with an outline — a list of the ideas you're going to present in the order you're going to present them. It's basically just a map of the thing you're going to build.

Outlines show how your writing flows from one idea to the next and how those ideas relate to each other hierarchically. But outlining is actually a pretty crappy way to design most scenarios. Outlines are great when you move from one thing to the next in a linear fashion, but that's not always how roleplaying game scenarios work.

There are indeed **linear** roleplaying game scenarios — and it's not only perfectly okay to build and run linear scenarios but it's sometimes the best way to run a scenario. Sometimes, the players are just walking the road between the town and the dungeon and having road encounters on the way.

But there are lots of times when the players get to make choices. There are also times when the players' previous actions determine how they can move through the scenario (locked doors, unknown locations, conditional paths). And there are times when one scenario's outcome determines whether the players can travel to another (e.g., the giant ambush that leads to different climaxes depending on the outcome).

### What Scenario Shape Actually Is

How the action flows from element to element — that is what scenario shape is all about and why it matters. Different patterns of connection yield different kinds of experiences. The way you connect up the elements of a scenario affects the gameplay experience.

A node map is a dungeon map is a flowchart is a timeline. The difference is strictly in what gameplay the blocks represent. Sometimes they're encounters, sometimes they're rooms, sometimes they're events, sometimes they're objectives in a heist, sometimes they're characters in an intrigue plot. And sometimes the lines are hallways and sometimes they're clues and leads and sometimes they're favors owed and sometimes they're logical conditionals.

**That shit doesn't matter.** To see what matters, we have to zoom out. Consider how different shapes lead to different experiences and different macrochallenges.

## The Six Shapes of Adventure

These are six patterns that most scenarios tend to fit. More or less. But this ain't a definitive list and these aren't checklists or recipes. You absolutely should not treat them as unbreakable rules. Bright lines and hard categories and firm definitions limit your design.

### 1. Linear

Strings of elements in sequence. Maybe encounters on a road or events in a timeline as the heroes stay in a haunted inn for the night. The action starts at one end and progresses through to a conclusion. The outcomes might depend on how the players handle the events or when they exit the linear sequence.

### 2. Branching

Each element has a few different links and each leads to new elements so the gameplay spreads over time. The outcome usually depends on which exit the action gets to. The *giant ambush* scenario is a great example — the initial encounter has two outcomes (*rout the giant* or *fail to stop the giant's advance*) and each leads to a different climax. Branching scenarios are best kept small because they require building a lot of content that'll never see the light of play and the number of outcomes can balloon uncontrollably.

### 3. Pyramid

Branching scenarios in a mirror. They start with lots of possibilities and converge to a small number of possibilities. Mysteries and intrigues tend to follow pyramid structures — lots of initial leads or interactions that gradually work through to a couple of possible final climaxes or outcomes.

**Note:** Pyramid and branching scenarios fit very well together when stacked in sequence — the action first diverges before it converges to a climax. This *diverge-and-converge* pattern could be called a shape of its own.

### 4. Labyrinth

Classic dungeon shapes. All the elements are linked and interlinked and the action meanders around trying to find one of the outcomes. This also covers node-map style adventures and some kinds of mysteries and intrigues.

### 5. Hub-and-Spoke

A central scenario element connects to a bunch of other elements and the action tends to move out into the *spokes*, then back to the *hub*, and then into different *spokes* until an outcome is reached. Example: a ruined abbey with three or four different buildings opening onto a central courtyard. Or a *gain the respect of the town* adventure where the players run around meeting different townsfolk with favors and mini-adventures.

A hub-and-spoke scenario is really just a labyrinth with a central location. The repeated returns to a central location make it *feel* like a hub-and-spoke setup instead of a labyrinth — which shows how *shape* isn't the be-all and end-all of structure and why we still have to talk about *flow* and *progression*.

### 6. Open

The elements are kind of *free floating*. They're like little islands in a sea and the action can sail from island to island freely. Traditional hexcrawls are basically just open scenarios. Most roleplaying game towns are open scenarios — just a bunch of locations the players can freely access, floating like chunks of beef in a stew.

Hub-and-spoke scenarios are just open scenarios with the hub replaced by a puffy cloud around the spokes. An open scenario could be easily represented as a labyrinth with lines connecting all the elements to each other. This is more vague *gamefeel* bullshit.

## If Every Element is a Scenario…

Every different level of scenario has an outline and every outline has a shape and each of the little boxes on those outlines is itself a scenario with its own outline and shape.

Adventures can be linear sequences of scenes or open-ended scene soups. The arcs in a campaign can be a labyrinthine maze of adventures or just a straightforward sequence or an open sea of adventure. Every little box on each of those outlines can be expanded into an outline too.

### Example: The Ruined Abbey

Consider a ruined abbey adventure with five scenes:
- **The Forest Road** (linear lead-in to the hub)
- **The Courtyard** (hub, open structure with free-floating encounters)
- **The Dorms** (labyrinth spoke)
- **The Main Temple** (labyrinth spoke)
- **The Crypts** (labyrinth spoke)
- **The Cloister** (shortcut between The Dorms and The Main Temple, miniature hub-and-spoke)

The adventure has a hub-and-spoke shape even though it isn't perfect — there's a linear lead-in and a shortcut that breaks the shape a little. That's that *gamefeel* — the shapes don't need to be perfect.

### Key Takeaways

1. **You can outline the structure of any level in the scenario design hierarchy and give it a shape of its own.** Adventures can be linear sequences of scenes or open-ended scene soups. Arcs can be labyrinthine mazes of adventures or straightforward sequences.

2. **Every little box on each outline can be expanded into an outline too.** The scenes in your adventures can be hubs-with-spokes or linear gauntlets and the encounters themselves might be branching or pyramid encounters.

3. **Any diagram is just an arbitrarily chosen zoom level.** In the end, a roleplaying game is just a series of encounters played one after the other. You could draw a single giant diagram of all the encounters in an entire campaign — but you shouldn't.

This stuff gives you a way to think about — and plan out — how your design choices affect the gameplay experience. It also gives us a way to communicate about design. For example:

> When I designed my *Chain of Stars*, I wanted to emulate the top-level scenario structure of *Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past* and *Dark Souls*, but in three acts rather than five. The first would present a linear gauntlet of adventures that establishes the campaign's major threat. The second — and longest — arc would be an open arc wherein the players would gather resources from sites around the kingdom. The third and final arc would be a linear run-up to the final confrontation. Most of the adventures would feature a labyrinth as their setpiece scenes because I mostly wanted to focus on dungeon crawling.

## Warning: Incoming Recontextualization

There's a lot of complexity hidden beneath these deceptively simple ideas. Structure is more than shapes within shapes. There are still the broad concepts of **flow** and **progression** to contend with. Where you put the boxes on the map is only part of the design. You also have to decide what goes in which box and top-down design means understanding that every decision affects every other.

The fine details also matter — which branches lead to what, how many branches you have, what connects to what in your labyrinth. That's where we start discussing specific shapes and techniques like building loops, gating, planning critical paths, and that kind of crap.
