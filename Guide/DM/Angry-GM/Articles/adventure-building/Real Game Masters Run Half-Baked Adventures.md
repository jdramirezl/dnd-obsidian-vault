# Real Game Masters Run Half-Baked Adventures

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/real-game-masters-run-half-baked-adventures/)
**Date:** July 22, 2025
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

Real talk about being a real Game Master who talks a big game about True Scenario Designery and True Game Mastery but constantly admits he doesn't do half the crap he expects you to do. This is about the real content creator who told you that anything good enough is actually pretty great — but also spent the last year saying great design is hard work and demands skill and craft and hours.

---

## I Suck At This and So Do You

Three reasons GMs don't put enough work into their games:

1. **We're human.** We don't set aside enough time. When we do, we waste it.
2. **Design and prep are mostly tedious.** One part engaging fun to four parts tedious crap. The dreaming and designing is great. The writing and drawing and mathing and typing sucks.
3. **We're human again (differently).** Ridiculously high standards. If something's worth doing, it's worth doing perfect. "No one is perfect, but I should be."

The result: showing up with quarter-baked crap, filling in blanks at the table, improvising out of corners, making up encounters and maps on the fly, even fudging entire encounters with no stats.

Unlike most Internet GMs, Angry admits that some of that is bad for the game. He doesn't do it because it's good — he does it because he's human and the game must go on. But some of it is actually totally fine.

---

## Keeping It Real and Being True

Nothing in True Scenario Designery is about unachievable ideals. It's totally achievable and it's the proper way to design truly great games. The problem is that the same high standards that make us think we're never doing enough also keep us from recognizing how much we actually do and how great it actually is.

---

## Top Hat Down Scenario Designing Game Mastery

**Top-down design** means coming up with the big-picture gameplay experience first, then building the pieces that get you there. You make every decision based on how it fits your overall design vision.

When you take ten minutes to hastily sketch a dungeon map and make notes about challenges, as long as you're making your sketch intentionally and placing challenges with an eye toward purpose and progression, you're doing top-down design. That's called **blockout** — the simple, flowchart-like map of your scenario.

If your adventure involves a progression of goblin fights culminating in a boss fight, the actual makeups of individual fights don't matter except insofar as they feed the progression. It doesn't matter whether there are three goblins or four. From a CR perspective, it's easy to eyeball a fight to hit roughly the difficulty you want.

The point: you're not asking the Game Master to make design decisions — you're asking him to bring the Scenario Designer's decisions to life. The specifics of a mook fight aren't consequential and the consequential decisions weren't left unmade.

## Distinguishing Design and Prep... and Polish

### Design
Decision making, planning, and problem solving. You mostly do it in your head. Writing it down isn't designing — it's recording design. That poop where your brain suddenly unclogged and you solved a problem? That was technically design.

### Prep
Putting together the resources the Game Master needs to make the Scenario Design happen at the table. The form those resources take is entirely between the Scenario Designer (you) and the Game Master (also you).

What to write down: **whatever you absolutely have to remember and anything you can't come up with at runtime.** Trust Game Master you to do flavor text at the table. Don't write flavor text, skill check DCs, or detailed NPC notes unless you need to. But DO keep a list of important facts the players must learn (the Information Pool).

There's nothing wrong with relying on telepathy between Game Master you and Scenario Designer you. But don't over-rely on memory for anything that would ruin the adventure if forgotten.

### Polish
Beautifying your prep. Only two good reasons:
1. **Enhance the play experience** with visual aids, props, beautiful maps (but don't mapsturbate during prep time)
2. **You're designing for someone else's Game Master** — the more people will see it and the more profit it'll bring, the more polish it needs

Your homebrewed adventure notes don't need to look like a published module.

## It Ain't a Crime to Merely Build an Adventure

True Scenario Design takes a lot of time and care. You probably can't make every adventure a work of art. Like producing a TV season: produce a couple high-budget episodes for the premiere, finale, and sweeps, then do workhorse episodes, and throw in some bottle episodes.

That's Campaign Management and Angry's Hat Theory at work. Mix in adventures you know are good enough because good enough is better than you think, and that buys time for the next big plot point.

There is nothing wrong with Mere Adventure Building. But if you want to run your best possible games, it's worth spending as much energy as you practically, reasonably, humanly can on True Scenario Design. That's how you get good at it. Once you're good at it, even when you think you're Merely Building Adventures, you're actually Truly Designing Scenarios because you can't shut off what you know.

**Your half-baked adventures are probably way more cooked than you think they are.**
