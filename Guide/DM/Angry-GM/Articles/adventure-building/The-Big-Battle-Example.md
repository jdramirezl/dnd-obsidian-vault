# Angry Story Time: The Big Battle Example

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/big-battle-example/) | **Category**: Adventure Building

---

## Key Advice

- **Players declare intentions; the GM determines and describes outcomes.** Players don't narrate their own actions — they say what they want to do, dice are rolled, and the GM weaves the result into the narrative.
- **Resolve all necessary die rolls behind the screen, then describe everything in one cinematic pass.** Don't reveal mechanical reasons until the narrative moment is right. The Strength saving throw example shows this: everyone rolls saves, Angry resolves Cabe's attack, then describes the entire sequence as one flowing event.
- **Use Initiative as a tool to resolve simultaneous actions, not as a rigid turn structure.** Slip into mechanical initiative when chaos demands it, not before. The combat starts from a social scene and transitions seamlessly.
- **Summarize all declared actions into a single narrative beat before inviting the next player to act.** Instead of narrating each turn as an isolated event, wrap up what happened across multiple characters so players have a clear picture of the battlefield before deciding.
- **Interrupt players who discuss tactics out of character.** "Are you all letting your guard down to discuss your tactics?" maintains pace and immersion. Characters in combat don't have time for committee meetings.

## Summary

This article is an extended, narrative-prose example of a full combat encounter as run by Angry at his table. It's part of the True Game Mastery series and demonstrates pacing, flow, narration, and player management in practice rather than theory. The combat involves a party of four (fighter, wizard, rogue, cleric) at roughly 3rd level encountering a warlock, his bugbear bodyguard, a goblin shaman, and several goblin skirmishers in a cave.

The encounter begins as a social scene — the warlock is stalling the party while hidden goblins prepare an ambush. When the rogue (Cabe) detects the deception, he throws a knife, triggering a chaotic transition into combat. Angry demonstrates how to handle this: he pulls the rogue aside privately, resolves the rogue's sneak attack and the shaman's preemptive spell simultaneously using initiative rolls for just those two, asks everyone for saving throws without explaining why, and then describes the entire sequence as one cinematic moment before calling for full initiative.

Throughout four rounds of combat, the article demonstrates: the Declare-Determine-Describe cycle; how to handle ambiguous action declarations (Beth saying "magic missile the goblins" vs. Chris saying "stabby stabby"); the art of the cutaway between different parts of the battlefield; telegraphing monster status through narrative description ("staggered" = half HP, described through visible wounds); managing different player personalities (the impulsive rogue, the by-the-book cleric, the tactical fighter, the indecisive wizard); and maintaining relentless pace by steamrolling table talk and immediately inviting the next player to act.

The combat runs about 15-20 minutes at Angry's table. Key techniques include: describing spells by visible effect rather than name; having NPCs react with dialogue and emotion (the warlock cursing his goblins, the goblin's morale breaking); using the Help action narratively (Beryllia distracting the bugbear for Ardrick's advantage); and ending with a goblin looting the warlock's corpse — adding a dynamic, unexpected element that drives player action even as the combat winds down.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start combat from the fiction, not from "roll initiative."** Let the social or exploration scene escalate naturally. Only call for initiative when multiple parties are committed to acting.
2. **Resolve simultaneous pre-combat actions with targeted initiative rolls.** If one character acts and an NPC reacts before full combat begins, roll initiative for just those two to determine sequence.
3. **Batch die rolls, then narrate.** Ask for saves or rolls without explaining why, resolve everything behind the screen, then deliver one cohesive narrative description.
4. **Set the scene at the top of each round.** Before asking the first player to act, summarize the battlefield state — who's fighting whom, who's wounded, what's changed.
5. **Demand clear action declarations.** "Magic missile the goblins" is ambiguous (which goblins? how many missiles each?). "Stabby stabby" is clear when there's only one target in reach. Clarify ambiguity, accept clarity.
6. **Use "staggered" at half HP.** Describe visible wounds affecting performance at the halfway mark. Discourage players from speaking in numbers — use narrative descriptions of condition.
7. **Telegraph monster behavior and morale.** Describe the goblin's terror, the warlock's panic, the bugbear's rage. This gives players information to act on and makes enemies feel alive.
8. **Interrupt tactical discussions.** If players start strategizing out of character, remind them their characters are in a chaotic fight. Keep the pressure on.
9. **Keep combats to 15-20 minutes.** Maintain pace by immediately inviting the next player to act after resolving the previous turn. Don't let anyone deliberate too long.
10. **Add dynamic elements.** The goblin looting the warlock's corpse during combat creates urgency and player-driven action even as the fight is winding down.
