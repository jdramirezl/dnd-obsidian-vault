# Struction: An Introduction to Structure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/struction-an-introduction-to-structure/)
**Date:** July 18, 2025
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Overview

The opening lesson of the Scenario Structure module in True Scenario Designery. Introduces what "structure" means in the context of scenario design, explains the dual nature of gameplay structure and narrative structure, and previews the elements that make structures good.

---

## What Is Structure?

Structure is how a scenario's elements are organized — what comes before what, how things are grouped, and how they relate to each other. Any scenario will have *some* structure. What True Scenario Designers care about is building **deliberately, intentionally good** structure that makes the scenario satisfying and enhances the designer's goals.

### Three Design Approaches

| Approach | Description | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **And-Then** | Improvisational/iterative — each thing follows from the current thing | Least cohesive, most shallow. Players say "Remember when I did that thing?" |
| **Bottom-Up** | Build individual elements and string them together | Richer, more cohesive. Players say "Remember that awesome encounter?" |
| **Top-Down** | Figure out big-picture first, then break down into chunks | Emergent, dynamic, holistic. Players say "Remember that awesome adventure where all this stuff happened?" |

True Scenario Designery uses **top-down** design.

## A Tale of Two Structures

Every TTRPG scenario has **two** underlying structures:

### Gameplay Structure

How gameplay elements fit together:
- How players navigate the scenario
- How they move from challenge to challenge, scene to scene
- What they're doing in each
- How victories and defeats carry forward

**This has the most to say about how players feel as they play.**

### Narrative Structure

How the game's events fit into the story players experience:
- Rising actions, climaxes, act structures
- How tension builds and releases
- Plot arcs and character arcs

**This plays a role in what players think about the game after playing.** Many players are motivated by participating in a well-structured narrative, even if they don't self-identify as "story-focused."

### The Cake Analogy

A birthday cake must work as both a cake (delicious) and a decoration (looks like SpongeBob):

- **Gameplay structure = the cake working as a cake** (taste) — must be a cake first
- **Narrative structure = the cake working as a decoration** (appearance) — must fit the theme second
- A cake that looks okay but tastes amazing → people talk about how good it was
- A cake that looks amazing but tastes bland → disappointment
- **You can recover from mediocre appearance if the taste is great. You can't recover from bad taste.**

### The Good News

Gameplay and narrative structures overlap significantly:
- Goals ↔ Incitements
- Major challenges ↔ Climaxes
- Outcomes ↔ Resolutions

The two structures are similar enough that managing both isn't as hard as it sounds — once you understand them.

## Elements of Gameplay Structure

### Gameplay Shape

The "map" of the game — how the scenario looks as a flowchart, list, or timeline:
- Linear, branching, looping, open
- Different shapes benefit different kinds of scenes and adventures
- Mixing and nesting shapes affects how gameplay feels at different points

### Gameplay Flow

How engagement changes from one event to the next:
- Not strictly "pacing" (that's reserved for narrative)
- The feel of different activities: investigative/explorative vs. urgent/combative
- Broader than simple mode-of-play classifications (exploring, interacting, delving)

### Gameplay Progression

How gameplay builds on itself as players play through:
- **Challenge progression:** Enemies get harder in specific, building ways
- **Skill progression:** Players learn mechanics and strategies through earlier encounters that prepare them for later ones
- **Resource progression:** Players gain tools and the game escalates to demand their use
- Not just "tutorializing" — it's how players *git gud* and how the game *gits gud* to keep pressure up

> **Example from Fall of Silverpine Watch:** Players fight 4 zombies in an easy-to-control arena → then 5 zombies in a harder arena. Zombies have a coin-flip revival unless killed with fire/holy → later, the blacksmith regenerates unless hit with fire/holy. Early investigation scene teaches how to ask questions → later scenes demand more thorough searching.

## Elements of Narrative Structure

Narrative structure mirrors gameplay structure:

| Gameplay | Narrative |
|---|---|
| Shape | Act structure (e.g., three-act) |
| Flow | Pacing — how tension builds and releases while trending upward |
| Progression | Plot arcs, character arcs |

### Mapping Narrative to Gameplay

- Plot points → scenes or encounters
- Story arc → the game's critical path
- Side quest → a complete character arc for an NPC
- Final challenge → the story's climax

Because the two structures overlap, you can mostly map narrative elements to gameplay elements. It's hard, but not as hard as it sounds.

---

## The "Stand Back and Squint" Approach

A lot of structure evaluation is about broad impressions rather than counting encounters:

- Not: "This adventure is mostly interaction because 60% of encounters are interaction"
- Instead: "This adventure is primarily about exploration because players are asking questions and gathering information on their own initiative, even though they're doing it socially and it has a linear progression"

These broad impressions affect experience on a *feeling* level, not a *thinking* level — which is where engagement really lives.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Structure is about relationships between elements**, not just having elements
2. **Every scenario has dual structures:** gameplay (how it plays) and narrative (how it tells a story)
3. **Gameplay structure is primary** — it determines how players feel during play
4. **Narrative structure is secondary but not ignorable** — it affects post-game reflection
5. **Three elements of gameplay structure:** Shape, Flow, Progression
6. **Narrative structure mirrors gameplay structure** and can be mapped to it
7. **Top-down design** (big picture first, then details) produces the richest experiences
8. **"Stand back and squint"** — broad impressions matter more than granular counts
