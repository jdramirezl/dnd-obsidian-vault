# Everything You Ever Wanted to Know About Conflict

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/everything-about-conflict/)
**Date:** March 25, 2021
**Series:** Narrative BS for GMs

---

## Part 1: Conflict in Traditional Narratives

### What Is Conflict?

**Conflict is the struggle between opposing forces in a narrative.** The protagonist wants something but something is keeping them from having it. Or the antagonist wants something terrible and the protagonist stands in their way. Or both want exclusive things.

Conflicts aren't always fights. Most conflicts aren't fights. Sometimes characters even end up in conflict with themselves.

When it comes to character-on-character conflict, the conflicts are always driven by **motivations** — opposing needs, drives, desires. Think of the opposing forces as opposing motivations.

### Why Does Conflict Matter?

Conflict is the most important narrative element there is:

1. **Creates tension** — Uncertainty about which force gets what they want keeps audiences emotionally invested
2. **Reveals character** — When characters resolve conflicts (especially internal ones), they reveal who they really are, what they really want, and what they really value
3. **Drives character growth and transformation** — Characters must become something more (or something else) to overcome conflicts

Examples:
- Luke refusing to kill Vader vs. Anakin killing Dooku — defining character moments
- Eddie Valiant throwing away his booze in *Who Framed Roger Rabbit*
- Scott Pilgrim cleaning up his messes to beat Gideon
- Luke turning off the targeting computer

### How to Describe Conflict

**Internal vs. External:**
- **Internal conflicts** happen entirely inside one character's brain — different beliefs, desires, ideals, goals in opposition (Simba's need for revenge vs. desire to escape responsibility)
- **External conflicts** arise between a character and something else — other people, nature, society, technology, fate, supernatural forces. Any noun can be a source of conflict.

**Central vs. Secondary:**
- **Central conflict** — The one big conflict at the story's heart. Once resolved, the story's done.
- **Secondary conflicts** — Everything else. They provide a constant undercurrent of rising and falling tension, reveal characters, and drive growth in preparation for the final conflict.

### How Do Conflicts End?

Conflicts are **resolved.** Ways to resolve:

- **Destruction, defeat, neutralization** — One force is destroyed or rendered powerless
- **Giving up** — One side stops wanting what they wanted (*War Games*: Skynet realizes nuclear war is futile)
- **Understanding, compromise, reconciliation** — The most meaningful resolutions
- **Rejection of desires** — A character incorporates or abandons their conflicting wants

> The most meaningful resolutions usually involve understanding, reconciliation, and compromise.

**Jurassic Park example:** Alan Grant (rejects technology) and John Hammond (believes in technology over nature) don't struggle against each other directly. In the end, they meet in the middle — Grant embraces technology (reactivating door locks) and uses nature (T-rex attacking raptors) to escape. Hammond agrees the park was a bad idea. Grant sits comfortably on the helicopter hugging the kids.

## Part 2: Conflict in RPGs

### What Is Conflict in RPGs?

The biggest problem: **you can't control what the protagonists actually want.** Players decide character motivations. Maybe they'll pick good ones. Maybe they won't pick any. Maybe they won't tell you what they are. It's a total crapshoot.

Players aren't writers or storytellers. They don't know from conflict or motivation. They're the moviegoers who don't know why they're cheering for Luke Skywalker.

Further complications:
- **Protagonists have two sets of motivations** — character motivations AND player motivations (feeling powerful, looking cool, hanging out with friends, watching the world burn)
- **RPGs are emergent, reactive narratives** — You can't decide how conflicts will be resolved; you can only judge whether a conflict has been sufficiently resolved based on the latest crazy action some player pulled

> The main reason why most RPG narratives involve simple goals defended by boss monsters and gauntlets of minor obstacles is that the players suck at understanding narrative motivation and the GMs don't know how to help them.

### Why Does Conflict Matter in RPGs?

Conflict is the **lynchpin that holds the story parts to the game parts:**

1. **Tells you when to roll dice** — If there's a conflict to resolve, roll the dice. No conflict? Don't roll dice.
2. **Tells you when the game is done** — Games are only fun until there's nothing stopping the players from accomplishing the goal
3. **Defines every part of an RPG** — Encounters, scenes, adventures, campaigns can all be defined in terms of goals and the conflicts that keep players from them
4. **Tells you when to give XP** — When players resolve a conflict (through violence, negotiation, cleverness, etc.), they deserve experience points

### Describing Conflict in RPGs

RPGs introduce two special kinds of conflict beyond internal and external:

**Intraparty Conflicts** — Conflicts between two PCs. These are actually more like internal conflicts than external ones because the party acts as a unified collective protagonist. Each player plays a different voice in the collective protagonist's head.

- Can be interesting and revelatory
- Super dangerous — the metagame limits how much can happen and how they must be resolved
- When resolved through violence or bitterly, something in the game usually breaks
- Unresolved intraparty conflicts = the slow death of a campaign

**Metaternal Conflicts** — Conflicts between a character's motivations and their player's motivations. Example: a character wants to abandon the party, but the player doesn't want to quit the group.

- Usually game-breaking
- Unavoidable — they happen
- **Must always be resolved in the player's favor** — No one continues to play a game that's not satisfying their needs. Anything else leads to burnout and depression.

### How Do Conflicts End in RPGs?

It seems like there's only one way: **kill-or-be-killed.** Even in social situations, it's usually about cowing the NPC to give in. Players never give up on their goals, propose compromise, or settle for partial victories.

Reasons:
- Poor understanding of motivation (by players AND GMs)
- GMs, players, and games don't know how to handle anything other than winning
- No system support for motivations as a narrative tool

## Part 3: Homework

Over the next several game sessions:

1. **Identify and describe the conflicts that arise.** What's the central conflict in your campaign? In your current adventure? What needs or desires are driving the conflict? **Write it down.**

2. **Whenever you use dice to resolve an action**, identify the conflict in play:
   - What's in conflict? Why?
   - Can you spot the motivations?
   - Is it a good, strong conflict with powerful motives?
   - Is there a chance for revelation, growth, or transformation?
   - Is there a positive impact on tension?
   - Or is it just another die roll for the sake of a die roll?
