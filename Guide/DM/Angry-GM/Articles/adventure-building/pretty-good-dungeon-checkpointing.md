# Let's Build a Pretty Good Dungeon: Checkpointing

## Summary

The final conceptual essay before actual dungeon construction begins. Checkpointing is the practice of deliberately placing opportunities for players to rest, bank progress, and take diversions within an adventure. Angry argues that leaving rest decisions entirely to players is bad design because psychological biases (completion bias, hatred of going backward, loss aversion) cause players to either rest constantly or never — neither of which is a rational, agency-driven choice. By designing deliberate checkpoints, the GM creates space for good decision-making and controls pacing, tension, and challenge feel. The article introduces **depth** (the number of costly encounters along a path segment) as the key metric for checkpoint placement, and classifies four types of checkpoints that can exist in a tabletop game world.

## Key Advice

### What Is a Checkpoint?

A checkpoint is a deliberate opportunity created by the designer for players to:

1. **Rest** — Recover spent resources
2. **Bank progress** — Ensure they don't have to retread old ground if things go wrong
3. **Take detours/diversions** — Clean up side quests, explore side paths, do maintenance

### Why Players Can't Be Trusted to Checkpoint Themselves

- **Completion Bias** makes players resist stopping mid-path
- **Players hate going backward** — retreating feels like failure
- **Death spiral**: The farther you go, the more you lose by retreating, so the less willing you are to stop, even as you get weaker
- Left to their own devices, players either rest constantly (when it's safe/free) or never (when it costs progress)
- **Neither extreme is a rational choice** — both are driven by compulsion, not strategy

**Core rule: A pretty good dungeon does NOT let players rest unmolested anywhere inside it.** Don't let players make their own checkpoints. That's bad. Designers creating deliberately safe places? That's good.

### The Power of Checkpoint Placement

| Checkpoint Spacing | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Far apart** | More challenging, checkpoints feel more rewarding, players feel driven to push harder |
| **Close together** | Less demanding, players can unload more resources per encounter, feel like badasses |

Checkpoints are also **progress markers** and **tension managers**. They say "good job getting this far" and relieve accumulated stress. Checkpoint starvation creates anxiety.

### Depth and Path Segments

**Depth** = the number of costly/risky encounters along a path segment (from one navigational decision point to the next).

Think of your dungeon map as a series of **path segments** with measurable depth, not as rooms connected by hallways. Example:

- Guard room (always 1 encounter) → branches into two wings
- Each wing has 3–4 encounters before hitting a dead end or checkpoint
- Total depth per path never exceeds what the system says players can handle (4–6 encounters in D&D 5e)

**No path should ask players to go deeper than they can handle before hitting a checkpoint or a dead end that forces them back to one.**

### Four Types of Checkpoints

| Type | Description | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Safe Space** | A room that's genuinely safe to rest in (hidden, defensible, magically protected, shrine, etc.) | Easy to build, good for resting | Doesn't protect progress, doesn't enable side exploration |
| **Shortcut** | A one-way connection back to an earlier area (barred door, ladder, rope drop, secret door visible from one side) | Banks progress, reduces backtracking cost | Requires creative worldbuilding to justify |
| **Side Door** | Another exit from (and entrance to) the dungeon, discoverable only from inside | Full progress banking, enables retreat and return | Must be hidden/inaccessible from outside |
| **Fast Travel** | Teleportation circles, elevators, magical transport — a side door without the step count | Maximum convenience | Requires the most creative justification |

### Checkpoints Are Opportunities, Not Coercion

- Players don't have to rest at checkpoints — if they're feeling strong, they'll push forward
- Checkpoints actually encourage pushing harder ("we can always come back here")
- Checkpoints make retreating less painful ("we only have to go back to the last checkpoint, not the entrance")
- Once players learn checkpoints exist, they seek them sideways when feeling drained

### The Art of Checkpoint Design

The mechanical side is knowing about depth, path segments, and placing checkpoints at the right intervals. The thematic side is imagining what features of your dungeon world would allow for safe spaces, shortcuts, side exits, or fast travel — and making them feel like natural parts of the environment, not game abstractions.

**"My dungeon needs to meet certain mechanical criteria; how can I make sure it does while still making it feel like a thing in my world?"** — This is the actual art of pretty good dungeon design.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Map your dungeon as path segments, not rooms** — Think: "From the entrance, the dungeon branches into two wings. Each wing has X encounters before a dead end or checkpoint."
2. **Count the depth of every path** — For each possible route, count the number of costly encounters from the last checkpoint (or entrance) to the next checkpoint (or dead end).
3. **Enforce the system's limits** — In D&D 5e, no path segment should exceed 4–6 encounters without a checkpoint. Adjust for your system.
4. **Never let players rest freely in hostile territory** — Resting in a dungeon should be a desperate last resort, not a casual choice. This makes your deliberate checkpoints meaningful.
5. **Make checkpoints scream safety** — The room must be obviously, saliently safe. A shrine behind a waterfall, a magically sealed chamber, a hidden grotto. Train players to recognize checkpoints.
6. **Use shortcut checkpoints liberally** — Barred doors that open from one side, ladders you can kick down, secret doors visible from the back. These are the workhorses of checkpoint design.
7. **Place side doors for large dungeons** — For multi-session dungeons, give players discoverable alternate entrances so they don't have to retread the entire dungeon every session.
8. **Vary checkpoint spacing for pacing** — Tighter checkpoints in early areas (build confidence), wider spacing deeper in (build tension). Once players internalize the rhythm, you can manipulate their expectations.

> [!tip] The Depth Budget
> Before drawing a single room, decide your **depth budget** — the maximum number of encounters you'll place between any two checkpoints. For D&D 5e, a good default is 3–4 encounters per segment (leaving room for random encounters or restocking). Write this number at the top of your design notes. Every time you add an encounter to a path segment, tick it against the budget. When you hit the limit, the next thing on that path must be a checkpoint, a dead end, or a branch back to a previous checkpoint. This single constraint will do more for your dungeon's playability than any amount of room-by-room encounter balancing.
