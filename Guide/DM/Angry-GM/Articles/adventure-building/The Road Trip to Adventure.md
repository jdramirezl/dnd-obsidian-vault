# The Road Trip to Adventure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-road-trip-to-adventure/)
**Date:** February 3, 2021
**Series:** Start Your Own Campaign

---

## Overview

How to plan a long-running campaign with a complex plot without actually planning the whole thing upfront. Uses a road trip analogy: you don't plan every twist and turn — you set destinations and take whatever route gets you there. The core technique is **summarizing and unsummarizing** — zooming in and out of your plot at different levels of detail.

## The Road Trip Analogy

When planning a road trip, you don't map every road. You have destinations and take whatever route connects them. You set preferences (efficient, scenic, easy to navigate). You adapt when highways close, traffic jams happen, or you decide to skip a stop. You only care about local roads when you're near a destination — the rest is highway.

**Campaign plots work the same way.** They move between a small number of key destinations (plot points/twists/reversals). The spaces between are acts/phases/chapters. The number of phases doesn't matter — three-act, four-part, five-element, whatever. What matters is you can break any story into any number of parts.

## The Core Skill: Summary at Any Length

GMs must be able to reduce any plot to a description of any length — one sentence, two sentences, four sentences, three paragraphs, five hundred words. This skill is needed for planning adventures, recapping sessions, and telling others about your games.

### Harry Potter in One Sentence
> Apprentice wizard Harry Potter must master magic and fulfill his destiny by thwarting and then destroying the Dark Lord, thereby saving the wizarding world.

### Harry Potter in Three Parts
1. Harry discovers he's a wizard and must master magic while thwarting the Dark Lord's attempts to return.
2. After the Dark Lord returns and authorities turn against him, Harry must forge alliances and master his abilities.
3. After the Dark Lord destroys most of Harry's allies, Harry and friends must unravel the Dark Lord's power and defeat him.

The three or four or seven points are the **destinations on your itinerary.** The plot takes twists and turns along the way, but what matters is hitting those destinations.

## The Technique: Unsummarizing

Take a single-sentence summary and expand it into more phases/points. Then take the first point and expand that. Keep drilling down until you have enough detail for your next adventure.

### Example: Zombie Apocalypse Campaign

**One sentence:**
> The PCs survive a zombie apocalypse, discover a rogue scientist started the plague, and develop a cure.

**Three-act expansion:**
1. The PCs come together to survive after the zombie apocalypse.
2. Their survival secured, the PCs discover clues revealing a rogue government scientist started the plague.
3. With information gleaned, the PCs work to develop a cure.

**Unsummarize Act 1:**
1. A group of strangers agree to work together to survive.
2. The survivors establish a shelter and stock it with supplies.
3. The survivors discover something that makes them investigate the plague's source.

**Unsummarize the first point of Act 1:**
1. Scavengers meet while looting a gas station and team up to escape zombies.
2. Sticking together, they search a strip mall and find a survivor who tells them about a shelter.
3. They make their way through teeming streets to secure the shelter.

**Unsummarize into encounters:**
1. In the parking lot, survivors meet by chance while looking for supplies.
2. Inside the convenience store, they fight stray zombies and ransack the place.
3. Drawn by noise, zombies gather outside — sneak out or fight through.

## Key Rules

### Only Unsummarize What You Need Right Now
You only need to drill down into the part you're about to run. Don't plan the whole campaign in detail — the more you plan ahead, the more you'll throw out when things change.

### Flexibility Is the Point
- Skip destinations (players want to secure shelter before stocking supplies? Skip the strip mall)
- Add destinations (players love building out their shelter? Lean into it)
- Change destinations entirely (drop "cure the plague" and replace with "war with a rival settlement")
- Double back (send players to the strip mall after they've already secured the shelter)

### Scale Is Adjustable
The same technique works at every level:
- **Campaign:** 3–5 major plot points
- **Story arc:** Unsummarize one campaign point into 3–5 arc points
- **Adventure:** Unsummarize one arc point into 3–5 adventure points
- **Session/Scene:** Unsummarize one adventure point into encounters

At each level, you're just treating a summary as a mini-story and expanding it.

### Starting a Campaign
All you need is:
1. A one-sentence campaign concept
2. A 3–5 point expansion of that concept
3. Enough unsummarizing of the first point to write your first adventure

That's it. You can start running the game with just that. Everything else gets planned as you go.

## Practical Notes

- Add details to early acts that foreshadow later ones (super-zombies in hospital gowns hint at the scientist)
- You can add foreshadowing details retroactively as you play and make decisions
- Deviations from high-level plots are rarer than GMs fear
- There's nothing wrong with telling players they've gone too far off the reservation if they abandon the agreed-upon game
- The more freeform your game, the more likely deviations — and the more valuable this flexible approach
