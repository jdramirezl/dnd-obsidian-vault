# More Shapey Goodness

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/more-shapey-goodness/)
**Date:** December 9, 2025
**Series:** True Scenario Designery — Scenario Structure (Gameplay Shape)

---

## Core Thesis

Scenario Outlines (flowchart diagrams) are flexible planning tools where the boxes, lines, and arrows can represent different things depending on context and scale. The same outlining tools used for Campaigns and Adventures also apply to **individual Encounters**, enabling richer, more varied gameplay. Deliberately designing encounter shapes — rather than defaulting to "single box, arrow in, arrow out" — is what separates intentional design from lazy "open-ended creativity" excuses.

## Key Concepts

### What the Boxes Represent

The boxes on a Scenario Outline can be:
- **Lower-level Scenarios** (Adventures within a Campaign, Scenes within an Adventure, etc.)
- **Microchallenges** — individual gameplay challenges within a scenario
- **Decision Points** — where players make important choices with no gameplay beyond the choice itself
- **Interactions** — places where players interact with something significant

What matters is that the designer thinks the element is **significant enough to affect gameplay**, even if it's not gameplay itself. As long as everything is *about* goals, challenges, and dynamics, it's fine if some elements aren't pure gameplay.

If there's a bunch of stuff on your outline that isn't gameplay, you need to rethink what you're doing.

### What the Lines Represent

Lines between boxes show that game action can move from one element to another. They could be:
- Hallways between rooms
- Adventure hooks or leads in a mystery
- Possession of knowledge (learning something unlocks access)
- Passage of time
- Specific approaches to a scenario element

In a labyrinthine dungeon, most lines are just hallways — you'd specifically label the ones with gameplay significance (locked doors, hidden passages).

### What the Arrows Represent

**In-pointing arrows** (start of action) could be:
- The incitement/hook of a campaign or adventure
- The door to a dungeon
- The heroes' arrival
- Just highlighting which element the GM should start running

**Out-pointing arrows** (end of action) could be:
- Scenario outcomes
- Connections to the next scene/adventure
- Or nothing — some scenarios don't have a clear "pop out" point (e.g., "pillage the dungeon" where players decide when they're done)

A single out-pointing arrow does NOT mean only one outcome. The outcome may depend on what happened along the way.

### Everything Is Abstract and Contextual

Context changes what every symbol means. The same diagram could represent a Campaign, an Adventure, or a Scene. A Scenario Designer uses these outlines however they want — draw what you want, write what you want, leave off what you want.

## Outlining Encounters

Encounters have shapes just like other scenarios. Most GMs cram every encounter into the "square hole" (linear: arrow in → box → arrow out), but encounters can be:

### Linear Encounter
Single box, inflow, outflow. A fight between the entrance and exit of a room.

### Diverge-Converge Encounter
An ogre you can *either* fight *or* sneak past. Two approaches, same outcome.

### Branching Encounter
An encounter you can *interact* or *combat*, where each approach leads to a **different outcome**.

### Pyramid Encounter
Multiple approaches that change the gameplay of the same core challenge:
- **Main door:** Crystal Guardian attacks normally.
- **Balcony entrance:** Drop the Crystal Chandelier on it (surprise round + elevated position).
- **Secret side door:** Smash the Crystal Crystal first (weakens the guardian).
All paths lead to combat, but the approach changes the fight.

### Open Encounter
Multiple simultaneous elements the players can address in any order:
- Example from *The Fall of Silverpine Watch*: Horses spooked by a snake → Oona bleeding out, cart stuck in mud, panicked horses damaging harnesses, hidden snake, injured heroes. Players choose what to address and in what order.

## Open-Ended Creativity Bullshit vs. Intentional Design

**"I don't create games; I create problems"** is a cop-out from GMs who can't be bothered to design playable games.

Yes, RPGs are inherently open-ended. Players can try anything and GMs can adjudicate anything. Every encounter has an assumed "…or any other reasonable approach the players conceive of." That's the GM's job, not the designer's excuse.

The designer's job is to **deliberately build encounters that invite different approaches** and give the GM tools to handle them — not just drop an ogre in a room and blame the players when they can't figure out what to do.

There's a difference between:
- Knowing the GM *can* handle it if players want to sneak past the ogre
- **Deliberately designing** an encounter that *invites* sneaking as equally viable to fighting, with tools for the GM to run both, and consequences that ripple into future encounters

## The True Value of Outlining

It's not about following a process or categorizing things correctly. It's about:

1. **How drawing an outline makes you think about your design** — what questions does it raise?
2. **How thinking about your design shapes how you draw the outline** — what's significant enough to include?
3. **How all of that affects how you build** when construction time comes.

If every encounter on your outline is a single box with an arrow in and an arrow out, you've built a boring-ass scenario. Vary the shapes of your encounters.
