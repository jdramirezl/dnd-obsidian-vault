# Angry's Five Rules for Amazing Adventure Design

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/angrys-five-rules-for-amazing-adventure-design/) | **Category**: Adventure Building

---

## Key Advice

- **The game must include everything the players need to play it.** Every adventure must teach players how to win it — not by lecturing, but through play. If research is key to surviving an encounter, the adventure must show players that research is an option.
- **Action beats exposition.** Don't tell players what strategies to use — create situations where attentive, clever players discover the right choices themselves. Show, don't tell.
- **The most natural way to play should be the best way to play.** Design so that following player instincts leads to success. Don't punish players for doing what the game invites them to do (exploring a labyrinth, fighting monsters with combat-focused characters).
- **Nothing is absolute until the players see it.** Don't constrain yourself with world details players will never encounter. Don't track dungeon populations — design proper encounter progressions. The players only ever encounter the parts of the world that make for good gameplay.
- **No mechanics are the best mechanics.** Don't build game mechanics if there's any way to avoid it. Design monsters with exploitable behaviors and hide strategic clues — don't create "Exploration Points" redeemable at the Prize Counter.

## Summary

This article lays out the five core philosophies underlying Angry's approach to adventure (scenario) design. These aren't exhaustive rules but represent the foundational beliefs that drive all his adventure-building work, including the True Scenario Designery series.

**Rule 1: The Game Must Include Everything the Players Need to Play It.** Teaching the game isn't just about dice mechanics — it's about teaching gameplay dynamics, strategies, and the unique approaches each adventure requires. *The Fall of Silverpine Watch* teaches "exploring is how you win" not through a speech but through encounters: a ghost flees through a locked door, a key is hidden down a side path, and the critical path ensures players receive the message even if they bypass obstacles creatively. Every adventure is its own game and must arm players for victory.

**Rule 2: Action Beats Exposition.** Telling players "do research before exploring the ruins" plays the game for them. Instead, create situations where the right choices are discoverable by attentive players. How obvious you make those choices is your difficulty slider. The locked door and murder scene in *Silverpine Watch* teach exploration through action, not lectures.

**Rule 3: The Most Natural Way to Play Should Be the Best Way to Play.** This goes both ways: design games that are most fun when played naturally, AND don't punish natural play. Modern D&D characters are built for combat — punishing players for fighting monsters is a screwjob. Similarly, if you play monsters like they're trying to win, their behavior becomes logical and predictable, which actually *empowers* players to deduce and counter strategies. Playing monsters stupid makes the game harder, not easier.

**Rule 4: Nothing Is Absolute Until the Players See It.** Don't track dungeon populations — build proper encounter progressions. Don't map wilderness encounters to fixed locations — plan them for journey stages. Players never see the simulation behind the screen, so don't let it constrain your design. This isn't Quantum Ogres — it's recognizing that good gameplay trumps background simulation the players will never perceive.

**Rule 5: No Mechanics Are the Best Mechanics (The Mechanical Hierarchy).** Don't use mechanics if you can achieve the same effect without them. If you want exploration to be rewarded, design monsters with exploitable weaknesses and hide strategic clues — don't create a bonus system. The hierarchy: No Mechanics > Background Mechanics > System Mechanics > Scenario-Specific Mechanics, and Soft Mechanics > Hard Mechanics. Example: a plague timer should be "each day, X% chance someone dies" (soft, random) rather than "every 3 days, 10 people die" (hard, deterministic).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Teach through encounters, not exposition.** For every strategy players need, design an encounter that demonstrates it through play. If exploration is key, create an early encounter that rewards exploration.
2. **Use the critical path to deliver lessons.** Even if players bypass an obstacle creatively, ensure the critical path still teaches the intended lesson (like *Silverpine Watch*'s side path that isn't really a side path).
3. **Audit your adventure for "screwjob" designs.** Ask: "Does this punish players for doing what the game naturally invites them to do?" If yes, redesign it.
4. **Play monsters to win.** Their logical behavior creates predictable patterns players can learn, exploit, and counter. This makes combat a puzzle, not a dice-rolling exercise.
5. **Don't track what players can't see.** Skip dungeon population counts, fixed wilderness encounter locations, and other background simulation that constrains design without improving gameplay.
6. **Avoid custom mechanics when possible.** Instead of "+1 per clue found," design the boss monster so certain strategies (suggested by clues) are mechanically more effective within the existing rules.
7. **Prefer soft mechanics over hard ones.** Use randomized timers ("X% chance per day") over deterministic ones ("every Y days"). Leave wiggle room for dramatic pacing.
8. **Make information discoverable, not mandatory.** Players who find clues should have an advantage, but players who guess the right strategy through clever thinking should still be able to win.
