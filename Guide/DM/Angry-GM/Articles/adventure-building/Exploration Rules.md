# Exploration RULES!

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/exploration-rules/)
**Date:** June 7, 2017
**Series:** Hacking New Modes of Play

---

A rules module that extends D&D 5E to provide mechanical depth for exploration. This is the "Exploration Master Module" — the foundational framework on which other modules (traps, social interaction, crafting, etc.) will be built.

---

## What Is a Rules Module?

A set of rules and mechanics that **extend** the existing rules of D&D and allow for added mechanical depth of play. Extension is key — not changing the rules, just adding mechanics over them. Modules must be:

- **Self-contained** — easy to say what it does and when it should be used
- **Related** — all rules within a module should be related
- **Composable** — modules can be grouped together and use other modules as prerequisites

## Four Modes of Play

D&D can be divided into four major modes of play:

1. **Encounter Play** — Moment-by-moment decisions, actions measured in seconds. May or may not follow initiative order. Includes combat, chase scenes, action scenes, social interactions.
2. **Exploration** — The middle ground. Lacks the tension of combat, though surprises can happen. Covers shorter time intervals than travel. Players spend a few minutes on individual tasks. Moving scene to scene (dungeon rooms) or playing out extended single scenes (infiltrating a stronghold).
3. **Travel** — Moving from place to place over extended periods. Actions cover hours or days.
4. **Down Time** — Hours or days of activity. Long-term tasks: crafting, research, training, building reputations, carousing.

### Exploration's Focus

Exploration works at a slower pace than encounters. It is dangerous by way of **attrition and daily resource management**: hit points, hit dice, fatigue, non-immediate conditions, spell slots, character abilities, magic item charges, one-use magic items.

Things that pertain to longer-term (rations, water, climate, weather) don't belong in exploration. Things that pertain to very short-term (conditions ending after seconds, cantrips) belong in encounters.

## Tweaks to Core Rules

### Passive Perception and Passive Knowledge

Every skill has a passive component and an active component:

- **Passive** = no choice, no action, entirely internal. Noticing something, factual recall, recognition. Your eyes and brain just handle it.
- **Active** = choice and action, direct interaction with the world. Searching, researching, experimenting.

**Don't roll passive rolls.** Set a DC as normal. Quietly compare the DC to the result they would have if they rolled a 10. Keep a list of everyone's knowledge skills and passive perception scores. Dole out information without consulting the player.

> IF THE CHARACTER HAS THE PROFICIENCY OR RANKS IN THE SKILL, THEY GET THE INFO. I'VE BEEN DOING IT SINCE 3E AND THE PLAYERS HAVE NEVER NOTICED.

### The Proficiency Rule and the Tool Rule

A task that requires specialized tools generally also requires specialized knowledge and training. Characters may only attempt tasks using tools if they also possess the proficiency with those tools. The GM may decide specific tasks are exceptions but should be explicit. Improvised tools impose disadvantage.

### Group Checks (Revised)

When several PCs try to accomplish something as a group:

1. **If the group succeeds when any member succeeds** (e.g., searching): Roll a single check using the **highest** modifier and proficiency bonus available. If any member has advantage, the check has advantage. Apply the best single bonus.
2. **If the group fails when any member fails** (e.g., stealth): Roll a single check using the **lowest** modifier and proficiency bonus available. If any member has disadvantage, the check has disadvantage. Apply the single worst penalty.

## Exploration Tasks

Any action that takes more than one minute but less than ten minutes = an **exploration task.** Moving from one room/scene to another is also generally an exploration task (the party gathers in marching order, moves quietly, peers through doors, listens, etc.).

### Immediate vs. Non-Immediate

- **Immediate actions:** Kicking open a door, jumping over a pit
- **Non-immediate actions (exploration tasks):** Securing a rope and climbing, picking a lock, moving between rooms

### Quiet vs. Noisy

- **Noisy:** Can be heard at a reasonable distance (kicking open a door)
- **Quiet:** No louder than quiet talking (picking a lock, careful movement)

Default position: a task is non-immediate and quiet. A rushed task is almost always loud.

## Actions During Exploration

1. Player declares an action
2. GM determines: can it succeed or fail? Is an ability check required? Is it immediate? Is it quiet or noisy?
3. **If immediate:** Resolve immediately. If loud, Roll for Complications.
4. **If non-immediate:** Ask other players what they're doing during this time. Handle any immediate actions first. Once all players are committed to non-immediate actions or waiting, resolve each non-immediate task in turn, then **Advance Time.** If any actions were loud, **Roll for Complications.**

## Extended Tasks

Some very complicated tasks take longer than ten minutes. The GM determines how much time the task requires in terms of actions. Each action Advances Time once and, if noisy, may cause a Roll for Complications.

If the action requires a die roll and the roll fails, the character makes no progress — they may have to attempt further actions. The party may decide to give up, particularly if complications arise.

## Time and the Time Pool

The Time Pool is a pile of tokens (coins, candy, poker chips, or dice) visible to all players and the GM.

### Advancing Time

Whenever the party engages in non-instantaneous exploration tasks, moves between locations, or engages in an activity taking 1–10 minutes (e.g., ritual casting), the GM **Advances Time:**

1. Any effect with **10 minutes or less** remaining expires. Other durations are not affected.
2. Add a single token to the Time Pool (only one, even if multiple characters acted simultaneously).
3. **Exception — Pool full (6 tokens):** Do NOT add a token. Instead, **Clear the Time Pool** (see below).
4. **Exception — Rushing:** Do NOT add a token. Still Advance Time and expire durations.
5. After expiring durations and adding tokens, **Roll for Complications** if any tasks were loud or rushed.

The GM may also Advance Time if the party spends more than a few minutes debating — this prods a distracted party to action.

## Rolling for Complications

Roll for Complications when:
- The party attempts a loud or rushed task
- Before Clearing the Time Pool
- The party does something to attract attention (arguing loudly)
- The adventure calls for it (alarm trap, screaming prisoner)

### How to Roll

Roll a number of dice **equal to the number of tokens currently in the Time Pool.**

| Environment | Die Size |
|---|---|
| Less dangerous / sparsely populated | d8 |
| Normal | d6 |
| Extremely dangerous | d4 |

Adventure elements can change the die size (e.g., kobold lair during the day = d8, at night = d6, alarm raised = d4).

**If any die shows a 1:** A Complication occurs. Remove one token from the Time Pool, then resolve the Complication.

**If rolling with no tokens in the pool:** Roll one Complication Die. If it shows a 1, a Complication arises.

## Complications

A Complication is a random event, setback, or encounter prescribed by the adventure. The most common are random encounters with wandering creatures.

### Design Guidelines

- Encounters should be **Easy difficulty or easier** — nuisances, not serious threats
- No significant XP for dealing with Complications
- Wandering creatures don't have to be hostile (but outright friendly should be exceedingly rare)
- Other events: geological events, minor traps/hazards, conditions on an unlucky PC
- Keep them simple: one or two creature types with clear motivations, or a single event the party can react to
- For each expected adventuring day, design **4–6 possible Complications**
- Specific locations can have specific Complications keyed to them (e.g., sulfurous geysers in the geyser cave)
- Use Complications creatively to bring a dungeon to life

## Clearing the Time Pool

When there are already 6 tokens and Time Advances:

1. Expire effects with 10 minutes or less remaining (as normal)
2. Any effect with **an hour or less** remaining expires
3. Any effect with **more than one hour** remaining has one hour subtracted
4. **Roll for Complications** (6 dice) regardless of loudness/rushing
5. Whether a Complication occurs or not, **remove all tokens** from the Time Pool
6. Mark one hour of time on a log

The Time Pool gradually counts up to one hour of game time. Slight variation due to Complications removing tokens and rushing means the actual number of tasks per hour may vary — this accounts for the imprecise nature of time tracking.

## Time Flies (Shortcut for Longer Activities)

If the party engages in an activity taking more than 10 minutes, add one token per 10 minutes. Each token = a normal Advancement of Time. If the pool fills, Clear it as normal.

**Short Rest shortcut:** Assume one hour passes. Mark an hour on the log. Expire/decrement durations. Pick up 6 dice and Roll for Complications. The actual Time Pool remains untouched. This removes the possibility of interrupting a short rest but greatly simplifies the process and still provides consequences for the choice to rest.

## Summary

The Exploration Master Module provides:
- Tools for GMs and adventure designers
- Consequences for different player choices (visible and understandable)
- A framework for extending modes of play around exploration

**Key tradeoffs players face:**
- Bashing down a door is quick but loud
- Picking a lock is quiet but slow
- Rushing saves time but risks Complications
- Taking time is safer but fills the Time Pool

The module works fine on its own but will really shine when other rules modules (traps, social interaction, crafting, etc.) are built on top of it.
