# Nothing Here But Worthless Gold!

## Key Advice

Treasure — specifically monetary treasure — is **functionally worthless** in D&D 5e (and largely in other editions). The game presents gold as important and provides guidelines for distributing it, but gives players almost nothing meaningful to spend it on. This creates busywork for GMs and confusion for players. The problem is systemic and cannot be fixed without a massive overhaul.

## Summary

Angry identifies a fundamental design flaw in D&D: the game tells GMs to hand out treasure and provides detailed guidelines for how much, but provides almost no meaningful use for that treasure. The result is hours of pointless bookkeeping.

**Why gold is worthless:**
- **No upgrade path** — D&D has no meaningful gear progression you can buy. Players start with the weapons and armor they'll use forever, except fighters/paladins saving for plate by level 3.
- **Magic items can't be bought** — The only real upgrade path (magic items) is handed out by GM fiat, not purchased. There's a massive cultural taboo against magic item shops.
- **No meaningful utility items** — Potions, scrolls, and other consumables are also magic items and thus off-limits for purchase (in 5e, only basic healing potions).
- **No big-ticket items** — Strongholds, ships, and businesses exist as "strictly optional" rules buried in the DMG.
- **Trivial living costs** — Food, lodging, and supplies cost so little relative to treasure that they're meaningless after level 2.

**Key analytical ground rules:**
- Analyze the system as written, not what a clever GM *could* do with it. A microwave can kill fleas on a cat, but that doesn't make it a pest control device.
- Optional rules only count if the system explains why you'd want them. The DMG never warns that without money sinks, gold is worthless.
- The system should work for someone with zero game design knowledge.

**Why "money sinks" don't work:** A money sink just exchanges one worthless thing for another. For money to matter, spending it must involve **meaningful choices** — offense vs. defense, save vs. spend, upgrade vs. consumable. Without scarcity and competing priorities, money is just paperwork.

**Why there's no economy:** In RPGs, money spent just vanishes. It doesn't circulate. Gold is just another form of "points" — or it would be, if there were anything to spend points on.

**The quick fix:** Stop tracking money. Assume PCs can afford basic supplies and living costs. Most GMs already ignore ammo, rations, and encumbrance — go all the way.

**The big fix:** Redesign the entire game to include meaningful upgrade paths, utility purchases, and big-ticket items that compete for limited resources. This would require rebalancing magic items, treasure distribution, challenge ratings, and NPC equipment. It's a mountain of work and probably not worth it for D&D specifically.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop stressing about treasure distribution** — if you're running 5e, gold doesn't matter mechanically. Don't waste prep time on it.
2. **Consider dropping money tracking entirely** — assume PCs can afford basic supplies. If you already ignore ammo and rations, you're halfway there.
3. **Don't use money sinks** — giving players something worthless to dump gold into doesn't create meaningful gameplay. It's still busywork.
4. **If you want money to matter, commit to the overhaul** — you'll need to make magic items purchasable (with careful curation), add utility items, add big-ticket goals, and rebalance treasure. This is a massive undertaking.
5. **Understand the real problem** — D&D presents money as important when it isn't. The confusion this causes for GMs and players is the actual design flaw, not the absence of a money system per se.
6. **For meaningful money, look at other systems** — games like Hackmaster, ACKS, or older editions with gold-for-XP handle this differently. D&D 5e simply wasn't designed around money mattering.

> [!tip] The Core Problem
> "Where D&D does f$&% up is in presenting money treasure as important. Because that's an outright lie. And it confuses the hell out of poor GMs. And, worse, it forces GMs to do unnecessary work." The game would be better off either making gold meaningful or admitting it doesn't matter — instead, it does neither.
