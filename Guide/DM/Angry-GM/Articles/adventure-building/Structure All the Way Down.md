# Structure All the Way Down

> *Part of the ongoing course in advanced adventure, encounter, and campaign design, True Scenario Designery.*

## Scenarios are Made of Scenarios

Scenarios are recursive, fractal things at different scales:

- **Campaigns** — the largest scenarios
- **Arcs** — between campaigns and adventures
- **Adventures** — the baseline, "medium size" scenario
- **Scenes** — between adventures and encounters
- **Encounters** — the smallest scenario unit

Bigger scenarios are made of smaller scenarios. Structure is about how you string these together.

Adventures are the baseline scale — the purest, most complete, playable form of scenario. One adventure is enough for a truly complete RPG gameplay experience. That's why it's best to learn scenario design at the adventure scale.

**Key principle:** True Scenario Designers follow a top-down design approach. Design the whole model first, figure out what pieces you need and how they fit, then start clicking them into place. Don't just string encounters together like LEGO bricks.

## Skipping Scenario Scales

Some scenario scales are optional — arcs and scenes specifically. You can make a campaign without arcs, or an adventure without scene structure. But the way you divide up your scenario and group the parts changes the **playfeel** or **gamefeel**.

### Are Paths Arcs?

Adventure paths (like Paizo's) are **campaigns**, not arcs. They're complete, standalone, multipart gameplay experiences. Design intent matters — adventure paths aren't made to be arcs, so they aren't arcs.

## A Scenario Is a Scenario Is a Scenario

What makes an adventure a scenario is that its components are themselves scenarios. Scenes and encounters have goals, outcomes, challenges, and play dynamics.

Key implications:

1. **Arcs and scenes need goals, outcomes, and challenges.** They aren't just containers.
2. **Encounters have shapes and structures** — a combat is *linear*, a fight-or-sneak encounter is *branching*, a crime scene investigation is *open*.
3. **X-factors apply at every level.** A combat encounter could be an investigation or exploration scenario.
4. **Macroscopic challenges and play dynamics** arise from how outcomes of different parts feed into each other.

### Outcome Gating

If the scenario can't progress past a certain outcome in one of its parts, that part becomes an **outcome gate**. The whole adventure's outcome may turn on that one part.

**Outcome gating is perfectly valid.** If every adventure only allows failure in the final encounter, you're writing single-encounter adventures with long, meaningless prologues. Outcome gating succeeds or fails based on the design decisions around it.

### Death Isn't an Outcome

Death is not an outcome — it's a **game over**. "Don't die" is a system-level goal that transcends all adventures and competes with adventure goals. To a True Scenario Designer, a combat encounter that must be defeated to advance is a "can't lose scenario" — guaranteed victory unless they die.

## Example: The Lost Pickling Hut of Shrub Niggurath

Three scenes:

1. **In the Town of Malarkhamy** — Open structure, investigation
   - Can't-lose goal: locate the dungeon
   - Variable outcomes: how well-equipped the party is
   - Two options (crazed hunter guide vs. cursed journal), each with benefits and risks

2. **Navigating the Forest** — Linear gauntlet of hazards
   - Can't really fail, but setbacks chew up resources

3. **Exploring the Dungeon** — Branching/labyrinthine
   - Time-limited with cosmic horror consequences
   - Failure is now an option — outcome gates live here

### Structural Analysis

- **Top-level:** Linear (town → forest → dungeon)
- **Overall shape:** Labyrinthine (dungeon dominates play time and memory)
- **X-factor:** Mystery, investigation, exploration

Players won't remember fighting plant monsters — they'll remember the crazed hunter, the cursed journal, and solving a logic puzzle while their senses lied.
