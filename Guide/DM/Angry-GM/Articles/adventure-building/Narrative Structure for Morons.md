# Narrative Structure for Morons

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/narrative-structure-for-morons/)
**Date:** May 23, 2018
**Series:** Narrative Structure for Morons (Part 1 of 3)

---

Narrative – or dramatic – structure just refers to the shape of the events in a story. That is to say, the shape of the plot. As a GM, you plan a plot – at least, you outline a plot. Plots have a shape to them because the human brain is wired to expect stories to have a certain shape. If you want your games to be satisfying, they have to fit the basic outline of a good story. Certain things have to happen. And they have to happen in the proper order.

## A Tale of Three Acts

The three-act structure divides the story into three parts: the setup, the confrontation, and the resolution.

**Act I (Setup):** Establishes the world, introduces the characters, and invites us into the setting. Then we get an inciting incident that disrupts the hero's life and introduces a conflict. The hero takes a decisive action or accepts a quest – this is the first plot point, the transition between Acts I and II. Act I is usually short – it's like a foyer for the story.

**Act II (Confrontation):** Most of the story. The hero is doing things, gaining allies, exploring, learning, dealing with conflicts. Meanwhile, the conflict is growing – the stakes are rising. This is "rising action." Then, something happens – the second major plot-point. Often a reversal where the hero suffers a catastrophic loss. The hero ends up at a very low point, and tension is at its highest.

**Act III (Resolution):** The hero faces the conflict once and for all. The resolution of the conflict is the climax. After the climax, there's the falling action (denouement) – everything going back to normal, but the hero is different.

### Lessons for RPGs

Act I is usually very short in RPGs. Most exposition is assumed – the players know the heroes and the world. So Act I usually skips to the incitement and turning point. The heroes are introduced to the conflict and they decide to do it. That's it.

The three-act structure doesn't quite fit into TTRPGs because the most complicated part – the only part that needs writing – is Act II. And for larger adventures, you may want the adventure part split into several parts.

**Good lessons:** exposition, inciting incident, rising action, climax, resolution.
**Bad lessons:** everything else. Grade: C+.

## The Five-Room Dungeon

Invented by JohnnFour at Strolen's Citadel. A simple, plug-and-play format for quickly producing a dungeon that takes one or two sessions. Five rooms:

1. **Guarded Entrance** – establishes mood and theme
2. **Puzzling or Role-playing Room** – change of pace from room one
3. **Setback/Trick** – burns resources, creates a difficult position
4. **Climax** – the boss fight or big conflict
5. **Final Reward/Revelation** – or a plot-twist leading to further adventures

It's not a narrative structure and was never meant to be. 60% of the game is Act III, which doesn't work for a narrative. But for a dungeon adventure that is PART of a narrative, it works fine.

**Good lessons:** change up the pace by switching encounter types; it's better to go into the climax with weak heroes than strong heroes.
**Bad lessons:** this isn't really a narrative structure.

## The Hero's Journey (Campbell's Monomyth)

Joseph Campbell noticed that the most enduring stories followed a similar pattern. The monomyth follows the journey of the protagonist through certain stages:

The hero starts in the ordinary world → called to adventure → refuses the call → accepts after all → sets out into the unknown → meets allies, helpers, tricksters, obstacles → receives supernatural aid → undergoes a supreme ordeal → receives reward → journeys home → has been changed by the adventure.

This is just the three-act structure with more detail. Campbell wasn't talking about how to write a good story – he was talking about how lots of good stories end up looking the same. Trying to conform too hard to an exact structure misses the point.

Most of what Campbell identified doesn't fly directly in a TTRPG. Do you really need a scene at the start of every adventure where a character refuses to take on the adventure?

**Good lessons:** the three-act structure is a good start, but the devil is in the details.
**Bad lessons:** every story must have things like refusing the call and supernatural aid in the proper order like a checklist.

## A Good Narrative Structure for Every RPG Ever?

These three structures all provide some pieces of a good narrative structure for an RPG adventure. The key insight: any good model of narrative structure in an RPG should be based NOT around acts and segments, but around **turning points**. Considering the GM's primary tools are scenes and encounters, that makes a lot of sense.

We can build a list of turning points:
- **Turning Points that MUST Exist** (Three-Act Structure)
- **Turning Points that SHOULD Exist** (Screenplay Structure)
- **Turning Points that COULD Exist** (Monomyth Structure)

As a GM, when planning a story, figure out which turning points you want, build scenes around them, and figure out how the story will proceed based on the possible outcomes. With a bunch of turning points defined and sitting at your fingertips, when you want to add a new scene on the fly, you can add one that will actually impact the story.
