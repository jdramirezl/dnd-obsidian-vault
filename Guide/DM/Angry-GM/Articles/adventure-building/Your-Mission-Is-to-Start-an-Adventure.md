# Your Mission Is to Start an Adventure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/your-mission-is-to-start-an-adventure/)
**Date:** February 3, 2016
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## Every Story Has a Beginning

Every adventure has an opening scene. Don't call it a "story hook" — that term has been abused into meaninglessness. A story hook is any element that connects one part of a story to another. The opening scene's job is bigger than just hooking the heroes.

### Scene vs. Encounter (Quick Recap)

- **Scene:** No conflict/uncertainty — the bartender willingly shares information
- **Encounter:** A source of conflict creates uncertainty about whether heroes can accomplish the goal — interrogating a resistant cultist with a time limit

Scenes can serve multiple purposes (recap, exposition, planning, exploration, decision, discovery, preparation, encounter) and you can **combine** them freely.

---

## The Three-Fold Purpose of the Opening Scene

Every opening scene must do three things:

### 1. Present the Resolution (the Goal)

By the end of the opening scene, the party should be able to explain how they're going to win the adventure. Present them with a problem to solve.

> It's really hard to run a race if you don't know where the finish line is.

### 2. Provide the Motivation

Two kinds of motivation to address:
- **Character motivation:** Why the characters want to win (gold, justice, survival)
- **Player motivation:** Why the players want to play (big combats, mystery, exploration)

**Don't bury the lede.** If your players want to fight a dragon and the characters want gold, SHOW THEM A DRAGON AND GOLD. Don't hide the motivations behind vague mystery unless mystery IS the motivation.

> The dirt farmers could just say "dragon."

**Don't sell past the close.** Once the heroes accept the premise, stop selling. The opening scene is the least interesting part of the adventure — once you've made the sale, start the adventure.

### 3. Provide Entry Points into the Adventure's Structure

The opening scene is the first room of the adventure, not the door to the dungeon. It needs clearly marked exits:
- The party should know where they can go next
- At least one clear path forward
- Don't make transitions contingent on players **finding** them — if they don't find clues, the adventure is already over
- Communicate paths clearly through narration

> "Ahead on the road, you can see the lights of a village. This attack happened pretty close to the edge of the village."

---

## Additional Purposes (Optional)

Once the three core jobs are done, the opening scene can also:
- Provide information and/or resources (healing potions, history of the location)
- Allow exploration (asking NPCs questions, poking around a scene)

**But:** Don't cram extra stuff in before the resolution, motivation, and entry points are established. Until those are in front of the PCs, the adventure hasn't started.

---

## Opening Scenes Can Suck

Common belief: opening scenes must be amazing. **Wrong.**

What an opening scene must be is **SHORT**.

- The motivations sell the adventure, not the opening scene
- The promise of dragon, mystery, challenge, or a rich world — that's what sells
- A really amazing TV doesn't need an amazing shopping experience
- Short is better than good
- The opening scene is inherently the most boring scene in the adventure

**Exception:** If you're selling a rich, detailed world to explore, the opening scene has to present that world engagingly. But if the promise is "dragon," once you say "dragon," you're done.

---

## Gathering the Party

How to get the party together? **No one cares.**

- "You all meet in a tavern" is efficient and works when it makes sense
- The first meeting is dull and unfocused — there's plenty of time for getting-to-know-you later
- Any quick narration that covers the initial meeting and kickstarts the adventure is fine

## Types of Opening Scenes

### Exposition, Ho!

The easiest, quickest, most efficient way to open an adventure. Just tell the players the setup:

> "You've learned that something dragon-like has taken over a treasure-filled cave and is killing dirt weevils. The path starts west of the village. What do you do?"

Variations:
- **Flat narration:** You just tell them
- **In-world briefing:** An NPC explains it (allows player questions/interaction)
- **Mission briefing:** Built into the campaign structure (military games, organizations)

Use exposition openings whenever possible. The only problem: they lack agency, so they don't work when selling interaction, exploration, or moral complexity.

### The Mystery of the Mysterious Thing

A discovery scene — an object or revelation provides goal, motivation, and entry points:
- Treasure map (promise of treasure + directions)
- Strange artifact
- Crime scene (promise of mystery + goal to solve)

**Critical:** The mysterious thing must be tantalizing enough to scream motivation and goal. Don't give players a mysterious artifact and expect interest — tell them it's obviously of draconic origin and dates back 10,000 years. Sell the mystery upfront.

### The Exciting Inciting Incident

An opening scene that IS an encounter — presents a goal, conflict, and resolution:
- Attacked by werewolves in the village at night
- Great for selling specific types of action (fight = action adventure, horror event = horror adventure)

**Challenges:**
1. Must match the adventure — don't start a peace treaty adventure with an orc attack; start with a negotiation
2. Happens before the adventure starts — PCs are unmotivated and goalless, so the encounter must present a very clear goal
3. **Any encounter can fail** — the inciting incident must do its three jobs whether it succeeds or fails

> Whether the PCs negotiate, fight, surrender, or flee the orc encounter, the story is set up: goal is peace, motivation is clear, entry point is the village.

Often helps to frame the incident as part of another story to provide context.

### In Medias Res

Starting somewhere in the middle of the adventure. Motivations, goals, and structure are gradually revealed.

**Advanced and mostly unnecessary.** Can work for:
- Complicated mysteries
- Amnesia scenarios
- Mirror universe twists
- (Star Trek examples: Conundrum, Cause and Effect)

**Don't do it** unless it adds something very specific. It confuses players and is very hard to do well.

**Avoid the "Cold Open Gambit":** Starting with an exciting scene (usually combat) then flashing back to explain why. This doesn't solve the boring opening scene problem — it just puts it after a good scene. Better answer: just get through the opening scene quickly.

---

## Summary

1. Opening scenes have three jobs: **goal**, **motivation**, **entry points**
2. Don't bury the lede — sell your motivations openly
3. Don't sell past the close — once they're in, start the adventure
4. Short is better than good
5. Provide clearly marked exits from the opening scene
6. Gathering the party doesn't need to be elaborate
7. Exposition is the default best choice for opening scenes
8. Start at the beginning unless you have a very specific reason not to
