# This, That, or Other (Part 1): Why Design the Problem and the Solutions?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/this-that-or-other-part-1/)
**Date:** October 23, 2017
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## The Core Idea

Design obstacles with **multiple predetermined solutions** — like "immersive sim" video games (Prey, Deus Ex, System Shock) where major obstacles can be overcome through different skill sets.

This is the **"This, That, or Other"** approach: design 2–4 different solutions for each obstacle, while always leaving room for unexpected player approaches ("Other").

---

## Why Predesign Solutions?

Many GMs argue designing solutions is railroading or turns the game into a multiple-choice test. This is wrong.

### The Perfect Solution Fallacy

"Because I can't plan for every possible thing the players might do, planning for any possibility is pointless." You don't need to plan for everything — just the most likely approaches.

### Practical Reasons

1. **Mechanics at your fingertips.** If players take a predesigned approach, you already have the numbers. The game won't stall. (Monster stat blocks already do this — they include ability scores, skills, senses beyond combat stats.)

2. **Helps you adapt.** If players pick "Other" and it's kind of like "This," you're halfway there. Game mechanics are communicative — break DC 10 = fragile wood (flammable); DC 30 = stone/metal (not flammable).

3. **GM is never stumped.** If players can't find a solution, the GM knows a few and can hint through **affordance/signifiers** — playing up the NPC's fear, noting the NPC eyeing their purses, etc.

### The Main Reason: Differentiation and Consequences

Predesigning forces you to think about how approaches **differ** and what **consequences** follow from each.

---

## Outcomes vs. Consequences

**Winning isn't interesting. Losing isn't interesting.** What's interesting is what comes **after**.

- Breaking down a door → monster readies an ambush because it heard you
- Bullying an NPC for info → NPC sends goons after you later
- Sneaking past a monster → monster is still alive when you need to flee back

**Outcome** = did you get what you wanted?
**Consequence** = ripples through the rest of the game from HOW you did it.

- Picked the lock → monster is surprised
- Smashed the door → monster is ready
- Bribed the NPC → no retaliation
- Bullied the NPC → goons come later
- Killed the monster → spent resources but path is clear
- Snuck past → saved resources but monster still there

**Consequences make players feel their decisions matter.** They are the essence of roleplaying.

---

## The Choice Equation

Players naturally throw their best skills at problems. **This is smart, logical, and human.** GMs who complain about it are wrong.

### How Players Solve Problems (In Order)

1. Can we use one of our **best strengths** directly?
2. Can we find a **novel way** to apply a strength that doesn't obviously fit?
3. Can we fall back on a **lesser skill**?
4. If stuck with a **weakness**, how do we **minimize** it? (tools, help, etc.)

**This IS how open-ended problem-solving works.** The GM's job is to **mess with the equation**.

### How to Mess with the Equation

Make options **mechanically different** or give them **different consequences**:

- Lock DC much higher than break DC? (Complicated rusty lock on a flimsy door)
- Picking the lock is slow and the room is filling with water?
- Picking the lock is harder but the monster will hear you breaking in?
- Breaking through is the only way to maintain surprise? Or lose it?

**When options only differ in numbers, it's a pure math problem.** When mechanics or consequences differ, players actually think.

---

## The This, That, or Other Approach

Design 2–4 predetermined solutions per obstacle. "Other" is always valid.

You do NOT give players a list. Unless you deliberately telegraph solutions, players may never see the list. **The list is for you.**

### Benefits

1. **Preparedness** — adjudicate likely approaches instantly
2. **Differentiation** — forces meaningfully different approaches
3. **Consequences** — time to think about how approaches ripple forward
4. **Anti-bottleneck** — one poor approach won't accidentally fail the adventure (unless planned)
5. **Difficulty tuning** — adjust non-combat encounters beyond just changing numbers
6. **Player learning** — once players learn approaches have different consequences, they stop treating obstacles as pure math

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Predesign at least a few solutions** for each obstacle — it's part of designing the problem
- Don't design every possible solution; don't reject unexpected player solutions either
- **Consequences matter more than outcomes** — what's interesting is what happens AFTER
- The GM's job is to **mess with the choice equation** by making approaches differ in mechanics and consequences
- "Other" is always valid — predesigned solutions are for GM preparedness, not player constraint
