# Everything I Need to Know, I Learned on Zebes

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/everything-i-need-to-know-i-learned-on-planet-zebes/)
**Date:** May 20, 2014
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

Eleven tips for exploration and discovery in RPGs, drawn from the design of *Super Metroid*.

---

## 1. People Don't Want to Explore, They Want to Discover

Most people are naturally curious and will put aside their goals temporarily to satisfy their curiosity. But people don't want to explore — they want to **discover.** When we explore, we hope to find something new, unknown, unexpected. If people don't make discoveries, eventually they stop exploring.

## 2. Discovery Feels Good and It's Good For You

Discovery is a reward that can be both **intrinsic** (warm fuzzy feeling — pushing back the darkness, feeling more powerful) and **extrinsic** (actually useful — vital information, alternate approaches, maps, gold, magical items).

DMs often undervalue the "interesting but not useful" and load up discoveries with "useful." Good DMs mix it up:

- **Useful but not interesting**
- **Interesting but not useful**
- **Interesting AND useful**

## 3. Get Them Addicted to Exploration

If you want someone to do something over and over, **don't pay them off every time.** Rewards must be random and unpredictable — like gambling, loot grinding, or booster packs. This is addictive for two reasons:

1. **Fear of missing out** — If rewards are guaranteed, it's easy to wait. Random payoffs make you afraid to pass up opportunities because *this might be the one.*
2. **Acclimation** — Humans get tired of consistent stimuli. One jelly donut is a treat; three every day becomes ignorable.

Discoveries need to be far more rare than opportunities to explore. Mix it up: sometimes nothing, sometimes interesting-but-not-useful, sometimes useful-but-not-interesting, sometimes the totally awesome thing.

> You'll know when you hit the sweet spot because, one day, the party will be debating whether to put their urgent, time-sensitive quest on hold "for just a little while" to satisfy their curiosity, and they will be having a hard time making the decision. That's exploration addiction.

## 4. Empowerment Is Better Than Improvement

**Vocabulary** = all the things you can do, all the actions you can take. When encountering an obstacle, players consider their short list of practical options first.

The best discoveries **add words to the vocabulary** — new tools or new opportunities. This is why people are down on +1 swords and +1 armor. Those don't add new tools or opportunities. A ring of jumping is less exciting than boots of levitation, even if they both get PCs over the same pit, because one adds new vocabulary.

Opportunities need not be new abilities. A secret door or a key to an interesting door are great discoveries because they **promise new opportunities** — like winning free scratch-off tickets.

Always err on the side of adding new tools and new opportunities for the big stuff.

## 5. Always Show the Players the Lock Before They Find the Key

If you've got an obstacle that requires something special to get past it, make sure the players encounter the obstacle BEFORE they find the solution.

**Why:** Unsolved problems linger in the brain. The brain keeps them available and keeps working on them. So when you discover a solution, your brain makes the connection readily. But if you find a weird key first, it doesn't stay fresh — it isn't connected to a goal.

**Exception:** For tools that just make problems *easier* (not required to solve them), it's okay to find them before the problem.

## 6. Be Repetitive and Give an Orgy of Evidence

If you want players to notice something and figure something out, **don't be subtle.** Everything the GM sees in their head has to be translated into words, understood by the players, and translated back into an image. A lot more information gets lost in translation than most DMs think.

- In *Super Metroid*, you see 4 spots where you need more jumping before finding the Hi-Jump Boots
- Multiple clues in the same spot = an **orgy of evidence**
- You can never rely on players picking up on just one clue unless it's a really powerful clue

> Repeat yourself. Don't just have one wall of fire — have several. They won't notice the repetition, but they will feel smart and excited for figuring it out.

## 7. Never Make the Players Guess Whether They Are Making Progress

If you want players to continue down the right path, especially if they have to expend resources or take risks, **they have to know they are making progress.**

> If they don't know they are making progress, they will assume they are not. Then they will back off and try a different strategy instead.

Their attacks need to have visible effects. Their flowery words need to visibly sway the king. Show progress.

## 8. Let the Players Get Comfortable, Then Take Away Their Comfort

**Pacing** is how tension, excitement, and engagement vary over time. Amateurs assume you want to keep ratcheting up tension until the climax. But that just exhausts your audience. Pacing rises and falls.

> Adventures should be a roller coaster, not a ski-lift. Easy and hard, fast and slow, familiar and strange, calm and noisy.

Variables to work with: encounter type, familiarity with the situation, difficulty level. Vary them up.

## 9. Don't Let the Players' Desires Align All the Time

In *Super Metroid*, one room has narrow platforms over spiked pits with flying glowbugs. Kill the enemies? The room goes dark. Keep them alive? You have to dodge while jumping. **There's no right answer — that makes it a choice, not a puzzle.**

> Choices have no correct answers. A real moral choice is choosing between what is just and what is right, or choosing which evil you are more willing to live with.

When setting up encounters, **play goals or desires against one another.** Make them risk more for bigger prizes. Make the path through the room easier than the path to the treasure.

## 10. It's Okay to Be Optional

Exploration has to be a choice. It has to offer rewards. The downside for not exploring is not discovering. That means **exploration requires optional things.**

Hide some stuff away. Make it hard to find. Make it optional — not required to proceed:
- The secret backstory of a terrible NPC
- A powerful magical relic that is useful but not required
- A hidden area that is really different from the rest

Yes, some of your work will never exist in your world. But no one ever said being a DM is easy.

## 11. Use the Players' Natural Habits to Help Them... and Hinder Them

*Super Metroid* trains you to notice certain environmental details, then uses those habits to lead you to "optional" things that almost everyone finds anyway. It creates a reward for paying attention, reinforces the habit, and makes you feel clever.

### Common Player Tendencies

- Players tend to **explore open pathways before opening doors** (especially on a battle grid)
- In **goal-pursuing mode**, players follow any obvious path through a site, even if unconnected to their goal
- In **exploration mode**, players take side passages first and follow obvious paths last — they want to clear everything before they progress

### How to Use This

- Put optional things at the end of a hall behind an open doorway (not behind a closed door) to funnel players to them
- If players are too focused on a goal, make the path impossible to follow to force them into exploration mode
- Observe your players, watch their habits, then use those habits to design better sites and adventures

> This is true of non-site-based adventures too. Players on a murder investigation tend to follow any clear path of clues even if it doesn't obviously tie to the murder.
