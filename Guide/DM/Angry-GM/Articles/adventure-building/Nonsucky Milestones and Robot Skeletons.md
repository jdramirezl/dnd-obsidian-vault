# Nonsucky Milestones and Robot Skeletons: An Angry Table Tale

## Key Advice

Game mechanics are important, but the GM's job is to **hide the mechanical skeleton** beneath a narrative skin. Players need to know rules exist and are fair, but they shouldn't see the gears turning. When forced to use a blunt tool like Milestone Advancement, dress it up so well that players forget they're on rails — connect mechanical progression to in-world consequences of their actions.

## Summary

Angry uses two examples from his own games to illustrate the principle that mechanics should be felt but not seen — like Disney animatronics where you enjoy the ride without seeing the robot exoskeletons.

**The Tension Pool Example (Destroying Resources):** Instead of telling players "the dice say you lose a potion," Angry narrates an NPC's mischievous fox killing a farmer's goose, leading to a sheriff shaking down the NPC for compensation. The players face a choice: pay the fine or let the NPC hand over useful medical herbs. Mechanically it's a random supply-destruction event; narratively it's a story beat with a decision.

**The Milestone Advancement Example (Granting Resources):** Running a short-shot adventure path in D&D 3.5, Angry can't fit enough XP and treasure into short adventures. Milestone Advancement is unavoidable, but he refuses to just say "you level up because I said so." His solution:

1. **Hide the best gear in optional exploration** — reward risk-taking and curiosity with top-tier upgrades.
2. **Add a scoring element to each adventure** — something players can see and intuit (e.g., keep NPCs alive, protect cargo).
3. **Connect scoring to between-adventure progression** — more surviving sailors = more cargo delivered = more payment/supplies/crafting materials. Saving the scholar = her secret order owes the party magical items.
4. **Barely explain any of it** — tell players advancement is tied to success, but don't reveal the formula.

The prologue adventure "Waylaid" demonstrates this: shipwrecked on a cursed beach with skeleton attacks, the party must protect sailors and cargo while deciding whether to explore optional points of interest or evacuate quickly. Every choice has mechanical consequences wrapped in narrative logic.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Keep the engine under the hood** — players should know rules exist and are fair, but shouldn't see the mechanical scaffolding behind narrative events.
2. **When destroying player resources** (random complications, attrition), wrap the mechanical effect in a narrative event with a choice — never just announce "you lose X."
3. **When granting player resources** (milestone levels, equipment upgrades), tie progression to in-world consequences of player actions — never just announce "you gain X."
4. **Add a visible scoring element** to adventures — something players can intuit without being told (protect NPCs, preserve cargo, complete objectives).
5. **Connect scoring to progression** through narrative logic — better performance → more in-world rewards that happen to equal the mechanical progression you need.
6. **Hide optional treasure in exploration** — the best upgrades come from risk-taking and curiosity, not from the baseline progression track.
7. **Don't over-explain** — tell players the system exists, but let them discover how it works through play.

> [!tip] The Animatronic Principle
> Good RPGs are like Disney animatronic rides. The riders know it's a trick — they know Mickey Mouse is gears and servos. But they play along. If the rubber skin wears away and you can see the Terminator-like exoskeleton beneath, the magic is lost. Your job as GM is to keep the skin on the robots while making sure the riders know the ride is safe.
