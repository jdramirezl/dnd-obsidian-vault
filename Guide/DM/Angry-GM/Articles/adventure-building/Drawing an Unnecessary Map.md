# Drawing an Unnecessary Map of Nothing At All

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/drawing-an-unnecessary-map/)
**Date:** February 27, 2019
**Series:** A Very Angry Campaign

---

## Do You Even Need a Map

When a GM sits down to design the setting for their upcoming campaign, the first thing they do is start drawing a map. Every time. Angry almost always starts by making a map too — but stresses it's totally unnecessary.

**Mapsturbation** — all the crap a GM doesn't actually have to do to make a setting that he does anyway because he describes himself as a "world building" GM. Mapsturbation is totally fine as long as you do it alone and don't make anyone watch. Because no one gives a crap about your exposition.

The things you need to know about the setting to run a game in it almost never appear on the map.

## What a Setting Actually Does

A setting provides three things:

1. **Grounding** — Establishes a shared reality between GM and players. Specify the basic nature of the setting and then establish the exceptions. This can be done in a sentence: "like the D&D in the rulebooks except for this one detail."

2. **Backstory** — Provides context for the campaign. Setting backstory that begins with the creation of the universe is usually a totally pointless waste — unless the campaign is about resolving a conflict that started at the beginning of time.

3. **Adventure Space** — Provides a space for adventures and interconnected game elements (cities, NPCs, locations, dungeons) that GMs can grab and use. Because they are part of the setting, they are interconnected, saving the GM time.

**Optional bonus:** The setting also provides a way to anchor the players' characters to the world. Players feel like more of a part of the world if they can say "my character comes from this town or city or kingdom."

> It is a myth — a damned, dirty lie — that giving the players ownership of a corner of the world, giving them narrative control, intrinsically increases immersion any more than anything else does.

## How Much Do You Actually Need?

- **Grounding:** One sentence of exceptions from the standard fantasy baseline
- **Backstory:** Only what the campaign deals with. "A long time ago, there were lots of ancient kingdoms and empires. They all fell into ruin and left the landscape dotted with ancient adventuring sites filled with gold and magical treasure. Now get out there and have fun."
- **Game elements:** Make them up as you go. Need a city? Invent a city. Need a kingdom? Invent it. Don't sweat details until you need them.

Building a setting takes as much work as it needs. It can be a huge undertaking, or it can take ten minutes and involve three sentences. The amount you actually need is usually pretty close to the "ten minutes and three sentences" mark.

## Maps Are Optional But Valuable

A map is just a visual representation of all that crap — a way of organizing the geographical relationships between game elements and depicting the backstory and basic nature of the setting.

Even if you're designing a complex setting, you don't NEED a map. You just need grounding, important backstory elements, and a list of game elements.

**But maps make the game better.** Maps make the world more real, especially to the players. Unfurling a map in front of the players and pointing out where characters came from, where the adventure starts, and naming enticing locations — that makes the world real and incites curiosity.

> Name one of your forests on your map Ghostwood. For some reason, that particular name always gets someone to say "man, I want to go THERE!"

**Descriptive names** excite people because they say something about the place. Ghostwood, Bleak Hills, Serpents Teeth Mountains, and Sunderlands are far more exciting than Valenar and Cormanthyr and Va'a'shal'das't'ar'naeth'ven.

**You don't need a map. But if you can draw a map, draw a map.**

## Mapping the Setting: A Practical Example

Angry's Pathfinder campaign had no plan — just a vague premise: "you all came to the town of Redbridge for the Spring Festival where something will happen." No long-term plan, no backstory.

### What He Needed From the Map

- A potential conflict to fall back on: "escalating military conflict between two nations and who will stand up for the people who live on the border"
- The setting: The Western Kingdoms during The Dark Age — a collection of small, feudal kingdoms, sparsely populated with a lot of frontier, remnants of the old Zethinian Empire

### A Hometown for Every Archetype

The most important thing: the ability to tell each PC where they were from based on their background and character goals. The trick — **a hometown for every archetype:**

- Look through character generation options (races, classes, backgrounds)
- Make sure each combination has at least one place they might be from
- Don't make it obvious

This produced:
- **Mountains of the dwarves** and **forested valleys of the elves** (separated geographically)
- **Two feudal New Gods kingdoms** (Caelon and Bandery) — one vanilla medieval, one militaristic
- **One old, frontier, pagan kingdom** (Durst) — isolated on a peninsula, swampy, hard to get to
- **The Sunderlands** — wild plains, steppe lands, ruins for barbarians, nomads, and orcs
- **The Free City of Vyan** — merchant princes, guilds, mobsters (for noble/aristocrat types)
- **Unexplored trackless wilderness** — for hermits, rangers, fugitives, wizards, lone-wolf types

### Actually Drawing It

- Surrounded the Western Kingdoms by water for isolation
- Southern border with Zethinia: rugged mountains with a defensible pass
- Northeast: dwarf mountains. Southern mountains: elf valley
- East: open border with the Sunderlands
- Central land split between Bandery and Caelon
- Durst isolated on a peninsula
- Northern third: unclaimed land (forests, hills, mountains, untamed island)
- Vyan crammed on the southern shore, isolated by mountains and wilderness
- Redbridge placed dead center, inside Caelon but near the Bandery border
- Lots of empty space to look sparse

Started with a blobby sketch map, moved things around, then drew a nice map over it on a light table.
