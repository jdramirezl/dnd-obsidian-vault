# How to Build F$&%ing Awesome Encounters!

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-build-awesome-encounters/)
**Date:** July 23, 2013
**Series:** Getting the Most Out of Your Skill System (Part 4)

---

*This is the fourth part in the ongoing series: Getting the Most Out of Your Skill System. In the last part (Four Things You've Never Heard of That Make Encounters Not Suck), I told you all about four things that make encounters not suck. This article is where I actually put the pieces together and build an encounter the Angry way. Which is the AWESOME way.*

## The Seed: "From Tiny Acorns…"

The encounter seed is simply the starting point. The idea. The first thing in your head that starts off the whole process of building and running an encounter. A seed could be anything at all: a specific monster (a dragon), a specific location (inside an active volcano), a dramatic question that needs answering (can the PCs learn the identity of the assassin), a specific adventure purpose (an encounter to soften the party up before they get to the dragon), or just a cool scene or set piece you want to build an encounter around.

But it is important to remember that **a seed is not an encounter**. An encounter needs to be nurtured and cultivated. The only difference between preparing an encounter ahead of time and improvising an encounter is the amount of time you have to prepare everything you need to run the encounter. You still have to create a damn encounter.

## The Dramatic Question

Once you've planted the seed, you need to fertilize it. You can't run an encounter without a dramatic question and you can't build one without it either. The dramatic question is pretty easy to come up with. It might even be your seed. If not, you need to ask yourself what the PCs are trying to accomplish in the encounter. Why should they care? What do they get out of the encounter?

Remember that freedom is everything in an RPG. You don't want the dramatic question to assume anything about how the PCs are going to resolve the question. Remember the difference between "can the PCs continue their journey" and "can the PCs survive the spider attack" and "can the PCs kill the spiders?"

The caveat: it is okay for the dramatic question to assume things about how the PCs are going to resolve the question if you want to limit the PCs. Sometimes, the hallway only goes in two directions. Sometimes, the orcs really are just going to try to kill the PCs. It is fine. There are many different ways to give the PCs freedom and as long as the PCs are mostly free most of the time, the game can handle the occasional bottleneck.

**Figure out what the dramatic question is. Write it big at the top of your encounter building page.** Read it over and ask yourself if it assumes anything about how the PCs will resolve the scene. If you realize it does, ask yourself if that is okay.

## The Hook

An encounter's hook presents the PCs with a dramatic question that needs answering, it gives them a reason to care about answering the question, and then it calls them to act. Most DMs figure out the "presenting the PCs with a dramatic question" thing intuitively. Many even figure out the "reason to care" business. But many DMs screw up the **call to action**.

A good call to action does a couple of things:
1. It shows the players one or two obvious paths to their goal, or at least suggests some
2. It creates **exigency** — a need to act, a sense of urgency

By changing the hook, you add or remove assumptions from the dramatic question. A hook that starts with spiders attacking assumes a fight is imminent. A hook that starts with spiders milling about on the ceiling offers opportunities around a fight.

It is important to note that sometimes the hook is dependent on the actions of the PCs or the fall of the dice. For example, the spider cave could have up to three hooks: the heroes surprise the spiders, the spiders surprise the heroes, or neither side surprises the other. Each one must pose the dramatic question and call the heroes to action properly.

## The Primary Source of Conflict

The primary source of conflict is the most obvious source of conflict, the first one the PCs will see, and the one they will be most focused on resolving somehow. The primary source of conflict is usually visible in the hook. The call to action in the hook is usually a call to resolve the primary conflict.

Keep in mind that we haven't done any stats or mechanics yet and that is by design. **Game mechanics and statistics are the last thing you want to worry about.** Stats and mechanics are not encounters. A stat block, by itself, is not a source of conflict because a stat block has no reason to oppose the heroes. At this point, we just want to identify the primary source of conflict: what is it and why is it opposing the PCs.

## The Orc and the Pie: Your Encounter is Complete

Technically, putting aside all of the mechanics and statistics, your encounter might be finished now. You have everything you absolutely need to create a good dramatic scene: **dramatic question, hook, source of conflict.**

Take a look at Monte Cook's *World's Shortest Adventure*: There is a pie in the room but there is an orc in the way. It asks a dramatic question (can the PCs obtain the pie), poses that question with the hook (there is a pie and an orc), and presents a source of conflict (the orc wants to protect his pie).

But **complete is not the same as good**. If you follow only the steps above, you will have a complete encounter. But I want greatness. That means worrying about how the encounter is going to end, thinking about structure elements, and keeping an eye on decision points.

## How the Hell Will You Know When the Encounter is Over?

The encounter ends when the dramatic question has been answered. But what will that look like?

Two reasons to worry about the ending:

1. **So you can make sure you are okay with the likely outcomes.** Do you like those possibilities? Can you handle them? If the only possible failure is that the PCs end up dead, is that okay?

2. **So you can make sure you will know when the encounter is over.** You never want an encounter to overstay its welcome. This is where **structure elements** enter the picture — things that need some sort of mechanic or method of keeping score. "I need something to tell me when the spiders are driven off."

Structure elements allow you to decide when the players have failed and remind you to tell the players so and stop the encounter. They avoid **"The Encounter that Wouldn't Die"** — where players just keep repeating the same things over and over or keep "trying one more thing" even though the encounter was robbed of all excitement an hour ago.

## Do You Have Enough Decision Points?

The more complex an encounter, the more decision points it needs. When an encounter runs out of decision points, it becomes boring. **Nothing will destroy an encounter as efficiently as not having enough decision points.**

Decision points are spots where the players are asked to choose how to resolve the encounter. Every time you ask the player "what do you do," you have a decision point. However, if there is only one useful thing to do (or the player THINKS there is only one useful thing to do), **it doesn't count as a decision point.** "The lock is almost picked, you'll need to pick it just a little more, what do you do?" THAT IS NOT A DECISION! THAT IS A DM ASKING A PLAYER TO "PRESS X TO CONTINUE!"

Decision points come in **two general flavors**:
1. **Choosing which conflict to resolve** (which enemy to target, whether to try the gate or scale the wall) — "Mazes"
2. **Choosing how to resolve a particular conflict** (what spell to use, whether to bribe/fight/sneak past the guard) — "Obstacle Courses"

### How Many Decision Points Are Enough?

Once an encounter has run out of decision points, it should be finished in two or three dice rolls or people will get bored. If you have only one decision point, you shouldn't expect each player to roll dice more than two or three times before the encounter is over.

The more decision points you have, the bigger and grander and more complicated the encounter feels. Non-combat, non-interaction encounters tend to move faster and have fewer decisions than combats or interactions, but they still feel pretty substantial as long as they are run well. **A well run encounter with between five and ten decision points is a pretty grand and weighty scene.**

## Upping the Decision Point Ante

Most decision points are created by sources of conflict, so you can either add new sources of conflict or have your existing sources of conflict do things to complicate the situation.

But not all conflicts are created equally. Some sources of conflict don't really involve decisions. A locked steel gate where the only option is to pick the lock hasn't added a decision point — just an obstacle. Adding options to an existing source of conflict doesn't actually add decision points either.

Most decision points should be focused on **actions the heroes can take to resolve a conflict or pursue the dramatic question**. Make sure that at least some of the decisions are about how they can bring about victory, not just about how to avoid defeat.

There is a special kind of decision point: the **dilemma**. A dilemma occurs when a decision requires a hero to choose between multiple goals — usually a personal goal and the goal represented by the dramatic question.

Once you have decided that you need to add decision points, things get chaotic. A really well-built, complex NCNI encounter can actually be mapped like a dungeon (or a flowchart).

**Remember: if you want the players to know they have an option, you have to tell them.** Players are stupid and confused by subtlety. A secret door should always be the THIRD option, never the SECOND when you're trying to create decision points.

## Structure and Mechanics

Once you have the encounter designed, you can fill in the skill checks, difficulty numbers, stat blocks, and traps. But the key insight is:

**Structure elements** are ways to keep score so you know when it is time to bring your encounter to a conclusion. **Unique rules** are fiddly little bits that you need to pull off a certain conflict or decision point.

Guidelines for structure elements and unique rules:
- Keep them **as small as possible**
- Keep them **out of the player's way** as much as possible
- They are tools **for you, the DM**, to determine what happens when the players do things
- **Never explain the structure elements** and how they work except in the most rudimentary way
- **Never allow the players to play the structure element** — make them play the situation
- Visual aids help, but they are only aids
- If an element is going to be used again and again, devote more effort — but be wary of reusing them; they work best when tailored to the situation

## The Chase: A Complete Example

**Hook:** The heroes witness an assassin shoot down an important dignitary on the streets of Market Town. The assassin discards his crossbow and darts into a crowded market. Can the heroes catch the assassin before he disappears?

**Structure:** Measure the distance from the assassin to the heroes in spaces. The heroes begin five spaces behind. If no hero is within seven spaces, the assassin escapes. The chase plays out in ten rounds through different neighborhoods.

**The Rooftops:** Heroes can climb up (losing a space) and then gain a space each round with a successful skill check. Failure means falling, taking damage, and losing a space.

### Round-by-Round Breakdown

1. **The Market** — Crowded market, heroes can mitigate the crowd or climb to rooftops
2. **The Market (continued)** — City knowledge check to predict the assassin's route toward Crimetown Bridge; option to take a shortcut
3. **The Side Street** — Straight run, option to sprint (gain a space on success, lose one on bad failure)
4. **The Horse Street** — Assassin slashes a horse, creating a panicked animal charging at heroes; option to calm the horse or ride it
5. **The Winding Street** — Pause in the action, no major obstacles; second shortcut check
6. **The Crimetown Bridge** — Shortcut hero can attempt a tackle; assassin shoves someone into the river; rooftop heroes must descend; option to sprint
7. **The Crimetown Streets** — If no one is within three spaces, assassin tries to hide; option to sprint or climb rooftops again
8. **The Crimetown Streets (continued)** — More sprinting opportunities
9. **The Back Alleys** — If no hero is within two spaces (or four on rooftops), assassin disappears; narrow streets, no horses
10. **The Crimetown Market** — Final crowded market; heroes can mitigate the crowd

**End:** The assassin ducks into the Scumm and Villainy Bar and Grill. Any hero within two spaces knows where he went. The assassin disappears into a secret room and the owner covers for him. The heroes will have to ransack the place or question the owner — but that's a different encounter.

### Key Design Insights from The Chase

- **Multiple approaches**: chasing on foot, rooftop stalking, taking shortcuts, ranged attacks
- **Moral dilemmas**: save the panicked horse's victims or keep chasing? Save the drowning person or keep pursuing?
- **Pacing**: alternating between high-action decision rounds and brief narrative pauses
- **Multiple endpoints**: catch the assassin, follow him to the tavern, narrow down his neighborhood — different degrees of success
- **The structure element (the space tracker) is for the GM** — players see the visual aid but play the situation, not the mechanic
