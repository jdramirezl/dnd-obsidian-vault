# Scenario Design on a Need to Know Basis

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/scenario-design-on-a-need-to-know-basis/)
**Date:** July 11, 2025
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Overview

What do the players need to know about the scenario, and how should they know it? Using the "End the Goblins" example, Angry breaks down three types of player information — Knowledge, Deduction, and Intuition — and explains why restricting player information to the minimum necessary creates better gameplay.

---

## Core Principle: Players Should Know As Little As Possible

Players should only know what they **absolutely need** to play and potentially win. Everything beyond that degrades the experience.

### Why Restrict Information?

1. **Preserve immersion:** Abstractions remind players they're playing a game
2. **Maintain uncertainty:** Tension is a product of uncertainty
3. **Create mental challenge:** Less information forces more reasoning and deduction
4. **Improve retention:** People remember information better when they figure it out
5. **Increase attention:** People are more attentive when slightly lost or confused

---

## What Players Absolutely Need to Know

Two non-negotiable requirements:

1. **"What are we trying to do?"** — The current goal
2. **"How are we doing right now?"** — Whether they're making progress

Beyond these, players need enough information to **take reasonable actions** with a **reasonable chance** of victory. If they can't figure out what to do, they don't know enough.

---

## Three Types of Information

### Knowledge

- Clear, concise, unambiguous, explicit information players *receive*
- Most reliable but **least impactful** and most forgettable
- Requires nothing from the players
- **Use for:** The scenario's goal — always explicit

### Deduction

- Requires players to **reason** from other information
- Involves **player skill and luck** — introduces uncertainty
- Rewarding — earns a squirt of happy brain chemicals
- More impactful than Knowledge, but quick/cheap impact
- **Use for:** Bridging mechanics and challenges; figuring out courses of action

### Intuition

- Stuff players *feel* but may struggle to articulate — **emotional knowledge**
- Most powerful, useful, and best kind of information
- Humans make most choices based on emotional knowledge
- Most impactful and memorable, but subjective and unreliable
- Depends on designer's chops and GM's presentation
- **Use for:** Gameplay dynamics like momentum, pacing, overall game feel

---

## The Information Scale

| Quality | Knowledge | Deduction | Intuition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reliability** | High | Medium | Low |
| **Impact** | Low | Medium | High |
| **Memorability** | Low | Medium | High |
| **Player Skill** | None | High | Low (designer skill matters) |

**The sweet spot for player skill** peaks where Deduction meets Intuition.

- **Gamefeel is your bread and butter** — players should *feel* the most important elements
- If players absolutely must have information, **spoonfeed it as Knowledge**
- Don't make players deduce the abstractions — keep them focused on actions and effects in the fictional world

## Applied Example: "End the Goblins"

### Goal (Knowledge)

"There's goblins in that waterfall cave and they need to not be there anymore." Explicitly revealed.

### Why Players Don't Need to Know the Structure

Players come pre-programmed with assumptions:
- **"Go deep"** — goals are far from the entrance
- **"Cover ground"** — visit new areas before revisiting old ones

Given three linear gauntlets from the same entrance, players will naturally clear each path to the end. The design **nudges players toward victory on an Intuitive level**.

### Communicating Progress

The dungeon restocks daily (creating inertia), but players still need visible progress:

- **Shaman's Blessing:** Goblins have an obvious divine buff. When the shaman dies, it disappears. Thematically linked to the shaman's powers so players can Deduce the connection.
- **Kennelmaster's Wolves:** When he dies, wolves turn on goblins. Next visit shows injured wolves eating dead goblins — impossible to miss.
- **Chieftain's Authority:** When he dies, factional infighting. Signs of goblin-on-goblin violence. Maybe a surrendering goblin explains goblin politics.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Players should know only what they need** to play and potentially win
2. **The goal must be explicit Knowledge**
3. **Progress must be visible** — even when creating feelings of inertia
4. **Design so reasonable players can navigate without explicit instructions**
5. **Use the right information type for the right purpose**
6. **The most important elements should be felt, not known**
7. **Think about information delivery during pre-design**
