# Scenes: The LEGO Bricks of Adventure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/scenes-the-lego-bricks-of-adventure/)
**Date:** June 3, 2015
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## Overview

Scenes are the fundamental building blocks of adventures. An adventure has a Motivation (beginning), Resolution (ending), and Structure (how bits connect). Without scenes, there's no game to play. This article defines scenes, categorizes the types, and explains how to use them effectively.

---

## What Is a Scene?

> **A scene is a sequence of continuous, related actions.**

Scenes are where the game actually happens — where players make choices. The GM sets the scene, players act, then the GM transitions to the next scene.

### Key Properties

- **Continuous:** Actions flow until the situation resolves
- **Related:** All actions connect to the same situation
- **Variable duration:** Can span minutes, hours, or days of game time
- **Not the same as locations:** An empty room with nothing to do is NOT a scene

### Scenes vs. Locations

- Empty rooms aren't scenes — include them in transitions: *"You wander through an empty chamber and continue out the other side."*
- Hallway intersections ARE scenes (short ones) — every choice point is a scene
- A dungeon map is a location map, not a scene map

---

## Every Scene Must Have a Purpose

Like LEGO bricks, every scene contributes something:

- **Structural:** Workhorse pieces holding the adventure together
- **Aesthetic:** Must be there because the adventure demands them thematically
- **Flourish:** Decorative — wouldn't be missed but add flavor
- **Cool:** Exciting, memorable moments that make the game worth playing

> **Never say "move the story forward."** This phrase is meaningless. Every scene already happens in the context of the adventure. Scenes serve specific, identifiable purposes.

---

## Scene Types

### Exposition Scenes
- Exist to **impart information** to players
- Most adventures begin with one (presenting the main goal)
- Quickest, most efficient, and most boring way to deliver information
- Make them interactive: give info upfront, let players ask questions
- Can reveal backstory, context, motives, future hooks, foreshadowing
- **Low agency**

### Recap, Planning, and Character Scenes
- **Player-driven**, not GM-driven
- Recap: going over what they know
- Planning: discussing what to do next
- Character: interacting with each other in character
- **GM role:** Back off. Monitor for when the scene peters out, then transition.
- Players are bad at knowing when these end — **pacing is your job**

### Exploration Scenes
- Players **freely interact with the world** to discover information
- Information isn't given or guaranteed — it's there to find
- **Most important and most overlooked** scene type
- Not encounters — no conflict
- Controversial: shouldn't have die rolls (no conflict = no dice)

### Decision Scenes
- Short scenes where players **choose what to do next**
- Often built into other scenes
- Bland by themselves — use sparingly alone

### Discovery Scenes
- Give players **something valuable** — resource, ally, treasure, boon
- Bland by themselves — best combined with other scene types

### Preparation (Montage) Scenes
- Players **get something done** with decisions along the way (crafting, research, shopping)
- No conflict, but requires actions and choices
- Bland by themselves — best combined

### Encounter Scenes
- **The scene that makes the game worth playing**
- Goal locked behind **one or more sources of conflict**
- Can be combat, interaction, obstacle — resolution type doesn't define the scene

---

## Scenes Are Defined by Purpose, Not Resolution Method

Don't think "combat," "talk," "obstacle." Think:

- Impart information → Exposition
- Ask for a decision → Decision
- Get something done → Preparation
- Present a challenge → Encounter
- Let players discover → Exploration

---

## The Real Trick: Smoosh Scenes Together

> **Figure out all the scenes you need, then smoosh them into the fewest possible scenes.**

- Let boring workhorse scenes absorb elements from interesting scenes
- Don't eliminate all workhorse scenes (pacing needs them)
- Economizing makes scenes punchy and interesting

**Example:** Need a decision scene (left or right?) AND exposition about a god? Put an icon of the god at the intersection. Now it's decision + exploration.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Scenes are sequences of continuous, related actions** — not locations
2. **Every scene must serve a purpose**
3. **Empty rooms aren't scenes** — include them in transitions
4. **Nine scene types:** Exposition, Recap, Planning, Character, Exploration, Decision, Discovery, Preparation, Encounter
5. **Define scenes by purpose, not resolution method**
6. **Smoosh scenes together** for efficiency and punch
7. **Agency matters** — consider how much control players have
8. **Pacing is your job** — know when to transition
