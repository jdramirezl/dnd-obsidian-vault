# The Shape of Adventure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-shape-of-adventure/)
**Date:** November 11, 2015
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## Recap: Adventure Components

An adventure is a container for scenes:
- Begins with a **motivation** (reason for PCs and players to engage)
- Ends with a **resolution** (successful and unsuccessful outcomes)
- In between: scenes connected by **structure**

Once motivation and resolution are set, adventure building = **building scenes and joining them up**. But you need a plan for the overall shape before you start.

---

## The Shape of the Adventure

Think of your adventure like a flowchart or dungeon map. Every adventure has a **shape** that guides and constrains its structure — like how a tower dungeon constrains each floor to roughly the same footprint with stairs that line up.

There are **three basic shapes**: Linear, Branching, and Open.

These are NOT a spectrum between "railroad" and "sandbox." Branching is not a hybrid — it's a completely different creature. Each requires different design approaches.

---

## There Is No "Best" Structure

There isn't one. Shut up.

---

## Different Types of Freedom

The conventional wisdom that linear = constrained = bad is wrong. Freedom exists at **every level of play**:

| Level | Constrained | Free |
|-------|------------|------|
| **Action** | "Roll a saving throw" | "What do you do?" |
| **Encounter** | "Roll initiative" | "How do you deal with the orc?" |
| **Adventure** | "The dragon must be slain" | "The dragon will destroy the village, can you help?" |
| **Campaign** | Fixed storyline | Player-driven direction |

Any RPG is a mix of constrained and free choices. **The key is that player choices must have an impact on what happens.** When they don't, that's a railroad.

### Minimum Freedom Requirement

An adventure must have **at least two possible resolutions**. Usually success and failure, but not necessarily. You could have three success outcomes and no failure — as long as outcomes differ based on player choices.

---

## Outcomes vs. Consequences

- **Outcome** = did the player succeed or fail?
- **Consequences** = ripple effects from HOW the player handled the action

**Consequences matter more than outcomes for reflecting player freedom.** Outcomes are often random chance; consequences arise from player choices.

### The Orc on the Road Example

A desperate, starving, exiled orc attacks the party:

| Approach | Consequences |
|----------|-------------|
| Kill the orc | Spent resources (HP, spells) carry into next scene |
| Feed the orc, win him over | Gain an ally or information; lose food supplies |
| Knock him unconscious | Humiliated orc hunts them later, or leads his tribe to the PCs |

### The Dragon and the Village Example

| Approach | Campaign Consequences |
|----------|----------------------|
| Slay the dragon | Village prospers |
| Cut a deal | Dragon may starve the village, or they conquer neighbors together |
| Drive it off | Dragon becomes someone else's problem later |

**Even without failure, different approaches create different consequences = multiple effective resolutions.**

---

## Every Adventure Is Constrained

Constraints are **vital**. The GM needs them. The players need them. You set PCs in motion, point them toward a resolution, and they walk the path. The shape doesn't matter as long as you design it well.

---

## The Three Structures

### Linear Adventures (Obstacle Courses)
Heroes move through a **sequence of scenes in order**. Players don't choose the path. Examples: obstacle courses, timeline-driven adventures.

### Branching Adventures (Labyrinths)
Heroes **choose a path through interconnected scenes**. At each scene, they pick where to go next. Examples: traditional dungeon exploration, many investigations/mysteries.

### Open Adventures
Heroes move **freely from any scene to any other** with no defined connections. All scenes are just *there*. Examples: hex-crawls, some mysteries. Visualize as a gelatinous cube with bits floating in it.

### Key Difference: Branching vs. Open
Branching has **defined connections** between scenes (A leads to B or C). Open has **no connections** — any scene to any scene.

---

## Hybrid Adventures (Most Adventures)

Most adventures are **Frankensteined hybrids** of multiple shapes.

**Example — Mt. Mystical:**
- **Open:** Three dungeons on a map, visitable in any order
- **Dungeon 1:** Linear
- **Dungeon 2:** Branching
- **Dungeon 3:** Requires open-structure scenes to find entrance, then branching inside
- **Mt. Mystical:** Linear series of five challenges

Combining structures lets strong parts cover for weak parts. But you need to understand each individually first.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Every adventure has a **shape** — decide it before building scenes
- Three basic shapes: **Linear**, **Branching**, **Open** — each fundamentally different
- **Freedom** exists at every level of play, not just adventure structure
- **Consequences** matter more than **outcomes** for player agency
- Most real adventures are **hybrids** of multiple structures
- Constraints are vital and necessary — there is no total freedom
