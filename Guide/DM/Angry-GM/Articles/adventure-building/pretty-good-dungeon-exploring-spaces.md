# Let's Build a Pretty Good Dungeon: Exploring Spaces

## Summary

This article defines the concept of **explorable space** — not as a physical area, but as the number of distinct, meaningful options players perceive at any given moment. It's the mental checklist of "what can we do next?" that players maintain as they navigate an adventure. Angry argues that exploration isn't really an activity — it's a motivation (satisfying curiosity). The real activity is **navigation**: making choices about where to go and what to do next. The article establishes that the "path of least resistance" isn't the easiest path, but the path players are most likely to choose based on how attractive the options appear. The designer's job is to make the golden path's next step always be the most attractive option on the players' mental checklist.

## Key Advice

### What Is an Explorable Space?

An explorable space is the list of possibilities players can enumerate when they ask "What now?" It includes:

- Paths visible from the current location
- Every route they didn't choose at previous locations
- Every route that was previously blocked but might now be accessible
- Abstract options like retreating to rest

It is **not** the physical map. It's the players' mental model of their options.

### The Hand Rule

Never present more options than you can count on one hand (preferably with a finger or two to spare):

| Size | Options in Players' Heads | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| Small | ~2 | Focused, satisfying |
| Moderate | ~3 | Comfortable, engaging |
| Large | 4–5 (briefly) | Expansive but manageable |
| Too Many | 6+ | Overwhelming, aimless, paralyzed |

### Options Must Be Differentiated

If players can't distinguish between options, those options are meaningless. Twelve unopened, identical doors is not twelve choices — it's a chore list. Players need ways to tell options apart so they can make deliberate, purposeful decisions rather than random ones.

### Exploration Is Not an Activity

Exploration is a motivation — it's satisfying curiosity. "What's behind that door? What's making that noise? Where does this stream go?" When wandering stops generating new questions, the drive to explore dies. Players opening every identical door in a row aren't exploring — they're desperately searching for something to actually be curious about.

### Navigation, Not Exploration

The real activity is **navigation** — choosing paths through nonlinear spaces. Exploration is one reason players navigate, but not the only one. The entire pathing framework applies equally to mysteries, event-based scenarios, open worlds, and any nonlinear adventure structure.

### The Core Design Loop

1. Draw a map / design a scenario
2. Identify the golden path through it
3. Place the best experience along the golden path
4. Turn players loose inside it
5. Players build their mental "what next" checklist
6. **Make the golden path's next step the most attractive option on that checklist**
7. Ensure no other option leads to a terrible experience

## Actionable Steps

1. **Think in checklists, not maps** — When designing, ask: "At this point, what options will the players think they have?" List them out. That's your explorable space.
2. **Apply the hand rule** — If the list exceeds 5 at any point, you have too many options open. Close some paths, merge some options, or gate some behind discoveries.
3. **Differentiate every option** — No two paths should look identical. Even cosmetic differences (red tunnel vs. gray tunnel, ornate door vs. plain door) give players something to base decisions on.
4. **Seed curiosity** — Each area should raise at least one new question. A sparkly river, a strange sound, a rune-covered door — these give players reasons to navigate deliberately rather than randomly.
5. **Track the explorable space as it evolves** — As players explore, paths open and close. Map out how the checklist changes at key decision points to ensure it stays manageable and the golden path stays attractive.
6. **Test your design from the player's perspective** — Walk through your adventure as if you can only see what the players see. Can you tell the options apart? Do you know why one is more interesting than another?

> [!tip] The Curiosity Engine
> Every room, corridor, and discovery in your dungeon should either answer a question the players already have or raise a new one they'll want to answer. This is the engine that drives purposeful navigation. If a section of your dungeon doesn't do either, it's dead space — players will trudge through it mechanically rather than engage with it. Ask yourself: "What question does this room answer? What new question does it raise?"
