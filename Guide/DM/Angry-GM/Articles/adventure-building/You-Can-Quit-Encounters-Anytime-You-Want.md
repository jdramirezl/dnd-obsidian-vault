# You Can Quit Encounters Anytime You Want

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/you-can-quit-encounters/)
**Date:** May 27, 2020
**Series:** Encounter Building Basics

---

## The Problem: Mechanics Addiction

GMs are preoccupied with designing complex mechanical systems for encounters — especially non-combat encounters. They equate mechanics with depth and can't imagine a compelling encounter without elaborate rules.

D&D already gives you everything you need to run any compelling non-combat encounter. Special mechanics can make things easier, but if you convince yourself you **need** them, you have a dependency problem.

---

## You Don't Need Complex Mechanics: The Cliff Example

A 90-foot cliff descent encounter needs only:
- The height of the cliff
- The steepness of the cliff
- A reason for the players to climb it

That's it. The players, the GM, and the game's rules handle everything else.

### How It Actually Plays

On paper it looks like "just make a couple of die rolls." At the table, it's tense:
- Some characters aren't equipped for climbing — the party must mitigate risks and maximize odds
- Anchor ropes (easier than free climbing, but not automatic)
- *Feather fall* or *levitation* trivializes it — and that's **good** (clever resource use = earned victory)
- Rig a harness from a backpack and rope to lower the wizard
- Cast *guidance* on each climber
- Cleric climbs first with healing spells ready
- Everyone jumps and relies on healing at the bottom
- Follow the gorge around to avoid the climb entirely

All valid. All should work if the GM is worth a damn. **That's the point of a role-playing game** — players faced with an obstacle decide freely how to deal with it.

### Emergent Depth

Depth = the number of different ways a thing could play out depending on choices and resources. You don't need prescribed mechanics to create depth — the game world and its rules already provide it.

## How Overdesigned Mechanics Hurt Your Game

### The Cost of Design Time
- Designing mechanical systems takes hours of creative effort
- Reject any mechanic you can't design in five minutes

### Bias Against Player Agency

Prescribed mechanics create biases you can't see:
- **Bias toward using them:** You nudge players toward your designed actions
- **Bias against alternatives:** You unconsciously decide other actions aren't possible
- **Bias against player creativity:** If a player teleport-tackles the quarry during a chase, you might just move them ahead on the chase tracker instead of recognizing it should end the chase instantly

> "I can't just let the players shut down the encounter with one clever action" — But players figuring out clever ways to use resources to end encounters in one fell swoop **IS** earning a victory. It's the ultimate way to earn the best victory.

The more you've invested in designing mechanics, the more you demand before conceding victory. Deep inside, there's resentment for players who circumvent your brilliant systems.

### Rigid Mechanics Make Rigid GMs
- Rules should exist solely to resolve events whose outcomes are uncertain
- They shouldn't dictate what is or isn't possible

### Rigid Mechanics Make Rigid Players
- Give players a list of three special actions → they'll choose from those three every turn
- Their brains switch from "looking for a solution" to "evaluating existing solutions"
- Creativity takes more brainpower than evaluation — the brain is lazy

### Long-Term Atrophy
- GM adjudication skills atrophy like muscles — use them or lose them
- Players lose creative problem-solving skills
- GMs lament that players "just want to declare skills and roll dice"
- So GMs design MORE complex mechanics → the cycle worsens

## Rules for Designing Encounter Mechanics

If you do design mechanics, follow these rules:
1. **Any mechanic you'd have to explain to the players is probably bad**
2. **Any mechanic you can't keep in your head is probably bad**
3. **Any mechanic that took more than five minutes to come up with is probably bad**

### Pre-Adjudication vs. Prescribed Systems

There's a difference between:
- **Pre-adjudicating:** "Sprinting is likely to come up, so I'll decide in advance it's an average Athletics check with outcomes of: catch target, gain ground, lose ground, or pull a hamstring"
- **Prescribed systems:** Sprint action costs a stamina token, requires Constitution save, moves 3 extra squares on the chase board

The first is just doing your job ahead of time. The second creates all the bias problems above.

---

## What's Actually Missing

Something IS missing from most encounters — but it's not mechanics. It's about **how you run encounters**, not how you design them. Two important skills that affect both encounter design and execution (teased for the follow-up article).

The cliff encounter is perfectly serviceable as a wilderness encounter — fun, tense, potentially deadly. But by itself it's not a climax encounter. What's missing isn't systems and mechanics.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. D&D's existing rules + good GM adjudication = everything you need for any non-combat encounter
2. Complex mechanics ≠ depth. Emergent depth from player creativity > prescribed mechanical depth
3. Overdesigned mechanics bias GMs against player agency and bias players against creativity
4. The best encounters let players freely choose their approach — including approaches that trivialize the challenge
5. If you can't run an encounter without special rules, your adjudication skills have atrophied
6. Keep mechanics simple, hidden, and quick to design — or skip them entirely
