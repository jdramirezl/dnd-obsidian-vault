# A Plot, B Plot; One, Two, Three Plot

## Key Advice

A long-running campaign needs **3 to 5 plot arcs** juggled simultaneously to keep players engaged without overwhelming them. One arc should be designated as the **Major Arc** — the one whose conclusion ends the campaign — while the others serve as minor arcs or filler. The Major Arc paradoxically gets the **fewest** dedicated adventures to avoid story fatigue.

## Summary

Angry uses Star Trek series (TOS, TNG, DS9) as analogies for different campaign structures: **episodic** (monster-of-the-week, no continuity), **evolving** (one arc per "season"), and **juggled** (multiple arcs weaving in and out). He argues that for campaigns lasting more than a year, juggling multiple arcs is the only structure that sustains player interest.

He introduces key structural tools:
- **Godzilla Arc**: A plot that sleeps in the background, then suddenly erupts for a multi-session burst of activity before going dormant again.
- **Gray Goo Arc**: A plot that starts small and quietly grows until it consumes the entire campaign.
- **Colliding Arcs**: When two plot arcs intersect in a single adventure, creating high-tension, dramatic moments.
- **Non-Arc Arc**: Episodic filler adventures that provide variety and pacing relief.

The Major Arc defines the campaign's endpoint. All minor arcs should be resolved before the Major Arc concludes, or the campaign will feel unfinished.

## Actionable Steps

1. **List 3–5 plot arcs** for your campaign, including a "Non-Arc" filler category as one of them.
2. **Designate one arc as Major** — this is the arc whose resolution ends the campaign. Keep it simple and give it fewer dedicated adventures.
3. **Alternate arcs between sessions** — avoid running two adventures from the same arc back-to-back unless you're doing a deliberate Godzilla burst.
4. **Use PC backstories** to generate arcs — with 4–5 players, each backstory can seed its own arc.
5. **Plan arc conclusions in sequence** — wrap minor arcs first, then the Major Arc last. Consider having one arc's resolution enable another's.
6. **Experiment with Colliding Arcs** — occasionally combine two arcs in one adventure for dramatic tension and to make players feel the stakes.

> [!tip] The Godzilla/Gray Goo Framework
> Label each of your arcs as either a **Godzilla** (erupts periodically then goes dormant) or **Gray Goo** (slowly grows until it dominates). This gives you a built-in pacing plan: Godzilla arcs create exciting bursts, Gray Goo arcs build long-term tension, and filler episodes provide breathing room between them.
