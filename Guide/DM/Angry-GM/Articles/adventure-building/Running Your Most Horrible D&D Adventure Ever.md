# Running Your Most Horrible D&D Adventure Ever

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/running-your-most-horrible-dd-adventure-ever/)
**Date:** October 26, 2017
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

D&D is not made for horror. It's very bad for horror. D&D is built around themes that are the opposite of horror — self-empowerment. No addition of optional "sanity" systems will help. But if you insist on doing it, here's what horror is and how to do it right.

---

## What the Hell is Horror Anyway?

Horror is one of the most well-defined genres in fiction because it's defined entirely by the emotional response it's trying to provoke. Horror is about instilling visceral emotions — the bad ones that make you feel queasy, anxious, uncomfortable, and above all, afraid. But fear comes in different flavors.

---

## Fright

The most basic fear. Sudden, pants-crapping fear. Surprise, startlement, sudden panic. The purview of jump scares.

Fright's power comes from biology — your body shifts from calm to fight-or-flight without the clutch. Your body panics while your brain is still figuring out what's going on. Fright derives from a combination of **surprise, uncertainty, and urgency**.

**Problems with fright:**
- Over very quickly
- People build tolerance fast
- Those who don't build tolerance become fatigued instead
- In TTRPGs, things happen too slowly for good fright — describing stuff takes time, players can take all the time they want to respond

**Making fright work in a TTRPG (use sparingly):**
- Give incomplete information — be fast, snappy, and vague, then gradually add information
- Harangue people for immediate responses and make them suffer if they don't respond
- Don't ask for initiative rolls — nothing kills fright like an initiative roll

> **GM:** "You open the door. Suddenly, a huge shadowy shape lunges for you! What do you do?"
> **Alice:** "I, umm... what is it?"
> **GM:** "Too late! Make a Dexterity saving throw!"

The vagueness counteracts D&D's empowerment problem. When the threat is amorphous and players don't have time to think, you can get around it.

---

## Revulsion

Disgust, discomfort, or loathing. The feeling from creepy crawlies, rotting corpses, grisly torture scenes. Fear you feel in your stomach. It makes you sick.

Revulsion is **fear by implication**. We're disgusted because we're afraid of what it implies:
- Disfigured/diseased individuals remind us we're fragile
- Rats and filth are sources of disease
- Reptiles and insects are alien to our mammalian minds
- Zombies are humans stripped of agency and free will
- Werewolves are humans given over to animal rage
- Vampires are humans stripped of everything except parasitic greed

**Making revulsion work in a TTRPG:**
- It's not enough to say "giant rat" — describe the mangy, matted fur, the bald tail, the cold beady eyes, the stink
- Revulsion must be **sustained** — add small, repulsive details throughout the scene
- Be gleefully descriptive in short, sickening bursts
- For deeper revulsion, lead people from the thing to the horrible fear it represents — zombies should wear clothes, have ages and identities, wedding rings, laugh lines around empty eye sockets

---

## Dread

The fear of the unknown and the uncontrolled. Knowing something terrible is coming but not knowing what, when, or how it will turn out. The single most important emotion for running a horror game.

Dread is **slow-boil fear** — the most sustainable fear. The longer it goes on, the more powerful it becomes. It builds because we know SOMETHING is going to happen but never have enough information to know what and when.

**Dread is the twisted, evil twin of dramatic tension.** It provides pacing for a horror story and pulls the audience along just as tension does for any other story. Revulsion and fright provide the story beats (high-point scenes). Dread moves the story from scene to scene.

Dread, fright, and revulsion work together: moments of fright and revulsion are connected by a wavering sense of dread that rises and falls but trends ever upward to the climax.

The magic thing that turns dramatic tension into dread: **powerlessness**.

---

## Powerlessness

In a normal story, we want the heroes to succeed and we're pretty sure they will. In a horror story, we still want them to succeed but we're pretty sure they won't. They can merely survive or escape. Even if they defeat the villain, it's just an escape.

The question: **how much will they lose before they escape?** Friends? Family? Sanity? Humanity? The heroes are essentially powerless to create the outcome they truly want. They can only nudge the outcome in their favor.

### Sources of Disempowerment
- **Isolation** — trapped in deep space, no one believes you, no help coming
- **Lack of information** — our greatest tool is understanding; if we can't comprehend the terrors we face, we're powerless
- **Loss of agency** — mind control, natural disasters, forces beyond our control

Without disempowerment, dread is merely dramatic tension, and all the fright and revulsion in the world won't make it horror.

**This is why horror is so hard in D&D.** RPGs, especially D&D, are very empowering by nature. But disempowering players too much or in the wrong ways can ruin the game.

---

## The Danger of the Hopeless Protagonist

In a movie, characters don't have to know their situation is hopeless. But in an RPG, the protagonists ARE the audience. Players need to know they can affect the outcome to have any interest in playing. If nothing they do matters, they'll feel frustrated, not horrified.

When you disempower players, you walk a very fine line: too little and no dread; too much and you frustrate your players.

---

## How to Run Horror in D&D

General pointers:

1. **Dread builds slowly and relies on the unknown.** Accept that a horror game is a slow build.

2. **Decide what is at stake.** The best possible outcome should NOT be a victory — it should be an escape or survival. Make sure something gets lost no matter what. If survival is at stake, at least one player is going to die.

3. **Make the players care about the stakes.** If villagers are at stake, spend an hour or more letting players interact with them. Make them nice people with kids, elderly, families, pets. Humanize them.

4. **Find ways to disempower the players and leave no uncertainty about it.** If they're trapped by a storm, make it a killing storm — kill NPCs with it to prove it's dangerous. If the monster is too powerful, demonstrate it (have it eviscerate three owlbears). If a player doesn't get the message and tries to fight anyway, kill them.

5. **Pick one or two strong themes reflecting human fears.** Example: how people become animals or monsters when desperate or afraid. Combine with loss of agency and mind control.

6. **Use those themes to build revulsion and dread.** Villagers resort to cannibalism. A parent strangles her children to spare them. Best friends try to kill each other. Don't give away too much at any one time.

7. **TAKE YOUR TIME.** You cannot run a good horror story in three hours. Four or five hours at least. An all-night thing is better.

8. **Don't tell the players they are in a horror game.** Never use the word horror. Dread works best when it creeps up behind the players and wraps an icy hand around their hearts.
