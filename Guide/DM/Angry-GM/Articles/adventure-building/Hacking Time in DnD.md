# Hacking Time in D&D

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/hacking-time-in-dnd/)
**Date:** May 19, 2017
**Series:** Hacking New Modes of Play

---

## The Problem: Time is Meaningless in D&D

Many player behaviors that are optimal strategies also lead to terrible play experiences:
- **Searching everywhere** for secret doors and traps (boring but safe)
- **Resting after every encounter** (the 15-minute workday)
- **Picking every lock** instead of smashing doors
- **Traveling as slowly as possible** in 5E (all upsides: forage, never surprised, avoid encounters)

The common thread: **time**. All these strategies waste in-game time. But in D&D, the passage of time has almost no mechanical consequence.

### Why Existing Solutions Fail

**Resource consumption** (food, light) is tied to time, but foraging is trivial and supplies are cheap. Most GMs don't track it.

**Random encounters** are the only real downside to wasting time, but:
- D&D is iffy on whether/when/how to use them
- Most GMs skip them (they're a time-sink for the GM too)
- Players can't tell random from planned encounters
- Players find combat fun regardless, and it gives XP and treasure
- After the fight, the party can just rest again

**Old-school time tracking** (10-minute turns, tick marks) was a slog. Most GMs gave up on it.

### The Core Issue

Players CANNOT feel the passage of time the way characters do. They don't care if their characters sit around for ten hours. That time doesn't weigh on their shoulders because it takes one sentence to dismiss those hours.

D&D needs a mechanic to make players **dread** the passage of time.

## The Time Pool System

A visible dice pool in the middle of the table that grows as the party spends time and represents the chance of something bad happening.

### Basic Mechanics

Each die represents roughly ten minutes of time in a dungeon or dangerous location.

**Time Passes** whenever several minutes will pass in the dungeon before the players finish one or more tasks—ransacking a room, searching for traps, searching for secret doors, traveling room to room. Most combat encounters do NOT constitute Time Passing (they're only 1–2 minutes of game time).

### When Time Passes

The GM either **adds a die** to the Time Pool OR **rolls** the Time Pool.

**Die type** is based on location danger:
- **d4** — extremely hostile
- **d6** — normal dungeon
- **d8** — sparse dungeon
- **d10** — extremely sparse (theoretical)

**Pool capacity:** 6 dice. Once full, no more dice can be added—the GM must roll instead.

**Rolling the Time Pool:** Pick up all dice and roll. If any die shows a **1**, a **Bad Thing** happens.

The GM can also choose to roll INSTEAD of adding a die whenever the party's actions would attract undue attention (e.g., using crowbars to break through a stone door).

### After Rolling

- **If nothing bad happens:** return the pool to the table
- **If a Bad Thing happens and the pool wasn't full:** remove one die from the pool
- **If a Bad Thing happens and the pool was full:** clear the entire pool and mark that **one hour has passed** (durations expire, light sources go out)

### Short Rests

Option 1: Reduce short rests to 10 minutes (like 4E).

Option 2: Leave the Time Pool where it is. Roll 6 dice of the appropriate type. If any shows a 1, the rest is interrupted by a Bad Thing but can be resumed. Either way, mark off an hour. Remove one die from the existing pool.

## Bad Things: The Real Power

Bad Things don't have to be random encounters. They're a **planning tool**. The GM finds the thing in an adventure that hooks into the passage of time and uses it to advance the plot or provide setbacks:

| Adventure Type | Bad Thing Examples |
|---|---|
| Random dungeon | Minor vermin encounters (nuisance, no real reward) |
| Infiltration | A patrol is coming—hide, ambush, or run |
| Crumbling ruin | Collapse or seismic activity; part of dungeon closed off |
| Tidal cave | More of the dungeon floods |
| Cult opening a portal | Cult completes one of six steps; more demons in final encounter |
| Dragon's lair | Dragon rouses → realizes someone's in the dungeon → searches → finds them |
| Evading capture | Enemy gets closer; after four Bad Things, they ambush the players |
| Minor setbacks | Snake bite, ghostly moans requiring a save, temporary Bane effect |

### Manipulating the System

The Time Pool can be adjusted dynamically:
- Goblins escape and raise the alarm → switch from d6s to d4s
- Attack during the day when enemies are least active → use d8s instead of d6s
- Alert drops after a few days → dice size increases again

## Key Benefits

1. **Visible to players** — creates growing dread that mirrors character impatience
2. **Easy for GM to track** — no minute-by-minute bookkeeping
3. **Ties into existing durations** — ritual spells cause Time to Pass; 10-minute durations decrement on Time Passes; hour durations decrement when pool clears
4. **Rich hooks** — GM can hang interesting scenes, encounters, and situations off of it
5. **Aligns players with characters** — players feel the value of time

This system works for minute-to-minute gameplay (dungeon exploration, infiltration). Wilderness travel requires adaptation (covered in later articles).
