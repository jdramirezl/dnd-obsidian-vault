# Y'All Mind If I Rant About Doors?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/yall-mind-if-i-rant-about-doors/)
**Date:** January 20, 2026
**Series:** Adventure Building

---

## The Pet Peeve: "I Break Down the Door"

You do **not** break down a door. Not without a battering ram, a buddy, minutes of effort, and at least one wandering monster showing up. Or alternatively, an ax.

If the door is closed and pushing/pulling doesn't work, you **force the door** — apply force to the failure point:
- **Kicks:** Human legs put out a lot of force directed onto a small, precise area (the bolt/latch)
- **Charging/ramming:** Full body weight plus momentum — not directed force, but a lot of force

When players say "I break down the door," they're really saying "I Strength check the door." Push-button play at its ugliest — responding to the word "door" with "Strength check" like a dog responding to "walkies."

---

## The GM's Fault

GMs share the blame. When's the last time you actually **described** a door?
- Not a special door — a totally normal door
- Do you know what a normal D&D door looks like? How it's constructed?
- Or do you just draw rectangles on the battlemap?
- Do you differentiate between forcing doors and destroying them?

The typical exchange:
> "The door is locked." → "I break it." → "Strength check DC 15." → "Success." → "Moving on."

This trains players to treat doors as abstractions, not objects in a world.

## Why Weapons and Fire Don't Work on Doors

### Weapons
- **Swords:** Theoretically possible with a huge sword, but takes an exhausting hour and ruins the blade
- **Axes:** Weapon axes can breach a door with minutes of work, but the edge will be a dull, chipped mess. Tool axes are better (longer handles, more leverage)
- **Maces/bludgeons:** Can breach with sustained pounding over many minutes, but wood-hafted weapons start to split from vibration
- **War hammers:** Small heads — not sledgehammers. Not effective

Firefighters use long-handled tool axes and battering rams for a reason.

### Fire
- Solid masses of wood char before they burn — you can't hold a match to a log
- Need sustained flame for many minutes, plus kindling or accelerant
- *Firebolt* has "Duration: instantaneous" — it won't burn down a door
- Even if you get it burning: smoke fills enclosed underground areas, you have to wait for it to burn out, smoldering debris, no fire extinguisher in the Starter Kit

## The Real Problem: Doors as Speed Bumps

GMs don't put doors in dungeons expecting them to be potentially impassable obstacles. They see doors as speed bumps — assume players will get through with the right check.

> "The problem with lockpicking is that there are never any keys."

When's the last time you put a locked door in the party's way and left a key somewhere for them to find?

### The Terrible Mindset
- No modern GM is willing to put a door the party might never open
- "You can't make content players might not see" — **horseshit**
- If the possibility of failure doesn't exist, players have no agency
- The path of least resistance shouldn't be "roll better than a 5 on a d20"

## Historical Perspective: Doors Used to Matter

### Old School D&D
- Even odds (50/50) of forcing a basic stuck door required Strength 18 — almost nobody had that
- Most characters could force doors ~20% of the time
- Mid-level thieves had ~20% chance to pick a normal lock

### Modern D&D
- Low-level PCs can expect ~65% success rate on forcing/picking
- "Roll to bypass" isn't really gameplay — it should be a rare lucky break, not the default approach

## What Doors Should Be

A locked door should mean:
- **Search for a key** or an alternate route
- Occasionally the thief gets lucky — a delightful surprise, not the expectation
- Occasionally the barbarian forces it — but when that fails (which should be often), improvise a battering ram or settle in for loud, exhausting work
- Or accept never knowing what's behind that door

### Make Bypassing Costly and Risky

**Lock picking:**
- Time-consuming (quiet but chews up time as a resource)
- Requires special tools and training

**Forcing a door:**
- Quicker but might take multiple attempts
- Each blow is loud — alerts everything nearby
- Risks injury — you can really hurt yourself
- You can't be sure why the door isn't opening (stuck? locked? barred? painted on the wall?)

Both should feel like the **worst options** — costly, risky, unlikely to work.

---

## Why Doors Matter for Game Design

Doors are tools for controlling the player experience:
- A locked door sends players to the basement to find a key → they fight tutorial zombies before serious zombies
- A door that can only be opened from the other side → becomes a shortcut for leaving and returning after a rest
- Doors prevent access — that's what "door" means
- They're valuable for balancing free exploration with optimal pacing

### The Cascade Effect
When GMs treat doors as roll-to-proceed speed bumps:
1. Players internalize that expectation
2. Expectations change
3. System designers change games to meet those expectations
4. The ability to run good, engaging adventures is ruined

---

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Learn to describe doors** — what they look like, how they're constructed, how they open
2. **Know your locks** — external padlock or internal mechanism? Keyhole? Thumb latch?
3. **Use stuck doors** — swollen with moisture, warped by age, rusted hinges, calcified in a cave
4. **Put keys in your dungeons** — literal keys for literal locks
5. **Make forcing/picking unreliable** — the rare success should feel like a lucky break
6. **Describe all doors consistently** — if only special doors get description, players get paranoid at anything described
7. **Treat doors as significant obstacles** — potentially game-ending barriers, not tissue-paper obstructions
