# Plotty Plot Plotting (For Adventures)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/plotting-adventure/)
**Date:** May 26, 2017
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

Planning out plots is an essential part of writing your own adventures and campaigns. Adventures generally have very simple plots. But when it comes to a game you're going to run for six months or a year or longer, plot becomes much more central.

---

## Needle and Thread

**Plot** is a story element — one ingredient that goes into making a story. Most people think the plot IS the story. It isn't. A plot is just a sequence of events — the major events that get the story from the motivation and incitement to the resolution.

The plot is just a series of steps — **plot points**. A connect-the-dots puzzle. All stories are just all the crap that surrounds a series of connected dots.

### Plot Threads

Ongoing works (franchises, TV series, RPG campaigns) tend to have multiple plots — multiple motivations, multiple resolutions, and multiple connect-the-dots puzzles joining them.

A **plot thread** is a sequence of events that ties a given motivation to its resolution. Character arcs are a classic example, though RPGs generally lack them because players want to start as the character they want to play.

---

## Plotting is NOT Railroading

### Multiple Types of Freedom
- **Freedom to engage** — different groups solve the same plot point differently (frontal assault, stealth, disguise)
- **Freedom to fail** — the party might fail, changing or ending the adventure

### A Plot is Just an Outline
A game plan, not a straight-jacket. No one argues that monster combat strategy is railroading. If the strategy can't be executed due to player actions, the GM improvises. Same with plot. Improvising a NEW plan is easier if you had an OLD plan.

### Structure Matters
Satisfying stories have structure. Having a plot planned means your game is more likely to have a fun, satisfying structure. Even if you deviate, you still have a sense of the general shape.

### Plots Don't Have to Be Linear
You can build branches at key decision points. Most non-disruptive players aren't that unpredictable. Present reasonable choices and you can guess the two or three likely choices they'll make.

---

## The Points of Plots (In Simple Adventures)

Plot points in RPGs generally fall into three types:

### 1. Objectives
A specific goal the protagonists must accomplish. The most common plot points. They provide agency, accomplishment, work like bread crumbs, and break the adventure into manageable chunks. Can be linear or branching/open.

### 2. Events
Something that happens that protagonists must deal with. They generally don't have agency over the event itself. Unlike objectives (which advance toward desired resolution), events can advance toward EITHER desired or undesired resolution.

### 3. Villainous Plans
The rarest but extremely useful type. The villain is the hero of his own story trying to advance his own plot. To the heroes, villainous plot points look like events but are more dynamic — the GM invokes them as part of playing the villain and can modify them on the fly. They help define **when the adventure is lost**.

---

## By The Power of Plot (In Complicated Adventures)

Adding the villain's own plans creates two different plot threads:

**Heroes' Thread:** Attacked by zombies → learn about lich king → penetrate tomb → find four keys → defeat death knight → defeat lich king → *Favorable resolution*

**Villain's Thread:** Send agents to steal the Carrot of the Dead → kidnap Bunnyanna → wait for new moon → conduct ritual → open gates to Land of Dead Bunnies → raze Sunny Meadows → *Unfavorable resolution*

If the party learns of the villain's plans, they have multiple opportunities to disrupt them — stop the theft, stop the kidnapping, free the captive, fight off the army, or try to defeat the lich king before he finishes.

### The Magic of Plotting

By highlighting key plot points leading from motivation to each resolution, you:
1. Identify the minimum stuff you need to prepare
2. Have an outline for running the adventure dynamically
3. Can adapt on the fly (captured the MacGuffin? The villain's agents now hunt the heroes — you already have their stats)

Spell out the major objectives, events, and villainous plans from start to each resolution. Figure out what you need for each plot point. Fill in the space between with fun adventure bits.
