# Flight to Elturel: Designing Part 1 (Part 2)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/flight-to-elturel-part-2/)
**Date:** September 2, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Expect the Worst; Plan for the Best

Have some sense of how the adventure will go, but don't try to cut off all possible escape routes. Because you've worked out backstory, motivations, and resolutions, you're familiar with what's happening in the world and why. If players attempt something unexpected, you can figure out how the world reacts.

**An adventure is a goal and a bunch of reasons why the players can't just take two steps and cross the finish line.**

### Contriving Situations (Writing an Adventure)

Baldur's Gate and Elturel were chosen to force players into the wilderness:
- **Baldur's Gate** — lawful inside the walls, completely lawless Outer City. Stedd thinks he's wanted for murder, so he won't go inside the gates — cutting the party off from institutions, temples, and the harbor.
- **The Outer City** being lawless means Zhentarim mercenaries can act with impunity, and the party can fight back without legal consequences.
- **Elturel** — most logical overland destination: beacon of light/goodness, two orders of paladins, nearest major city, easy to navigate to via the River Chionthar.

## Adventure Building, Scene by Scene

Break adventures into broad chunks of continuous action called **scenes**, then nest encounters inside. This allows layering mechanics over several encounters. **Align scenes with session breaks.**

1. **Scene 1** (one session): Escape from the Outer City of Baldur's Gate
2. **Scene 2** (one to two sessions): Cross the wilderness to Elturel

## Conflicts and Antagonists

Before slapping together encounters, figure out what the scene is really about. Goal: "safely escape from Baldur's Gate with Stedd in tow."

### Central Antagonist: The Zhentarim

Needed a military/mercenary organization that could field several patrol teams. The Zhentarim — mercenaries for hire with a bad reputation — fit perfectly.

**Created a face for the organization:** Sternhelm Kresk, a hobgoblin bounty hunter lieutenant. Militaristic, proud, monstrous (so the party immediately identifies him as the enemy). Leads Stalwarts (soldiers) and trains bloodhounds. If Kresk is killed, the organization sends another face and the relationship continues.

### Second Central Conflict: Stedd's Mood

Need human drama, but minimize internal party drama at campaign start. Stedd is the perfect candidate — a panicked, abused kid with uncontrolled magical powers.

**Stedd's Mood Meter** (ten-point scale):
- Drops when stressed: fights, enemies get close, reminders he's unloved/unwanted/dangerous
- Rises when the party comforts him, keeps him safe, builds trust
- At mood 1: rolls Wisdom save each round or panics — flees, casts a spell, or both
- Can't cast spells otherwise (can't control his magic)

**The entire system is those few sentences.** No complex mechanics needed.

### Don't Reveal the Mood Meter

Everything the characters would know is visible at the table if you're doing a good job. The mechanics would give players too much information. **Player-facing mechanics make players interact with the mechanic instead of the thing in the game world.** If you can convey information without directly referencing a game mechanic, that's always the better way.

## Planning the Specific Encounters

First session of a new campaign is short. Planned for **three encounters**:

### Encounter 1: The Hook

PCs arrive at a tavern. Stedd is already there, disguised as an urchin begging table to table (hidden in flavor text). After light role-playing, Stedd gets backhanded by a rough laborer. Described as drawn, undernourished, abused — but clean and not starving. He explains he's looking for an escort to Elturel.

### Encounter 2: The Zhentarim Arrive

The moment the party agrees to hear Stedd out, Zhentilar Stalwarts arrive. They tell Stedd they're bringing him back to his master. One is sent to "run and get Kresk and the others." A fight breaks out. Started Stedd's mood at 2 so he'd likely panic during the fight — wanted players to see his stress in action.

### Encounter 3: Dogs and Mouse (Stealth Encounter)

Five patrol groups (two soldiers + one bloodhound each), plus two fixed groups at intersections. ~20 soldiers total, 5 bloodhounds. Mapped a two-block area around the tavern.

**How it played out:**
- Party moves point to point on the map (mostly concealed based on line of sight)
- GM moves Zhent forces in a rotating search pattern
- Kresk methodically follows the party's trail from the tavern (if the party stops, he catches up)
- Goal: reach the edge of the map safely

### The Three States of Stealth Enemies

1. **Patrolling** — moving around, using passive Perception
2. **Interested** — heard/spotted something, moving to investigate, making active Perception checks
3. **Coming to kill you** — spotted the party, drawing swords, rolling initiative

**Most GMs skip the "interested" phase** and go right to "you failed stealth; everyone comes to kill you." This is why stealth encounters are boring for most GMs. The interested phase gives the party a chance to react and fix the problem before it becomes a full-blown emergency.
