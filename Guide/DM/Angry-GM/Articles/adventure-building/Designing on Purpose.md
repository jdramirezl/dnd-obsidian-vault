# Designing on Purpose

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/designing-on-purpose/)
**Date:** June 28, 2024
**Category:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Core Thesis

Scenario Design — Adventure Design, Campaign Design — **is** Game Design. Tabletop RPGs like D&D aren't games by themselves; they're piles of disconnected mechanics. When you write a Scenario, you're finishing the Game Design process. You're designing a game.

Game Design is about building **satisfying gameplay experiences**. It's not primarily about the story or the simulation (though those are part of it). It's not about just throwing situations at players to see what happens. True Scenario Designers don't leave things up to chance or wild player whims.

**Designing on Purpose** (Purpose-Driven Design): Recognizing a gameplay need and building compelling gameplay around it. Accommodating Game Design principles in every aspect of the game.

---

## Merman's Mystery: An Example

> "Say you're running a mystery and the players come to the wrong conclusion. Do you tell them?"

From a gameplay standpoint: **yes**. Players have to know whether they've won or lost. That's how games work.

This doesn't mean stopping the action to announce "You have answered correctly!" It means that once the game's over, the players must know whether they did well or not.

### Practical Example: The Fall of Silverpine Watch

A murder-mystery side quest. At the end, an NPC companion asks the players what they think happened. Based on their answer:

- **Mostly right:** "That makes perfect sense. I bet you got it right."
- **Close but missing details:** "I don't know… it sounds like you're missing something."
- **Totally wrong:** "I totally don't buy that theory. Sorry. But it doesn't matter because you killed the ghost and we've got to move on."

That scene exists solely because players have to walk away knowing whether they did a good job. It fits the story (the NPC is naturally curious) and the simulation (she gives her opinion, not a definitive answer).

## The Power of Purpose

Designing on Purpose lets you avoid problems and adjust difficulty.

### Avoiding Perpetual Adventures

A **Perpetual Adventure** is one players can keep trying over and over — no firm failure state or they don't know when they've failed.

Example: Ghost asks PCs to avenge his murder. PCs accuse the wrong person. Ghost doesn't vanish. They accuse another. And another. They just keep stabbing suspects until they get the right one.

**Solution:** Invent a reason they can't keep trying. Maybe the ghost has until midnight to ascend or be trapped forever. That's Designing on Purpose to forestall a problem.

### Adjusting Difficulty

If players get one shot at delivering their solution (high-stakes, unforgiving), adding a companion character who periodically says "That doesn't make sense; are you sure you didn't miss something?" gives players margin for error and a chance to rethink before locking in their answer.

---

## Don't Mistake Mechanics for Purposes

The temptation is to find one-size-fits-all mechanical solutions. "Every adventure needs a timer" (Ticking Time Bomb). The problems are real and timers are A solution — but they're too specific and constrictive to work in every Scenario.

**Focus on *this* problem in *this* Scenario** and find a unique, creative solution perfect for it. That's hard but gets easier with practice.

Good general-purpose tools (Tension Pool, Clocks from Blades in the Dark) solve common problems without overly constraining designers. Compare to the Ticking Time Bomb or 4E Skill Challenges — too rigid.

## Design Statements

> To properly Design on Purpose, you must first identify your Purpose, and second, Design.

Before designing a Scenario, you must know what you mean to design. A **Design Statement** describes, at minimum:

1. **Starting Point** — How it begins
2. **Goal** — What the players are trying to do
3. **Outcomes (Plural)** — Different ways it can end, for good and ill
4. **At least one Challenge** — Usually the biggest one

### Example

❌ "The players have to kill a dragon."

✅ "A village lies under the thrall of a dragon. The heroes arrive and the villagers beg them to defeat or drive off the dragon. The heroes must confront the dragon. If they kill it or drive it off, they save the village. If they fail, the dragon burns the village in a rage and flies off to find a new settlement to terrorize."

**Do exactly the minimum.** The more you bog it down beyond the minimum, the less room you leave for actual design later.

### Unstated Design Statements

Everything in your game has a Design Statement — every character, encounter, reward, scene, random roll. You won't write a paragraph for every element, but everything must be there for a reason.

**Before putting ANYTHING in your game, pause and ask: "Why am I even doing this?"**

## Purposes for Game Elements

You can add elements for many reasons:

- **Tentpole Purpose** — Things you want to include because you think they're cool. The flash-of-inspiration things that get you excited to build a game.
- **Goal Purpose** — Elements needed to serve a specific campaign/adventure goal (e.g., an adventure to acquire the metal for the Devilkiller Greatsword).
- **Narrative/Fantasy Purpose** — Story themes, plot developments, world-building needs (a village needs a butcher).
- **Gameplay Purpose** — A particular mode of play, a gameplay need (e.g., an NPC to check answers with).

**Don't bother classifying purposes.** They're different but it doesn't matter.

### The Rule

**Any purpose is okay, but everything must also serve a Gameplay Purpose.** "It doesn't make the game worse" doesn't count. Good means more than *not bad*. Anything you add must **improve the gameplay experience.**

---

## Game Design Über Alles

Game design must rule over all — but that doesn't mean it always comes first. It'd be ridiculous to say every Scenario must be chosen by the needs of the game. The game doesn't care whether it's about killing a dragon or solving a murder.

But:
- If your Tentpole Encounter doesn't work from a gameplay perspective and you can't fix it — it's got to go. Maybe the whole Scenario needs a rewrite.
- If an encounter only exists for Gameplay Purposes (like a fight with rats because the game needs a fight) — that can stay. It makes for a better game.
- Encounters don't have to advance the plot. But they DO have to serve a Gameplay Purpose.
- If you're running modern D&D, you've got to chew up players' resources periodically. If you don't want to do that, recognize the Gameplay Need you're leaving unfulfilled and find a different way to fill it.

---

## Economizing and Elegantifying

The best gameplay elements do lots of things at once. Look for existing elements that can carry additional loads. After design is done, look for elements you can merge.

**A Scenario with fewer multi-purpose elements is better than one with lots of single-purpose elements.** Don't invent a new NPC to give a clue when the butcher in town can do it.

But don't overdo it — one villager handling everything detracts from the feeling of exploring a village.

---

## But What If I Can't?

If there's a hole in the gameplay and you can't find a way to fill it — **throw the adventure away and design something else.** There's no shame in that.

Some adventures just can't be built. Labyrinths, for example, just suck in tabletop RPGs.

If a game element isn't serving a Gameplay Purpose — cut it. Things can exist in your world without appearing on camera and wasting game time.

---

## Summary

- Scenario Design means knowing what makes a good gameplay experience and finding clever, creative ways to provide one.
- Start every design by identifying your purpose and describing the gameplay experience (Design Statement).
- Everything must serve a Gameplay Purpose even if included for other reasons initially.
- If you can't make something work gameplay-wise, cut it or scrap the design.
- Scenario Design is a **creative problem-solving exercise** — finding unique ways to turn what you want into actual good games and plugging gameplay gaps.
- If you're waiting for a checklist, formula, or step-by-step guide, you're playing the wrong game.
