# The Big Boneyard Beach Battle

> A Table Tale about running a mass battle — and the deeper lesson about when rules matter and when they don't.

## The Tale

Newbie adventurers aboard the ship Aster, bound for Old Zethinia, are forced to abandon ship after a volcanic explosion. They're stuck on a narrow cursed beach surrounded by rocky bluffs. After setting up camp, the party scouts the perimeter and discovers dozens of ancient rusted swords and spearheads driven point-down into the beach. The wizard detects necromantic energy spreading as the evil red moon Krisma rises.

The party stays at ground zero. Skeletons attack from behind. After dispatching them (burning all healing), they return to find the camp under attack — sailors fighting skeletons on two fronts.

The party joins the southern skirmish, eventually clears it, then dashes to the northern front where sailors are going down. Two sailors fall. One is saved by a PC's spell; Eight Toes dies because a PC failed a Heal check.

## The Big Reveal: How the Battle Was Actually Run

**He didn't run it by the book. He made most of it up.**

- Only the parts nearest the PCs were run with real mechanics
- One or two *real* die rolls per round for NPCs, plus a handful of fake ones
- Far fewer dice than running it RAW would require
- Minis moved on a map, gripping story told
- The fight on the other side of camp was described cinematically based on what the PCs could hear

### Why Two Sailors Went Down

Not punishment — **consequence**. Two things happened:

1. The party left camp to scout and stayed at the magical nexus instead of returning — a perfectly valid choice that happened to be wrong for the situation
2. The party got held up at the southern skirmish due to tactics and bad rolls — not wrong play, just slow

The GM evaluated player actions and determined consequences. That's the job — far more important than any rule in the book, and more fair than a random number generator.

## Do Rules Matter or Not?

### The Progression

1. **Start by following the rules** — that's how you learn how the game should work, feel, and play
2. **Start making rulings and house rules** — that's how you learn to tinker, trust your judgment, and take risks
3. **Realize you don't always need the rules** — your judgment is always with you; the rules are a middleman

### Key Principles

- Rules matter **until you realize they don't** — you have to start by following them to learn what works
- Most GMs ignore 90% of the crap in any book — the best ones especially
- Does it matter if a Climb DC is 17 or 15? It does not
- The rules aren't your job — they're your **tools**. You don't have to use them if you don't need them
- Only a very small number of rules actually matter

### The Secret Training Method

Many of Angry's systems (companion NPCs, tension pool, etc.) are systematic ways to do what he just *does* at the table without mechanics. The expectation: you use them for a while, learn how things should work, then give up on the mechanics and just make the right things happen.

> The rules do matter until you realize they don't. Following the rules is how you learn. Eventually, you learn the rules aren't set in stone and your judgment counts for a lot. Then you start making rulings. And then there comes a day when you realize only a very small number of the rules actually matter.

### What Actually Matters

- Consistency and fairness
- Agency and engagement
- Verisimilitude
- Assessing player actions and making them live with the consequences
