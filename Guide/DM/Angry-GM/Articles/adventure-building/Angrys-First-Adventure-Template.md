# Ask Angry: Angry's Awesome First Adventure Ever Template

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-angrys-first-adventure-template/) | **Category**: Adventure Building

---

## Key Advice

- **Use pregenerated characters for new players.** Don't make character creation their first experience. Keep sheets simple — only include what will actually come up in the game. The goal is to make players want to come back and make their own characters later.
- **A first adventure is a sales pitch, not a campaign starter.** Run one standalone adventure. If players want to return, start a real campaign from scratch. Don't burden the first session with campaign setup.
- **Teach gameplay dynamics, not rules.** The rules are easy to teach as they come up. What matters is teaching how a roleplaying game *works*: the GM describes, players respond, the conversation flows as call-and-response.
- **Never start with an unstructured "you can roleplay now" scene.** New players need a clear goal and structured interactions before they can handle open-ended play. Don't ask them to interact before they understand the basic game flow.
- **The tutorial should be a quarter to a third of the adventure, then open up.** Design a linear sequence of encounters that teach the right things in the right order, then let the adventure become a real, honest-to-goodness adventure.

## Summary

This article provides Angry's complete template for building introductory adventures for first-time RPG players. It's the formula he used for *The Fall of Silverpine Watch* and uses whenever introducing new people to any RPG system. The template is a structured sequence of tutorial encounters that teach gameplay dynamics in the right order, followed by a transition into a real adventure.

**General Tips:** Use pregenerated characters (simple, no clutter, only relevant abilities). The first adventure should be a standalone sales pitch, not a campaign starter. Don't lecture about rules — explain things the first time they come up, only as much as needed. New players face intimidation from rules, dice, terminology, performance pressure, and the open-ended nature of RPGs. Give them solid, straightforward things to focus on.

**The Template (in order):**

1. **Introduce the World Through Narration.** A short paragraph describing the fantasy world — dangerous, wonderful, nothing like reality. This establishes that the game is about the GM describing and players responding.

2. **Tell the Players the Goal.** State the adventure's objective directly. Assume they've already been hired. Switch to addressing players as "you" — teaching that players *are* their characters during play.

3. **Let Players Introduce Characters.** Go around the table. Keep it light — name, race, gender, one detail from the character sheet. Don't demand invention yet.

4. **The First Interaction.** Set up a mediated NPC interaction where the NPC speaks to each character individually. Oona Tealeaf asking about backgrounds, a gate guard checking names, a priest offering blessings. This teaches the call-and-response conversation dynamic.

5. **The First Challenge.** A single skill/ability check with a clear approach. A fallen tree needing a Strength check, a river to swim across. This teaches action adjudication and core mechanics.

6. **Other Challenges As Needed.** Depending on the system: complex checks, investigation, etc. Two critical encounters to include:
   - *Everyone Doing Everything All At Once* — multiple characters taking different simultaneous actions (spooked horses, injured NPC, hidden snake). Teaches parallel action resolution.
   - *Now You Explore On Your Own Initiative* — a non-urgent, interactable scene (abandoned campsite, strange monument). Teaches player-driven exploration.
   - **Combat** — straightforward, modest difficulty, unambiguous hostility. Best placed after the simultaneous-action encounter but before the exploration encounter.

7. **A Chance to Campfire and Chill.** A deliberate rest opportunity after combat. Demonstrates rest mechanics, creates relief after tension, and opens space for in-character interaction. Use an NPC or gentle prompts to get players talking.

8. **The Real Adventure Begins.** Mark the transition with a threshold (arriving at the adventure site, weather changing, descending to a new level). The tutorial's linear structure gives way to open-ended play. Show, don't tell — the players shouldn't know they were in a tutorial.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Build pregens with only relevant abilities.** No full spellbooks to prepare from — just a list of already-prepared spells. No background features that won't come up. Abbreviate everything.
2. **Write a one-paragraph world intro.** Hit the key notes: fantasy, dangerous, different from reality. Keep it short and digestible.
3. **State the goal upfront.** "You've been hired to escort Oona through Silverpine Forest." No mystery about what the adventure is — the mystery is in how it plays out.
4. **Design a mediated first interaction.** An NPC who has a reason to speak to each character individually. Use character sheet details as conversation prompts.
5. **Design a simple first challenge.** One check, one clear approach. Fallen tree, locked gate, narrow river. Easy is better — the point is teaching the mechanic, not testing the players.
6. **Include a simultaneous-action encounter.** Multiple urgent things happening at once that demand different characters act independently. This is where you teach parallel resolution.
7. **Include a player-driven exploration encounter.** Something interesting but not urgent. Let players poke, prod, and ask questions on their own initiative.
8. **Run combat after simultaneous actions, before exploration.** This sequence builds naturally: one action → parallel actions → structured combat → self-directed exploration.
9. **Force a rest and encourage interaction.** Use an NPC or direct suggestions ("Does the bard play music at camp?") to get players talking in character during downtime.
10. **Mark the transition clearly.** A physical threshold (arriving at the dungeon, descending stairs, weather change) signals the shift from tutorial to real adventure. Don't announce it — just let the game open up.
