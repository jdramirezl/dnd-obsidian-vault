# Puzzles Suck

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/puzzles-suck/)
**Date:** March 30, 2018
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

## Puzzles Suck, But Not for the Reason You Think

Conventional wisdom says "puzzles are terrible" or "challenge the characters, not the players." This conclusion is wrong.

### Two Kinds of Players
1. **Puzzle-lovers** — like having their brains challenged, want to solve the sphinx's riddle
2. **Puzzle-haters** — no interest, roll their eyes at the color-coded runic tiles

It's rare for a group to have more than one or two puzzle-lovers. But this isn't new — not everyone enjoys social scenes or combat equally either. GMing is about giving everyone a chance to enjoy their favorite elements.

### "Character-Solved" Puzzles Don't Work
"I solve the puzzle" / "make an Arcana check" / "18" / "you solved it" — does nothing for either type. Puzzle-lovers feel cheated. Puzzle-haters don't care because they could have spent the same roll disabling a runelock.

### Know Why You're Including Puzzles
- Because you have players who will engage with them? Great.
- Because you feel obligated? Don't.

---

## Why Puzzles Actually Suck

### 1. Puzzles Are Contrived
Most puzzles have constraints. TTRPGs are open-ended by nature. To make traditional puzzles work, you must add constraints to prevent players from killing the gargoyles, walking away, or casting a spell that invalidates everything.

Puzzle-lovers will go along with contrivances (they suspend disbelief). Puzzle-haters won't and will try to break the puzzle.

### 2. Context Problems
The "solve the soup cans" problem. Puzzles make barely any sense in the game world. Puzzles as security measures say "I'm willing to be robbed by anyone who could do well on an I.Q. test" instead of just using a padlock and keys.

### The Core Issue
Without buy-in that puzzles are more gamey than role-playey, they don't fit well into TTRPGs.

---

## Don't Sweat the Small Stuff

If you want standard puzzles and have buy-in from puzzle-loving players: just make the puzzle, shove it in with an excuse, and let suspension of disbelief do the rest.

**If players break the puzzle with clever spells or out-of-the-box thinking?** Let them. Breaking the puzzle with cleverness is just "solving the puzzle." The GM always loses.

---

## Non-Optional Puzzles and Forever Puzzles

### Non-Optional Puzzles
If the entire adventure hinges on a puzzle, you're taking a big chance. But if the climax IS the puzzle, that's fine — the whole adventure built to that moment. Don't place a puzzle on the only door to the dungeon unless you want to risk not running the adventure.

### The Forever Puzzle
The puzzle the party tries to solve forever — they can't get it but won't give up. Players never admit defeat as long as they're allowed to keep trying.

**Solution: Treat puzzles like a website login page.** Fail and you get locked out. The puzzle breaks, electrocutes someone, or sounds an alarm. Cost of failure should ramp up over time.

Consider: What's at stake? How will the party know they've failed? How will you encourage them to walk away?

## Floundering and Flailing

When the party is genuinely struggling but engaged:

### The Universal Hint System Approach
Prepare clues that ramp up in how much they reveal:
1. **First layer:** Ensure the player understands the puzzle and constraints
2. **Middle layers:** Guide thinking in the right direction
3. **Final layers:** Outright give the answer

How far you go depends on: how important is it for players to feel they solved it themselves, and how optional is the puzzle.

### Don't Gate Clues Behind Die Rolls
If you've made clues available based on wants and needs, hiding them behind a lottery is messed up.

### Angry's Approach: Two Layers of Clues

**Layer 1 — Understanding the puzzle:** Handed out immediately when the party encounters the puzzle. Tied to skills but never rolled.

> "Because of your Arcana training, you can figure out you have to press a certain sequence of colored rune tiles in a proper order."

**Layer 2 — Guiding their thinking:** Stays hidden until a player starts asking questions. Swerve from their question to a clue if needed.

> "Bob, being trained as a painter, might suspect something odd about the layout of primary and secondary colors."

## Still Hate Puzzles?

Puzzles have niche appeal. They're always constrained, contrived, and require buy-in. If you're not inclined to like puzzles, you probably don't want them in your game.
