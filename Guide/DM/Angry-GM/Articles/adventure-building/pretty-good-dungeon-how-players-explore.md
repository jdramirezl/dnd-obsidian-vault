# Let's Build a Pretty Good Dungeon: How Players Explore

## Summary

This is the deepest article in the series, diving into the psychology of how players actually make navigational decisions. Angry identifies two operating modes — **Seeking Mode** (goal-focused, efficiency-driven) and **Exploration Mode** (completionist, curiosity-driven) — and explains how players constantly flip between them. He introduces **Completion Bias** (the drive to finish what you started before doing anything else) and the **Threshold Effect** (pausing at major transitions to reconsider unfinished business). Finally, he ranks the tiebreaker heuristics players use when options are roughly equal: Exigency > Recency/Availability > Unclosed Loops > Salience/Memorability. The core takeaway: players don't make direction decisions — they make action decisions. They want to know what lies at the end of a path, not which compass direction it goes.

## Key Advice

### Forward, Sideways, and Backward

Players constantly classify every option as one of three things:

- **Forward** — Leads toward the current goal (progress)
- **Sideways** — A diversion or side path (exploration)
- **Backward** — Retracing steps (feels like failure)

Players don't want to choose between "north" and "east." They want to choose between "chase the monster" (forward) and "investigate the sparkly river" (sideways). **Always give players enough information to classify directions as actions, not compass points.**

### The Two Operating Modes

| Mode | Priority | Behavior | Triggered By |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Seeking** | Move toward a goal efficiently | Ignores side paths, follows anything that looks like progress | Urgency, strong goals, emergencies |
| **Exploration** | See everything, miss nothing | Avoids the "forward" path, prefers sideways | Default state, finishing a goal, no clear next objective |

**Most groups default to Exploration Mode** unless something pushes them into Seeking. They constantly look for excuses to switch to Seeking (by finding mini-goals) because Exploration feels like non-progress, but they resist finishing the main goal because that ends the exploration.

### The Paradox of Player Navigation

Players want to explore sideways while always seeking forward. They oscillate: Explore until something worth Seeking appears → Seek it → Complete it → Explore for the next thing to Seek. Smart dungeon design exploits this by:

- Building dungeons that invite Exploration Mode
- Scattering progress indicators like cookies
- Providing opportunities for short, rewarding diversions at regular intervals

### Completion Bias

Once players commit to a path, they want to finish it before making another major decision. This means:

- Players following the "red tunnel" will ignore a gray side tunnel until the red tunnel ends
- Players chasing a monster won't stop to explore unless something more urgent or interesting appears
- To get players to re-evaluate their choices, you must **break** their Completion Bias

### Five Ways to Break Completion Bias

1. **Let them finish** — The path ends naturally (dead end, goal achieved)
2. **Show something sexier** — A more interesting, useful, or urgent option appears
3. **Block the path** — An obstacle forces them to go elsewhere (players resist this hard)
4. **Recontextualization** — A discovery changes the meaning of something they left behind (finding a key for a previously locked door)
5. **Door to before** — A path unexpectedly loops back to a previous area (triple combo: ends the path + moves them backward without choosing to + recontextualizes old options)

### The Threshold Effect

When players reach a major transition (stairs to next level, boss door, new biome), they pause and ask: "Before we move on, should we go back and…?" Thresholds trigger a full re-examination of the "what next" list. They don't guarantee players will turn back — just that they'll consider it.

### Tiebreaker Heuristics (Ranked by Power)

When options are roughly equal, players prefer:

1. **Exigency** — Something that will expire or be lost if not acted on NOW
2. **Recency / Availability** — Whatever is closest or was most recently discovered
3. **Unclosed Loops** — Unfinished business (unsolved puzzles, locked doors, blocked paths)
4. **Salience / Memorability** — Whatever is most visually striking, unusual, or promises a reward

These can be stacked and their weights can be shifted by clever design.

### Directional Decisions Are the Worst

Asking players to choose between two identical-looking tunnels going in different directions is a coin toss. Brains hate coin tosses. Players will argue forever at nondescript intersections even though they know they'll explore everything eventually. **Make every direction look different.** Even just different colors.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Label every path** — For each navigational choice in your dungeon, determine: can the players classify this as forward, sideways, or backward? If not, add environmental cues that let them.
2. **Design for mode-flipping** — Alternate between sections that encourage Exploration (open areas with multiple curiosities) and sections that trigger Seeking (urgent goals, clear objectives, time pressure).
3. **Use Completion Bias deliberately** — Design paths that have clear beginnings and endings. Don't interrupt a path with too many forks. Let players finish one thing before presenting the next major choice.
4. **Place "doors to before" strategically** — Loop paths back to previous areas to break Completion Bias, move players backward without the psychological cost, and recontextualize old options.
5. **Stack your heuristics** — Don't rely on a single factor to guide players. Make the golden path's next step both recent AND salient AND an unclosed loop when possible.
6. **Differentiate every direction** — No two paths from the same room should look identical. Use color, material, sound, smell, temperature, architectural style — anything to break the tie.
7. **Manage the "what next" list size** — Close paths as players explore them. Don't let the list of unexplored options grow too large (causes stress) or shrink to zero (causes aimlessness).
8. **Use thresholds as pacing tools** — Place major transitions (level changes, biome shifts, boss doors) at points where you want players to pause and consider cleaning up side content.

> [!tip] The Action Decision Reframe
> Never describe a navigational choice as "north or east." Always describe it in terms of what the players think lies in each direction. Instead of "There are doors to the north and east," try: "The northern passage slopes downward and you can hear rushing water. The eastern door is scratched and scuffed, with fresh muddy bootprints leading through it." Now players aren't choosing a direction — they're choosing between "investigate the water source" and "follow the monster's trail." That's an action decision, and player brains handle those far better than direction decisions.
