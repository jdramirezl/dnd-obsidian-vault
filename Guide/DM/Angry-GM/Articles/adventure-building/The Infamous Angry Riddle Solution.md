# The Infamous Angry Riddle Solution

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-infamous-angry-riddle-solution/)
**Date:** April 28, 2017
**Series:** Angry Table Tales

---

## Overview

The solution to the Infamous Angry Riddle Contest. Walks through the logic, explains the multiple layers of trickery, and discusses fairness, challenge design, and how to create difficulty that tests players rather than dice.

## The First Trap: The Statue's Self-Referential Statement

The statue makes **four** statements, not three:

1. "I speak only one true sentence."
2. "The copper chest is deadly to touch."
3. "The bronze chest is deadly to touch."
4. "The copper chest is safe to touch and contains the Crown."

**The critical insight:** Statement #1 is NOT part of the trustworthy instructions — it's part of the puzzle itself. In a standard logic puzzle, the narrator delivers trustworthy instructions ("one always lies, one always tells the truth") separately from the suspect clues. Here, the booming voice (trustworthy) delivers the setup, but the statue itself claims how honest it is. The statue is making a claim about its own honesty — why should it be honest about how often it lies?

### Working the Logic

**Can statement #1 be true?** If so, all others are false. But statements #2 and #4 about the copper chest can't both be false (the chest can't be neither safe nor deadly). Contradiction. **Statement #1 is false.**

**Can all statements be false?** Then the copper chest is both NOT safe and NOT deadly. Contradiction.

**Can three be true?** Statement #1 is false, so the other three must be true. But #2 and #4 contradict each other about the copper chest. Contradiction.

**Therefore exactly TWO statements are true.** One must be about the copper chest (to avoid contradiction). Since #1 is false, the other true statement must be #3: **the bronze chest IS deadly.**

This is what New Guy missed. By treating "I speak only one true sentence" as a trustworthy instruction rather than a suspect claim, he reached the exact opposite conclusion — and got killed. **The puzzle was designed so that the obvious mistake leads to exactly the wrong answer.**

## The Second Trap: The Logic Puzzle Is Incomplete

After eliminating the bronze chest, you're left with: either the copper chest is deadly OR it's safe and has the Crown. One is true, one is false. **There is no way to determine which from logic alone.**

This trapped the contest participants. People assumed the logic puzzle would lead to the Crown. When they hit a wall, they assumed they missed something. They couldn't get past the idea that a puzzle might not have a complete logical solution.

## The Actual Solution: Risk and Sacrifice

The booming voice said: "A ruler must take great risks and make great sacrifices." Wisdom alone wasn't enough.

**The correct answer:** After eliminating the bronze chest through logic, someone must open one of the remaining two chests. There's a 50% chance that person dies. But either way, the party finds the Crown — if the opener dies, the Crown is in the other chest.

- Without solving the logic: 66% chance of death per guess
- After solving the logic: 50% chance of death on one guess
- **Wisdom mitigates the risk but doesn't eliminate it**

The Crown is in the **copper chest.** The iron chest is the "barbarian trap" — the unmentioned option that people gravitate toward when frustrated with the logic ("the statue didn't mention iron, so it must be safe").

## Fairness Analysis

**The puzzle IS fair because:**
- The booming voice told them death was the risk, explicitly
- The players knew there was a 66% chance of death per blind guess
- The voice said wisdom alone wasn't enough — risk and sacrifice were required
- The door was open — they could leave at any time
- The Crown was optional (Ragnar could claim his throne the hard way)
- The odds and costs were clearly spelled out before anyone was at risk

**The puzzle challenges the assumption that:**
- Logic puzzles always have complete solutions
- Puzzles in D&D never require actual sacrifice
- The "instructions" of a logic puzzle are always trustworthy

## Key Takeaways for Puzzle Design

### Subverting Expectations While Remaining Fair
- Take normal expectations and subvert them while ensuring the puzzle gives players ALL the information
- Be very careful about the separation between trustworthy instructions (narrator) and suspect clues (puzzle elements)
- If you shift instructions into the puzzle content, make sure the trustworthy narrator delivers only trustworthy information

### The Deeper Question
How do you create and adjust difficulty so that **the players, not the dice**, feel the difference? Making numbers bigger just makes it less likely to roll the right result. The assumption is players compensate with strategy, but that's haphazard.

One answer: subvert normal expectations while being scrupulously fair and giving players all the information. But that's not the only answer.

### Designing Traps Within Traps
- The bronze chest trap punishes the obvious logical mistake
- The obvious mistake leads to the **exact opposite** of the correct conclusion
- A wrong death gives no new information — the party is no better off
- The iron chest is the "frustration trap" for people who give up on logic
- The incomplete logic is the trap for people who assume puzzles always have clean solutions

### On Player Death and Consent
- Players knew the risks upfront and opted in voluntarily
- The GM planned for failure (the party could walk away and pursue the goal differently)
- Guaranteeing success IS railroading — forcing an outcome regardless of player actions
- More honest than most traps or combat: odds and costs were explicitly stated
