# Abstract Dungeoneering

## Key Advice

When a dungeon space is too large and empty to map room-by-room, use the **Zone Exploration Mechanic**: divide the dungeon into thematic zones, create an event list for each zone, and let random rolls determine what the PCs stumble upon as they wander. This preserves player agency and decision-making while skipping the tedium of mapping empty rooms.

## Summary

Angry presents a lightweight system for abstracting exploration in large, mostly-empty dungeon spaces (libraries, ruins, cave systems) where room-by-room mapping would be tedious but full abstraction would remove player agency.

**The Zone Exploration Mechanic:**
1. Divide the dungeon into **thematic zones** (neighborhoods) that are architecturally distinct.
2. Draw a **flowchart** showing how zones connect.
3. For each zone, create an **Event List** containing: transitions to other zones, unique discoveries (treasure, monsters, secrets, clues), and a wandering monster entry.
4. Number the entries and assign a die. When PCs explore, roll to see what they find.
5. Cross off unique discoveries once found; check off landmarks/transitions once discovered.

**Advanced Variants:**
- **Time Constraint**: Assign time per exploration roll; use for tidal flooding, timed events, monster sleep cycles.
- **Tracking a Foe**: Successful tracking checks follow a predetermined trail; failure means random wandering.
- **Traps**: Add trap entries to the event list (one-time or repeating).
- **Cat and Mouse**: Roll for enemy movement simultaneously; same location = encounter.
- **Race to the Thing**: Both PCs and NPCs searching; whoever finds it first wins.
- **Treasure Map**: Gives PCs the ability to choose specific destinations within a zone.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Identify zones** — break your large dungeon into 4–8 thematic sections with distinct visual/architectural identity.
2. **Draw a zone flowchart** — show connections between zones (not a full map, just a node graph).
3. **Build an event list per zone** — include zone transitions, unique discoveries, secret doors (with passive DCs), and a wandering monster entry. Assign a die size.
4. **Prep tactical maps** only for fixed encounter locations and 1–2 generic maps per zone for wandering monsters.
5. **Run it**: describe the zone flavor, roll the event list, let PCs decide whether to continue exploring or move to a discovered transition.
6. **Layer in advanced mechanics** as needed — time pressure, tracking, cat-and-mouse, or racing against rival parties.

> [!tip] Zone Design Shortcut
> Each zone's event list should have roughly **equal parts** transitions, unique discoveries, and repeatable events (wandering monsters/traps). This ensures PCs find exits at a natural pace, always have something interesting to interact with, and face ongoing tension. If your list is mostly transitions, the zone feels empty; if it's mostly monsters, it feels like a grind.
