# Narrative Structure for Advanced Morons

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/narrative-structure-for-morons-3/)
**Date:** June 20, 2018
**Series:** Narrative Structure for Morons (Part 3 of 3)

---

This is the third part of the series. It builds on the basic list of plot points from part two and adds "spicy" turning points drawn from Joseph Campbell's Monomyth. These are optional turning points – like spices – that you don't use all at once, don't use the same ones every time, and use sparingly.

## Recap: The Basic Turning Points

> **(Optional) A New Life:** The heroes' lives start a new course, one that will bring them into contact with adventure.
> **The Call to Adventure:** Something happens to make the heroes aware of a major conflict that they have to resolve and a good reason to want to resolve it.
> **(Optional) It's Personal:** An event occurs to make the upcoming conflict personal and to make it impossible for the heroes to accept failure.
> **(Optional) The Big Screw/The Final Test:** Something major turns against the heroes and they have to deal with it.
> **The Climax:** The heroes face whatever conflict has been building from the beginning.

## A World of Disparity: The Overworld and the Underworld

To use the Monomyth turning points properly, you must understand two key concepts.

**The Overworld** is the familiar, everyday, normal world that normal people live in. It's home and hearth, civilization, order, light, the understood and familiar. The important part is that it's the world that normal people understand. They feel safe there. They feel in control. They understand the rules of that world. Roughly speaking, the Overworld is order. It's law. It's a place where the people know all the rules.

**The Underworld** is chaos. It is the unknown, darkness, the frightening, the mysterious, the fantastic. It's "here there be dragons." It doesn't have to be lawless or devoid of rules, but it will seem that way because the rules aren't known or understood. It is a world that defies easy mastery. Roughly speaking, the Underworld is chaos – the world of the unknown, the unexpected, the mysterious. It's also the world of adventure.

These are thematic worlds, not literal ones. D&D has represented these themes in several ways: the Law-vs-Chaos dichotomy, the age-old adventure structure of "leaving town to seek adventure in the wilderness or dungeon, and then coming home."

One of the important aspects of the Monomyth is that the heroes gradually conquer the Underworld – not by killing everything in it, but by eventually mastering it. They come to understand how it works and begin to use its rules to their advantage. Luke Skywalker eventually masters the Force. Harry Potter eventually masters magic. D&D characters start as weak apprentices and eventually become powerful.

### Two Campaign Structures

Most D&D campaigns deal with the Overworld/Underworld in one of two ways:

1. **Ongoing cycle:** The heroes traipse into the Underworld to have adventures and then return to the Overworld on completion. Each new journey takes them farther afield into more fantastic territory.

2. **Extended journey:** The heroes leave the Overworld early and spend most of their time in the Underworld until the end of the campaign. Even the civilized parts of those worlds should still FEEL like the Underworld – the characters should never feel completely comfortable.

## Archetypes

The Monomyth includes archetypes – character templates that fill specific roles in the story. Campbell gave them evocative names (the Goddess, the Dragon, etc.) but archetypes aren't a matter of function; they are a matter of form. A "damsel in distress" has to be a visibly vulnerable, sympathetic character in need of rescue – it doesn't have to be a young woman. It can be anyone or anything that fills the role.

Many archetypes can be classified as "belonging to the Overworld" or "belonging to the Underworld." The Goddess is a being of the Underworld. The Supernatural Helper is generally encountered in the Overworld but has traveled in or is part of the Underworld.

## Life in the Overworld

The heroes begin their stories in the Overworld. Several optional turning points can occur here, after the Call to Adventure:

### Sweetening the Deal / But Thou Must… / A Shove Out the Door

These three turning points are failsafes on the Call to Adventure, designed to make the heroes reconsider if they refused the call. You can't use the same strategy twice, and honestly, if you try more than one, you'll be accused of railroading.

- **Sweetening the Deal:** A scene shows there's another, additional, previously unknown benefit of taking on the adventure.
- **But Thou Must…:** A scene demonstrates that the heroes don't have a choice, or the alternative is so bad they'd be stupid not to accept.
- **A Shove Out the Door:** A sympathetic character shows up and talks the heroes into accepting the call.

### It's Dangerous to Go Alone, Take This. Or Take Me.

Once the heroes have accepted the call but before they've left the Overworld, they usually equip themselves. They might end up with useful information (like a map or riddle), companions, or a useful trinket. All of these things should be of the Overworld. Their companions should be people like them. Information should be iffy – legends, myths, incomplete maps. If anything supernatural is on offer, it should require something extra before it can be used.

### When You Are Ready to Learn, the Teacher Appears

The last thing that might happen in the Overworld – the VERY last thing before the heroes leave – is encountering the **Supernatural Advisor**. They don't have to be magical. But they have to know more about the Underworld than anyone else. They have to be wise and world-weary. They have to have spent time in the Underworld. This is your Gandalf. This is your Obi-Wan Kenobi.

The Supernatural Advisor is generally more powerful than the heroes and must have some reason not to simply do the adventure for them. Their role is to act as a teacher, advisor, and guide. And the best part is, you can kill them later to prove that *It's Personal*.

## Adventures in the Underworld

### Into the Underworld / The Guardian at the Gate

After the heroes leave the Overworld, it is very useful to have a scene that signifies the point at which the heroes have entered the Underworld. This is "crossing the threshold." It can be subtle – it just marks the transition and says, thematically, "okay, this is it, you're on your own now, in a world you don't understand."

Sometimes, entering the Underworld requires resolve, risk, or sacrifice. There might be a **Guardian at the Gate** – a literal monster, a warning, a physical obstacle. The point is to put something in the way that makes the heroes prove they really want to go on this adventure.

### Trials and Tribulations

From this point on, the heroes are in the Underworld. This is where the adventure takes place. Campbell discusses many things here – different types of challenges on the Road of Trials. Eventually, they reach the deepest, darkest part of the story and confront the major conflict, the Supreme Trial. Along the way, the hero might face Temptation, Meet the Goddess, Battle their Brother, Journey through the Night, undergo a Ritual Death or Sacred Marriage, deal with the Devil or the Trickster. It all comes down to "fill your adventure with a wide variety of encounters."

### Out Through the In Door

After the adventure is over, include a bookend to Entering the Underworld – a scene where the heroes are crossing the threshold back into the Overworld. This scene can be subtle. It just marks the transition between the chaos of adventure and the familiar world of home.

### You'll Never Escape

Sometimes, the heroes might face one more obstacle before they leave the Underworld. This is similar to a Guardian at the Gate, but in reverse. It's a useful way to resolve an earlier, unresolved plot arc. It can be a pursuit, a temptation to stay, or consequences catching up.

This scene has to be significant enough to represent a serious threat but CAN'T overshadow the Climax. Make it involve a substantially different obstacle than whatever the climax entailed. For maximum effect, it should involve something the heroes couldn't have done before the adventure, or resolve an emotional conflict connected to the adventure's themes.

## The Complete Turning Point List

> **In the Overworld**
> **(Optional) A New Life:** The heroes' lives start a new course, one that will bring them into contact with adventure.
> **The Call to Adventure:** Something happens to make the heroes aware of a major conflict that they have to resolve and a good reason to want to resolve it.
> **(Optional) Sweetening the Deal/But Thou Must…/A Shove Out the Door:** If the heroes have refused the call to adventure, something happens to offer additional incentive, make it impossible to refuse, or convince them to change their mind.
> **(Optional) It's Dangerous to Go Alone, Take This. Or Take Me:** The heroes are offered mundane assistance in the Overworld – information, allies, or tools. If supernatural, something is required before they can be used.
> **(Optional) The Teacher Appears:** A Supernatural Teacher offers the party aid, advice, and guidance. Though of the Overworld, the Teacher is wise in the ways of the Underworld.
>
> **In the Underworld**
> **(Optional) Entering the Underworld/The Guardian at the Gate:** The party must cross the threshold and enter the Underworld. They might have to prove their resolve by overcoming a minor obstacle or defeating a foe.
> **The Road of Trials:** The heroes encounter all sorts of exciting adventure stuff – obstacles, foes, temptations, allies, boons, and hard choices. This is basically the adventure itself.
> **(Optional) It's Personal:** An event occurs to make the upcoming conflict personal and to make it impossible for the heroes to accept failure.
> **(Optional) The Big Screw/The Final Test:** Something major turns against the heroes and they have to deal with it.
> **The Climax:** The heroes face whatever conflict has been building from the beginning.
> **(Optional) Out Through the In Door/You'll Never Escape:** Having resolved the major conflict, the party leaves the Underworld. However, they might encounter a minor – but significant – obstacle barring their path. One that doesn't overshadow the climax.
