# Breaking the Jell-O Mold: Open Adventure Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/breaking-the-jell-o-mold/)
**Date:** December 30, 2015
**Category:** Adventure Building (Theoretical)

---

## Open Structures

The **open structure** is an adventure where scenes are floating out there — players choose which to engage, when, in what order, and might skip some or fail at some. Players have complete freedom to define the structure.

Think of it like a Jell-O mold dessert: a solid mass of Jell-O with chunks of fruit floating in it. The fruit chunks are the scenes. The PCs swim from chunk to chunk in any order.

---

## Why Sandboxes Suck

When most people think "open adventure," they think of handing PCs a hex map and saying "go find the fun." That ISN'T an open adventure — it isn't an adventure at all. It's missing the three components of an adventure:

1. **Motivation and Resolution** — A race has a finish line, a starting line, and a clear objective. Just pushing someone off a diving board isn't a swimming race.
2. **Structure** — The Jell-O is as important as the fruit. Without it, you're just throwing fruit in a bowl (fruit salad). The PCs must be aware of scenes and able to freely transition between them. Burying fruit in a sandbox means PCs just deal with whatever they stumble on.

**An open structure still has to be an adventure first** — clear resolution, motive, and a way for players to transition between scenes that they understand.

---

## What True Open Adventures Look Like

Adventures involving a series of different tasks adding up to a specific goal:

- **Secure a council vote** — Win favor of enough council members for a majority
- **Recover five crystals from five shrines** — Each shrine has a particular obstacle
- **Appease several nature spirits** — PCs graded on each task
- **Mystery investigations** — Can also be open structures

---

## The Mega Man Problem: Pacing and Narrative

In open adventures, you have no idea what order PCs will confront challenges. You can't build narrative structure into adventure structure. We expect rising tension, rising stakes, building toward a climax — but you can't control the order.

> "You may not have noticed this, but your brain did." — Mr. Plinkett

You don't notice narrative structure consciously, but when it's missing, you don't like it.

### The Mega Man Solution

Each stage had its own narrative and pacing structure — a self-contained story with rising stakes and a climax.

**Every scene in your open adventure must be "tight."** Each scene must be worthy of being the last scene. No throwaway scenes.

**The test:** If you played that ONE scene and then had to cancel the game, would it be a good session? If no, do better.

---

## Optional Scenes and Failure

If all five scenes are required, does the open structure matter? Probably not. **Open adventures benefit most from optional scenes.**

Think about why each scene is optional and how it contributes without being required:

- **Council vote** — Need a majority, not all
- **Nature spirits** — Playing for a high score
- **Murder investigation** — Need enough clues, not all clues
- **Five crystals** — Make each crystal carry a particular power for a weapon; succeed with some, none, or all. Add a deadline (magical eclipse in three days) so whatever they have is what they're stuck with.

> Adventure writing is 10% having an idea that doesn't suck and 90% making that idea into something actually playable.

---

## Making the Jell-O Visible

The structure must be visible to players — they must know what scenes are available and how to reach them:

- Handed a list of councilors
- Spirits announce the tasks
- Investigation includes a list of suspects/witnesses
- Crystal staff comes with a map

**If PCs don't know what's available, they aren't controlling the pace — they're just bumbling around.**

Hidden scenes that require discovery technically break the open structure (creating dependencies between scenes).

---

## Complex Structures: The Jell-O Parfait

**Complex structures** nest the three adventure types inside each other.

### Example: The Crystal Staff Adventure
- Five shrines (open structure)
- Wind shrine = one puzzle (single scene)
- Fire/water shrines = gauntlets (linear)
- Earth/gelatin shrines = mazes with obstacles (branching)
- Starts with a tomb discovery (linear opening)
- Ends with a crystal dragon fight (linear climax)

The open structure is just the middle of a linear adventure.

### How It Works

A single scene ("can the heroes recover the earth crystal?") can be replaced with a tiny adventure:
- **Resolution:** Heroes get the crystal or lose it forever
- **Motivation:** Inherited from the bigger adventure

**Subadventures** have structure but inherit motivation from the parent adventure and pass resolution back to it.

### Why Structure Matters When Nesting

Each structure has its own issues. When nesting, those issues can infect the whole — or you can use one structure's strengths to shore up another's weaknesses:

- **Open inside linear** → Amplifies failure problem but fixes agency problem
- **Linear inside open** → Fixes pacing problem (linear is well-paced by design)

### The Need for Punctuation

We need rises and falls, climaxes and resolutions, clear victories and defeats, tension reset periodically. Build around motivations, resolutions, and structures — not one long continuous superadventure.

---

## Summary

- Open adventures require clear motivation, resolution, and **visible structure**
- Sandboxes without structure are not adventures
- Every scene must be tight enough to stand on its own
- Open adventures benefit greatly from **optional scenes**
- **Complex structures** nest different types to leverage each type's strengths against others' weaknesses
- Every adventure is a dungeon. Every dungeon begins with a map. Every map is basically just a structure.
