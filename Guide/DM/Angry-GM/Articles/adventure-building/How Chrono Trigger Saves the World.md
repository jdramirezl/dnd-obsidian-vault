# How Chrono Trigger Saves the World

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-chrono-trigger-saves-the-world/)
**Date:** December 7, 2022

---

I love video games. And not just because they provide me with fun, immersive gameplay experiences without five mouth-breathing morons on hand to f$&% up my fun. I also love video games because they make me a better GM. And if you want to be a great GM, there's no better media to immerse yourself in than video games. Forget books, forget movies and streaming shows — especially movies and shows produced after 2010; those will make you a worse GM — just play video games. Any video games.

And I mean *just play* video games. Don't play them critically or analytically. Don't watch other people play them. Don't play them and then discuss them. Don't skip playing them and read plot summaries. Just play them. Yourself. It'll make you a better GM.

## Play the Chrono, Save the World

*Chrono Trigger* is one of the best roleplaying video games ever made. It was developed and published in 1995 by Square Co., Ltd. for the Super Nintendo Entertainment System. The development team — nicknamed the Dream Team — comprised the biggest names in the Japanese roleplaying video game industry: Hironobu Sakaguchi (the *Final Fantasy* guy), Yuji Hori (the *Dragon Quest* guy), and Akira Toriyama (the other *Dragon Quest* guy and also the *Dragon Ball Z* guy).

*Save the World* is a name for a particular style of tabletop roleplaying game campaign. An **STW campaign** focuses mainly — but not necessarily exclusively — on a single goal — usually, the resolution of a single, major conflict — and the stakes are usually really high. Take *The Lord of the Rings* — there's a single, major conflict (the Darklordosaurus wants the magical Macguffin and the heroes want to destroy it) and there are very high stakes (if the Darklordosaurus obtains the magical MacGuffin he'll conquer and enslave all the good and nice people of Middle Earth).

STW campaigns are a crapton of fun! The big problem with them — and the big reason people s$&% on them — is that they're not as easy to put together and run as they seem. Done wrong? Well, if you're lucky, you end up with an excuse plot tying a bunch of disconnected adventures together. If you're unlucky, you get a dull, sloggy, disengaging mess that neither you nor your players will ever finish.

*Chrono Trigger* is a near-perfect example of an STW campaign done right. Structurally and narratively, if you rip off *Chrono Trigger* for your TTRPG campaign, you'll have a perfect *Save the World* campaign.

## From Humble Beginnings…

*Chrono Trigger* is a party-based, turn-based, Japanese-style roleplaying video game about a bunch of friends who save the world from Cthulhu. But you wouldn't know that from how the game starts. Because it starts with a teenaged blank-slate protagonist named Crono visiting his kingdom's Millennial Fair and meeting a girl named Marle.

An accident flings Marle back through time. Crono follows, finds himself in the Middle Ages, and tracks Marle to a castle where she's been mistaken for the Queen. Her presence changes history — the Queen's going to die now, which means Marle will never get born. Paradoxed!

Crono and Lucca (a scientific genius friend) rescue the Queen, restore Marle, and return home. But Crono is arrested for kidnapping the princess. The trio can only escape by using another Time Portal — which takes them to the post-apocalypse.

In the future, they find computer records revealing that in 1999 AD, a city-sized lava monster named Lavos burrowed out of the ground and destroyed the world. Marle gives a rousing speech about how she doesn't accept this future and that, with time travel, the heroes should be able to prevent this from ever happening.

**And that's when the game becomes a Save the World campaign. Four hours in and maybe a fifth of the way through the game.**

The party then discovers Lavos was supposedly created by the evil Fiendlord in the Middle Ages. They travel through time — to Prehistory, the Middle Ages, and beyond — acquiring the magical Masamune sword, fighting the Fiendlord, and discovering that Lavos is actually an ancient horror that's been sleeping underground for thousands of years. The heroes continue their investigation, team up with allies across time, and eventually get a flying time ship.

## Lesson One: Campaign Structure

Playing through *Chrono Trigger's* story feels smooth. There's a natural and unbroken progression from one event to the next. If you didn't look very closely, you'd never know there was actually a very solid TTRPG adventure-arc-campaign structure underlying the whole game.

CT's campaign comprises a series of **major chapters or plot arcs**, each of which is further divided into a series of **isolated adventures**. The first plot arc is an introduction — it shows you how time travel works and introduces the main characters and the world.

The structure's masterful, but mostly invisible. Events flow naturally together on the player's side of the screen, but the GM's got everything broken down into arcs and adventures.

## Lesson Two: Nested and Shifting Goals

Goals keep players moving. Without goals, players sit around with their thumbs up their a$&es. But note the plural. One goal isn't really enough to drive a game.

As you play *Chrono Trigger* you always have **three goals**:

1. **Short-term goal** — the thing you're trying to accomplish right now
2. **Mid-term goal** — the thing the short-term goal will help you accomplish
3. **Long-term goal** — which is how you finish the game

Moreover, you always know how your short-term goal relates to your mid-term goal, but you rarely know exactly how the mid-term goal fits into the long-term goal. At least precisely.

The long-term goal is mostly always *prevent Lavos from destroying the world*. The mid-term goals define the different chapters (e.g., *prevent the Fiendlord from creating Lavos*). The short-term goals define individual adventures (e.g., *recover the Masamune*, *break the siege at Zenan Bridge*).

That combination ensures the players always know where they're going and why, but without requiring they know everything. The game can keep an air of mystery without leaving the players totally lost. And that's what allows *Chrono Trigger* to keep moving the goals — after hours of adventure, the heroes defeat the Fiendlord only to discover he didn't *create* Lavos. Later still, the player finds out there's really no way to stop Lavos from existing. The only solution is to destroy Lavos when he does wake up.

## Lesson Three: Never Steal the Win

CT plays a little *shell game* with its long-term goals. Often, what you think is a long-term goal turns out to be a mid-term arc. Every time you think you've found a way to prevent Lavos from existing or waking up, it turns out there's more to the story.

That s$&% can get old. Players don't like the GM yelling, "surprise, what you did was pointless because there's another layer" every time they accomplish something big. That leaves the players feeling like they're not getting anywhere.

But in *Chrono Trigger*, **every victory is still a victory**. The Fiendlord didn't birth Lavos, but he was driving the Monster War in the Middle Ages and now that he's dead, his forces crumble and the war is over. The kingdom is saved. The world is better. That's something to feel good about.

**No goal's futile in *Chrono Trigger*.** New villains don't just fill in for previously defeated villains. It's more like, "that villain didn't cause all the world's woes, but he did cause a lot of them and those woes are over now so good job."

Too many STW games with complex central mysteries fall into the trap of moving the goalposts whenever there's a big reveal and thereby invalidating the good the party has accomplished so far. **When the party ticks off a goal, no matter what else happens, that goal should be a win. And the players have to know it.**

CT also spaces out its victories well. Every two or three gameplay sessions, the party should win in the short term. Every dozen or so sessions, they should win the mid-term.

## Lesson Four: Raise the Stakes; Expand the Scope

Tension measures the amount of uncertainty about and emotional investment in a story's outcome. In good stories, tension wobbles up and down but generally trends upward until it peaks at the story's climax.

The obvious problem with Save the World campaigns is that, when the entire f$%&ing world is already at risk, there's no way to raise the stakes. **That is why *Chrono Trigger* doesn't lead with the whole world-saving thing.** It starts with a little princess rescue and then a timeline restoration and some personal survival and finally a plot about feeding the hungry. So there's plenty of room to raise the stakes.

As more and more of Lavos' true nature gets revealed, the stakes just keep going up:
- First, Lavos is just one mad wizard's attempt to destroy all the humans
- Then, it's an ancient horror exploited by the mad wizard
- Eventually, it's revealed Lavos has woken up and wiped out civilization before — an endless cycle of destruction
- Finally, it turns out Lavos is consuming the world and will launch baby lava clams into space to start the cycle on other planets

As stakes rise, **scope expands**. The first arc shows three time periods. The second introduces Prehistory. The third introduces the Ice Age (with a floating magical sky kingdom). Later, the party gets a flying time machine. The expanded scope makes room for the rising stakes — expanding the scope makes the world *feel* bigger so the world-ending cataclysm will destroy even more cool s$%&.

Each plot arc starts with a major revelation that raises the stakes and adds one new time period to explore. The players need time to process each raise and to settle into their newly expanded world.

## Lesson Five: Everything is Personal

The real reason CT starts small is to give the player time to *care*.

A world-ending cataclysm is just too damned big for the human mind to give a s$&% about. It's abstract. The human mind can't get emotional about it. **People can't care about cities or kingdoms or countries or planets or worlds. People care about people.**

*Chrono Trigger* gives you a lot of people to care about. Lots of NPCs exist just to put a face on the disaster and CT takes the time to humanize them. Kino the Cave Boy is insecure and out to prove himself. Doan the Survivor has lost all hope and given in to despair. The NPCs are the faces of the world, but they're also human faces.

Each of the main characters also has a personal investment in the main story that goes beyond "if the world ends, where will I keep all my stuff?" Each character has a chance to resolve their character arc through a sidequest near the game's end. Each arc is connected to the main story in a deeply personal way, each empowers the character, each allows the character to grow, and each explores one of the game's main themes in a personal, human way.

**This is THE key to running an engaging Save the World game.** The players can't care about the world so the world must have things in it to care about. And they can't just Save the World, they've got to Create a Better World.

There's no formula for this. No checklist. No manual. No mechanics. This thing — the most important thing — rests entirely on the GM's ability to present a world the players can care about. And that ability is an intuitive art.

## Bonus Lesson: Selling the Game Without Giving Everything Away

As a GM, you've got to sell players on your campaign. But you can't do that and also keep the stakes and scope small so they have room to grow.

The *Chrono Trigger* manual demonstrates the perfect level of detail for a campaign pitch — vague about the story, capturing the spirit but not the specifics. And the dialed-down opening gives the pitch a chance to sort of fall out of the player's head.

It's totally fine to sell your campaign with a pitch like this:

> You're a group of unlikely heroes who discover a world-endangering plot involving ancient magic and primordials and stuff and you're the only ones in a position to foil it. Do you want to play that game?

And then launch with:

> The peace of your idyllic village is shattered one morning when someone discovers the mill is overrun with dire rats! Go kill those dire rats!

Provided you run a really fun adventure with engaging characters.
