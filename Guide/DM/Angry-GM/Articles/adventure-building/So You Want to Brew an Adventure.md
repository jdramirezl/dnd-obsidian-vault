# So You Want to Brew an Adventure?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/homebrew-an-adventure/)
**Date:** April 10, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Overview

The introductory article for the "Practical Adventure Building" series. Sets expectations for homebrewing adventures: why you should do it, what an adventure actually is (and isn't), and why published adventures are terrible models for what you need to create.

---

## Why Homebrew?

### The Only Good Reason

The only reason to build your own adventures is that **you enjoy the act of creating them and running them for players.**

- **Not** cheaper or easier than buying modules
- **Not** because published adventures are bad (they're fine)
- **Is** a lot of work, hard, and takes skill you won't have at first
- **Is** deeply satisfying for natural creators and entertainers

### The Right Mindset

- Your work **will** get dirty, broken, and partially unused at the table — that's the point
- Don't worry about saving unused content or being efficient
- Adventure building is where true GMs shine
- World-building is lonely fun (mapsturbation) — most never reaches the table

---

## What an Adventure Is NOT

Published adventures are **terrible models** for homebrewing. They contain massive bloat:

- Maps of entire towns with dozens of described locations
- Rosters of NPCs with detailed backgrounds and motives
- Optional side quests most players never engage with
- Walls of text describing setting details GMs rarely read or use
- Extensive backstory and history

This exists so GMs feel they're getting good value. It's **not what makes the adventure work at the table.**

---

## What an Adventure Actually IS

### Working Definition

> An adventure is a **single, self-contained story** in the life of the heroes focused on a **central conflict or problem**. It starts by presenting a goal or problem and ends when that goal is achieved, lost, or becomes moot.

### Caveats

- Adventures can have multiple plotlines and conflicts
- Adventures aren't actually stories — the story emerges from gameplay
- Until played, an adventure is a **plot** — a sequence of possible, planned events
- The definition is like defining "chair" — hard to nail down, but you know one when you see it

### From a Game Perspective

An adventure is just:
- **A goal** for the heroes to pursue
- **A plan** for how they might pursue it and what might happen along the way

### As a Physical Thing

Everything you need to run it at the table. What you need depends on your skill:
- Experienced GM: one piece of graph paper with a map and scribbled notes
- Newer GM: pages of detailed notes filling binders
- Some GMs write things down just to remember them, never referring to notes again

### What Every Adventure Needs (Usually)

- A **goal** and an **endpoint**
- At least one **conflict**
- **Scenes and encounters** connected together
- A **structure** (shape of connections)
- A **pace** and a **duration**

---

## Three Forms of Published Adventures

1. **Short modules/scenarios:** Softcover booklets (~50 pages) or PDFs. Sometimes connected into adventure paths.
2. **Big honking hardbacks:** Thick tomes with multipart stories. WotC's current format.
3. **Boxed sets:** Fell out of favor. Occasionally making a comeback.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Homebrew because you enjoy it** — not to save money or prove superiority
2. **An adventure is a goal + what happens along the way** — everything else is optional
3. **Don't emulate published adventures** — they're bloated products, not efficient tools
4. **Your needs as a GM determine what goes in your adventure**
5. **Accept that your work will get messy at the table**
6. **The series teaches a process** that empowers you to change it for different results
