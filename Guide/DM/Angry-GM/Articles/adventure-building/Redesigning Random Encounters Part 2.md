# Neither Random Nor Encounter: Redesigning Random Encounters (Part 2)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/redesigning-random-encounters-2/)
**Date:** November 30, 2018
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

In the first article, I explained that random encounters – used properly – create urgency and tension but also emphasize the players' agency. They are a part of a game that is visibly outside the GM's control and outside the planned game balance, and the players' actions directly impact the odds of stumbling across one. The basic system: whenever the players spend time on something or do something reckless, roll a die. If it's a one, they have a random encounter.

That was only half the battle. The rest is about actually designing the encounters.

## What IS a Random Encounter

A good encounter presents a dramatic question and has a source of conflict. A good encounter invites players to make decisions. The game is actually ABOUT making decisions. So how could a list of random monster names be good enough to define a random encounter? Just because they are random doesn't mean they get to NOT be encounters.

At the VERY LEAST, a random encounter table should list things like:
- "A hungry giant spider thinks the party might be good eatin'"
- "A goblin patrol ambushes any non-goblins it encounters and tries to run them out of the goblin's territory"
- "A sinister goblin surrenders to the party and then feeds them false information in the hopes of leading them into a deathtrap"

That at least presents a basic dramatic question and source of conflict.

## Planning the Random

Random encounters don't have to be random encounters. The OCCURRENCE of random encounters has to be random. The actual encounter itself doesn't have to be. That part doesn't face the players.

You don't get anything useful by having a list of potential encounters and randomly determining which one happens. And you actually lose some things if you do. The GM "being surprised" by random encounters is not an actual, valuable thing.

**The key insight:** Figure out how many random encounters you need (generally half as many as planned encounters, never more than about 3-4 per session). Then design exactly that many good encounters. You're never going to use more than four, so WHY WOULD YOU DESIGN TWELVE?

The encounter itself isn't random. What's random is **when** they occur, **where** they occur, and **if** they occur at all. They are basically optional encounters that can happen anywhere at any time.

## Roving Plot Points

Every adventure contains at least a few plot points – reversals, turning points, complications – that can happen at any time in any place. Those are great fodder for random encounters. Because random encounters provide natural tension and connect the players' actions to rising tension, it makes sense that they trigger plot points.

**Example – a four-encounter plot arc for a dungeon with a rival:**

1. The party encounters sabotaged traps or dead, looted monsters. They know someone else is in the dungeon.
2. A chance meeting with the antagonist and his party. There's a tussle. The antagonist runs.
3. A group of monsters ambushes the party – the antagonist set them up.
4. The heroes encounter the antagonist on his way out with the treasure. His monstrous allies jump in to cover his escape.

As the players waste time and do stupid things, victory gets farther from their grasp and the antagonist becomes more dangerous. You turned random encounters into a way of gradually advancing the plot.

## The Narrative Micro Cosmos

Random encounters can tell miniature stories. They present an inciting incident and then present several desirable things that each cost something. Like all good stories, the choices reveal something about the characters involved.

**Design principle:** Start with a simple choice. Figure out why each option is attractive and desirable. Then figure out what the party has to pay to make the choice. That price has to matter.

A random encounter should always – ALWAYS – involve a cost. A random encounter that has an overall good outcome isn't doing its job. Rewards and benefits from random encounters should be minor and should always carry a risk and a cost.

**Examples:**

- **Parched land:** A stretch of barren land lies across the most direct route. Go through (faster but no foraging, little cover) or go around (safer but days out of the way, more potential random encounters).
- **Injured creature:** An injured goblin barely alive. Ignore it, kill it, or spend healing resources on it – it might provide information or might betray them.
- **Injured animal in a trap:** Crazed with pain, it might attack anyone who approaches. Can the party just leave it to die?

## Making Things Complicated

Sometimes, random encounters are just sucky occurrences that make life harder. They use up resources or impose difficulties:
- "Some of your food is infested with worms"
- "You've got leeches"
- "You caught a cold"

Every complication has to happen directly to the party. Otherwise, you lose the vital connection between the players' actions and the random consequences. Random encounters are a very player-facing mechanic.

If you build in a "food goes bad" encounter, build extra opportunities to find more food in the adventure. But simply undoing the encounter ruins the whole system. Giving the players the opportunity to spend resources and come up with clever plans to solve a complication is good. Inflicting a complication and then saying "JK guys, here's some new food" is not.

## The Big Picture: How I Use It

1. **Figure out how many random encounters I need.** Generally half as many as planned encounters. Never more than about three per session.

2. **For short adventures (1-2 sessions):** Use a mix of Complications and Binary Choice encounters. Maybe one Plot Point encounter per session.

3. **For three-session adventures:** Try to work a three-part Plot Point Arc into the list. Spread them out so they don't all happen at once. The last item on the list is usually the conclusion of the arc.

4. **For multi-environment adventures:** Come up with different random encounters for different parts. Usually one or two extras as backup.

5. **Compile into a list and work through them in order.** Carefully spread out Plot Points. Adjust encounters slightly for logic – if the party makes a loud noise and triggers a roll, that results in a monster ambush type complication.

6. **Sometimes, just show up with four "hungry monster wants to eat you" combats.** Because that crap is actually fun too. And sometimes, a good fight is all the game really needs.
