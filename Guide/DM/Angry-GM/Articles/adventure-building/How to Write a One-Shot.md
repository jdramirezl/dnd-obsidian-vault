# Ask Angry: How to Write a One-Shot

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-write-a-one-shot/)
**Date:** November 30, 2016
**Category:** Adventure Building

---

## Terminology

A **one-shot adventure** is entirely self-contained. It doesn't build on, establish, or exist in the middle of an ongoing story. Players come in blindly with no knowledge of prior events. When done, win or lose, they never see those characters again.

A one-shot can be played over multiple sessions. But if you're limiting yourself to one block of time with no follow-up, that's a **single-session adventure**.

Single-session adventures DO NOT have to be one-shots. You can build an entire campaign around playing a different single-session adventure every week — great for groups with irregular attendance.

---

## One-Shot Adventures

An adventure begins with a motivation, ends with a resolution, and contains scenes linked by structure. By strict definition, every adventure IS self-contained. But most GMs let the edges get fuzzy over time — motivations established in prior adventures, unresolved endings, dangling plot threads.

In a one-shot, the GM can't do ANY of that:

1. **Must begin with clear motivations** for players and characters.
2. **Must end with a clear, unambiguous resolution.**
3. **Any questions the adventure establishes must be answered** (or at least answerable) by the end.

### Motivating Characters

Players must understand the goal, why it's important, and why it'll be fun — from the outset. The motivation must work for ALL characters. You can't count on any history or relationships except what you explicitly write in.

**Two approaches:**
1. **Create characters yourself and hand them out.** Each character gets a motivation matching the adventure.
2. **Give specific instructions about the premise** when asking players to make characters (e.g., "you will be rescuing a princess from a dragon — explain why your character is willing to do this").

### Chekhov's Gun

Be careful not to pique curiosity about things that aren't important. If there's an interesting bit of lore, a character with mysterious motives, or a backstory — it MUST be paid off during the adventure. **Don't include any details that won't pay off IN THAT ADVENTURE.**

Same for mechanical elements:
- Less treasure needed (players won't spend it later)
- No magic items that won't be useful in the current adventure
- Assume ANY magic item given WILL be used — don't include anything that breaks the adventure
- XP doesn't matter

## Single-Session Adventures

Two key challenges:
1. Every minute of game time is a limited resource.
2. The game MUST be finished by the end of the session — no matter what.

**The players MUST see the resolution.** If you run out of time, you can't skip the ending without ruining the adventure.

### Structure Constraints

Single-session adventures CANNOT be player-driven. Most are **GM-driven** with constrained structures — **linear or branching** that loops back:
- Both paths lead to the same conclusion
- No dead-ends requiring backtracking
- No false leads or red herrings
- **Every path must lead forward**

### Failure States

If the adventure can fail, failure must be very obvious. **Time constraints work wonderfully:**
- The ritual completes in six hours
- The princess dies in 24 hours
- The dark god awakens at midnight

The GM controls the flow of time and can synch it with session time remaining.

### The Greatest Secret: The Mutable Adventure

**Write the beginning and the ending, then have a pile of scenes you can add or remove in the middle.**

Great formats:
- **Road adventures** — Get from A to B; skip to the end when time runs low
- **Chase adventures** — Follow something and catch it before it arrives
- **Race adventures** — Get somewhere before someone else
- **Escort adventures** — Keep a person/thing alive/intact

### Constrained Content Alternative

Design exactly enough content to fill the time.

**Typical time estimates (modern D&D/Pathfinder):**
| Element | Time |
|---|---|
| Combat | ~1 hour |
| Non-combat obstacle/puzzle | ~30 minutes |
| Interaction scene | ~15 minutes |
| Adventure beginning | ~15 minutes |
| Adventure resolution | ~15 minutes |

**4-hour session example:** Beginning (15m) + Resolution (15m) + Climactic battle (1h) + Two battles (2h) + One obstacle (30m) = 4 hours

You can build complex branching adventures using game time as a resource — if the PCs defeat an obstacle they go to Path A, if they fail they go to Path B, both paths equal in time.

### Minimize Non-Game Time

- Exposition: minimum
- World building: waste of time in single-session adventures
- Setting: as generic as possible
- Quest-giving scene: as abbreviated as possible — deliver as brief exposition

**Anything that isn't game should be kept to a minimum.**

---

## Summary

- **One-shot adventures** must be self-contained — motives, resolution, nothing else.
- **Single-session adventures** must provide a way to control time flow, skip to the end, or be carefully designed around the clock.
- Every minute of game time is too valuable to waste on anything that isn't playing the actual game.
