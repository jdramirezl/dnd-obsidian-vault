# Plot Architecture 101

## Key Advice

**Plot structure** — the pattern of phases a plot moves through — is essential for RPGs, even more so than for books or movies. Structure helps players understand goals, make strategic decisions, and trust the GM. It empowers player agency rather than restricting it. Understanding plot structure also makes GMs better at both planning and improvising.

## Summary

Angry lays the groundwork for a series on plot structure, explaining what it is, why it matters, and why RPGs need it more than other media. This is a foundational/introductory article — specific structures (three-act, five-point, kishotenketsu) are promised for future articles.

**What is plot structure?**
- A plot is just a sequence of events in a story. Structure is the *pattern* or *shape* of those events.
- Most good stories follow recognizable patterns — not in what happens, but in what *kinds* of things happen *when*.
- Plots are like houses: every house is different, but most follow intuitive patterns (front door → living area, kitchen → dining room, bedrooms deeper inside). Plots have similar intuitive patterns.
- Our brains have been trained by 50,000 years of storytelling to expect certain plot shapes. Stories that fit these shapes are easier to digest, keep audiences invested, and feel cohesive.

**Why structure matters:**
- **Audience expectations** — Structure promises patterns will happen. Audiences know something will disrupt the hero's normal life. If nothing does, they check out ("the movie was slow to get started").
- **Cohesion** — Structure ensures events don't feel random. Human brains are pattern-recognition machines that will invent nonexistent patterns rather than accept chaos. Structure provides a skeleton for the brain to hang narrative details on.
- **Managed surprise** — Structure doesn't preclude surprises; it ensures surprises come at the right times. Well-structured plots foreshadow twists so audiences blame themselves for not seeing them coming, rather than blaming the author.

**Why RPGs need structure MORE than other media:**
- In books/movies, the author controls everything. If the audience gets lost, the story keeps moving and things eventually make sense.
- In RPGs, the audience IS the main characters. If the story is incoherent, the *characters* get lost and the *players* can't make smart choices.
- Good plot structure **empowers players** — it establishes goals (which every game needs), enables planning and strategy, and fosters trust between players and GM.
- Counterintuitively, plot structure *increases* the sense of player agency.

**Planned plot vs. actual plot:**
- Smart GMs distinguish between the plot they planned and the plot that actually happens. They don't force the planned plot, but they don't abandon planning either.
- Good structure makes planned and actual plots converge naturally. Players are motivated by the structure itself to pursue the things the GM prepared for.
- Example: Your *Dragonslayers for Hire!* adventure starts with a devastated village (motivation), reveals the dragon's hoard (incentive), and introduces a deadline with children's lives at stake (urgency). This isn't railroading — it's structure doing its job.
- Understanding structure also makes improvisation better. GMs who understand what kinds of events belong at what point in a plot can pull new details out of thin air and have them feel right.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Accept that plot structure is a tool, not a cage** — it empowers players and improves games rather than restricting freedom.
2. **Plan your plots** — distinguish between the planned plot and what actually happens. Planning makes you prepared; flexibility makes you adaptive. Both are needed.
3. **Use structure to motivate players** — establish goals, provide urgency, and create stakes early. This isn't railroading; it's giving players the context they need to make meaningful choices.
4. **Learn what kinds of events go where** — introductions happen early, stakes escalate in the middle, confrontations happen near the end. Knowing this helps both planning and improvisation.
5. **Trust that structure increases agency** — when players understand the shape of the story, they can plan, strategize, and make informed decisions. That's more agency, not less.
6. **Don't confuse structure with rigidity** — structure is a pattern, not a script. The same structure can produce wildly different stories depending on player choices.

> [!tip] The House Analogy
> Plots are like houses. Every house is different, but most follow intuitive patterns — front doors open into living areas, kitchens connect to dining rooms, bedrooms are deeper inside. You won't walk through a front door and step in a toilet. Plot structure works the same way: it's the intuitive pattern that helps audiences (and players) navigate the story without getting lost.
