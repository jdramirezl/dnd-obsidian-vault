# A Very Special Adventure

## Key Advice

A true holiday adventure must be **about** the themes of the holiday, not just decorated with its trappings. The difference between a Christmas *movie* and a movie *set at Christmas* is whether the story's central conflict and resolution are driven by holiday themes like family, community, charity, hope, and faith. Apply the same distinction to your adventures.

## Summary

Angry dissects what makes a holiday story actually *about* the holiday versus merely using it as backdrop or decoration:

- **Backdrop** (Die Hard, Gremlins): Christmas is used for juxtaposition and contrast. The story isn't about Christmas themes.
- **Decoration** (romantic holiday comedies): Christmas amplifies emotions but the story is about something else (romance, comedy).
- **True Holiday Story** (A Christmas Carol, Home Alone, Christmas with the Kranks): The central conflict involves holiday themes being twisted, corrupted, or forgotten, and the resolution requires those themes to be embraced.

He identifies the core Christmas themes as: **family, community, charity/compassion, hope/faith/optimism**. A true holiday adventure must have its central conflict rooted in these themes and must ultimately affirm them as positive values.

Key structural elements for holiday adventures:
- The adventure needs an **in-world holiday** (an expy for Christmas) — ideally established on the game calendar well in advance.
- The resolution can include a **earned deus ex machina** — a miraculous turn that rewards the characters for embracing the right values.
- Holiday adventures are essentially **morality plays** — resolution depends on choices and virtues more than dice rolls.
- Add NPCs and subplots that represent or test different holiday themes (like the old man in the church in Home Alone).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Identify 3–4 core themes** of the holiday you're building around (e.g., family, community, charity, hope for a winter holiday).
2. **Root the central conflict in those themes** — something has been twisted, corrupted, or forgotten. Don't just add snow and wreaths to a normal adventure.
3. **Create an in-world holiday** and establish it on your game calendar months in advance so it doesn't feel like a sudden "very special episode."
4. **Add NPCs who embody or test the themes** — a bitter priest, a generous stranger, a community in conflict. Each should give the PCs a chance to engage with a holiday value.
5. **Allow an earned miraculous resolution** — if the PCs embrace the right values, reward them with something extraordinary. The miracle must be earned through choices, not just dice.
6. **Prepare for improvisation** — holiday adventures are morality plays where player choices matter more than combat. Have multiple thematic threads ready so you can steer toward whichever theme the players engage with.

> [!tip] The Die Hard Test
> Ask yourself: "If I removed the holiday from this adventure, would anything change?" If the answer is no, you've only used the holiday as decoration. A true holiday adventure *breaks* if you remove the holiday, because the themes of the holiday ARE the themes of the adventure. The conflict can't exist without them and the resolution depends on them.
