# Every Adventure's a Dungeon

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/every-adventures-a-dungeon/)
**Date:** April 22, 2015
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## The Planning Dilemma

It's okay to plan adventures. It's okay to wing it. It's okay to plan the broad strokes but improvise the individual scenes. As long as the players have decisions to make and those decisions carry consequences, it's completely okay.

> The only difference between planning and improvising is that, when you're improvising, you've only got a few seconds to plan so you'd better be good at planning in seconds. That's it. You're still planning.

## The Dungeon: Where the Medium Becomes the Message

Dave Noonan once defined a dungeon as **a place where the structure of the game becomes more obvious.** That's the best definition ever.

In gaming parlance, a dungeon is a location in which the heroes wander from location to location on the map freely. At various locations, they encounter encounters — obstacles, monsters, treasure, traps, tricks, riddles, whatever.

A dungeon is just an **interconnected pile of encounters.** A dungeon map is just a map of encounters and how those encounters are connected. Add the reason why the party is going in and imagine one encounter has the goal at the other end — you have a complete adventure: **motivation, resolution, and structure** holding together a pile of encounters.

## The Sleight of Tentacle: Maniac Mansion

Using the classic LucasArts adventure game *Maniac Mansion*, Angry demonstrates the most important point about adventure structure:

### The Mansion as a Dungeon Map

*Maniac Mansion* is basically one big puzzle dungeon. You wander from location to location, picking up items, encountering NPCs, and solving puzzles. It can be drawn as a dungeon map — rooms connected by doorways and stairs.

But you can also represent it as a **flowchart** — room names connected by lines. Both maps are functionally equivalent.

### The Mansion as a Plot Flowchart

Reframing the map in terms of **encounters** rather than rooms:

- **Porch:** Front door is locked. Ring the doorbell → Weird Ed sends you away. Find the hidden key → enter the front hall.
- **Front Hall:** Go to the kitchen → Nurse Edna chases you (caught = dungeon). Go upstairs → Green Tentacle blocks your path (give right food = pass, otherwise sent back).

This encounter-based flowchart is **functionally the same** as the room-based map.

### The Murder Investigation

Now imagine a murder investigation adventure:

- **Crime Scene:** Search for evidence. Find a bum in the alley (dead end — he was passed out). Find a matchbook from Club Hapgood (lead).
- **Club Hapgood:** Front door → bouncer blocks path (bribe to pass). Back alley → open window → bodyguard confrontation (hide or fight; knocked out = interrogation by Don LeChuck).

Map this as a flowchart and **it looks exactly like the Maniac Mansion encounter flowchart.** The structures are identical.

## Every Adventure is a Dungeon Adventure

> The truth of the matter is that every adventure is a dungeon adventure. An adventure's structure just shows how the scenes and encounters are interconnected. But you can map ANY adventure as a dungeon.

The hallways between rooms in the dungeon are not really hallways at all. They are just **transitions** — the thing a GM says to move the game from one scene to another:

> "You walk down the eastern hallway. After thirty feet, you come to a big room filled with heads in alcoves."

> "You drive across town to Club Hapgood. You find a parking space, feed the meter, and check out the club."

> "You engage the hyperdrive and the stars streak out ahead of your ship..."

Hallways, lines on a flowchart, roads on a map, or hexes of empty space between planets — **it's all the same.**

### But Dungeons Are More Constrained?

Not really. The party in a murder investigation follows leads and clues — just like following doors in a dungeon. When you put the matchbook for Club Hapgood in the scene, that's no different from putting a door that leads from Crime Scene to Club Hapgood.

If the party decides to go somewhere unexpected, you just add a line and a box. Either in your head or on your actual notes.

> No matter how "sandboxy" your adventure might be, you're still transitioning from scene to scene, from encounter to encounter, you're still following a map. And you're still offering leads, exits, to new scenes.

## You Can Already Build an Adventure

Between this article and the previous one, you have enough to build an adventure:

- You need **motivation** (why the party is doing this)
- You need **resolution** (the goal/ending)
- You need **structure** (how encounters connect)
- You need **encounters**

Structure is just about how the party moves from encounter to encounter and scene to scene. If you can draw squares and lines and say "this is why you're here" and figure out where the ending is, you can build an adventure.
