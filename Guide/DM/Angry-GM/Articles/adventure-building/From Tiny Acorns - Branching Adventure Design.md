# From Tiny Acorns: Branching Adventure Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/from-tiny-acorns-branching-adventure-design/)
**Date:** January 13, 2016
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## The Most Common Structure

Branching structure is the most common structure for an RPG adventure. Each scene is connected to a few other scenes, such that players can choose a route through the adventure. Unlike an open structure, choices are constrained. The classic example is the dungeon crawl: each room has a few different exits. But a murder mystery can have the same branching structure — leads at a crime scene each represent transitions to other scenes.

Branching structure is a compromise between the totally unconstrained open adventure and the totally constrained linear adventure. It strikes a balance between choice and constraint.

## The Terror of the Blank Page

Most people do best when they choose from a limited list of options. Drop people in a void and most have trouble getting started. Constrained choices make things easier for players and GMs alike.

Most GM prep is about balancing agency vs. constraints. Create a game that offers freedom of choice but is constrained enough to be manageable. Branching structures straddle that middle line nicely.

## Where Structure Becomes Challenge

Branching structures combine agency from open adventures with constraints from linear adventures to create something unique. Part of the challenge can become navigating the adventure itself — finding the most efficient path that minimizes contact with traps and minotaurs.

## Exploration vs. Pursuit

- **Exploration adventures:** The party wanders scene to scene hoping to stumble on the endpoint. The goal is simply to navigate the structure.
- **Pursuit adventures:** The party knows their goal and chooses scenes based on which are most likely to let them accomplish it. The challenge is navigating the structure efficiently.

**Pursuit adventures are always better than exploration adventures** — even if the players don't know they are in one. This has to do with pacing.

## The Hidden Pursuit and the Ballooning Pyramid

Exploration adventures have pacing problems — you can't know how players will navigate. Pursuit adventures fix this: because the party moves generally forward, you know which scenes come early and which come late.

**Pyramid construction:**
- Early in the adventure: lots of choices about where to go
- Closer to the goal: paths converge
- Fewer choices until everything converges on a resolution

**The Ballooning Pyramid:** Most adventures start with one scene (the hook). The first few scenes expand options with lots of transitions. Then, as the party gets deeper, transitions contract. The adventure expands then contracts — like a diamond shape.

You can structure any adventure as a ballooning pyramid whether the party knows it or not. This turns an exploration adventure into a pursuit adventure without telling the players — they get free exploration while you get pacing control.

## Bottlenecks and Dead-Ends

### Dead Ends

A scene with no transitions out of it. Dead ends serve purposes:
- **In search adventures:** Every eliminated possibility is useful
- **In navigation adventures:** A major setback — a bad choice that costs the party

Use sparingly and telegraph them with clues.

### Bottlenecks

A scene the players HAVE TO pass through. All paths converge there, then diverge on the other side. Bottlenecks are **insanely useful:**
- Convey important information
- Introduce characters or concepts
- Place the recurring villain where the party will encounter them
- Ensure the party has proper resources for later challenges

Many GMs wrongly say "never have a bottleneck" — but they are powerful tools when you understand how they can go wrong.

## Failure in Branching Adventures

Failure in any given scene is easier to deal with than in linear adventures — multiple paths exist. But:
- Players can fail enough to cut off all possibility of success — make sure they can KNOW when that happens
- **Bottleneck failure:** You must plan what happens when players fail at a bottleneck, since there's no other path

A bottleneck can have a way out that corresponds with failure — additional scenes to get the same information they would have gotten from succeeding. Understanding failure handling makes bottlenecks one of the most powerful tools in adventure design.
