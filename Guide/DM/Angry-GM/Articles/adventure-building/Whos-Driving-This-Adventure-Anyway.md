# Who's Driving This Adventure Anyway

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/whos-driving-this-adventure-anyway/)
**Date:** November 10, 2016
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building

---

## The Dynamics of Plot

Every adventure joins a bunch of scenes together, providing a motivation to push players and characters to navigate scenes until they reach a resolution. The core components: **motivation**, **structure**, **scenes**, **resolution**.

GMs plan scenes in advance (encounters and non-encounter scenes serving different purposes). Players also create scenes by doing unexpected things, forcing the GM to improvise.

The overlooked concept: **Plot Dynamics** — who is actually driving the plot forward.

---

## Player Driven vs. GM Driven

Plot Dynamics exist on a **spectrum** based on who drives the plot. Not a binary — most adventures have elements of both.

### GM Driven Plot

- Players have limited control over the sequence of events
- Simplest example: the obstacle course / gauntlet adventure — heroes deal with each obstacle in turn
- Event-based games are also GM Driven — things happen at prescribed times/conditions and heroes react
- GM Driven plots aren't automatically linear — the adventure can branch based on outcomes
- **Key distinction:** Just because players change the outcome doesn't mean they're driving the plot. If players are basically reacting to events, the GM is driving

### Player Driven Plot

- Players have complete control over the sequence of events
- Simplest example: the heist adventure — infiltrating a stronghold, assassinating someone, robbing a casino
- Players freely gather information, plan operations, and execute however they see fit
- Exercises in creative problem solving — investigate, determine resources needed, acquire them, execute
- The GM is stuck reacting/improvising to whatever the players decide
- Not necessarily more open-ended or branching than GM Driven plots

---

## No Pure Dynamics

It's almost impossible to create a purely Player Driven or purely GM Driven adventure:

- Players can seize control of the dynamic with a single unexpected choice
- Most GMs keep at least a light hand on the rudder
- Most adventures involve push and pull

**Different levels can have different dynamics:**
- An individual scene can be GM Driven (orcs leap from underbrush — react!) or Player Driven (PCs have element of surprise — choose approach)
- A GM Driven plot can contain many Player Driven scenes

---

## Who Drives the Dungeon

Classic dungeon crawl example:
- **Seems** Player Driven (players are exploring) but **is actually** GM Driven — players react to room contents, choose from limited undifferentiated paths
- It's a branching obstacle course

**But** if players have a specific task (e.g., find lost adventurers quickly), the adventure veers Player Driven — they choose search patterns, use tracking, employ scrying magic, etc.

---

## Why It Matters

Player Driven and GM Driven adventures:
1. **Do different things** — create different experiences
2. **Are designed in different ways** — require different prep approaches
3. **Work better for different player groups** — match to your table

---

## Growing into Player Driven Adventures

Player Driven adventures put pressure on **both** players and GM:

### Pressure on Players
- Not all players handle proactive roles well
- Players staring gormlessly at each other = can't handle driving the plot (nothing wrong with that)
- Many players prefer a reactive role — the challenge of overcoming obstacles in pursuit of a goal

### Pressure on the GM
- Must be quick on your toes — lots of improvisation
- Unlike players (limited to one character's actions), the GM reacts with an entire world
- Easy to get overwhelmed with so many pieces and moves

### When Player Driven Works Best
- **Experienced gamers** handle the pressure better
- **Expressive players** who wander afield and come up with unusual plans
- **Familiarity with the world** — players need to know what's possible, what resources exist, how to find things
- **Genre familiarity** — police procedurals, superhero games, Lovecraftian investigations all have conventions players need to know

### Natural Progression
- Most groups start GM Driven and grow into Player Driven over a long campaign
- As players become familiar with the world, system, and genre, they become more capable of driving
- Character advancement provides tools for a more active role
- Mirrors character power progression: young heroes react to local problems → experienced characters take control of world events

---

## There Is Nothing Wrong with GM Driven Adventures

- Easier to run, play, and plan
- Many players enjoy them
- As long as players can affect outcomes through choices and consequences, agency is preserved
- Many experienced groups never move beyond mostly GM Driven — and nobody is sadder for it
- Sneering at GM Driven plots = not understanding that different people play for different reasons

## How to Design Each Type

### Angry's Law of Plot Dynamics

> **GM Driven Adventures** provide the players with scenes and events to react to.
> **Player Driven Adventures** provide the players with opportunities to plan and resources to use.

### GM Driven Design (the default)
- Plan a flowchart of scenes and encounters
- Design the encounters
- Decide when and where scenes occur
- Could be a dungeon map, murder investigation, or timeline of events

### Player Driven Design
- Planning scenes is a waste of time — you won't know which ones will happen
- Instead: **detailed world building**
- Know the layout, defenses, NPC stats, weaknesses, faction responses
- For a heist: temple layout, defenses, cultist stats, idol location, security weaknesses
- For political intrigue: supporters/opponents, dirty secrets, motivations, fears, locations, protections
- The more you know about all the moving parts, the better you can react to whatever the players do
- Running a Player Driven adventure = making up a dungeon as you go with a pile of prepared elements

### Accept the Inevitable
- Players will eventually do something you're not prepared for
- The more you DO know, the better you can make up what you don't
- Then you can go back to nice, simple GM Driven adventures — because it's just easier
