# The Monsters Know Better Than You What They're Doing

## Core Thesis

Monster stat blocks tell you **what happens at the table** — not what monsters can do, must do, or why they do it. They are game code, not character sheets. When you play monsters to win using their stat blocks, their personality and behavior emerge naturally from the design. The best way to play a monster is the way its stat block rewards.

> "You are executing a game. You are not roleplaying monsters."

## The Monsters Know What They're Doing

Reference to Keith Amman's blog [The Monsters Know What They're Doing](https://www.themonstersknow.com) — tactical advice for running D&D monsters. The title's hidden insight: monsters already know how to use their abilities. GMs who dumb down monsters because of low Intelligence scores are sabotaging their own games.

### Intelligence ≠ Tactical Acumen

- **Sentience gives higher potential** for tactics, but undeveloped intelligence is useless. Most GMs and players aren't tactical geniuses either.
- Intelligence helps with **strategy** (long-term, complex value judgments), not moment-to-moment **tactics** (fight-or-flight decisions).
- Spiders have 300+ million years of evolved hunting prowess. Zombies are optimized for consumption by the evil spirits animating them.
- **Don't play monsters stupid.** Playing stupid isn't realistic — it just makes your game worse.

> "Build your combat encounters fair, but play them to win."

## The Shackled Hag — A Case Study

### The Setup

A player's party had *dimensional shackles* (block teleportation) and was about to fight an **arch-hag** (MM 2024) without the anathema needed to kill her. The GM worried players would shackle the hag to prevent her *spiteful escape*.

### The Analysis

*Dimensional shackles* only block teleportation. They don't bind, restrict, or incapacitate. If the hag drops to 0 HP without her anathema present:

1. She **doesn't die** (she can only be destroyed near her anathema)
2. She drops to **1 HP** instead
3. She would normally teleport away — but can't with shackles on
4. She **stays**, furious, at 1 HP, still able to claw, cast lightning, curse, hypnotize, and counter spells

> The players haven't found a cheaty win — they've trapped an unkillable, furious immortal in their proximity. They miscalculated badly.

### The Deeper Question: Would a Hag Shackle Herself?

Mechanically, a hag could wear *dimensional shackles* voluntarily to prevent her own *spiteful escape*, making her an unkillable combatant who never has to leave. **She would never do this.** Not because the rules forbid it, but because:

- *Spiteful escape* isn't a compulsion — it's a **choice the hag always makes**. The stat block tells the GM this must happen, meaning the GM doesn't get to override her decision.
- Arch-hags don't like fighting. They prefer torment, psychological torture, cursing over killing.
- Even unkillable immortals fear being trapped (one lucky *hold monster* + shackles = buried in an oubliette forever).
- The 2d6 days before she can return isn't a magical lockout — it's how long she **chooses** to hide before feeling safe.

## Stat Blocks as Game Code

### What Stat Blocks Actually Tell You

The arch-hag's *spiteful escape* text:

> "When the hag drops to 0 Hit Points, it dies only if it is within 30 feet of its anathema. Otherwise, the hag drops to 1 Hit Point and teleports to a harmless demiplane, and it can't return to the plane it left for 2d6 days. When the hag teleports away, each creature within 60 feet is cursed."

Breaking this down step by step:
1. **Doesn't die** at 0 HP without anathema (independent fact)
2. **Drops to 1 HP** (prevents incapacitation — happens before teleportation)
3. **Teleports away** (separate action, blocked by shackles)
4. **Can't return for 2d6 days** (meaningless if she didn't leave)
5. **Curses on teleport** (conditional on teleporting — "when" means "if")

The description says her "preparations **allow** it to magically slip away" — she's not forced, she's enabled. The invulnerability comes first; the escape is a behavioral choice the stat block encodes.

### Personality Through Mechanics

Good monster design encodes personality into stat blocks. Examples:

- **Berserkers** (MM 2024 p.37): Advantage on attacks and saves when bloodied. Galaxy-brain play: you *want* your berserkers bloodied, so don't avoid opportunity attacks. Berserkers can't be controlled by threatening them — that's how berserkers behave.
- **Blood hawks** with *pack tactics*: cluster attacks on single targets.
- **Mammoths**: charge to knock down, then trample the prone target.
- **Ghouls**: First ghouls *claw* to paralyze; subsequent ghouls double-*bite* paralyzed targets (advantage + doubled damage).

> "If you play the monster to win, it will behave as that monster should. The better you are at playing to win, the more personality the monsters have."

## Protecting Your Game from You

### The Optimizer Voice

When the GM's inner optimizer whispers "you could combine this monster with this item and…" — be very suspicious. More suspicious than you'd be of a player with a third-party splatbook.

> "The devil quotes scripture, and he quotes it in your voice."

### Guard Rails in Design

D&D monster design operates at three levels of enforcement:

1. **Advice and flavor text** — lowest stakes, easily ignored (most of the DMG)
2. **Tactics/synergies built into stat blocks** — medium stakes, followed naturally by playing to win
3. **Hard rules, guard rails, and failsafes** — highest stakes, for game-breakingly important situations (like *spiteful escape* preventing an hours-long unwinnable slog)

*Spiteful escape* is a **failsafe**. It protects against:
- GMs who skip the lore and run an arch-hag as a standard boss fight
- Players who don't realize they can't win and refuse to flee
- Hours-long stalemates where no one is having fun

### The Social Encounter Parallel

> "You don't play the non-player characters; you're not playing a roleplaying game. You resolve actions and describe the results."

Same applies to monsters in combat. You make tactical choices within the rules, but when the rules take a firmer hand and say something must happen, the outcome is what it is. Your job is to make it make sense to the players.

## Key Takeaways for GMs

1. **Play monsters to win** — their personality emerges from optimal play of their stat blocks
2. **Don't override stat block behavior** because you think you're smarter than the design
3. **Stat blocks describe what happened**, not the full range of what's possible — explain the "why" in narration
4. **Intelligence doesn't equal tactics** — even mindless creatures use their abilities effectively
5. **Be suspicious of your own optimization impulses** — if you're combining monster abilities with items to create unbeatable combos, you're probably breaking something
6. **Arch-hag anathema = epic-level challenge design** — when characters have mountain-moving abilities, game mechanics alone can't challenge them; you must challenge the *players* to make and execute plans
