# How to Run a Biblical Campaign

## Summary

Angry explains the **campaign bible** — a personal, ugly, sloppy reference document that tracks your campaign's facts, plans, and history. Borrowed from TV/film screenwriting, a campaign bible serves three purposes: **Consistency** (never contradict established facts), **Cohesion** (new content fits the existing world), and **Creativity** (random browsing sparks new ideas). The article covers the three-part structure (roadmap, adventure summaries, fact sheets) and four critical mistakes GMs make with their bibles. The key insight: your bible is an explorer's journal, not a published setting guide. Write only what has been established in play, keep it brief, and read it often.

## Key Advice

- **A campaign bible is a personal reference document** — not a setting guide, not player-facing, not pretty prose
- **The Three C's:** Consistency (stick to established facts), Cohesion (new content fits old content), Creativity (random browsing sparks new connections)
- **Four mistakes to avoid:**
  1. Writing it for others to read (it's only for you)
  2. Not reading it (write AND re-read regularly)
  3. Writing it like a book (use bullet points, sentence fragments, lists — not prose)
  4. Over-organizing it (sloppy organization promotes creative collisions)
- **Only record facts that emerged during play.** Until you say something to the players, it isn't true. Unspoken backstory locks you into decisions prematurely.
- **Keep entries minimal.** The less you write about an NPC or location, the more flexibly you can use them later.
- **Three-part structure:**
  1. **Roadmap** — campaign plan, plot points, or random adventure seeds
  2. **Adventure Summaries** — one page per adventure, bullet-point events and facts learned
  3. **Fact Sheets** — lists of NPCs, locations, factions, etc. with key facts only
- **Creativity comes from random collisions.** Periodically browse your bible without looking for anything specific. When unrelated facts bump into each other in your brain, new ideas emerge.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Get a simple notebook** (or basic document) with three sections: Roadmap, Summaries, Fact Sheets
2. **After each session**, write a one-page bullet-point summary of significant events and facts the players learned
3. **Add NPCs/locations to a running list** with 2-3 key facts each. Only give them a full page when they accumulate too many facts.
4. **Only write down things the players actually learned.** No backstory that hasn't been revealed.
5. **Read your bible randomly** between sessions — flip to a random page and just read. Let ideas collide.
6. **Resist the urge to organize.** Don't color-code, cross-reference, or build a wiki. Keep it sloppy enough that you stumble across things you weren't looking for.
7. **Keep the roadmap minimal** — a goal, key plot points, and a few adventure seeds. Don't over-plan.
8. **Never share your bible.** If players need setting info, create a separate, minimal handout.

> [!tip] The Explorer's Journal
> Treat your campaign world as Terra Incognita — undiscovered territory that you explore through play. Your campaign bible is your explorer's journal, not an encyclopedia. Start it nearly empty and fill it as you discover your world session by session. The less you pre-define, the more creative freedom you retain. An NPC with only "works for the Firemagian government" written down can become anything later — a loyal spymaster, a bitter traitor, a secret double agent. An NPC with a full backstory can only ever be that one thing.
