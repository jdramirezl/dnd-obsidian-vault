# How to F$%& CR: A Practical Example of Monster Building the Angry Way

## Summary

Angry walks through his alternative monster-building system using a practical zombie example — both as a **reskin** of the existing Monster Manual zombie and as a **bespoke build from scratch**. The system replaces CR with three axes: **Tier** (level range), **Threat** (low/medium/high), and **Organization** (solo/pair/group/gang/mob). The article demonstrates how to read stats from a reference table, adjust for concept, and design interesting monsters that force players to change tactics — not just bags of hit points with a punch attack.

## Key Advice

- **Every monster needs three things:** a Tier (level range), a Threat level (low/medium/high), and an Organization type (how many appear together)
- **Tier defines level range:** Apprentice (1-2), Journeyman (3-5), Adventurer (6-8), Veteran (9-11), Champion (12-14), Heroic (15-17), Legendary (18+)
- **Threat is a design focus tool, not a balance calculator.** It helps you aim during design and catch over/under-tuned monsters. Medium threat (-1 to +1) is the sweet spot for most monsters.
- **Organization defines complexity:**
  - Solo = complex, self-sufficient, multiple strategies
  - Gang/Mob = simple, few choices, teamwork abilities
  - The formula: 1 Solo = 2 Pairs = 4 Group = 8 Gang = 16 Mob
- **Aim for medium threat almost always.** GMs tend to overpower monsters because they forget D&D is attrition-based.
- **Stat ratings (Poor/Average/Good) affect threat by ±1 each.** Total threat from -3 to +3 is viable; -1 to +1 is medium.
- **What makes a monster interesting is NOT numbers** — it's mechanics that force players to change tactics or make choices.
- **The "One-Two Punch" technique:** Give the monster a conditional strong attack that requires setup. The weak attack sets up the strong one. This creates strategy without giving the GM choices to agonize over.
- **Match complexity to organization.** Gang monsters get one simple trait and straightforward actions. Solo monsters get multiple strategies and options.
- **Undead Fortitude redesigned:** Simplified to a flat DC 10 Con save (instead of variable DC 5 + damage) for faster resolution at the table with 8-12 zombies.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start with concept:** What is the monster? How does it fight? How does it FEEL to fight it? What's the best way to beat it?
2. **Assign Tier, Threat, and Organization** based on the concept
3. **Read baseline stats from the Angry table** — AC, HP, Attack, Damage, Save DC
4. **Adjust stats with Poor/Average/Good ratings** to match the concept (e.g., zombies: poor AC, good HP, average attack, good damage)
5. **Design ONE defining trait** that captures the monster's identity. Match trait complexity to organization size.
6. **Use the One-Two Punch** for group/gang monsters: a setup attack and a conditional payoff attack
7. **Evaluate damage as a group**, not per individual — gang monsters deal damage collectively
8. **Simplify bookkeeping** for large groups: flat DCs, passive traits, no recharge rolls
9. **Add vulnerabilities/resistances** to reward player knowledge and spell variety

> [!tip] The One-Two Punch
> For monsters that appear in groups, design a weak "setup" attack and a strong "payoff" attack that can only be used conditionally. Example: Zombie grabs (1 damage, target grappled) → Zombie bites (1d6+3 damage, only on grappled targets). This creates emergent strategy — once any zombie lands a grab, all nearby zombies switch to biting. PCs must decide: break the grapple or keep attacking? No GM decision-making required per monster, but the encounter is tactically rich.
