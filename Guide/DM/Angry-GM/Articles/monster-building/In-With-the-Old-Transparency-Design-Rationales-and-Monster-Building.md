# In With the Old: Transparency, Design Rationales, and Monster Building

## Summary

Angry reflects on two underappreciated design principles from D&D 4th Edition: **design rationale** (knowing WHY you're building something and what it should look like when done) and **transparency of design** (clearly labeling what things are and why they exist). He argues that 5E threw out the baby with the bathwater when it rejected 4E's approach. The article culminates in Angry's own working system of **eight monster combat strategies** — four offensive and four defensive — that serve as design rationales for building tactically interesting monsters and varied encounters.

## Key Advice

- **Design rationale = purpose + blueprint.** Before building anything (class, monster, mechanic), define what it IS, what it DOES, and WHY it exists. This focuses design, helps evaluate ideas, and ensures the final product serves its purpose.
- **Transparency of design = showing your work.** 4E labeled everything with its role and power source. This made the game easy to design for, even though those labels had no mechanical impact during play.
- **4E's monster roles were revolutionary for encounter design:**
  - Soldiers (absorb hits, protect allies)
  - Skirmishers (mobile, damage priority targets)
  - Brutes (power through defenses)
  - Artillery (ranged damage, minimize risk)
  - Controllers (limit PC options, battlefield control)
  - Lurkers (swift attacks, then hide)
  - Leaders (buff other monsters)
- **5E's monsters are too bland and unfocused.** Generic humanoids are boring; other monsters are gimmicky or lack strategic depth.
- **Angry's Eight Monster Strategies (4 Offensive, 4 Defensive):**
  - **Bruisers** (offensive) — overpower PC defenses via boosted attack/damage
  - **Snipers** (offensive) — ranged attacks targeting distant/squishy PCs
  - **Skirmishers** (offensive) — outmaneuver PCs to reach priority targets
  - **Sappers** (offensive) — reduce enemy defenses (acid, curses, debuffs)
  - **Healers** (defensive) — undo PC progress (use sparingly — feels bad)
  - **Zoners** (defensive) — control movement (lockdown, push, slow, difficult terrain)
  - **Blockers** (defensive) — control targeting (absorb attacks, impose penalties, forced targeting)
  - **Buffers** (defensive) — increase ally defenses (armor bonuses, temp HP, resistances, cover)
- **Monsters can be solo or group.** Solo monsters need multiple strategies for versatility. Group monsters can specialize.
- **Offensive vs. Defensive creates encounter pacing.** Offensive monsters = fast, frantic, reactive fights. Defensive monsters = slow, methodical, proactive fights. Mixed = balanced.
- **Adding strategies increases complexity without changing CR** — if the strategies are exclusive (use THIS or THAT), the monster's best-round output stays the same.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Before building any monster, write a one-sentence design rationale:** "[Monster] is a [offensive/defensive] [solo/group] creature that [strategy description]"
2. **Pick 1-2 strategies from the eight** and design all traits/attacks to serve those strategies
3. **For solo monsters**, give at least two strategies (one offensive, one defensive) for versatility
4. **For group monsters**, specialize in one strategy and add teamwork abilities
5. **Vary monster roles in encounters** — mix offensive and defensive strategies on the enemy team for interesting tactical puzzles
6. **Use the offensive/defensive split** to set encounter pacing intentionally:
   - All offensive enemies → fast, dangerous, reactive fight
   - All defensive enemies → slow, grinding, proactive fight
   - Mixed → balanced, varied fight
7. **Label your monsters with their strategy** in your notes (e.g., "Goblin Archer — Sniper, Gang") to make encounter building faster
8. **Design terrain to complement monster strategies** — Zoners need chokepoints, Snipers need elevation, Skirmishers need open flanks

> [!tip] The Eight Monster Strategies
> When designing a monster, pick from these eight strategies to focus your design:
> **Offensive:** Bruiser (overpower defenses), Sniper (ranged priority targeting), Skirmisher (mobile priority targeting), Sapper (reduce enemy defenses)
> **Defensive:** Healer (undo progress — use rarely), Zoner (control movement), Blocker (control targeting), Buffer (boost ally defenses)
> Fuzzy borders between strategies are a feature, not a bug. A single ability can serve different strategies for different monsters. The goal is a design focus, not a rigid taxonomy.
