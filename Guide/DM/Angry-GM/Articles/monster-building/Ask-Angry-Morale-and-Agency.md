# Ask Angry: Morale Systems and Player Agency

## Summary

Angry answers a reader's question about whether to use morale checks for player characters. The article becomes a deep dive into **player agency** — the principle that a GM should never dictate what a PC thinks, feels, or chooses. Morale checks (die rolls that force retreat/cowering) violate this principle when applied to PCs. The article distinguishes morale mechanics (for monsters) from **sanity mechanics** (for PCs in horror games), explaining why the latter can be acceptable when they are genre-central and interactive.

## Key Advice

- **Morale checks are for monsters, not PCs.** Historically in D&D (pre-3E) and wargames, morale was always a monster-side mechanic. Applying it to PCs crosses the agency line.
- **Player agency = the character's skull is the boundary.** Game mechanics should affect things external to the character's mind. Telling a player what their character thinks, feels, or decides is off-limits.
- **Sanity mechanics get a pass under two conditions:**
  1. Loss of agency is a central theme of the genre (horror, cosmic dread, bestial urges)
  2. The mechanic is interactive and choice-driven — players can manage, push against, or lean into it
- **Morale mechanics fail both tests** in most combat-focused RPGs — they are neither genre-central nor interactive. They're just "roll or lose your turn."
- **Fear spells get limited tolerance** because they are external forces (magic), temporary, and the player's mind is still "in there" — will is suppressed, not replaced.
- **Never tell players how their characters feel.** Describe the scene vividly and let them decide the emotional response.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Remove PC morale checks** from any combat-focused RPG unless the game is explicitly horror-themed
2. **Keep morale for monsters** — it makes combat more dynamic when enemies flee, surrender, or rout
3. **If you want fear/stress mechanics for PCs**, make them interactive:
   - Give players ways to resist, manage, or recover from the effect
   - Impose penalties (disadvantage, resource costs) rather than dictating actions
   - Allow rallying — let PCs inspire each other to overcome fear
4. **Use the "skull boundary" test** for any mechanic: does it cross into the character's mind? If yes, reconsider
5. **For social skill checks against PCs**: provide information ("you can't tell if they're lying") rather than dictating belief ("you believe them")

> [!tip] The Agency Litmus Test
> Before implementing any mechanic that affects PC behavior, ask: "Does this tell the player what their character thinks, feels, or chooses?" If yes, it violates player agency. Instead, impose mechanical consequences (disadvantage, resource drain) and let the player decide how their character responds to those consequences.
