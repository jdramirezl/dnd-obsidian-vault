# Monster Building 101: It's Alive, IT'S ALIVE!

## Why Now?

Custom monsters are an extremely powerful tool. After actually knowing how to build an adventure, it's the most important tool in the adventure building toolkit. At least in D&D. The designers of D&D actually did really well in the DMG for 5E providing robust tools for building custom monsters.

This series breaks down into THREE lessons:
1. **Lesson One** — Custom monster building the RIGHT WAY (this article)
2. **Lesson Two** — Custom monster building in D&D 5E
3. **Lesson Three** — Custom monster building in Pathfinder

---

## Why Skinjobs Suck

A skinjob or reskin occurs when you take a monster from the Monster Manual and use the monster's stats, but call it a different monster and change its appearance.

The thing with reskinning is that, ideally, **theme and mechanics work together in a beautiful synergy**. The whole becomes greater than the sum of its parts.

Example: Turning kobolds into monkey-beings for a monkey temple. But looking at lemurs — they have tough bites, a powerful leap, they mob prey, and have amazing climbing ability. Kobolds just have that mob thing. The lemurians could leap up to the ceiling, drop on invaders, and mob them. That sort of thing invites players to get creative.

When form matches function:
- Players can make educated guesses about tactics before seeing them in action
- When caught by surprise by a weird ability, they say "that actually makes sense, should have seen that coming"
- It **empowers** the players

---

## Close Enough isn't Good Enough

Both Pathfinder and D&D 5E have tables of expected stats by CR. You can slap together a monster by picking a row on that table. If you want to suck.

Both systems have a beautiful internal logic, a consistency. Players and GMs learn that consistency and it gives them **cues**:
- Light armor + consistently missing attacks → spellcaster won't throw Dex saves
- Not physically imposing → wrestling/shoving more likely to succeed
- Players make decisions based on all the cues you give them, consciously or not

It's ESPECIALLY important if you share a monster beyond the table not to simply fake it.

## Lazy GMs Need Not Apply

Custom monster building is like painting miniatures. It's a lot of work. It's something you have to enjoy for its own merits. Most GMs never need to touch it. But if you're going to touch it:
- It takes time and patience
- There's a right way to do it
- Your efforts get better with time and practice

## Monster Building Basics

### Understand the System

You cannot build a solid custom monster without a good understanding of the system. In D&D 5E:
- Saving throw DC: `8 + Proficiency Modifier + relevant Ability Score Modifier`
- Pathfinder: `10 + half HD + relevant Ability Score modifier`
- Know attack roll components, damage calculation, skill bonuses, feats, creature types

Building a 5E monster requires three books. Pathfinder takes two.

### Know Why You're Building What You're Building

Before building, know what purpose the monster serves. You don't need a "good concept" first. But you DO need to know:
- Is it self-sufficient or part of a group?
- If part of a group, what does it do?
- What are its goals?
- How powerful is it in concrete game terms?

**Examples:**

> "I need a final guardian for my temple, defensive by nature, should outlast the party. Difficult challenge for 4th level PCs. CR 5."

> "I need little humanoid temple servants, same niche as goblins/kobolds. Work as a group, monkey themed, against 4th level PCs. CR 1 tops."

### Figure Out Your Target Numbers

You want to figure out roughly where your combat statistics need to be. Always **work backwards** into the creature — the opposite of building a PC.

### Ability Scores Come Last

Ability scores are the LAST THING you come up with. They're where you have the most freedom. When damage is coming up short, tweak Strength. That increase trickles through to other things, keeping the creature consistent.

### Tweak, Tweak, Tweak

You'll bounce back and forth a lot. Constitution puts HP too high → take away a HD. Extra Strength gives too much damage. Just accept the iteration.

### Concepts Get Tweaked Too

Your concept isn't superior to the mechanics. It's got to go either way. Sometimes game mechanics need to feed back and affect the concept. If you can't accept that, you can't build custom content.

### Know When to Stop

You're never going to get it perfect. The numbers won't quite match. Tweaking one thing breaks another. **Strive for precision, but have a reasonable tolerance for error.**
