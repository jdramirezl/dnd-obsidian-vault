# The Rest of the World of D&D According to Rosewater… According to Angry

Continuation of the Rosewater analysis (elements 6–10). Concludes with a major perspective shift: D&D should be evaluated not as a game, but as a **toolbox for building games** — and it's designed for players, not for the GMs and designers who must actually build those games.

## Rosewater's Remaining 5 Elements

### 6. Surprise (Tension / Uncertainty)

Every game needs unpredictable elements. Angry prefers the term **tension** — moments where you hold your breath because you genuinely don't know what's coming.

**Sources of surprise in TTRPGs (ranked):**
1. **Other human brains** — four players making unpredictable decisions, sometimes surprising even themselves. This is what makes TTRPGs the MOST surprising type of game.
2. **The vast possibility space** — anything players and GM can imagine can happen, with far fewer constraints than other games.
3. **Dice** — random outcome determination. The lowest, most basic form of surprise.

**How rules undermine surprise** (via the hypothetical game *Hypothetica: The Gedanken Chronicles*):
- **Overly effective universal tools**: If every hero can shift damage types at will, encountering a fire elemental surprises no one — everyone just switches to ice.
- **Punishing improvisation**: If failing carries harsh consequences and retries are banned, players default to their one safe option every time.
- **Overcomplicated systems**: If a wilderness realm requires 15+ stats to define, the GM can never improvise new areas, so exploration becomes railroaded.

**D&D's problem with surprise**: The d20 mechanic simultaneously has too much and too little riding on each roll. D&D compensates by adding more rolls. "Bounded accuracy" tried to fix this but solved the wrong problem.

### 7. Strategy (Player Skill)

Strategy = the ability to get better at the game over time. If better choices exist, worse choices exist. If you can improve, you can start out bad.

**The uncomfortable truth**: Strategy implies exclusion. If skill matters, some people will be bad at the game. This raises real accessibility concerns (social skills, reading, math, dyslexia) — worth discussing, but separate from the design question.

**Why strategy matters beyond "getting good":**
- Humans are wired to find skill acquisition satisfying
- Improvement adds depth — new options, higher challenges, different play experiences
- **Without strategy, choices don't matter.** You could make random choices and have the same chance of success. Strategy and surprise exist in dynamic tension:
  - Pure strategy = assured outcome (just pick the right choice)
  - Pure surprise = choices are meaningless (outcome is random)
  - Good games need both, with the needle set somewhere between

**D&D's strategy problem**: Minimal strategy is built into the system. Numbers are stacked in the players' favor regardless of choices. Resources boost already-substantial odds; even spent, players still have good chances by the book. Yet D&D **promises** strategy — "your choices matter, you live or die by your decisions." The gap between promise and delivery is the issue.

### 8. Fun

Rosewater: Fun is subjective, can't be designed or reasoned out, only discovered through playtesting. Angry agrees.

**Is D&D fun?** Yes. Obviously. It's successful, it's grown the fanbase, people enjoy it without even playing it (streaming, etc.).

**The perspective shift**: D&D being fun may be its biggest problem. Fun and hook are the **easiest** elements on the list — the lowest barrier to entry. Playing pretend elves with friends is fun almost regardless of rules quality. The people who have the least fun are people like Angry who can't stop analyzing the system.

### 9. Flavor

Flavor provides context for mechanics and creates emotional investment. More importantly, **when flavor and rules work together, they help you remember how things work.**

- Poker has no flavor. Chess has thin flavor. M:tG has optional flavor.
- D&D has lots of **surface flavor** (world details, lore) but most of it is decorative — like M:tG flavor text rather than mechanics-explaining flavor.
- Example: Spell slots are confusing because the flavor doesn't explain the mechanic. Old Vancian "memorize and forget" casting was weird but memorable because the flavor matched the rules.

> Games with a particular setting are ALWAYS stronger, more approachable, and more popular than generic systems.

### 10. A Hook (Elevator Pitch)

D&D has a strong hook:

> "You explore a world of magic and monsters and take on a series of grand adventures, gaining power, wealth, and fame; and you can do almost anything you can imagine in that world."

No further analysis needed — this is solid.

## The Big Perspective Shift

### D&D Is Not a Game — It's a Toolbox

The original premise: How many of Rosewater's qualities are baked into D&D vs. how many must the adventure designer bring?

**Theory**: The more elements baked into the system, the fewer the designer must provide. Missing elements don't prevent good games, but they mean bad designers (especially homebrew GMs) will produce bad games — and **take the blame** for systemic failures.

> "Just like when your 2nd-level party gets killed by three night hags in Curse of Strahd, you don't complain about D&D being deadly. You say 'whoever wrote Curse of Strahd really messed up.'"

### The Console Analogy

D&D is like a game console. Nobody loves the console itself — they love the games. The best console is the one that's **easiest to make great games for**. D&D should be evaluated on how well it empowers GMs and designers to build great adventures.

### Advice Is Not Enough

> "If it's so important and you know it's that important, why didn't you actually design it right into the core rules I paid $150 for?! YOU'RE THE GAME DESIGNER!"

Advice is a copout. If catch-up mechanics are important, build them into the system. Don't tell GMs to figure it out.

### The Real Problem: Player-Focused Design

D&D wasn't designed for adventure designers — it was designed to be **fun to play**. Massive playtesting ensured players would have tons of options, feel powerful, never be penalized, and enjoy character customization. It's a player-focused game.

> "The system isn't designed for designers to make good games with, it's designed for players to buy and demand that GMs run for them."

**The pattern**: GMs are doing increasing amounts of work to hack D&D. They need more levers, more tools, more ways to manage pace, tension, strategy, and surprise. Many are frustrated. Many want to switch systems. But their players won't let them — because D&D is fun and accessible for players.

### What D&D Needs (Implied)

- A **limit break / catch-up system** (like Final Fantasy) so GMs don't have to design around death spirals
- **Generalized staged-effect mechanics** that could build exhaustion, disease, curses, poisons, injuries, demoralization — instead of each being a separate, bloated text block
- **Adventure-scale mechanics** for goals, pacing, inertia — not just encounter-level tools
- More levers and hooks beyond HP loss

> "What really drives me crazy is how much I have to work to bring any lever or hook or incentive or disincentive into the game that isn't hit point loss."
