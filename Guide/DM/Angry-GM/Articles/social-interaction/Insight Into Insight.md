# Insight Into Insight

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/insight-into-insight/)
**Date:** September 23, 2020
**Series:** Run Better Interactions

---

## The Problem with Insight

Most GMs use Insight wrong. They treat it as a passive lie-detector or a "save vs. deception" that fires automatically whenever an NPC lies. This breaks two core assumptions of RPGs:

1. **Player agency is sacrosanct.** Neither the GM nor dice can tell players what their PCs must think, believe, or do (outside supernatural mind control). Old Sense Motive rules that force a PC to *believe* a lie on a failed roll violate this.
2. **Player skill must matter.** If a single Insight roll tells you "he's lying" with certainty, it's an "I Win" button that removes all reasoning and problem-solving from the interaction.

## Four Inarguable Assumptions

1. You can't ignore **player skill** — players must figure things out themselves
2. You can't ignore **avatar strength** — character stats must matter
3. Talking in-character is **just for funsies** — it's acting, not a game mechanic
4. **Player agency is sacrosanct** — dice don't dictate beliefs or behavior

## Passive Scores: Use Them

D&D 5E's passive check mechanic (assume a roll of 10 + modifier) is elegant and solves real problems:

- **Eliminates metagaming from die rolls.** When you ask for a Perception check, players *know* something's hidden nearby — even on a failure, that knowledge taints their decisions. Passive checks avoid this entirely.
- **Distinguishes passive from active skill use.** Passive = abilities/training working automatically (noticing an air current, recognizing a rare plant). Active = interacting with the world (searching, examining, probing).

Objections to passive scores (wanting surprise from dice, "too much bookkeeping" for five two-digit numbers) are not valid. The mechanic is too valuable to skip.

## Don't Give Away the Whole Answer

GMs give players too much information too quickly. When a PC's Passive Perception beats the DC for a secret door, don't say "you see a secret door." Say "you notice a faint air current from the right wall." Let the players deduce what it means and investigate further.

**Be clear and unambiguous, but also be miserly.** Give the smallest useful information. Make players work for answers. Draw them into the scene.

## How Insight Actually Works (Based on Real Psychology)

Paul Ekman's research on micro-expressions shows that reading people isn't about detecting lies — it's about **spotting emotional states that don't match the words being said.** A suspect insisting he didn't commit assault while leaking anger expressions doesn't prove guilt — maybe the anger is about something else entirely.

This maps perfectly to better gameplay: Insight delivers **strong clues that don't give away the whole story,** driving MORE investigation rather than stopping it cold.

## The Correct Way to Use Insight

### Passive Insight (Automatic)
Whenever an NPC speaks or acts in a way that doesn't align with what they're feeling — lying, hiding motives, concealing emotions, under supernatural influence — compare each PC's **Passive Insight** to the DC.

On success, tell the player **what they perceive, not why:**
- "The NPC seems surprised to see you, but is trying to hide it"
- "The NPC is concealing anger about something"
- "The NPC seems nervous about this routine exchange"

### Active Insight (Player-Initiated)
When a player wants to probe an NPC — learn about personality, motives, emotions — they converse with the NPC and make an Insight check. They must specify what they're looking for or at least where they're looking:

- "I think he's lying about his alibi — can I lean on his guilt?"
- "I want to figure out what's making her so nervous"

The GM describes the probing conversation and reveals information proportional to the roll. **Insight only reveals information — it doesn't change behavior.** Getting the NPC to admit they're lying requires a different social skill.

### Insight as Faux Pas Prevention
Occasionally, if a player declares an action that might have unintended disastrous consequences, call for an Insight check. On success, warn them and let the PC catch themselves mid-sentence. Reserved for situations where a particularly insightful character would notice a subtle cue others would miss — not for obviously stupid actions (those get a flat warning).

## Key Principle

Insight works exactly like Perception for traps and secret doors:
- Passive scores catch automatic clues
- Active use requires interaction and specificity
- Results are clues that drive further investigation, not complete answers
