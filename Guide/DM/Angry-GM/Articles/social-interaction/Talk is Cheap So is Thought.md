# Talk is Cheap; So is Thought

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Talk is Cheap; So is Thought](https://theangrygm.com/talk-is-cheap-so-is-thought/)
**Date:** October 31, 2019
**Series:** Rant / Game Design Philosophy

---

## Summary

A rant about the "deliberation trap" — the tendency of players (and game designers) to over-discuss, over-think, and committee-decide their way into paralysis. Deliberation cannot create new information, kills good ideas indiscriminately, wastes precious game time, and drains energy. The solution: recognize when you're stuck, act on imperfect information, and iterate.

---

## The Deliberation Trap

### What It Looks Like
- Players face a difficult decision with incomplete information.
- They call a sidebar and discuss at length.
- They go back and forth, repeating arguments, imagining failure modes.
- They spend 5x longer than needed and usually pick the worst option anyway.

### Why It Happens
- Players (and designers) believe that thinking and talking will eventually produce the right answer.
- They don't recognize that **deliberation cannot introduce new information**.
- Once every member has voiced their perspective, there's nothing new coming into the equation.

## The Three Costs of Over-Deliberation

### 1. Wasted Time
- Time is the most liquid and most limited asset.
- Game time spent deliberating is game time not spent *playing*.
- Energy levels crater by the end of a long deliberation — whatever solution they pick, they aren't excited about it.

### 2. Idea Murder
- No idea is perfect. Every solution has tradeoffs and unintended consequences.
- A group of people will reduce any idea to shreds with piranha-like efficiency.
- Deliberation kills ideas indiscriminately — it can't tell good ideas from bad ones.
- Ideas that survive the initial smell test are all equally likely to be good or bad. You can't tell the difference by thinking harder.

### 3. Idea Maiming
- Deliberation identifies the *wrong* flaws — potential problems that turn out to be non-issues or corner cases.
- Refining based on those phantom flaws introduces new, unforeseen problems.
- Polish and iteration should come from **testing**, not thinking and talking.

## The GM Knows Better

- GMs face the same problem constantly: judgment calls with incomplete information.
- Good GMs know an okay call *now* beats a perfect call after three hours of rulebook-flipping.
- There are no perfect calls — only calls and consequences to deal with later.

### The GM's Decision Process
1. **Recognize** when you don't have enough information to make a call.
2. **Seek specific information** if you know where to find it quickly (this is research, not deliberation).
3. **Make a decision** based on what you have. Trust your gut.
4. **Deal with consequences later** — you can always go back and fix a bad call.

## Advice for Players

- If the party has been deliberating for more than ~5 minutes, you don't have enough information.
- **Stop talking. Start doing.** Identify a fact that would lock in a solution, then figure out how to obtain it.
- No plan survives contact with the enemy. You're supposed to be heroes — take a risk.
- The game is low-stakes compared to real life. A dead character hurts, but it's not the end of the world.

## Advice for Game Designers

- Ideas are starting points, not finished products. Game design is 10% idea, 99% iteration.
- Don't ask the internet for opinions — opinions are not information.
- Write the idea down as simply and unpolished as possible. Use it at the table. Gather data. *Then* polish.
- If you develop a habit of shipping ugly first drafts, you'll eventually get good at producing solid first drafts. But you never stop producing ugly ones too.
- Asking people to predict how a mechanic will *feel* based on reading an incomplete version in isolation is useless.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Thinking** will never get you information you don't have. It can only reach conclusions from existing data.
- **Talking** gets you different perspectives, but unless you're consulting an expert with specific knowledge, it doesn't add much real information.
- Recognize the deliberation trap: if you're going in circles, you need *action* or *new information*, not more discussion.
- An imperfect decision implemented now is almost always better than a perfect decision implemented never.
- "Of all sad words in deed and pen, the saddest are these: it might have been."
