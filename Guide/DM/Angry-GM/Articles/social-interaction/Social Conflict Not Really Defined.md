# Social Conflict Not Really Defined

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Resolving Social Interactions: Social Conflict Not Really Defined](https://theangrygm.com/social-conflict-not-really-defined/)
**Date:** August 29, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

## Summary

Part 1 of a three-part module on Resolving Social Conflict. This lesson lays the psychological groundwork: what drives human social behavior, why NPCs resist, and how social conflicts actually end. Social Conflict is just another game challenge — not a special snowflake that bypasses normal action resolution.

---

## Social Interaction Is Just Another Challenge

- Social Conflict works like any other part of the game: players choose actions, characters take them, mechanics determine outcomes, GM describes results.
- You don't design "Social Encounters" — you design Encounters and the players choose whether to resolve them through combat, stealth, interaction, or whatever.
- An NPC is a treasure chest with a social-shaped lock. The information/cooperation is the treasure.

## Social Actions

1. **Player declares** the action (describes it or speaks in character).
2. **GM uses mechanics** to determine the outcome — die rolls or not, but character skills always factor in.
3. **GM describes** the outcome (narrates or acts as the NPC).

## When Interaction Becomes Conflict

- Conflict arises when involved parties have incompatible desires, motivations, or goals.
- But Social Conflict can arise even when goals are *compatible* — because the human brain isn't goal-oriented or logic-driven. It's about feelings.
- Most GMs have such a limited understanding of human social nature that they see too many things as automatic successes or impossibilities. Be more generous about what's possible.

## What Drives Human Social Behavior

### 1. Risk and Loss Aversion (Strongest)
- People hate losing things and hate risking losing things.
- Fear (avoid risk/loss) and anger (prevent/punish risk/loss) are the strongest emotions.
- The brain doesn't distinguish mental stress from literal threats to life.

### 2. Mood
- Temporary emotional state. Bad mood → more obstructive. Good mood → more willing to help.

### 3. Rapport
- People help who they like, obstruct who they don't.
- **In-group bias:** if someone seems "on your team," you trust and listen to them more.

### 4. Personality and Temperament
- Default/long-term emotional state + how emotions respond to external influences.
- Personality can be overridden by mood, rapport, and especially fear/anger.
- Feedback loops: emotionally reactive people are more affected by temporary mood; resilient people hold their baseline.

### 5. Actual Motivations, Desires, and Goals (Weakest)
- At the bottom of the list. Most people's actual desires are overridden by fears, anger, moods, and dispositions most of the time.

### 6. Trust (Underlies Everything)
- Humans can't predict the future or read minds — they must trust each other.
- Every social interaction has an underlying poker game: take at face value, or bluff/counterbluff?

## Understanding NPCs Through This Lens

- GMs don't decide what NPCs do — NPCs are game constructs. GMs make their actions look like plausible human behavior.
- All the psychology above is a toolbox of excuses to explain whatever outcome the mechanics produce.
- **Example:** A regent refuses to talk when threatened. Why? Maybe he thinks the threat is a bluff. Maybe he's more afraid of what happens if he talks. Maybe he sees the PCs as enemies. The failed Intimidation check *reveals* who the regent is.
- Nothing is true about an NPC until it happens at the table.

## How Social Conflicts End

### 1. Retreat
- PCs walk away with nothing. They lost. Totally acceptable — but players never do it voluntarily.

### 2. Compromise
- PCs accept less than they wanted, or pay a very high price.

### 3. Exchange
- Both parties agree to a mostly-even trade. PCs get what they want but pay for it (favor, money, promise).
- **This is the default success state for most Social Conflicts.** Most social conflicts are about settling on a price, not winning people over.

### 4. Conversion
- NPC willfully gives PCs what they want without receiving anything in return.
- Exceedingly rare without threats or deception (which amount to "Social Robbery").
- Most GMs and players wrongly think this is what successful social conflict looks like.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Social Interaction is the most humanizing element of the game — it brings the world to life.
- Err on the side of allowing die rolls in social situations; most GMs are too restrictive.
- NPCs resist for emotional reasons (fear, mood, rapport, personality) far more than rational ones.
- The default successful outcome is Exchange, not Conversion.
- Use the psychology toolbox to explain mechanical outcomes, not to predetermine NPC behavior.
