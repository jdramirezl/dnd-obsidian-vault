# Help! My Players Are Talking to Things

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/help-my-players-are-talking-to-things/)
**Date:** August 5, 2013
**Series:** GMing Basically

---

## InterACTION! Is Just an Action

A social **InterACTION!** occurs when a PC tries to get something from an NPC by talking at them — information, items, assistance, cooperation, etc. The key insight: **InterACTIONS! follow the exact same adjudication rules as every other action.** Player declares intention + approach → GM determines if it can succeed/fail → dice roll if warranted → outcome + consequences.

Don't develop weird hang-ups about social actions being fundamentally different from combat or exploration actions. They're not.

## Role-Playing vs. Roll-Playing: A False Debate

The spectrum runs from "never roll dice, just act it out" to "roll for everything, no speaking in character." Both extremes are wrong.

- When a player speaks in character or describes what their PC says, they are **declaring an action** — intention + approach
- Of course dice should be rolled when dice need rolling
- Of course what the PC says should have an impact
- **Don't give bonuses for "good role-playing"** (i.e., good acting). Acting skill varies by player and is irrelevant to the in-game situation. It's the same as giving a bonus for good penmanship on a character sheet.

## Speaking vs. Describing: Also a False Debate

First-person ("Let us in or I'll kick your ass") vs. third-person ("My character threatens the guard") — both convey the necessary information. Let each player use whichever style is comfortable. As a DM, learn to switch between both and match each player's preference.

## Parsing InterACTIONS!

Tease out the **intention** (what the player wants to accomplish) and the **approach** (how they're trying to do it). Approach is trickier in social actions because the surface action is always "the PC says something" — you must determine the nuance: Is it true or false? Hostile or polite? Offering something? Threatening?

**Don't just fit the action into a skill.** Playing for sympathy, offering a convincing argument, and seduction all fall under "Persuasion" but are very different approaches that elicit different responses.

If unclear, ask the player: "That sounded threatening — did you mean to threaten the NPC?"

## The Tennis Match

A single InterACTION! usually emerges from a back-and-forth conversation. The player builds up intention and approach over several exchanges. The DM plays the NPC, bantering until the player puts themselves in a position to "score" — that's when you halt the scene and resolve the action with dice.

## Objections and Incentives

**If the NPC has no reason to refuse to help the PCs, there is no InterACTION! Do not roll dice.**

The NPC is not the obstacle. The **reasons why the NPC won't cooperate** are the obstacles.

- **Objections:** Reasons the NPC has to *not* help — fear of trouble, spite, cost, religious vows, protecting someone, etc.
- **Incentives:** Reasons the NPC has to help — something in it for them, desire to do right, personal feelings, avoiding harm, etc.

Before running an InterACTION!, you need at least one Objection. If you can't think of one, you don't have an InterACTION!

## Three Ways an InterACTION! Can Succeed

For an InterACTION! to have any chance of success, the player's approach must do one of:

1. **Overcome an Objection** — remove or neutralize the reason the NPC won't help
2. **Appeal to an Incentive** — strengthen an existing reason the NPC wants to help
3. **Create an Incentive** — provide a new reason for the NPC to help

If the approach doesn't do any of these three things, it cannot succeed. Don't roll dice.

## Outcomes and Consequences

After resolving the die roll:

- **Short-term consequences** change the current interaction — the NPC's attitude shifts, trust increases or decreases. These can apply bonuses/penalties to future InterACTIONS! in the same scene (just like a Bane spell in combat).
- **Long-term consequences** can be passive (NPC remembers the party next time) or active (NPC reports the party to enemies, sends a gift, hires thugs).
- **If the players can't connect consequences to their actions, the consequences don't count.**

Don't spiral into infinite consequence chains ("making failure interesting" taken to extremes). Some interactions just close and that's fine.

## Responding to InterACTIONS!: Response, Reason, Opening

When the scene continues after a resolved action, the DM's response should:

1. **Response:** Indicate success or failure ("I can't help you" / "I want to help")
2. **Reason:** Explain why the scene isn't over ("but I'm afraid I'll be in danger")
3. **Opening:** Give the players something to respond to — state an Objection or Incentive they can tackle

This formula keeps conversations moving and prevents players from spinning their wheels. As you improve, you'll roll all three into a single natural sentence.

## The Four Ps: Bringing NPCs to Life

Quick NPC characterization on an index card:

1. **Personality/Phrasing:** A single word defining the NPC's dominant tone (helpful, suspicious, angry, bureaucratic). Shapes how you phrase everything they say.
2. **Posture:** A physical stance to adopt while playing the NPC. Helps you stay in character and conveys information to players.
3. **Pause:** A verbal/physical indicator you use when you need thinking time ("Hmmm…", breaking eye contact, stroking chin).
4. **Pfidget:** A repeated tic or habit (puffing imaginary pipe, drumming fingers, glancing around suspiciously). Makes NPCs feel alive.

Combined with Objections and alignment, this fits on an index card and can be improvised in the bathroom before a scene.
