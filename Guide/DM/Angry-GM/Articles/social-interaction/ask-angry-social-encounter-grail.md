# Ask Angry: The Quest for the Holy Social Encounter System Grail

## Key Advice

D&D doesn't need a mechanical "social combat" system — it needs **encounter design tools** for social encounters. TTRPGs are uniquely equipped to handle social interaction because they're run by human brains that are hardwired for conversation. The problem isn't resolving social encounters; it's that GMs have no framework for **defining and describing** them. Every rule you add constrains the open-endedness that makes social interaction feel real.

## Summary

Angry responds to a reader asking whether any RPG has a good social encounter system. He acknowledges a few decent ones (FFG's Legend of the Five Rings, Green Ronin's A Song of Ice and Fire RPG) but argues the entire quest for a "social combat system" is fundamentally misguided.

His core arguments:

1. **TTRPGs don't need social mechanics the way video games do.** Video games fake social interaction because computers can't understand language. Human GMs are literally hardwired for it. Rules exist to compensate for hardware limitations — social interaction isn't one of them.

2. **Every rule is a constraint.** Rules provide tension, strategy, consistency, and fairness, but they also reduce open-endedness. Social interaction is the most open-ended thing humans do. Over-mechanizing it turns conversations into button-pressing exercises.

3. **"Social combat" systems fail because they model the wrong thing.** Most designers copy combat mechanics (social HP, specific actions, turns) because combat is fun. But social interaction doesn't play out like a fistfight. The result feels like nothing except rolling dice to push numbers around.

4. **What's actually missing is a framework, not a system.** GMs know how to build a combat encounter (monsters, map, CR budget). But for "talk your way past the guard," there's nothing. The real need is tools for defining objectives, NPC dispositions, and the general strategies players might employ.

5. **Angry's own approach:** Define objections and incentives for the NPC. Use those to invite players to act and evaluate effectiveness. Flavor with personality traits and a dwindling patience meter. Let the conversation be the conversation.

He also notes that D&D's Charisma stat and anemic skill system are the biggest obstacles to good social interaction, and that you can't truly play a character who is "substantially mentally different from you."

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop trying to build "social combat."** Don't model social encounters on combat mechanics — no social HP, no initiative, no specific action menus
2. **Define the social encounter like you'd define a combat encounter:**
   - What is the NPC's objective/disposition?
   - What are the NPC's objections to helping the PCs?
   - What incentives could change the NPC's mind?
3. **Use those objections and incentives** to invite players to act and to evaluate the effectiveness of what they say
4. **Flavor with personality traits** and track the NPC's dwindling patience (a "whatever stat") as a soft timer
5. **Let the conversation be the conversation** — the players should choose what to say, and the GM evaluates the result. Don't replace dialogue with skill checks
6. **Accept the player-skill component.** Social interaction in a TTRPG is one of the few areas where player skill naturally maps to character skill. Lean into it rather than trying to abstract it away
7. **Consider the tone of your world.** If you want social encounters to matter, design environments with "security holes" to exploit — just like you'd put chandeliers in a room for acrobatic combat

> [!tip] The Missing Framework
> Before your next social encounter, write down three things: (1) What does the NPC want? (2) Why would the NPC say no to the PCs? (3) What could change the NPC's mind? These three questions are the social encounter equivalent of choosing monsters and drawing a map. They give you everything you need to run the scene and evaluate player actions.
