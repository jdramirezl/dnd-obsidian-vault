# Villains and Characters — Villains AS Characters

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Villains and Characters: Villains AS Characters](https://theangrygm.com/villains-and-characters-villains-as-characters/)
**Date:** February 22, 2017
**Series:** Give Non-Player Characters Character

---

## Summary

How much personality does a villain actually need? Not much. A villain is defined by goal, plan, resources, and limits — plus ONE rich detail. Don't write backstories, don't make them sympathetic, and don't expect them to survive being in the same room as the PCs. Let villains come to life organically through play.

---

## The One-Dimensional NPC Is Fine

- Most NPCs are one-dimensional (shopkeeper, busdriver) and that's perfectly okay.
- Dimensions are for main characters and supporting cast.
- As an NPC gets more screen time, they need more details to feel real — but only gradually.
- **The NPC Jar:** every NPC gets the same-size jar. The more space their role takes up, the less room for personality quirks. Villains fill their jar with plot.

## Villains Have Off-Screen Presence

- Villains spend most of the story off-screen, but they're still "on camera" through their effects on the world.
- What makes a villain a villain (vs. a boss monster): the villain *reacts* to PC actions and forces PCs to react in turn.
- Every trap set, threat issued, and plan adapted is an interaction — even without face-to-face contact.

## How Much Personality Does a Villain Need?

- A villain needs: **Goal + Plan + Resources + Limits + ONE rich detail.**
- **Limits:** what the villain will or won't do (kill innocents? harm children? steal?).
- **The one detail** should be loaded — exploitable, story-affecting. Not a candy-bar quirk like "hates purple."
  - Dark knight's misguided chivalry prevents harming women/children.
  - Necromancer refuses to kill anyone not actively trying to kill him.
  - Cult must ritualistically mutilate victims even when it's tactically stupid.

### Good Personality Traits vs. Candy Bars
- **Good:** "afraid of appearing weak" → PCs can goad villain into rash action. Story-relevant.
- **Bad:** "hates the color purple" → empty calories. Wastes jar space.
- Random personality lists are loaded with candy-bar quirks. Avoid them.

## Letting the Villain Come to Life

- Don't run the adventure — **run the villain.** Make decisions *as* the villain based on goal, plan, resources, limits, and that one detail.
- Personality emerges organically through improvisation. The ogre who revels in fear escalates brutally when thwarted — you discover he's vengeful and sociopathic *by playing him*.
- Leave blank space. Over-specified villains never get the "spark of life."
- For arc/campaign villains, add details slowly. Plans change every adventure — that's already a lot of content.

## Don't Backstory

- Don't write a backstory. Just don't.
- Players don't care about origin stories (except ones they cause).
- Backstory doesn't help you play the villain — *playing* the villain does.
- If backstory ever becomes relevant, you can reverse-engineer it from everything you've already discovered through play.
- Pre-written backstory constrains the villain and robs you of the joy of discovering it later.

## Don't Sympathy

- Sympathetic villains (morally justified, grey-area motivations) create unwinnable moral arguments *after* the climax.
- Players are the protagonists — if they sympathize with the villain, the denouement becomes a pizza-topping-level debate about ethics.
- It's fine for a villain's motivations to *make sense*. But push too far into moral grey and you ruin the ending.
- Morality and ethics are personal — there's no universal resolution to "was the villain right?"

## Don't Recurring (In the Same Room)

- **If the villain and heroes end up in the same room, the villain is dead.**
- Players will do *anything* to kill the villain. They'll ignore warnings, distractions, hostages, and social consequences.
- Players assume killing the villain = winning the adventure. Nothing else matters.

### Why Escape Plans Fail
- **Guaranteed escapes** (teleportation ring, wall of force) work exactly once. Players will counter it next time.
- **Second escape plan** feels like a contrived screwjob — players see you pulling tricks out of your ass.
- **Escape plans with any chance of failure** will fail because players are too bloody-minded and persistent.

### The Rule
- If you ever let the PCs be in the same room as the villain, have a plan for a world where that villain no longer exists.
- Recurring villains work great *off-screen* — driving adventure after adventure from the shadows. Just never let them meet the PCs until you're ready for the final confrontation.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Villains are plot devices first, characters second. Goal + Plan + Resources + Limits + one loaded detail.
- Don't over-specify. Let the villain grow through play.
- Backstory is the least important part of the story — that's why you're not playing it.
- Sympathetic villains ruin endings. Recurring villains die the moment PCs can reach them.
- Run the villain, not the adventure. Personality emerges from decisions made in character.
