# Not Ready to Manage Social Interaction

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/not-ready-to-manage-interaction/)
**Date:** September 9, 2020
**Series:** Run Better Interactions

---

## Four Core Assumptions

### 1. You Can't Ignore Player Skill

Players must use their own brains to decide what arguments to make, what lies to tell, what leverage to use. They must figure out what the target wants, why they're opposing, and how to overcome that opposition. Same as deciding which spell to cast or how to solve a puzzle. You can't remove the player's brain from the equation.

### 2. You Can't Ignore Avatar Strength

Character capabilities determine the odds of success or failure. Players' plans affect the odds, but when there's a question about outcome, random chance and character statistics decide. There will be times when the best move is to let someone else's character handle things. There will be times when an incompetent character succeeds where a competent one failed.

### 3. Talking In Character Is Just for Funsies

Players can communicate their character's words in first person or third person — both are equally valid. Speaking in character adds atmosphere but doesn't change the substance of the game. No bonus points for good acting. Not everyone is comfortable with it, and it's not required.

### 4. Player Agency Is Sacrosanct

PCs can **never** be subjected to social actions. No NPC-vs-PC persuasion, intimidation, or deception rolls. No PC-vs-PC social rolls either. Every player must have complete control over what their character thinks, believes, and chooses to do. (Exception: magical mind control like charm/domination spells.)

## What Is a Social Action?

Any action where a PC tries to **affect the behavior of one or more NPCs** by communicating with them — talking, writing, gesturing, intimidating, befriending.

**Social actions are about influencing behavior, not thoughts.** What's in the NPC's head doesn't matter. What they *do* is all that counts. Does the army arrive? Does the goblin reveal the secret passage? Does the blink dog accept the food?

## Adjudicating Social Actions — Same Four Steps as Any Action

### Step 1: Determine Intention and Approach

Two complications unique to social actions:

**Background noise:** Conversations have filler — pleasantries, banter, posturing. Not every spoken line is an action. Players shouldn't roll dice after every line of dialogue. There's back-and-forth punctuated by occasional die rolls.

**Implied intentions:** Social actions are open-ended. Players rarely state "I want to accomplish X by doing Y" explicitly. When a player says "I work for the company, I'm undercover," they're really saying "I want to trick the guard into letting me in by claiming I'm an undercover agent."

**Two solutions:**
1. Always know what the players are trying to accomplish. If unsure, stop and ask outright.
2. Listen to everything and assume there's an action buried in it. Interpret what they say in context of their goals.

**Slow down.** Think in terms of action declarations first, formulate responses second. Don't get lost in character and respond before you think.

### Step 2: Can It Succeed? Can It Fail? Is There a Cost?

**You don't role-play. Role-playing is for players, not GMs.** An NPC is a game construct — an obstacle, like a monster or a trap. You don't decide how the NPC behaves. You determine outcomes and narrate results.

NPC motivations, goals, and reasons to oppose the players aren't role-playing things — they're the equivalent of attacks, traits, and vulnerabilities on a monster stat block. They create conflict and force players to puzzle out strategies.

**You don't decide the outcome.** You decide if there's any *possibility* the action might work and any possibility it might not. The dice and rules determine what the NPC actually does — not you. A bribe isn't an automatic win button. The NPC might have too much integrity, might be worried about accepting money from obvious criminals, might realize the PCs can afford way more.

**Safe defaults:**
- Any reasonable social action that could succeed could also fail
- Every social action has a risk/cost — failed actions can wear down patience, cause offense, or create lingering resentment
- Even successful intimidation can fester into revenge later
- Even successful persuasion can wear off once the NPC has time to reconsider

**Consequences matter.** A successful Intimidation check might work now, but the festering anger might lead to a revenge plot later. Consider the lingering effects on the game world.

Set proper DCs and grant bonuses/penalties based on how likely the approach is to bend the NPC's behavior.

### Step 3: Determine the Outcome

The skill check does NOT determine how well the lie was told. It determines **whether the NPC's behavior is swayed toward the desired outcome**. It could be a poorly told lie to an extremely gullible guard, or a poorly told lie to a bitter guard on his last day who's stopped caring. The d20 represents everything in the universe that might have affected the outcome that couldn't be accounted for or foreseen.

### Step 4: Narrate the Results — Unambiguously

**GMs narrate. They don't play.** Mix third-person narrator description with first-person dialogue. Your goal is to clearly communicate reality, not win an Academy Award.

Real social interaction has massive subtext — tone, body language, facial cues. Players need that information but you can't reliably convey it through acting alone. That's why you narrate.

**Bad (acting only):**
> "I can't let you in without written authorization or the say-so from my supervisor."

**Good (narration + acting):**
> The guard says firmly, "I can't let you in without written authorization or the say-so from my boss." It's clear he has his orders and intends to follow them to the letter, even if he believed your story.

**Also good (showing uncertainty):**
> The guard thinks it over and says uncertainly, "That doesn't sound right. You really should have some ID." He seems nervous about turning you away, but he doesn't sound convinced either.

Narration should:
- Make the NPC's behavior (the outcome) clear
- Reveal information about the NPC
- Suggest other courses of action
- Invite further action — or convey that none is possible

**NPCs don't think. They behave.** They behave based on the game's rules and the players' actions. You determine behaviors and narrate excuses for those behaviors. Be unambiguously clear about everything a reasonable person would perceive if they were actually in that situation — tone, body language, demeanor. Don't give everything away, but don't leave anything up to misinterpretation either.

## Key Takeaway

Social actions work like any other RPG action — same adjudication steps, same balance of player skill and character ability. The GM's job is to determine whether an action can succeed or fail, let the dice decide, and narrate the result clearly. Don't think for NPCs; determine their behaviors based on rules and player actions, then describe those behaviors unambiguously.
