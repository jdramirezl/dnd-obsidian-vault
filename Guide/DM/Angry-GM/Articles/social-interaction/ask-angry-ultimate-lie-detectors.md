# Ask Angry: Ultimate Lie Detectors

## Key Advice

When players acquire a powerful tool (like lie detection), **don't sabotage it** — build the game around it. The "screwjob arms race" of constantly finding ways to negate player abilities is toxic. Instead, accept that the PCs have won this battle and use their capability as a **story engine** that creates new, more interesting problems.

## Summary

A reader's party has a Helm of Telepathy and Zone of Truth, making them an "ultimate interrogation team." The GM can no longer use lies and betrayal as plot devices and wants advice.

Angry's response is blunt: you gave them the tool, they'll use it, and they'd be stupid not to. Don't try to nerf it, find loopholes, or create contrived situations where it doesn't work — that's the "Screwjob Arms Race" and it always ends badly. Instead:

1. **Accept the new reality.** You can't rely on lies anymore. That was one tool in your GM toolkit. Learn to live without it.
2. **Build the game on top of the ability.** Use the *Pushing Daisies* model — the detective can talk to the dead, but that doesn't solve the mystery. Knowing someone is lying doesn't always help you.
3. **Consider the social consequences.** What happens when you detect the king is lying — surrounded by his high-level advisors? Can you even cast Zone of Truth in that situation? Can you make others believe you?
4. **Let enemies adapt.** A villain who knows about the PCs' abilities might protect themselves (like Magneto's helmet). Cult cells might compartmentalize information so no single member knows enough to be useful.
5. **Create moral complexity.** People lie for all sorts of reasons. What happens when you catch someone lying to protect their starving children? Or when both sides of a peace negotiation are lying?
6. **Use the reputation.** Once known as lie detectors, the PCs get called in for legal disputes, peace treaties, and cult investigations — creating entirely new adventure hooks.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Never give players a tool unless you're okay with them using it constantly** — players don't "overuse" tools; they use them
2. **When a tool removes a problem, don't sabotage it.** Accept the new status quo and build on it
3. **Avoid the Screwjob Arms Race:** Don't create contrived situations where the ability doesn't work, don't exploit technicalities in spell wording, don't make witnesses "subjectively truthful"
4. **Ask "what happens next?"** — If the PCs can detect all lies, what are the second-order consequences?
   - Powerful people won't want to be near them
   - Enemies will compartmentalize information
   - Organizations will seek them out for their abilities
   - Moral dilemmas arise from catching sympathetic liars
5. **Use the ability as an adventure hook generator** — lie detection becomes the PCs' calling card and draws them into increasingly complex situations
6. **Challenge yourself as a GM.** Build mysteries that don't hinge on someone lying. Build betrayals that work even when the betrayer believes they're telling the truth (because they were manipulated too)

> [!tip] The Pushing Daisies Principle
> When your players gain a powerful information-gathering ability, adopt the *Pushing Daisies* model: the ability always works, but the information it reveals creates more questions than it answers. Knowing someone is lying is step one — figuring out *why* they're lying, what the truth actually means, and what to do about it in a world of politics and power is where the real adventure begins.
