# Resolving Social Actions

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/resolving-social-actions/)
**Date:** October 27, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

## Core Principle

NPCs aren't characters you roleplay — they're **game constructs** that exist to provide players with challenges. Convincing a witness is the same as jumping a chasm or picking a lock. Evaluate outcomes from a game-mechanical perspective, reward strategic thinking, and use the characters' skills and stats. But do it all **subjectively, with good judgment.** Too many abstract mechanics make social encounters feel like board games and kill emotional investment.

## The Social Stat Block

Five things to know about an NPC before running a social encounter:

### 1. Source of Conflict
Why is the NPC acting as an obstacle? Takes a narrative sentence to describe. Multiple Sources = harder encounter. New ones can arise through play.

### 2. Disposition
The NPC's attitude toward the PCs. Five categories: **hostile → disrespectful → indifferent → respectful → super-friendly.** Sets the base difficulty for all social actions. Determines how far the NPC will go for someone.

### 3. Patience
How much of the PCs' prodding the NPC will tolerate before shutting down. Always ticking down. Rarely restored (only when a player says exactly the right thing). When exhausted → **stonewalling** — the NPC stops listening entirely. All social actions auto-fail.

### 4. Motivation
What the NPC values, pursues, or considers meaningful — **apart from the Source of Conflict.** Each Motivation is a double-edged sword:
- **Vulnerability:** Wealth-motivated NPC → offering something valuable can overcome the conflict
- **Resistance:** Wealth-motivated NPC → trying to get them to part with something is perilous

More Motivations = more alternative strategies = easier for smart players.

### 5. Mood
A temporary emotional state — situational modifier (buff/debuff). Primary emotions: Neutral, Happy, Sad, Angry, Disgusted, Afraid, Surprised. Use an **Emotion Wheel** for secondary/tertiary emotions.

### Building the Stat Block
- **Source of Conflict:** Why is this encounter happening?
- **Disposition:** How does the NPC feel about the PCs? (Default: snap judgment based on appearance/context)
- **Patience:** How long will they put up with this? (Default: ~6 social actions for moderate difficulty)
- **Motivation:** What do they value apart from the conflict? (Default: one broad motivation)
- **Mood:** How are they feeling today? (Default: neutral)

**Keep this in your head, not on paper.** The more mechanical it feels, the less human the NPCs will seem.

## How Social Actions Map to the Stat Block

- **Connect** → usually shifts Disposition (sometimes Mood)
- **Understand** → usually learns what's on the Stat Block
- **Convince** → usually wears down Source of Conflict (sometimes changes Motivation)
- **Negotiate** → usually uses a Motivation to overcome the Source of Conflict

These are loose guidelines, not rigid categories. Don't obsess over classification — just figure out how the action affects the NPC's stats.

## Resolving Actions

### Look for Patterns
Players say lots of things; most don't work as standalone social actions. Watch for cumulative effect. **Rule of three:** if players pursue the same basic point three times collectively, let whoever made the third point resolve the action.

### Assign Outcomes
Before touching dice, decide what happens on success and failure:
- **Win/Loss:** Resolves or ruins the conflict
- **Progress/Setback:** Moves closer to or further from resolution
- **Buff/Debuff:** Changes the situation to make future outcomes more/less likely
- Some actions only matter if they succeed (or only if they fail) — that's fine

### Set Difficulty
1. Start with **Disposition** (base difficulty)
2. Adjust for **action type** (Convince = hardest → Negotiate → Understand → Connect = easiest)
3. Adjust for **Mood and Motivations** (plays into or grinds against)
4. Adjust for **NPC's skills/stats/role** (authority figure with high willpower = tough to bully)

Every action gets a bespoke difficulty. Five seconds of thought, max.

## Describing Outcomes: GMs Act

You know the game-mechanical result. Now portray it as a believable human response.

### Making Stuff Up
If the outcome doesn't make sense given what you know about the NPC, **invent a reason** why it does. Add it to what you know about the NPC. Act like it was always true. People are irrational — use that.

### Telegraphing
Every resolved social action is a chance to reveal something from the Stat Block. **Be obvious.** NPCs can literally say "I don't trust you" or "I can't deal with this, I'm too depressed." Goal: by encounter's end, savvy players should know the Source of Conflict (from the NPC's perspective) and how the NPC feels about the party.

### Updating the Stat Block
After describing the outcome, mentally update: Has Mood shifted? Disposition changed? Remember that actions have consequences beyond their outcomes — a failed bully attempt might leave the NPC Angry *and* liking the party less. Every social action chews up some Patience.

## Stonewalling: When Patience Runs Out

When an NPC is out of Patience, they've stopped listening. **All social actions fail — don't roll dice.**

How spent NPCs behave varies:
- Friendly NPC → distant, short perfunctory remarks
- Fearful NPC → tries to excuse themselves or flee
- Hostile/Angry NPC → might throw a punch if not left alone

**Don't be afraid to tell players flat-out that their approach cannot work.** Soft version: narrate the futility. Hard version: voice-of-God declaration that social actions have no impact — find another approach.

## Social Strategy Guide (Sidebar)

A general strategy for winning social encounters:

1. **Connect** — find common ground, build rapport
2. **Understand** — get the NPC talking, discover goals and motivations
3. **Assert** — clearly state your want or goal
4. **Convince** — show that helping you benefits them (or at least doesn't harm them); appeal to Motivations
5. **Reconnect** — if the NPC slips away, note a common interest
6. **Negotiate** — close the gap once you're near resolution
