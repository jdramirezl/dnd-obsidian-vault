# What Even Is an NPC

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/what-even-is-an-npc/)
**Date:** July 13, 2016
**Series:** Give Non-Players Character (NPCs)

---

## The Real Definition of an NPC

An NPC is **anything in the game that the GM actually role-plays as**. Not just humanoids with dialogue — spiders protecting eggs, a living city that reshapes its layout, a hostile post-apocalyptic landscape. If the GM makes decisions for it based on situation and motivations, it's an NPC.

**Role-playing** = considering a hypothetical situation, projecting yourself into a character's mind, and making the choice that character would make. It's about making choices based on situation and motivation — not speaking in a funny voice.

### Why Some Things Aren't NPCs at Some Tables

- A spider that's just pre-programmed to attack = not an NPC (no decisions being made)
- A spider protecting its eggs that threatens, retreats, or fights based on PC actions = NPC (GM is making motivated decisions)
- A god that bestows a scripted blessing = not an NPC
- A god that decides to reward a PC for an unexpected act of devotion = NPC

## NPCs as Game Elements vs. NPCs as Characters

Adventure design treats NPCs as **functional game elements** — the dragon king exists to deliver exposition, the shopkeeper is an interface to the equipment list, the evil princess is a boss fight. This is necessary for good scene structure.

But if NPCs are *only* functional robots, players will never care about them emotionally.

## Why Emotional Investment Requires NPCs to Be NPCs

RPGs have two sides: **RP** (story — what people get emotionally invested in) and **G** (game — what people strive to win). Both matter equally.

All stories are about people. We get emotionally invested because characters have **fears and motivations** we can understand as human beings. A well-structured game demands NPCs that serve functions in the story. An emotionally engaging game demands NPCs that trick players into thinking they are alive.

If your world is full of people — beings with understandable motivations acting on them — players will start to care. Nothing else creates emotional investment.

## The Core Technique: Don't Run the Game, Run the NPCs

Instead of approaching interactions as scenes to manage, approach them as if each NPC is your player character and **you have to win**.

- The dragon king hiring mercenaries? Play him like a desperate father who doesn't know if these strangers will say yes
- Giant spiders? Those are YOUR babies in those eggs. Posture, threaten, fight only if pressed, shield the eggs with your dying body
- The evil princess? She's a sociopath who delights in toying with minds — play her to win

**Don't script NPCs, role-play them.** Whatever scene they are in, figure out what they want and why. Assume you're a player and they won't get what they want unless you win. Then play to win.

### What This Creates

- Gnolls played as wild and feral → players **fear** them
- Orcs played as vicious savages → players **hate** them
- A villain who toys with victims → players find her **disturbing**
- A spider dying over its eggs → players feel **guilt**

When NPCs have motivations that are clear and consistent in their actions and choices, they seem alive. The world becomes about people. Players get invested.

## Key Takeaway

An NPC is anything the GM role-plays — anything the GM makes motivated decisions for. The secret to emotional engagement is treating every NPC like your own player character: understand what they want, then play to win. This single shift — from managing scenes to role-playing characters — transforms how players experience the game world.
