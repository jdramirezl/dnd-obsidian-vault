# How to Corrupt a Warlock (Or Anyone Else)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-corrupt-a-warlock/)
**Date:** August 22, 2022
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## What Corruption Means

Corruption is getting people to take actions they **know — or should know — are morally wrong.** That's it. It requires:

- **Objective morality** in your game world — a set of moral rules that are true for everyone, a metaphysical property of the cosmos. Without this, corruption is meaningless.
- **Decisions and choices** — corruption happens inside players' heads, not through game mechanics. You're literally trying to trick your players into violating the moral framework.
- **Consequences** — in the Angryverse, going full evil means losing your PC. It's a lose state, same as death.

## Shoulder Angels and Shoulder Devils

People are naturally inclined to do bad things because the bad thing is usually the quickest, easiest path to what they want. Choosing *not* to do bad things requires restraint, discipline, and self-control — that's **virtue.**

Every moral choice triggers an internal conflict between impulse and virtue — the cartoon Shoulder Angel vs. Shoulder Devil. Your job as GM isn't to *be* the Shoulder Devil. It's to **help the player's Shoulder Devil win the argument.** Just put your thumb on the scale.

## The Three Shoulder Devils

### 1. Devil of Desire (Glutton/Lust/Greed)
Speaks for basic impulses, urges, appetites. Promises pleasure. The **weakest** devil — most people with a moral compass learned to control these first. Rarely useful for corrupting PCs directly (in-world pleasures don't sway players much), but works on strong roleplayers.

### 2. Devil of Despair (Wrath/Cowardice)
Promises an end to pain, suffering, negative emotions. Hard to ignore because ignoring it means willful suffering. **Especially powerful** because suffering diminishes mental energy, and Shoulder Angels require a lot of mental energy. Everyone is vulnerable to this one.

### 3. Devil of Righteousness (Pride)
The sneaky one that **disguises itself as a Shoulder Angel.** Goes on about the greater good, ends justifying means, good intentions being everything. The most dangerous because it flies under the Angel's radar. Driven by hubris — the belief you can arbitrate good and evil for everyone and foresee all consequences.

## Devil Mechanics

- **Devils level up.** Every victory makes them stronger. Indulgence → habit → addiction. Each win makes the next one easier.
- **Devils evolve.** Desire → Despair (feeding desire stops making you happy, but not feeding it makes you sad) → Righteousness (mad at the world → must fix it by any means).
- **Devils summon allies.** When threatened, one devil can invoke another. "If you stop now, everything you've done was for nothing" (Despair). "Think of the glory that awaits" (Desire).

## The Corruption Process

### 1. Pick Your Target
Any PC. Warlocks are ideal (corruption is built into the class). Paladins and clerics also great targets. You don't need player permission — they gave it when they sat down.

### 2. Guess Which Devil Shows Up First
Consider the player and character's motives. How strongly does the player identify with the character?

- **Gamey players:** Appeal to gameplay motives (magic items, abilities, avoiding penalties)
- **Strong roleplayers:** In-world payoffs work (wealth, glory, acceptance)
- **Default guesses:** Warlocks → Desire. Paladins/clerics → Righteousness. Everyone → Despair.

### 3. Enlist a Corrupting Agent
An in-world NPC, creature, or entity that whispers poison. **Never corrupt ex cathedra** — players must trust the narrator. NPCs can lie; the GM cannot. Corrupting agents don't have to be deliberately evil — a friend with a skewed morality works just as well as a devil.

### 4. Cultivate an Innocent Relationship
Establish the agent as a trusted friend or beneficial ally. Give them camera time, make them helpful, don't ask much in return. Build trust over time.

### 5. Wait for an Opportunity (or Create One)
Watch for moments when a Shoulder Devil might show up — moral dilemmas, arguments about prisoners, shortcuts with questionable ethics. **Start small.** Make the immoral choice mostly harmless, victimless, wrong on a technicality. Easy XP for the Shoulder Devil.

### 6. Whisper Sweet Poison
Through the Corrupting Agent, offer a small, reasonable-sounding point that helps the Devil's case. Not a grand speech — just a take.

**Angry's Top 10 Corrupting Arguments:**

1. **Rationalize desires:** "You deserve to be happy too, don't you?"
2. **Downplay consequences:** "That's a small price to pay."
3. **Feed negative emotions:** "I'd be mad too. Don't you just want to punch that guy?"
4. **Turn responsibility outward:** "How can you accomplish anything if no one gives you a chance?"
5. **Appeal to pride:** "If people like us were in charge, we'd never let that happen."
6. **Amplify losses:** "Are you really willing to lose everything you worked for over this?"
7. **Amplify intentions:** "You didn't mean for any of that to happen."
8. **Appeal to moral relativism:** "From another perspective, that would be totally fine."
9. **Appeal to harm-based morality:** "If no one's getting hurt, how can that really be wrong?"
10. **Appeal to the greater good:** "Yes, there's a cost, but think how much better off everything will be."

### 7. Repeat — Worse Each Time
Wait for the next opportunity. If you did it right, each one comes faster. Watch for the PC making worse choices, advocating for them more, or hesitating longer before good choices. Engineer higher-stakes situations. Try to trigger Devil evolution. Take it slow.

If the moral needle isn't moving, try a different Devil or a new Corrupting Agent. If the player's compass stays true, concede the win — you can't win them all.
