# Antagonizing Your PCs

## Key Advice

Every creature — from an owlbear to a shopkeeper — is an **NPC first** and a stat block second. Antagonists are just "conflicts with brains." They come in three flavors: **Obstacles** (accidental, temporary conflicts), **Enemies** (motivated, ongoing opposition), and **Villains** (NPCs whose goals the PCs actively oppose). Design all antagonists with **Motivations**, **Approaches**, and **Limits** — this framework writes the encounter for you and prepares you for improvisation.

## Summary

Angry argues that the false distinction between "monsters" and "NPCs" cripples encounter design. Every autonomous creature has motivations, and treating them as NPCs opens up dynamic, multi-solution encounters. The article defines three types of antagonists:

- **Obstacles** block the PCs accidentally. Their goals temporarily conflict with the PCs'. Both sides can walk away satisfied. An owlbear defending its lair is an obstacle — scare it, sneak past it, or kill it.
- **Enemies** oppose the PCs directly. Their motivations are defined by the PCs' goals. The conflict festers in the background, creating scenes and encounters until something major changes. A vengeful merchant sending thugs is an enemy.
- **Villains** are antagonists whose goals the PCs oppose (the inverse of enemies). They create entire adventures. Covered in a separate article.

Each antagonist needs three things:
1. **Motivations** — what they want that creates conflict
2. **Approaches** — how they'd ideally achieve their goals
3. **Limits** — how far they're willing/able to go

Antagonists can change: obstacles become enemies, enemies can be resolved, and allies can become antagonists through PC actions. The key principle: **play the NPCs, not the plot.**

## Actionable Steps

1. **Treat every creature as an NPC** — even a giant spider has motivations (hunger, territory, protecting eggs) that determine how it behaves
2. **For each antagonist, write three things:**
   - **Motivation:** What does it want? (one sentence)
   - **Approach:** How does it prefer to achieve that? (one sentence)
   - **Limit:** What won't it do / what can't it do? (one sentence)
3. **Classify your antagonist:**
   - **Obstacle** = temporary, accidental conflict; both sides can walk away satisfied
   - **Enemy** = ongoing, motivated opposition; conflict persists until something major changes
4. **For obstacles:** Keep it simple — one sentence covers motivation, approach, and limit. Only prep stats you'll actually need (combat stats if violence is likely, social encounter tools if negotiation is likely)
5. **For enemies:** Think bigger — approaches should describe how the enemy creates or impacts scenes across the adventure. Limits should cover both willingness and capability
6. **Watch for antagonist transitions:**
   - Obstacle → Enemy (e.g., orcs realize PCs are hunting their chief)
   - Enemy → Resolved (PCs leave the area, pay off the merchant, etc.)
   - Ally → Antagonist (PCs insult or betray a friendly NPC)
7. **After each session**, review which NPCs the PCs interacted with and note how those NPCs feel about the PCs now — this generates future enemies and encounters organically

> [!tip] The Owlbear Test
> Before running any "combat encounter," ask: Why is this creature here? What does it want? What would make it stop fighting? If you can answer those three questions, you've turned a bland stat block into a dynamic encounter with multiple possible resolutions — even if the players still choose to fight.
