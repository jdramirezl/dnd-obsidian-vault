# Angry's Two-Note NPC

## Key Advice

You don't need to role-play NPCs — you need to **portray** them. Role-playing (projecting yourself into another mind) is something human brains are bad at. Acting (adopting mannerisms to convince others you're a different person) is something human brains are good at. Start each NPC with exactly **one humanizing trait** written on an index card, and let the character grow organically through play.

## Summary

The article dismantles the assumption that GMs need elaborate backstories and deep role-playing to create compelling NPCs. Angry argues that human brains can't truly role-play other minds — we always operate as "you, but..." with modifications. Instead of fighting this limitation, GMs should embrace it by using the **Two-Note NPC** method: define an NPC's **game purpose** (one sentence) and **one humanizing trait** (one sentence from seven categories). Write the trait on an index card, keep it visible during play, and let the character develop naturally. Over time, new traits will emerge organically as you portray the NPC consistently, and you write those on the back of the card.

The seven categories of humanizing traits are:
1. **Personality** — least useful as a starting trait; emerges naturally
2. **Vulnerability, Fear, and Pain** — best for quest-givers, object NPCs, extras
3. **Hopes, Dreams, and Aspirations** — positive counterpart to suffering; good for extras and quest-givers
4. **Values, Virtues, and Beliefs** — creates alliances and enmities; great for allies, villains, wildcards
5. **Flaws and Failings** — makes villains and tragic figures; can create pity without respect
6. **Self-Awareness** — powerful but requires lots of screen time
7. **Challenge, Growth, and Agency** — most powerful humanizer but needs long-term investment

## Actionable Steps

1. **Write one game purpose** for each NPC in a single short sentence (e.g., "gives the party the quest to find the missing shipment")
2. **Choose one humanizing trait** from the seven categories and write it on the front of an index card in big letters
3. **Match trait category to NPC role:**
   - Quest-givers/rescued NPCs → Suffering or Hopes
   - Obstacle NPCs → Suffering (exploitable) or Hopes (win-win resolution)
   - Villains/enemies → Flaws, Values, or (sparingly) Suffering
   - Extras/townsfolk → Mix of Suffering (60%) and Hopes (40%) for natural tone
   - Allies/wildcards → Values and Beliefs
4. **Keep the index card visible** during play — glance at it to keep the trait in mind
5. **Don't waste your one trait on Personality** — it will emerge naturally through portrayal
6. **When a new trait reveals itself** during play, write it on the back of the card
7. **Use sympathetic villains sparingly** — suffering-based villain backstories can accidentally remove their agency and moral responsibility

> [!tip] The Index Card Method
> Keep a stack of blank index cards at your table. Even for improvised NPCs, take 10 seconds to jot down a game purpose and one humanizing trait. Over sessions, your card collection becomes a living NPC reference that grows organically. The front has the defining trait; the back accumulates discovered traits over time.
