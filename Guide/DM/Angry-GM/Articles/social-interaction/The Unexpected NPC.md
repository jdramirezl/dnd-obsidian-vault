# The Unexpected NPC

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Unexpected NPC](https://theangrygm.com/the-unexpected-npc/)
**Date:** September 7, 2016
**Series:** Give Non-Player Characters Character

---

## Summary

How to handle NPCs that players create on the spot — either by focusing attention on background extras or by deciding a certain NPC must exist. The key: don't panic, don't over-prepare. Pick one word, play the scene, and let the NPC grow organically over time.

---

## Two Types of Unexpected NPCs

### 1. The Elevated Extra
- A nameless, faceless background NPC that players suddenly focus on.
- Players are like cameras — when they zoom in on a blurry extra, that extra must come into focus.
- Like an impulse purchase: players didn't enter the scene intending to befriend an NPC, but something caught their interest.
- May have a purpose (players think the NPC is useful) or may be pure exploration/curiosity.

### 2. The Imagined Into Being
- Players decide a certain NPC *must* exist and seek them out.
- "There must be an oracle in this city of wizards." "I need to find a new martial arts master."
- Usually created for a specific purpose — information, resources, character development.
- Essentially an Elevated Extra whose existence was only theoretical.

## The Core Process

### Step 1: What's the Point?
- Figure out *why* this NPC suddenly exists.
- Are the players trying to accomplish a quest goal? A character goal? Just exploring?
- **Never assume an Unexpected NPC will become recurring.** Focus only on getting through this one scene.

### Step 2: Can This Work? Can It Fail?
- Standard action adjudication: can the interaction succeed? Can it fail?
- Small purposes (fun conversation, world details) → almost always succeed, no encounter needed.
- Big purposes (information about a murder, gaining a powerful contact) → can fail, may need a full social encounter.
- If it can succeed AND fail → you have an encounter. Use the InterACTION! system.

### Step 3: Pick a Word
- Choose **one adjective** to define the NPC: angry, friendly, suspicious, paranoid, flirtatious, bored, scheming, etc.
- Write it on an index card. Keep it visible to you (not players).
- Play that word to the hilt. Overdo it. First impressions are caricatures — that's fine.
- **Screen time equals personality.** In the first scene, one word is all you need.

### Step 4: Play the Scene
- Respond to what the players say. Pretend you're that NPC. Whatever comes out of your mouth is fine.
- That's it. Seriously. That's all it takes for one scene.

### Step 5: Don't Be Afraid of Asspulls
- Details will spontaneously emerge as you play the NPC — the urchin flinches when threatened, the sage gets excited about draconic culture.
- These unplanned details are *discoveries*, not mistakes. Go with them.
- Asspulls aren't limited to the NPC — organizations, holidays, historical figures, and gods can all emerge from an NPC's mouth mid-scene.

## After the Scene: How Characters Are Born

### Keep Track
- Write the NPC's name, race, and one-word trait on an index card. Keep it.
- Add details that emerged during play.
- Between sessions, review your NPC cards — ask yourself *why* certain details exist and flesh them out.

### Organic Growth
- Players may return to an NPC they enjoyed interacting with.
- An NPC who proved useful becomes a resource the players revisit.
- When writing future adventures, flip through your NPC collection — Elevated Extras and Imagined NPCs make great returning characters.

### The Rule: Screen Time = Details
- NPCs become more detailed the more you play them.
- Don't front-load personality. Let it accumulate through play.
- Most Unexpected NPCs never go beyond one scene — and that's fine.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Don't panic when players create an NPC on the spot. You only need to survive one scene.
- One word is enough personality for a first appearance.
- Purpose before personality — understand what the scene needs to accomplish.
- Let NPCs grow organically through play. Details you discover are better than details you invent.
- Keep index cards. Review them between sessions. That's how throwaway extras become beloved recurring characters.
