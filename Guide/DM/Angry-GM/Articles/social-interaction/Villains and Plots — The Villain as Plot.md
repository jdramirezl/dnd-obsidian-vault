# Villains and Plots — The Villain as Plot

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/villains-and-plots-the-villain-as-plot/)
**Date:** January 31, 2017
**Series:** Give Non-Players Character (NPCs)

---

## Villains Aren't Just NPCs

A villain is more than a character — a villain is a **living, breathing manifestation of an adventure**. The villain's goals define the motivation and resolution for adventures, plot arcs, or entire campaigns.

Three types of antagonist NPCs:

- **Obstacles:** Temporary, accidental opposition (the guard blocking entry)
- **Enemies:** NPCs that oppose the PCs' goals (the cult trying to stop the quest)
- **Villains:** NPCs whose goals the PCs are motivated to oppose — the PCs become *reactionary*

With enemies, the players act and NPCs get in the way. With villains, the NPC acts and the PCs are obliged to get in the way.

## Creating a Villain = Planning an Adventure

Every villain needs three things:

1. **An NPC with a goal** — concrete and specific
2. **A motivation for the players** — why they must oppose the goal
3. **A resolution** — concrete ways the adventure can end (success *and* failure states)

The villain's goal defines everything about the adventure: how it starts and all the different ways it can end. Without a concrete goal that can be permanently ruined, the adventure can only end in success or death — failure is no longer viable.

### Example: Building Out a Villain Concept

- **Devil sowing chaos in a trade town** → Why? To corrupt souls → Whose? The town's lawful priest-leader → Goal: push the priest to commit an evil act → Resolution: priest is corrupted OR devil is exposed/banished
- **Magical item thefts** → Who? Cranium rats enslaving the thieves' guild → Why? Gathering materials to open a portal → Resolution: super-hive summoned OR rats' plan disrupted
- **Fallen paladin on a killing spree** → No failure state (endless murder until stopped) → Better: she's trying to become a lich to escape divine punishment → Resolution: she achieves immortality OR is killed/defeated/reformed

## The Plan — Adventure Structure

Once you have goal, motivation, and resolution, figure out the villain's **plan**: what they're doing to accomplish their goal. The adventure becomes about **discovering and disrupting** that plan.

### Why Use a Villain at All?

Villain adventures are **dynamic** — the GM isn't completely reliant on the players to drive all the action. The villain takes actions and the adventure constantly changes. Instead of going from 0% to 100% progress, the adventure starts at zero and forces push toward either +100% (PCs win) or −100% (villain wins).

### How to Plan a Villain Adventure

1. **Figure out the unopposed plan** — what happens if the PCs do nothing?
2. **Determine the hook** — how do the PCs get involved?
3. **Map the collision course** — how do PCs get from the hook to clashing with the villain's plan? (This early phase is usually a static adventure — investigation, leads, clues)
4. **Plan contingencies** — once PCs and villain collide:
   - How could PCs disrupt the plan?
   - How might the villain respond to disruptions?
   - What resources does the villain have to work against the party?

The GM invents at least some of the adventure structure on the fly in response to events.

## The Villain as Arc or Campaign

A single adventure resolves a villain's **plan**, not necessarily the villain. The plan is a product of a longer-term **motivation** — a desire or drive. Even if today's plan fails, tomorrow is a new opportunity.

### Recurring Villain Methods

**Shifting Goals + Rising Stakes:** Over time, the villain becomes more ambitious. The devil starts with individual souls → wants a promotion → wants to rule Hell → wants to become a god. Every campaign has a limited lifespan because eventually the stakes become "the entire cosmos" and there's nowhere left to go.

**The Vengeful Villain:** After failing, the villain pins it on the PCs and their goal becomes destroying the party. They sink into the background as an occasional enemy/obstacle, then resurface with new plans.

**The Chessmaster:** The villain has a complex, long-term plan with many steps. Each adventure resolves one step. You can decide retroactively that a previous plan was just one piece of a grander scheme.

**The Rogues Gallery:** A roster of villains the PCs have encountered. Any can drive an adventure or appear as an obstacle/enemy. Allows sprinkling villain adventures through a campaign without focusing on one long-term plot. You can always add someone new without it feeling forced. Stakes should still trend upward over time.

**The Power Behind the Power:** When a villain is permanently defeated, reveal they were working for a bigger, more dangerous villain. Introduces a new threat while making the story feel interconnected. Almost always comes with rising stakes.

## Key Takeaway

Writing a villain isn't creating an NPC — it's planning an adventure. The villain's goal drives the motivation, the plan provides the structure, and the resolution defines how it all ends. Villain adventures are inherently more dynamic and are a great place to learn improvisation because the villain's responses to PC actions generate new scenes naturally.
