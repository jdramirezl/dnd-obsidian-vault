# Declaring Your Players' Social Actions

## Key Advice

In social scenes, the GM must **silently translate** the chaotic flow of player conversation into proper action declarations — identifying the **Intention** (what the action could accomplish) and the **Approach** (how the character is doing it) — without breaking the conversational flow. Players rarely know what they're trying to accomplish; it's the GM's job to figure out what each remark *could conceivably achieve* using five outcome lenses.

## Summary

Part of the True Game Mastery series, this article tackles the hardest part of running social encounters: the **Declare** step of the Declare-Determine-Describe cycle. Three things make social scenes uniquely difficult:

1. **Conversations don't follow the D-D-D cycle.** People blurt things out, make pleasantries, repeat themselves, and interrupt. The more engaged the table is, the messier it gets.
2. **Social declarations don't look like declarations.** There's no "I stab the orc." Instead, players yell things like "You're a damned dirty liar!" — and the GM must parse that as a proper action declaration including body language and tone.
3. **Players don't know their own intentions.** People don't strategize through social encounters; they feel their way through, one sentence at a time.

The GM's job is to mentally reframe every player utterance into: *"In an attempt to [INTENTION] the NPC, my character says/does [WHATEVER] by using [APPROACH]."*

### The Five Outcome Lenses

These are not "social actions" or buttons to press — they're **GM-side lenses** for categorizing what a player's words could accomplish:

1. **Connect** (Build Rapport) — Finding common ground, showing interest, flattering, expressing sympathy. Makes the NPC like the PC more.
2. **Assert** — Stating needs, making requests, issuing demands. Tells you the player's overall goal. Response is usually "I accede" or "I refuse."
3. **Understand** — Learning what's in the NPC's head — their wants, fears, values. Comes from questions, active listening, or intuiting emotional responses.
4. **Convince** — Changing the NPC's goals, priorities, or understanding. The broadest and most common lens. Includes persuasion, reasoning, coercion, and blackmail.
5. **Negotiate** — Offering something the NPC values in trade. Covers direct trades, implied promises, and even seduction (an implied promise of *something* valuable).

### Approaches vs. Outcomes

D&D's social skills (Persuasion, Intimidation, Deception, Insight) describe **Approaches** (demeanor), not Outcomes. Any skill can serve any outcome. Read them as: "...using charm," "...using fear," "...using deceit," "...using intuition."

### Three Foundational Rules

- **Players choose; characters act.** Players must decide what their characters say and how.
- **It doesn't matter how players communicate.** First-person dialogue or third-person description — both are valid.
- **NPCs don't choose; game mechanics determine their responses.** The GM portrays the result as if it were a logical human choice.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Slow down.** Don't rush to respond. Use "umm" or a pause to hold your place in the conversation while you process what the player just said
2. **Pay attention to everything** — words, tone, body language, gestures. It's all part of the social action declaration
3. **Mentally translate every player utterance** into: *"In an attempt to [Connect/Assert/Understand/Convince/Negotiate], [Character] says/does [X] using [Approach]"*
4. **Make a cheat sheet** with the five outcome lenses and keep it behind your screen:
   - **Connect** — build rapport, find common ground
   - **Assert** — state a need or demand
   - **Understand** — learn what the NPC thinks/wants/fears
   - **Convince** — change the NPC's mind or priorities
   - **Negotiate** — offer something of value in trade
5. **Don't ask players what they're trying to accomplish** — they don't know. Figure out what their words *could conceivably achieve* and assign the most reasonable outcome
6. **Don't conflate Outcomes with Approaches.** Match D&D skills to *how* the character is acting (charm, fear, deceit, intuition), not *what* they're trying to achieve
7. **Practice outside of high-stakes scenes.** Parse social actions during campfire chatter, party planning, and casual NPC interactions to build the skill before it matters
8. **Provide "Coyote Time"** — like the hidden grace period in platformers, help players succeed by interpreting their messy social actions generously. Warn them before they say something catastrophically stupid, just as you'd warn about an attack of opportunity

> [!tip] The Social Outcome Cheat Sheet
> Write these five words on a sticky note behind your GM screen: **Connect · Assert · Understand · Convince · Negotiate**. For every player utterance in a social scene, ask yourself: "Which of these five things could this remark accomplish?" That single question transforms chaotic table chatter into actionable declarations you can resolve. Don't share this list with players — it's a GM-side tool, not a menu of social combat actions.
