# Ask Angry August 2020 Mailbag

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-august-mailbag/)
**Date:** September 2, 2020
**Category:** Ask Angry

---

## Summary

Four questions covering: why you should write your own adventures for Old School Hack instead of converting D&D modules, why you can't force players to roleplay more (and shouldn't try), how to handle the adventuring day problem during wilderness travel in D&D 5E, and how to run a D&D campaign with military/naval command (including a deep dive into wargaming scales).

---

## Key Points

### Q1: Old School Hack — Convert Modules or Switch to D&D 5E?

**Answer:** Neither. Write your own adventures for OSH.

- OSH is lightweight, fun, and easy to work with — you can make stuff up on the spot.
- Converting modules from other systems doesn't work because OSH has unique features (like arenas in combat) that other games' encounter designs don't leverage.
- **Arenas:** Combat takes place across multiple interconnected battlefields (OPEN, DENSE, HAZARDOUS, TIGHT), each with mechanical features affecting weapons and tactics. Characters can shove or throw each other into new arenas, expanding the fight dynamically.
- D&D modules think in single rooms with open floor space — they don't think in terms of arena expansion.
- You can use D&D 4E/5E Monster Manuals for inspiration on unique monster powers, but build encounters from scratch.

### Q2: How to Encourage Players to Roleplay More

**Answer:** You can't. Stop trying.

- GMs have ideas about how RPGs "should" be played — how much interaction, exploration, lore interest, etc. But not everyone finds all that fun.
- You can't force people to like things they don't like. You can't argue fun rationally.
- **"Roleplaying" as Joe defines it (extended NPC interaction) isn't actually roleplaying.** Roleplaying is making decisions a fictional character would make in a hypothetical situation. D&D is 95% combat engine — the only things central to it are dungeons, dragons, and killing stuff.
- **The solution: JUST TALK TO YOUR PLAYERS.** Ask why they don't engage with NPCs. Listen. Respect what they say. You might discover:
  - They just don't find it fun (nothing you can do).
  - They're nervous or don't know what to do (you might not be driving the action properly).
  - They don't like your NPCs.
- **The polka analogy:** Angry likes polka music but his friends don't. Sometimes friends will go to a polka show because they like beer and spending time together. Your players might go along with interaction occasionally if you integrate it into stuff they do like — but don't expect them to love it.

### Q3: The Adventuring Day Problem During Wilderness Travel

D&D's strategic gameplay revolves around resource management across ~6 encounters per adventuring day. This creates problems for wilderness travel where you can't fill every day with 6 encounters.

**The design tension:**
- Individual encounters are trivial if the party is fresh — short adventuring days are easy steamrolls.
- But you can't load every travel day with 6 encounters (unfun, causes unwanted level-ups that break dungeon balance).
- You also can't ignore the wilderness — it's supposed to be dangerous. Without encounters, the world map becomes a fast-travel menu.

**Two combined solutions:**

1. **Don't care too much** — run 2 substantial encounters every 3 days on average, plus minor obstacles and decisions between. Players aren't that sensitive to challenge.

2. **Adjust encounter difficulty by context:**
   - Planned adventure encounters → Medium or Hard
   - Incidental/wandering encounters → Easy
   - Wilderness travel encounters → Deadly

This makes travel feel dangerous without overloading it.

### Q4: Running a Military/Naval Command Campaign in D&D

**The vision:** Two modes of play — standard D&D adventuring plus large-scale military command (armies, strategy, logistics).

**Key insight:** You need two different games — D&D for individual action and a separate wargame for mass combat. D&D's mechanics are built for individual characters and break down for groups, formations, and units.

**Why D&D mass combat homebrew usually fails:**
- Most systems just apply individual stats (AC, HP, attacks) to groups of 50 orcs — but combat between formations doesn't work like combat between individuals.
- Unit classification, size, training, flanking, and terrain matter more than individual stats.
- Engagements involve simultaneous fighting (not turn-based blow trading) and units rarely fight to destruction in HP terms.

**Two scales of wargaming to know:**
- **Skirmish-level** — each mini = one fighter, small warbands. D&D can already handle this. Examples: Chainmail, D&D Miniatures, Necromunda.
- **Campaign/strategic-level** — each counter = hundreds of soldiers. This is what you want for army-vs-army battles. Examples: old hex-and-counter games (Gettysburg, Panzergruppe Guderian).

**Recommended resources:**
- **Fistful of Lead** (Wiley Games) — generic skirmish rules with a *Bigger Battles* expansion for scaling up.
- **Empires series** (War of Life Gaming) — generic campaign-level wargame for various eras including fantasy. Very inexpensive with printable counters.
- **Wreck Age** (Hyacinth Games) — hybrid RPG/skirmish game with base-building (post-apocalyptic, but interesting for how it manages multiple game modes).
