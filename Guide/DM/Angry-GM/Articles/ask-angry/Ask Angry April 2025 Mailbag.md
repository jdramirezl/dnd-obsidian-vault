# Ask Angry April 2025 Mailbag — Investigation Skill and Interesting Mechanics

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-april-2025-mailbag/)
**Date:** March 31, 2025
**Category:** Ask Angry

---

## Summary

Three questions covering: how to properly use the Investigation skill in D&D 5E (and information-gathering skills in general), what makes a spell or magical item "mechanically interesting" (nothing — mechanics aren't interesting), and an analysis of separating attack rolls from damage rolls and using cards instead of dice.

---

## Key Points

### Q1: How to Use the Investigation Skill in D&D 5E

5E's Investigation skill is badly conceived — no one really knows how it's supposed to work. But the question is really about how to use *any* information-gathering skill properly.

**The framework:**
- **Perception** — find information that's concealed from notice.
- **Investigation** — get information that's visible but needs to be assembled from multiple details using reason.
- **Medicine, Arcana, Religion, History, etc.** — get specific kinds of information using expert knowledge in the right situations.

**The core principle:** A die roll should always *precede* gameplay, never *end* it. Gameplay is making decisions and drawing conclusions — figuring things out. Skills provide raw facts; players assemble those facts into conclusions.

**Bad example:** Rolling Perception to find "It was Eustace of Murderville" written by the victim — this ends gameplay.

**Good example:** Rolling Perception to find red hair in the victim's fist — this leads to more gameplay (investigating suspects, disproving alibis, checking if evidence was planted).

**Investigation in practice** (from The Fall of Silverpine Watch):
- A desk with an unfinished letter, half-burned candle, and dried-out ink → Investigation reveals someone was interrupted, left calmly, intended to return but never did.
- A suit of armor → Investigation reveals it's well-worn (not ceremonial) and conspicuously missing a weapon.

**Handling specialized knowledge (Medicine, Arcana, etc.):**
1. Do subject-matter research (AI tools like ChatGPT are great for this).
2. Describe what the character sees in layperson terms.
3. Explain the significance to an expert.
4. Stop before giving the conclusion — let the player figure it out.

**Example:** Livor mortis on the wrong side of a body → describe the bruising, explain what it means to a medical expert, but let the player conclude the body was moved.

### Q2: What Makes a Mechanically Interesting Spell or Item?

**Answer:** Nothing. Mechanics don't make things interesting. Mechanics are the opposite of interesting.

- **MDA Framework** (Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics): You don't start with mechanics. You start with a concept — what does the thing do in the game world? What interactions does it create? What problems does it solve or cause?
- Design the concept first, then build mechanics to make it happen, then pare them down to the simplest, most elegant form using as many existing mechanics as possible.
- "Come up with something mechanically interesting" is an unanswerable request. "Here's what I want this item to do in the game" is something Angry can help with.

### Q3: Cards vs Dice, and Separating Attack from Damage Rolls

**Analyzing mechanics requires:**
- Understanding every mechanic has both costs and benefits.
- Being intellectually honest and rigorously dispassionate — shut off your preferences.
- Considering the human factor (psychology always matters).

**THAC0 analysis** (as an example of honest mechanical analysis):
- Subtraction is measurably harder for human brains than addition — children show aversion to it by kindergarten.
- But THAC0 had a benefit: without modifiers, you knew instantly if you hit when the die landed (tension curve payoff).

**Separating attack and damage rolls — benefits:**
- Variable damage that can differentiate attack types.
- Without separate damage, damage is locked to the attack-vs-AC differential.
- Fixed damage (no damage roll) lets players calculate exact hits-to-death, turning combat into pure optimization — which is why critical hits were invented.

**Separating attack and damage rolls — costs:**
- Two rolls per attack = efficiency loss (and the time sink isn't the toss, it's all the checking, pre-math, post-math).
- **Loss Aversion** — the damage roll re-contextualizes the attack roll. A bad damage roll makes a successful hit feel meaningless. People feel losses more than gains (~2x ratio). Rolling both together is even worse: you see the damage you *would have* dealt on a miss.
- Extra randomness favors the underdog, working against balanced encounters.

**Verdict for D&D:** Splitting attack and damage is a good choice — easy, efficient way to add damage variation and differentiate weapons. The costs aren't severe enough to outweigh the benefits. But for a different game (less action-focused, higher stakes), the answer might differ.

**Cards vs dice:** Probably a gimmicky waste of time for D&D. But the real question should be: what gameplay experience are you trying to create, and how did you conclude cards are the best way to introduce randomness?
