# Ask Angry: Spelljammer, Zelda, and Paper Mario

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-spelljammer/) | **Category**: Ask Angry | **Date**: July 21, 2015

---

## Key Advice

- **Never point your GMing gun at something you aren't willing to pull the trigger on.** Don't stake the entire campaign on a single adventure's success unless you're prepared to end the campaign.
- **Sissy GMing is defining stakes and then not following through** — not the act of designing around catastrophic failure. Thinking ahead about failure is *good* GMing.
- **Adventures and campaigns should be constructed so players can recover from failure more easily early on** and face escalating consequences toward the end.
- **When the party fails, the failure must be tangible and clear.** The worst failure is one where the players don't even know they've failed and keep banging their heads against an impossible wall.
- **Avoid the Ocarina of Time Paradox:** don't create a quest where the best strategy is to do nothing. Give heroes a reason they MUST act.

## Summary

A GM running a Spelljammer campaign asks how to handle failure when each adventure is a link in a sequential chain — fail one, and the whole campaign dies. Angry's response is a masterclass in designing failure-resilient campaign structures.

**The GMing Gun Principle:** It's not "sissy" to design around catastrophic failure. Sissy GMing is when you define stakes and then fudge to avoid consequences. Smart GMing is recognizing when your stakes are too high and restructuring before the problem occurs.

**Make Failure Tangible:** Instead of "the artifact is behind a door you can never open" (invisible, frustrating failure), introduce a rival faction also seeking the artifacts. Failure means the enemy gets the artifact — clear, dramatic, and recoverable.

**The Compass/Radar Design:** Instead of a strict sequential chain (artifact 1 → 2 → 3), make each artifact detect nearby artifacts with increasing range as you collect more. This creates a dynamic where:
- Multiple factions race to collect artifacts
- Losing one artifact doesn't end the campaign — it changes the dynamic
- Having more artifacts makes you both more powerful AND a bigger target
- The endgame becomes a climactic confrontation between factions

**The Ocarina of Time Paradox:** In OoT, the villain couldn't access the magical gems — they were safely locked away. The heroes gathered them, making them vulnerable, and the villain stole them all. The optimal strategy was to do nothing. Don't design quests where inaction is the best choice.

**The Paper Mario Solution:** In Paper Mario: TTYD, the artifacts sealed away an evil power, but the seal was weakening. Heroes HAD to gather them to reinforce the seal. Villains wanted them to control the power. Inaction meant the seal breaks and everyone loses. This gives every faction a compelling reason to act.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Audit your campaign's failure points.** If a single failed adventure ends the whole campaign, restructure so failure changes the situation rather than ending it.
2. **Introduce rival factions** seeking the same goal. Failure means the rival advances, not that the game is over.
3. **Make failure visible.** Players should always know when they've failed and what the consequences are.
4. **Design artifact/macguffin quests with non-sequential collection** — radar-style detection rather than breadcrumb trails.
5. **Ensure heroes MUST act.** Create a ticking clock or deteriorating status quo so that doing nothing is the worst option.
6. **Think about why villains AND heroes want the same thing** — and why neither side can just destroy one piece to prevent the other from completing the set.
7. **Scale consequences:** early failures should be recoverable setbacks; late failures should be dramatic and high-stakes.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Don't stake your campaign on a single point of failure. Design so that failure changes the game rather than ending it, and make sure every faction — heroes and villains — has a compelling reason to act.
