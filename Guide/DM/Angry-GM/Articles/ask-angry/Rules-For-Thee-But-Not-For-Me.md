# An Ask Angry Very Special Episode: Rules For Thee But Not For Me

## Key Advice

- **"If the players can do it, so can the monsters" is a terrible attitude.** Using monster reciprocation to punish players for mastering the system is adversarial and destructive. Players are just using the tools you gave them.
- **Fix exploits through conversation, not arms races.** If a combo is genuinely broken, put on your game designer hat, call a meeting, explain the issue, and collaboratively rebalance. Offer a trophy or reward for their cleverness.
- **Symmetry ≠ Fairness.** Forcing PCs and monsters to follow identical rules often creates *unfair* gameplay, not fair gameplay. PCs and combat foes have fundamentally different roles and needs.
- **Monsters don't need PC rules.** A combat foe doesn't need social skills, spell slots, or the full PC action economy. Weighting monsters down with unnecessary PC mechanics makes them worse, not fairer.
- **Asymmetry serves cooperative games.** In a players-vs-game structure, fair challenges are often asymmetrical. Players can use whatever the game allows; the GM is limited to actions that create fair and reasonable challenges.
- **D&D's action economy is broken by forced symmetry.** Legendary Actions and Lair Actions are duct-tape fixes because the "everyone gets one turn" symmetry puts solo monsters at a massive disadvantage against a party of 3-5.
- **The GM is the emergency backup game designer.** When the system breaks, the GM's job is to identify the problem and fix it collaboratively — not to weaponize the same exploit against the players.

## Summary

Published June 5, 2024. A reader named Alex asks about the common GM refrain "if the players can do it, so can the monsters." Angry spends the entire column unpacking why this seemingly simple question reveals deep, false assumptions about symmetry and fairness in RPG design. He argues that the retaliatory "I can do it too" response is a terrible way to handle perceived exploits — the correct approach is collaborative game design at the table. More broadly, he demonstrates that PCs and monsters serve fundamentally different roles in the game, and forcing them to follow identical rules often produces *less* fair gameplay, not more. D&D's Legendary Actions, Multiattack, and spell slot systems are all symptoms of forced symmetry creating problems that require clumsy patches.

> [!tip] Core Principle
> Symmetry does not guarantee fairness. In cooperative RPGs, fair gameplay challenges are often asymmetrical — PCs and monsters have different needs, different roles, and should follow different rules designed for those roles. When something seems broken, talk to your players and fix it together rather than escalating an arms race.
