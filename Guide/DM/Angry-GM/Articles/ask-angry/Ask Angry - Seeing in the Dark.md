# Ask Angry: Seeing in the Dark

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-seeing-in-the-dark/) | **Category**: Ask Angry | **Date**: March 22, 2016

---

## Key Advice

- **Darkvision is NOT worth multiple feats or ability score increases.** All racial abilities together are worth roughly one feat. Darkvision is a fraction of that.
- **Most GMs treat light as binary** — either you can see or you can't — which makes darkvision nearly irrelevant in practice.
- **Carrying a light source in the dark makes you visible from an enormous distance.** A candle flame is visible from 30 miles away in darkness. Everything underground can see you coming.
- **Traveling in total darkness without a light source is genuinely dangerous** — uneven ground, unexpected drops, and disorientation can cause serious injury.
- **Darkvision SHOULD be terrifying in the hands of enemies** but D&D doesn't treat it that way, and most GMs don't either.

## Summary

The article tackles whether darkvision is overpowered as a racial trait. Short answer: no. In 5E's racial balance math, all racial abilities together (beyond the universal +2 to one stat) are worth roughly one-and-a-half feats. Darkvision is just one piece of that package.

But the more interesting discussion is about how lighting and visibility *should* work versus how they *actually* work at most tables. In theory, lighting is a critical tactical element: light sources telegraph your position to everything in a dungeon, dark-dwelling creatures have a massive home-field advantage, and scouting without darkvision is nearly impossible because you need light to see but light reveals you. In practice, nobody tracks torch durations, light radii, or the distance between the light-bearer and each party member. Once someone has a torch, the dungeon is "lit" and nobody thinks about it again.

D&D makes it easy to ignore lighting — Light is a cantrip, everburning torches are cheap, sunrods were freely available in 4E. The rules for different light sources are needlessly varied without being complex enough to matter. The result: lighting becomes a binary (can see / can't see) rather than the rich tactical layer it could be.

The real value of lighting in RPGs is as a **constraint on perception** — it limits how far PCs can see, providing a natural fog of war for dungeon exploration. This mirrors how the dungeon itself constrains the battlefield compared to open wargaming.

The practical problem with darkvision is that unless *everyone* in the party has it, the group still needs a light source — which negates the advantage. A darkvision scout can gain some value, but most dungeon creatures also have darkvision, so the element of surprise is situational at best. Darkvision is such a pain to adjudicate with mixed parties that it might be better to just remove it from all PC races.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Don't overvalue darkvision** when balancing homebrew races or granting boons. It's worth a fraction of a feat at most.
2. **If you want lighting to matter**, simplify the rules: binary states (lit / dim / dark) with clear, consistent consequences for each.
3. **Remember that light sources are visible from very far away.** If the party carries a torch into a dark dungeon, everything knows they're coming.
4. **Consider what happens when PCs try to go lightless.** Humans in total darkness risk injury from uneven terrain and can't effectively scout or fight.
5. **Use lighting as fog of war** — limit descriptions to what the party's light source can actually illuminate.
6. **For mixed darkvision parties**, decide upfront how you'll handle it. Either make lighting matter consistently or don't bother tracking it at all.
7. **Darkvision creatures still see in grayscale with limited range** — they'd use light sources in their own settlements for the same reasons surface-dwellers do.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Darkvision isn't overpowered — it's underutilized. Lighting should be a meaningful tactical constraint, but D&D makes it too easy to ignore. Either commit to tracking it or don't pretend it matters.
