# Ask Angry April 2024 Mailbag

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-april-2024-mailbag/)
**Date:** April 10, 2024
**Category:** Ask Angry

---

## Summary

Three questions covering: why GMs should forbid players from sharing their characters' inner thoughts, why Angry always starts campaigns in taverns (and what that actually means), and how to handle players who overplan and get stuck in the "Thinking and Talking Trap."

---

## Key Points

### Q1: Should GMs Ask Players What Their Characters Think?

**Short answer:** Absolutely not. Forbid players from sharing inner monologues.

- **Kills interaction** — if everyone knows what everyone's thinking, no one needs to ask "why did you do that?" Asking about motivations is the biggest driver of interaction in team-based games.
- **Encourages Main Character Syndrome** — treating fellow players as an audience rather than teammates. This is the real damage livestreamed games have done to the hobby — not unrealistic expectations, but the idea that each player should put on a show.
- **Removes mystery and misunderstanding** — in real life and non-book narratives, we infer what people think from their actions and words, which is inherently more engaging than being told.
- **People aren't deliberate** — most human choices are impulsive, happening before conscious reasoning. Over-rumination leads to inhuman, overly logical reasoning that doesn't reflect how real people (or good characters) behave.
- The science of impulse control and self-reflection supports this: we learn to understand our own impulses by interacting with others and inferring their inner lives, not by being told.

### Q2: Do You Always Start in a Tavern? Does That Mean Starting with Interaction?

**The "Always Start at an Inn" rule** is real but flexible:

- **Why Angry does it:**
  1. Tweaks the postmodern hipsters who obsess over avoiding clichés — Angry runs great games starting with the tropiest cliché of all.
  2. Reminds him that how a game starts has little bearing on its greatness.
  3. Reminds him he doesn't need to write great games — greatness happens at the table, in how you run.
  4. Eliminates Blank Page Syndrome — knowing the first scene's setting makes it easy to start writing.

- **Starting at an inn ≠ starting with Interaction** — characters don't have to chat. They could fight goblins, escape a cage, or exterminate cellar rats. The inn is just the setting.
- **Starting at an inn ≠ strangers meeting by chance** — anything could have happened before the inn. The characters might be prisoners, investigators, or an established group.
- **The real rule:** Don't start with unstructured "talk to each other until something happens." Either give players a goal before the first scene or drive the interaction yourself with NPCs. Players don't have the context or comfort to just start playing off each other cold.

### Q3: What to Do When Players Plan Too Long

Players overplan not because they enjoy it, but because they don't know how to plan properly. They spitball ideas and shoot them down looking for guaranteed success — "the perfect is the enemy of the good."

**The Thinking and Talking Trap:** Players Think and Talk in circles, rejecting every idea that has any flaw, never refining any idea into an actual plan.

**Three escalating interventions:**

1. **Impatience and Urgency** — Express polite out-of-game impatience. Tap your foot, look at the clock, say "get on with it." This is an out-of-game problem requiring an out-of-game solution. Often works because players recognize they're stuck and your nudge implies "I promise it'll be okay."

2. **Join the Discussion** — Talk directly to the players about what they're doing. Help them see they're going in circles. Point out things their characters would notice. Encourage them to either refine one idea into a plan OR just take the direct approach. Don't give them a plan or evaluate their plans with hidden information.

3. **Nope the Hell Out** — Call the session. "You have a week to work out your plans. Next session, we start at the castle gates. Be ready." Either this breaks the trap or you get a night off — win-win.

**Root cause:** If players routinely overplan, either the game is too complex for them or you've taught them that anything less than perfect success has catastrophic consequences. The problem is the game you're running, not the players.
