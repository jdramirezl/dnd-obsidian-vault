# Ask Angry: The Suckiest Ability Scores Ever!

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-the-suckiest-ability-scores-ever/) | **Category**: Ask Angry | **Date**: July 16, 2015

---

## Key Advice

- **You cannot play a character smarter than you are.** Your brain literally cannot come up with ideas it can't come up with. Intelligence as a stat gets in the way of letting players make choices and solve problems — which is the whole point of an RPG.
- **Charisma is not a magical aura of likability.** It's the raw ability to project your will onto others — to make people listen and pay attention. High Charisma used to be an asshole makes people REALLY hate you. Hate is a reaction too.
- **Charisma is a broken catchall for all social interaction.** Whether you lie, threaten, persuade, seduce, or confuse — it's always Charisma. A locked door can be finessed (DEX), smashed (STR), or magicked open, but a guard encounter is always Charisma regardless of approach.
- **Social skills should be paired with whatever ability score matches the approach**, not locked to Charisma. Threatening someone? STR + Intimidation. Intellectual debate? INT + Persuasion. Technical performance? DEX + Performance.
- **Let players play their characters as smart or dumb as they want.** Don't mandate "your character is too dumb to figure that out."

## Summary

Angry dismantles Intelligence and Charisma as ability scores, arguing both create more problems than they solve.

**Intelligence** is problematic because RPGs are fundamentally about players making choices and solving problems. Intelligence checks either hand players answers (removing the puzzle) or block them from engaging ("your character is too dumb"). Nobody wants either outcome. The "get a hint" roll is a band-aid that doesn't fix the underlying issue. A hypothetical Angry RPG would simply not have Intelligence — the system would be designed not to need it, the same way D&D doesn't have "reflexes" or "beauty" as separate stats.

**Charisma** fails for different reasons. First, most GMs demand players describe exactly what they say during social encounters, unlike physical actions where "I break down the door" suffices. This is unfair — "I want to persuade the king using the evidence we found" should be as valid as "I use my crowbar." Second, Charisma is the sole determining factor for ALL social interaction regardless of approach. A gymnast impressing a crowd should use DEX, an intellectual debate should use INT, and physical intimidation should use STR — but it's always Charisma because two people are talking. This limits who can be effective in social situations and creates bad gameplay.

**The Fix (Hypothetical):** Remove Charisma entirely. Social skills (Persuasion, Deception, Intimidation) become bonuses applied to whatever ability score matches the approach — just like any other skill check. How likeable someone is depends entirely on the choices the player makes. Remove Intelligence too — design the system not to need it.

**For D&D specifically:** You might get away with removing Charisma using the variant rule (PHB p.175) that allows pairing any ability score with any skill. Reassign Charisma-based spellcasting to other stats. Fixing both INT and CHA might push D&D too hard — at that point, play a different game or push Angry until he writes his own RPG.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Use the 5E variant rule (PHB p.175)** to decouple ability scores from skills. Let the approach determine the ability score.
2. **STR + Intimidation** for physical threats. **INT + Persuasion** for intellectual arguments. **DEX + Performance** for acrobatic displays. Match the stat to the method.
3. **Don't require players to give speeches** for social checks. "I want to persuade him using X leverage" is sufficient, just like "I break down the door" is sufficient for STR checks.
4. **Never tell a player their character is too dumb** to attempt something. Let players make choices freely.
5. **If a player with high CHA plays an asshole**, let the world treat them like a high-impact asshole. NPCs will really hate them — because Charisma makes you hard to ignore, not likeable.
6. **For puzzles:** either run them for players who enjoy solving puzzles (using player skill) or don't run them at all. "Roll to solve" isn't a puzzle.
7. **Consider reassigning spellcasting stats** if you remove Charisma: Sorcerers → WIS, Bards → INT, Warlocks → CON, etc.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Intelligence blocks players from doing what RPGs are for (making choices). Charisma is a redundant catchall that limits social encounters to one stat regardless of approach. The fix: pair social skills with whatever ability score matches the method, and let players play their characters however they want.
