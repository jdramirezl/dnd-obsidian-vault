# Ask Angry August 2023 Mailbag

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-august-2023-mailbag/)
**Date:** September 18, 2023
**Category:** Ask Angry

---

## Summary

Three questions covering: how to get players to accept being captured (you can't if they're brats — and that's a relationship problem), best practices for using music at the game table, and what "Beer and Pretzels" means in tabletop gaming.

---

## Key Points

### Q1: Getting Players to Accept Being Captured

**Short answer:** No — not if your players will literally quit the game rather than play the scenario.

- Players who threaten to walk away from the table rather than engage with a scenario they don't like are exhibiting toxic, abusive relationship behavior. They're toddlers throwing tantrums.
- **"Getting the Garden to Weed Itself"** — run the scenario you want to run and let toxic players remove themselves. You'll either end up with a healthier gaming relationship or more free time. Win-win.
- Don't be so desperate to run games that players can bully you by threatening to withhold participation. The moment you give in, you stop being a Game Master and become a puppet.
- If players grumble but stay, that's fine — don't let up. "This is the game; play it or don't." Players can't resist a good game for long.
- **Exception:** If a player respectfully says they're uncomfortable with genuinely heavy content (gore, sexual themes), that's different from refusing a classic "captured by slavers, break out" scenario.

### Q2: Music at the Game Table

Angry is not against music — he's against GMs using it badly. Which they inevitably do.

**Communication trumps everything:**
- Music makes it harder to hear speech. Electronic communication already reduces audio quality. Video chat is worse.
- Unless you invest in autoducking (software that automatically reduces music when someone speaks) or let everyone adjust voice-to-music volume independently, **do not use music in online games.**

**Pacing trumps mood:**
- Switching tracks for every scene change breaks pacing unless you can do it without pausing narration or breaking eye contact.
- You're not a movie director with an orchestra — you can't manage dynamic music and run the game simultaneously.

**The right way to use music — fire-and-forget:**
- Pick a tone for the entire session. Find a few hours of matching music. Let it loop in the background.
- If you have 2–3 major scenes (town, wilderness, dungeon), have a playlist for each and switch during natural transitions (loading screens, bathroom breaks).
- **Background music is like makeup — it works best when no one notices it's there.**

**Music choice rules:**
- **No recognizable tracks** — brains enter "recall and identify" mode ("Wait… is that… MYSTIC CAVE ZONE!").
- **No lyrics** — humans can't hear language without trying to comprehend it.
- **No jarring genres** — electronic/chiptune music throws some people off. Orchestral music is safest: timeless, versatile, rarely breaks immersion.

**Bottom line:** Using music well is deceptively tricky with a lot of work involved. Consider whether there's a better way to spend your prep time.

### Q3: What Is a "Beer and Pretzels" Game?

- A casual play experience — not meant to be taken seriously or tax you mentally.
- The term comes from the midwestern wargaming community decades ago.
- In RPGs, it refers to the **attitude of participants**, not a specific system. It can mean silly random jokiness, but it can also just mean kick-in-the-door hack-and-slash dungeon crawling without deep character play.
- The key is low emotional investment and low cognitive load — high in Abenegation/Submission (MDA terms).
- In board/wargaming, Beer and Pretzels games are light on rules, quick to set up and break down.
- **Recommended game:** *Kobolds Ate My Baby* (KAMB) — uses the BEER Engine (Brawn, Ego, Extraneous, Reflexes). The rulebook is entertaining even if you never run it.
