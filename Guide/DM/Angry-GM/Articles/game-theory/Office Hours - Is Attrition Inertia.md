## Key Concepts

### What Is Attrition?

- **Attrition is a gameplay dynamic where individual challenges chew up resources** rather than being designed to end the game outright. The overarching challenge is managing resources so you can always handle the next encounter.
- **Even encounters that can't kill you still matter** under Attrition—they cost resources, making future encounters harder.
- **Attrition makes strategic choices meaningful.** Without it, there's no reason to agonize over pressing forward vs. retreating, exploring side paths vs. staying on course, or sneaking past monsters vs. fighting them.
- **Attrition plays well with other dynamics** like Challenge Progressions and Boss Fights. Managing resources well gives you an advantage at the climax; managing poorly means you might not survive it.

### What Is Inertia?

- **Inertia is a gameplay dynamic that either pushes the game toward a loss or opposes/undoes the players' progress toward victory.** It's not a challenge to overcome—it's a force pulling the game toward disaster.
- **Weak Inertia** costs progress (undoes what players have achieved).
- **Strong Inertia** increases the odds of losing over time.

### Attrition Is NOT Inertia (By Itself)

- **Retreating to rest doesn't cost real progress in modern D&D.** Time is mostly meaningless, adventures are static (monsters don't respawn, reinforce, or flee), and players can return to where they left off. Retreating just hits pause.
- **Resources are cheap in modern D&D.** Short rests are freely available, a single night restores all spell slots, food is cheap, encumbrance is trivial, and cantrips replace torches. Recovery is easy and risk-free.
- **Attrition doesn't pull toward a proper Loss State.** A well-designed adventure has designed Outcomes including Loss States (e.g., "the plague kills everyone"). Attrition doesn't move the game toward those endings—it just determines whether characters die before reaching any proper ending. Dying is a Game Over, not a Loss.

### Old-School vs. Modern D&D

- **Old-school D&D had real Attrition teeth:** wandering monsters, prohibitive encumbrance, high supply costs, long recovery times. Retreating meant losing ground and fighting through reconquered territory.
- **Modern D&D has stripped those mechanics.** No wandering monsters during rests, trivial supplies, fast recovery. Attrition's bite is gone.
- **Ironic observation:** Old-school D&D (where Attrition was strong) should theoretically have needed *less* deadly encounters, while modern D&D (where Attrition is weak) should theoretically need *more* deadly encounters to create meaningful challenge.

### How to Make Attrition Useful

- **Attrition is a pacing dynamic in modern D&D.** It creates rest-or-push decision points and rewards good resource management, but these aren't meaningful by themselves.
- **A boss fight or climactic encounter turns Attrition into Inertia.** If mismanaging resources makes the boss harder, Attrition leads to a proper Loss State.
- **Timers make retreating costly.** If every day wasted resting carries consequences (random NPC deaths from a plague, villain advancing plans), then Attrition becomes Inertia. Soft timers work as well as hard timers.
- **The Tension Pool is a tool for creating Inertia from Attrition.** (Confirmed by Angry in comments—the Tension Pool with a good complications list brings Inertia to the game.)
- **Attrition is an unfinished Inertia mechanic.** It's not Inertia by itself, but it's a great ingredient for Inertia if you know how to cook with it.

## Summary

D&D's Attrition dynamic—where encounters chew up resources creating an overarching resource-management challenge—is not the same as Inertia (a force pulling the game toward loss). In modern D&D, retreating to rest is free, resources recover easily, and nothing changes while you're gone, so Attrition alone doesn't cost progress or increase loss odds. However, Attrition becomes a powerful ingredient for Inertia when combined with boss fights, timers, or tools like the Tension Pool that make time and resource expenditure consequential. True Scenario Designers can't rely on Attrition alone for meaningful macro-challenge—they need to hang additional mechanics off it.
