# The Best and Worst of D&D 3.5

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Best and Worst of D&D 3.5](https://theangrygm.com/the-best-and-worst-of-dnd-3e/)
**Date:** December 21, 2022

---

## Summary

A subjective top-5 best and top-5 worst list for D&D v.3.5, examining what the edition did brilliantly and where it fell apart. Not a nostalgia trip — a design analysis of what worked, what didn't, and why.

---

## The Five Best Things

### 1. How You Roll: Fail by Five, Take 10, and Take 20
- **Fail by Five**: Graduated failure based on how badly you missed the DC — not just pass/fail.
- **Take 10**: Skip the die roll when unstressed; calculate as if you rolled 10. Eliminates unnecessary rolls.
- **Take 20**: Skip the die roll when time is unlimited and failure has no cost; calculate as if you rolled 20.
- Together, these three rules drastically reduced the GM's ability to wreck the game with unnecessary die rolls.
- Later editions experimented with versions of these but missed key nuances. (Passive Checks ≠ Take 10.)

### 2. Internal Mechanical and Flavorful Consistency
- Everything worked the same way (d20 Core Mechanic) and the same rules applied to everything in the world.
- Chapter 10 of the PHB explained how magic worked *fictionally*, not just mechanically — why wizards prepare spells, what components are for, how spellcasting interacts with items.
- The DMG explained *why* wizards and clerics invented the magical items they did.
- If you knew the rules, you understood the world. If you understood the world, you knew the rules.
- No handwaving. Nothing was "just fluff" or "just crunch."

### 3. Monster Customization (DIY Bestiary)
- **Advancing**: Systematically give any monster extra HD to make it bigger/tougher.
- **Class levels**: Slap class levels on any intelligent monster (goblin war chief, shaman, etc.).
- **NPC classes**: Five weaker-than-PC classes perfect for customizing mooks.
- **Templates**: Step-by-step processes to create demonic, angelic, undead, half-dragon variants.
- **Build from scratch**: Full monster-building rules in the Monster Manual itself.
- The MM was a fully customizable, do-it-yourself monster toolkit.

### 4. Humble Low Levels (Zeroes to Heroes)
- 1st-level characters felt like neophytes with room to grow.
- Limited skills, talents, abilities, and equipment at low levels.
- Classes gained modest abilities throughout their career — you had to wait for and earn the best stuff.
- Contrast with 5E: low-level characters are "delicate superheroes" — glass cannons from the start.
- In 3E, you can't start as Aragorn, but you can start as teenage Aragorn.

### 5. The Dungeon Master's Guide
- **Objectively the best DMG ever published** (per Angry).
- Contains: biome surveys with features/hazards/obstacles, dungeon terrain types, NPC stat tables at every level, poisons/traps/diseases with build-your-own rules, settlement-building chapter, complete glossary and index.
- Understands its tripartite role as instruction manual, reference book, and toolbox.
- You can accuse it of being overstuffed, but nothing's missing.

### Honorable Mention: Opportunity Attacks
- Extensive Attack of Opportunity rules = robust **Zone of Control** mechanics.
- If you're in something's melee reach, make a melee attack or suffer consequences.
- Essential for making melee combat meaningful alongside ranged/magical combat.
- Later editions wore down OA rules for being "complicated" and "annoying," leading to melee combat becoming the worst combat option in 5E.
- "If you want a strategic-and-tactical combat game that mixes melee and ranged combat, you need robust Zone of Control mechanics."

## The Five Worst Things

### 1. Character Planning Bullshit
- Cool options (feat trees, prestige classes) had endless prerequisites.
- Wasting one skill rank could lock you out of a prestige class forever.
- The game pushed players toward planning 20 levels in advance instead of evolving organically.
- Best fantasy archetypes (swashbuckler, draconic sorcerer, gish builds) were gated behind high-level prestige classes requiring carefully planned progressions.

### 2. Death and Dying Rules
- Negative HP down to -10 = dead. Two contradictory consequences:
  - **Low levels**: 10-round bleed-out timer was very lenient — players safely ignored dying allies until after the fight.
  - **High levels**: A single full attack could take a PC from conscious to dead in one turn — the 10 HP buffer was meaningless.
- Can't have a relaxed attitude toward death at low levels and instant death at high levels in the same game.

### 3. The Character Sheet
- Intimidatingly complex for new players.
- A barrier to entry that scared people away from an otherwise amazing game.

### 4. Fiddly Little Minutiae
- Tons of tiny, specific rules (precise DCs for every imaginable situation).
- Most DCs fell into the Easy/Medium/Hard/Very Hard scale (increments of 5) anyway — calculating exact DCs wasn't worth the effort.
- Lots of small rules that were easy to forget during play (skill synergies, Dodge feat, etc.).
- "A rule you forget to use is a rule that doesn't exist."

### 5. X per Day Abilities
- Barbarian rage, bardic music, turn undead, wild shape, lay on hands, smite evil — all limited to X uses per day.
- "Stupid, arbitrary gamey bullshit" that exists only for game balance.
- No in-world justification for why uncontrollable bloodlust has a daily charge limit.
- 3E started this trend; 5E leaned into it hard with ki points, inspiration dice, expertise dice, etc.
- Angry's position: there are better ways to design abilities than ammo counters on actions.

## Design Principles Extracted

- **Good rules reduce reliance on perfect GM judgment.** Fail by Five, Take 10, and Take 20 provided solid mechanical rules for skipping unnecessary dice rolls.
- **Consistency between rules and fiction matters.** When rules and world reinforce each other, GMs can figure out answers to edge cases logically.
- **Customization tools > pre-built stat blocks.** A toolkit approach to monsters is more valuable than a static bestiary.
- **Low-level vulnerability creates meaningful progression.** Starting weak and growing strong is more satisfying than starting as a fragile superhero.
- **Zone of Control mechanics are essential** for tactical combat that mixes melee and ranged.
- **Elegance matters.** Don't have two separate magical systems with overlapping effects and different arbitrary resource pools when one would do.
