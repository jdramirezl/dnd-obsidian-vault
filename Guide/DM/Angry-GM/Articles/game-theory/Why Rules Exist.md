# Why Rules Exist (The Death of the Gygax)

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Death of the Gygax: Why Rules Exist](https://theangrygm.com/why-rules-exist/)
**Date:** February 8, 2017

---

## Summary

A foundational article on why RPG rules exist, what they do, and the three categories all rules fall into. Framed as a rebuttal to the Gygax quote "the secret we should never let the gamemasters know is that they don't need any rules," this article argues that rules are essential — not optional — and that understanding their purpose makes you a better GM and game designer.

---

## The Death of the Author (Opening Framework)

- Roland Barthes' 1967 essay: a work of art should be judged on its own merits, not solely on authorial intent.
- Creators have biases and subconscious ideas that find their way into works unintentionally.
- Art endures because we reinterpret it through modern experience — if only authorial intent mattered, relevancy would be limited to the decade it was written.
- **Applied to Gygax**: He crystallized the RPG idea, but 40+ years of refinement means his words are no more authoritative than any other expert's. "Trust the tale, not the teller."

---

## The Role of the Rules

### The Fundamental Game Loop
1. Players are presented with a **situation**.
2. They make **decisions** based on their characters' goals and motivations.
3. There's an **outcome** and **consequences**.
4. The outcome creates a **new situation**.
5. Repeat until goals are accomplished or characters die.

### Why You Can't Just Make It Up
- Without rules, players could just decide actions *and* outcomes — that's writing a novel, not playing a game.
- **What makes a game a game is uncertainty.** The outcome isn't clear from the beginning; you have to play it out.
- Even adding a coin flip (random good/bad outcome) isn't enough — players still pick both outcomes, so nothing truly unexpected happens.

### The Paradox of Games
- **Tension/uncertainty** makes the game exciting (risk of the unexpected, things going wrong).
- **Player influence** makes the game feel fair (skill, strategy, cleverness affect outcomes).
- These two forces are in constant push and pull. Both are necessary.

### Why a GM Alone Isn't Enough
- A human GM provides tension, surprise, and ways for players to influence outcomes.
- **But**: no one can tell the difference between a GM making perfect decisions and one behaving completely arbitrarily — everything happens inside the GM's head.
- Players can't assess their chances or predict outcomes, so they can't make good, rational decisions.
- Example: The cliff-climbing scenario where GM and player have completely different assessments of difficulty, leading to a death that feels arbitrary.

### The Answer: GM + Rules
- Rules are a **transparent tool** the GM uses to determine outcomes.
- Because players understand the rules, they can **assess likely outcomes and consequences**.
- Rules keep GMs and players on the same page about how the world works.

## The Three Categories of Rules

### 1. Rules of Resolution
- **The most important rules.** The tool for determining what happens when a character attempts an action.
- Simulate a reality — they're the framework for cause and effect inside the game.
- Tell players and GM "when you do X, the likely outcome is Y."
- Include randomness to simulate imperfect assessment (the die roll accounts for incomplete information — that's why telling players the DC isn't metagaming).
- Provide a shared language: "+5 to climb" and "DC 15" mean the same thing to everyone regardless of flowery descriptions.
- **Purpose**: Translate the rules of cause and effect from the real world into the game world.

### 2. Rules of the Impossible
- Describe things in the imaginary world that **don't have real-world analogues** (magic, psionics, etc.).
- Also cover things that *do* exist but can't be reliably translated from experience (zero gravity, exotic environments).
- More detailed than Rules of Resolution because they can't rely on shared real-world understanding.
- Tell players what *else* is possible beyond normal human experience.
- Example: A knock spell needs extensive explanation — what is a spell? What is magic? What are its limits? — while "breaking down a door" needs almost none.

### 3. Rules of Structure
- Exist because an RPG is also trying to be a **fun, challenging, engaging, satisfying game**.
- Don't simulate reality — they often make no sense in-world.
- Examples:
  - **Turn-based combat** (real battles aren't neat and orderly)
  - **Encounter balancing** (the real world doesn't level up with the characters)
  - **Hit points** (abstract away injury complexity; let you take multiple hits without dying)
- Also called **"Acceptable Breaks from Reality."**
- Work at **cross purposes** with Rules of Resolution:
  - Rules of Resolution translate reality → game world.
  - Rules of Structure translate game world → fun game.

---

## The Balance: Resolution vs. Structure

- There's always a balancing act between simulating consistent reality and creating satisfying gameplay.
- Every player has a different preference for where the balance point should be.
- **RPG players tend to prefer erring on the side of reality** — even when they claim otherwise.
- Why: RPGs present a story about a fictional world that must be consistent and comprehensible. Six different imaginations need to make informed, rational decisions and arrive at similar conclusions. The world has to *work* as a world.
- Too much abstraction breaks the medium. Board games can be abstract (no one cares that *Settlers of Catan* isn't realistic), but RPGs need their world to fit inside the human imagination without breaking.
- Players who claim to be okay with abstraction for "narrative" often end up complaining about arbitrary GM whims and nonsensical rules.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Rules are not optional.** They serve the essential function of making the game fair, transparent, and playable.
2. **Rules serve one function in three ways**: Resolution (simulate reality), Impossible (extend reality), Structure (make it fun).
3. **Understanding rule categories helps you**: know what you're changing when you modify rules, design better homebrew, and avoid breaking the game.
4. **The GM is not the only rule.** A GM without rules is indistinguishable from an arbitrary dictator, no matter how good their judgment.
5. **Transparency enables good decisions.** Players need to understand the system to make rational choices — hiding everything in the GM's head short-circuits human decision-making.
