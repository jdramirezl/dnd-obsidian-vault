# Challenge Or Goal?

## Summary

Part of the True Scenario Designery series. Angry dissects a critical and common mistake in adventure pre-design: confusing the adventure's **goal** with its **challenge**. Using a Discord community member's flawed premise as a case study, he demonstrates that "stop the ritual" is a goal, not a challenge — and that failing to distinguish between them cripples your ability to design meaningful gameplay, momentum, and inertia dynamics. He also introduces the concept of the **x-factor** — the adventure's primary mode of player interaction — and shows how choosing an x-factor first can help you define a proper challenge.

## Key Advice

### Goals vs. Challenges — The Critical Distinction
- A **goal** is something the players accomplish. A **challenge** is something they overcome.
- Goals tell players what they've got to do. Challenges explain why they can't just do it.
- Goals tend to be brief and concise. Challenges require prose descriptions.
- Goals provide context (story/fantasy). Challenges transition to gameplay.
- **If your goal and challenge are the same sentence, you've messed up.** They can't be identical.
- **If your challenge is a single imperative phrase** ("stop the ritual"), something is wrong.

### The Litmus Test
Use this formula: *"The players must accomplish X by doing Y, and Y is hard because of Z."*
- X = Goal
- Y + Z = Challenge
- If you can't fill this out, go back to the drawing board.

### The X-Factor
- An adventure's **x-factor** is the primary mode of player interaction that spends the most time in the spotlight.
- It's not the only thing players do, but it's what they do most — or what most directly achieves the goal.
- The x-factor provides a through-line between goal, challenge, momentum, and inertia.
- Your challenge should naturally "scream" your x-factor. If it doesn't, you've likely confused goal and challenge.

### Four X-Factor Examples (Same Goal: "Stop the Ritual")

| X-Factor | Challenge |
|---|---|
| **Exploration** | Villain is hidden in a sprawling necropolis. Players must find him quickly while time pressure mounts. |
| **Acquire** | Players have a relic that needs holy power from scattered nodes across a ruined city. They must gather enough energy while navigating dangers. |
| **Interact** | Information is spread among cultists. Players must extract it through interrogation, deception, or infiltration — in a civilized area where violence has consequences. |
| **Exterminate** | Villain is at the heart of a monster-filled necropolis. Players must cut through hordes and have enough resources left for the boss fight. One perfect run. |

### Describe, Don't Outline
- The point of pre-design is to **describe** what you plan to design — not outline, list, or define.
- Bullet-point summaries lose vital information. Use prose to capture the full picture.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Always state goal and challenge separately.** If they sound the same, rethink your premise.
2. **Apply the litmus test:** "Players must accomplish X by doing Y, and Y is hard because of Z." If you can't fill it in, your challenge is underdeveloped.
3. **Choose your x-factor first** when struggling with challenge design. Pick the primary mode of interaction (explore, acquire, interact, exterminate) and build the challenge around it.
4. **Write your pre-design in prose**, not bullet points. Describe the adventure's goal, challenge, and dynamics in full sentences.
5. **Check that your challenge implies gameplay.** It should explain what the players will be doing and what stands in their way — not just restate the story objective.
6. **Use x-factors to create variety.** The same goal can produce radically different adventures depending on which x-factor you choose.

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> Goals tell players what to do. Challenges explain why they can't just do it. If your goal and challenge are the same sentence, you've skipped the most important part of adventure design — the gameplay. Pick an x-factor (explore, acquire, interact, exterminate) first, then build a challenge around it. Use the formula: "Players must accomplish X by doing Y, and Y is hard because of Z."
