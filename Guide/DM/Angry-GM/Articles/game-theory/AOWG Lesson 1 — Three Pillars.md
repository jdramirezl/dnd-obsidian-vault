## Principle 1: Whatever Happens is Okay

You play the game to find out what happens. Surrender to the emergent, unpredictable nature of RPGs.

- Players will demolish setpieces, dawdle past climaxes, make insane charges, drop items into volcanoes
- None of that wrecks the game — it IS the game
- Your job: start the ship on a good course, yank the wheel toward satisfying gameplay whenever you can
- Game design knowledge helps you tell good directions from bad — but you steer vaguely, not precisely
- Moment-to-moment fun keeps everyone happy; long-term satisfaction keeps them coming back

**Corollary:** You don't have to be okay with *literally* anything. Don't let players do things that make you dislike them, their characters, or your game.

---

## Principle 2: Mechanics Don't Make the Game (And Sometimes Make It Worse)

Everything at the table plays out the same: situation → player choice → resolution. If you can resolve one action at a time, you can handle anything.

**The Merilla example:** A stock NPC minstrel with no stat block produced deeply compelling social drama across multiple sessions — none of it mechanically designed. If she'd been statted up, the stats would have been wrong for the situations that arose, and the GM would have ignored emergent opportunities.

**The automation trap:**
- Mechanics automate decisions so you don't think — but then you *won't* think
- If you rely on a plate-spinning robot, you never learn to spin plates yourself
- Letting mechanics do your thinking lets you fake it better, not run a better game

**What to do instead:**
- Trust yourself to handle the game moment to moment
- If there's a rule in the book and you remember it, use it; otherwise figure it out
- Review rules you know you'll need; don't invent new ones pre-emptively
- Don't completely dismiss balance — but don't calculate fair challenges either

## Principle 3: The Minimum Prep Time Needed for a Good Session is Zero

Every GM should be able to fill an entire session with a good game with nothing prepared.

**Why:** If something unexpected happens in minute one, one session is how long you have before you can plan. Knowing you CAN last means unexpected situations never phase you.

**Why you can already do this:**
- You have a Monster Manual, DMG, treasure tables, and maps in the back
- The game's system serves as a trellis to hang ideas on
- You are never working from whole cloth
- Most of GMing is responding to player actions — sessions are self-sustaining once started

**The snob problem:** Most GMs turn their noses up at published monsters and random treasure. But monster stats are like paint — it's not the paint that makes the painting.

**Practice:** Show up to one session with nothing. Whatever you improvise becomes the foundation for next week's prep.

## Practical Takeaways

- [ ] Internalize: whatever happens at the table is okay — delight in the chaos
- [ ] Stop designing mechanical systems for things you can adjudicate moment-to-moment
- [ ] Practice running a session with zero prep — even once
- [ ] Trust the game's existing materials as perfectly good building blocks
