## Key Advice

- **"Should" is a dangerous word.** It implies moral rightness or fairness and creates the false impression that there's an inherently correct answer. Replace "should a GM be paid?" with "could a GM be paid?"—a pragmatic question with a practical answer.
- **Accepting money opens you to outside influences.** It's never just money. Monetization brings expectations, obligations, and pressure from whoever is paying—whether that's players, advertisers, or an audience.
- **GMs are easily replaceable.** There are no significant barriers to entry for GMing. Any player can step behind the screen. This means the supply of free GMs undercuts any attempt to charge for the service.
- **People won't pay for what they can get for free.** The abundance of GMs willing to run games for fun makes it very difficult to charge players directly. Supply and demand, not morality, determines whether you get paid.
- **To make money GMing, offer something unique.** Celebrity, expertise, a unique experience (like True Dungeon or escape rooms), or a media product (streaming, content creation) can justify compensation. Generic GMing cannot.
- **Professional GM streamers aren't paid by their players.** Matt Mercer and similar figures are paid by audiences, advertisers, or producers—not by the people sitting at their table. The product being sold is entertainment, not a game session.
- **The "should" trap applies to all game design decisions.** Whether it's pixie barbarians, inclusion, or any other design choice, chasing "should" leads to subjective moral arguments that can't be resolved. Pragmatic analysis of costs, benefits, and trade-offs is more productive.
- **Everyone has different "shoulds" in their head.** Moral frameworks are subjective. You can't force the world to conform to your ideal, and being too attached to your "should" leads to disappointment or despair.

## Summary

Angry tackles the question "should GMs be paid?" by dismantling the word "should" itself. "Should" implies moral rightness and leads to unresolvable subjective arguments. The practical question is "could a GM be paid?"—and the answer is: maybe, but probably not much. GMs are easily replaceable (low barriers to entry), there's an abundant supply of people willing to GM for free (they enjoy it), and players won't pay for something readily available at no cost. To make money, a GM must offer something unique: celebrity status, a premium experience, or a media product. Professional streaming GMs like Matt Mercer aren't paid by their players—they're paid by audiences and advertisers for entertainment. Angry extends this analysis to game design broadly: the word "should" creates false moral imperatives around subjective preferences. Whether it's paying GMs, including pixie barbarians, or any other design decision, pragmatic cost-benefit analysis beats moral posturing. He also warns that accepting money for creative work inevitably introduces outside influences and obligations—it's never just money.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Replace "should" with "could" or "will" in design discussions.** When debating any game decision, ask what's practical and what trade-offs exist, not what's morally right.
2. **Don't expect to be paid for generic GMing.** If you want compensation, you need to offer something players can't get elsewhere—unique expertise, a premium experience, or entertainment content.
3. **If you monetize your GMing, understand who's actually paying you and why.** Advertisers pay for audiences. Audiences pay for entertainment. Players pay for unique experiences. Know your actual product.
4. **Accept that taking money changes the relationship.** Once players become customers, their expectations shift. Be prepared for that dynamic change before you charge.
5. **Don't chase "should" in your game design.** Your artistic vision, your players' preferences, and practical constraints all matter more than abstract moral arguments about what a game "should" include.
6. **Recognize that the GM role is its own reward for many people.** If you're not having fun GMing, the answer isn't to get paid—it's to figure out why you're not having fun and fix that.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Whether a GM "should" be paid is an unanswerable moral question. Whether a GM "could" be paid depends on supply, demand, and uniqueness. Most GMs can't charge because the service is freely available. If you want money, offer something no one else can—or build an audience. And remember: taking money always comes with strings attached.
