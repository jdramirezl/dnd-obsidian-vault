# Ask Angry: How to Playtest an Entire System on Three Hours a Week

## Summary

A reader designing a homebrew TTRPG asks how to efficiently playtest with only 3 hours per week. Angry delivers a reality check: playtesting isn't the final quality-assurance step — it's where the real design begins. The first draft of anything is garbage. Design is an iterative process of breaking, rebuilding, and breaking again. The designer is also the worst possible playtester for their own game because they unconsciously fix problems on the fly and test the game in their head rather than the one on paper.

## Key Advice

### Playtesting Is the Beginning, Not the End
- If you think "all I need to do now is playtest," you think you're almost done. You're not. Getting to playtesting is like reaching the Bell Gargoyles — the real work starts here.
- The concept of **"fail faster"** is central to game design. Get your rough design to a playable state, break it, find what works, rebuild, repeat.
- Design is active and iterative. Expect to throw out huge chunks and start over.

### You Are a Terrible Playtester
- As the designer, you unconsciously fix problems while running the game. You adjust numbers, clarify wording, and smooth rough edges without noticing.
- You playtest **the game in your head**, not the one you wrote down. You won't notice what's hard to understand, hard to remember, or easy to overlook.
- You only have one perspective — your own. Everything works for you because you designed it for yourself.
- You're biased. Every designer is either their own best or worst critic, but never honest and objective.

### You Need Other People Running Your Game
- Other GMs running your game for other players is essential for any game you plan to sell.
- Ideally, watch silently without helping or explaining. Take notes.
- If you can't observe directly, get structured feedback via surveys and unstructured prose reports.
- Build a community (Discord, local game stores, online) to find playtesters.

### Know Your Goals
- **Personal game for friends?** Just play it, laugh through mistakes, fix as you go. No pressure.
- **Planning to sell it?** You owe it to every paying customer to put in the hard work. Playtesting must be rigorous, with multiple groups and GMs.
- Three hours a week is enough — it'll just take a long time. And all your gaming time will be consumed by playtesting, which is work, not fun.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Define your goal first.** Personal use? Free distribution? Commercial sale? This determines how much testing you need.
2. **Start playtesting immediately** with whatever rough draft you have. Don't wait for a "finished" version.
3. **Embrace "fail faster."** Break the game quickly, identify what works, rebuild, repeat.
4. **Get other GMs to run your game** as soon as possible. You cannot be your only playtester.
5. **Build a community** around your game early — Discord, local game stores, online forums. You need volume.
6. **Prepare for the grind.** Playtesting isn't fun. It's repetitive, frustrating work. Decide how much you're willing to invest.
7. **Collect structured feedback.** Design surveys targeting specific mechanics alongside open-ended play reports.

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> The first draft of anything is garbage. Playtesting isn't the finish line — it's the starting gun. You are the worst possible tester for your own game because you test the game in your head, not the one on paper. Get other people running it, watch silently, and prepare to throw out everything that doesn't work.
