# The Game Designer and the Clown

## Core Thesis

GMs fall into two archetypes: the **Game Designer** and the **Clown**. A Clown puts on a show and makes sure everyone laughs. A Game Designer designs a great game and puts it in front of people, confident it will give them a great time — but sometimes fails. Great GMs must be Game Designers, not Clowns.

## Two Endemic GM Issues

1. **"How do you make your players care?"** — Getting players to treat the game world as a real place rather than a construct. Caring about NPCs, places, events.
2. **"Playerphobia"** — Fear of saying no to players. "I'd love to run that, but my players would reject it." Inability to set firm limits.

These two issues are **strongly correlated**. Playerphobic GMs have an infinitely harder time creating games their players care about. GMs who lay down the law end up with emotionally engaged players.

## The Expression Problem (MDA Framework)

Using the **MDA (Mechanics, Dynamics, Aesthetics)** design framework, Angry identifies 8 gameplay aesthetics. **Expression** — playing to express yourself creatively — doesn't play well with the others, for two reasons:

1. **Constraints rub creatives the wrong way**, but constraints are what make Fantasy, Narrative, and Challenge work.
2. **Self-expression is inherently selfish** — hard to share in a team-based game except as performance.

> The higher you prioritize Expression, the more everything else suffers.

This leads to two kinds of games:
- **The Expression Game** — all about inviting players to create and portray characters with ultimate creative freedom.
- **The Fantasy Game** — inviting players to lose themselves in another reality, a world they can care about.

## The Tax Code Example

Angry describes a session where players arrived in a town with aggressive tax policies — gate taxes, transaction fees, confiscations. What should have been an hour of downtime became an obnoxious logistical puzzle over copper pieces. The result: players were **emotionally invested**. They were angry at the town, concerned for its people, and curious about why things had changed. Standard D&D quests became secondary to investigating the tax code.

**Key insight**: Things that seem like terrible GM ideas (annoying taxes, inconvenience, bookkeeping) can create deep emotional engagement when done deliberately by a confident GM.

## The GM Confidence Principle

> A GM has to be so confident in their ability to run a great game that they can say no to any player at any time without explanation, because the game they run will be better than whatever the player is asking for.

- Your job is to run a great game, not to make people smile.
- Even if your players are great GMs themselves, at your table they are players, not co-designers.
- Players want stupid things that give a laugh in the moment but undermine the long-term experience.
- Being willing to risk running a terrible game is prerequisite to running a great one.

## On Expression in RPGs

Expression works when it's about **what you do**, not **who you are**:
- RPGs offer infinite freedom to choose and act — unlike video games with three dialogue options.
- But there is no promise of "freedom to create" or "freedom from consequences."
- If you want total creative control of everything that happens to your character, you want to write a book, not play a team-based game.

## Key Takeaways

- GMs are game designers, not entertainers. The entertainment is a consequence of running a great game.
- Saying "no" firmly and without apology is a core GM skill, not a character flaw.
- Playerphobia leads to disengaged players; confident GMing leads to invested ones.
- Expression as a primary aesthetic undermines the team-based, constraint-driven nature of TTRPGs.
- MDA is the correct design framework; GNS theory is rejected as incompatible and wrong.
