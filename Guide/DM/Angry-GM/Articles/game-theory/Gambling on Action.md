## Key Advice

- **Action adjudication has two levels.** Apprentice: determine if the action can succeed, can fail, and has a cost—then roll dice only when all three are true. Journeyman: break actions into Intent, Approach, Outcomes, and Consequences before touching the rules.
- **There's a spectrum between Law and Chaos in action resolution.** Pure Law = the GM determines everything with their brain. Pure Chaos = everything is random. Good GMing lives in between, but most GMs skew too far toward Law.
- **Sometimes the game should skew toward Chaos.** When players propose dramatic, high-risk actions outside the normal rules, the excitement of gambling on a single roll is part of the fun. Don't resist it.
- **Reframe player actions as gambles with Stakes and a Pot.** The Stakes are what the player risks on failure. The Pot is what they gain on success. The GM's job is to assess whether the Stakes are a sufficient wager for the desired Pot.
- **When Stakes are sufficient, override the normal rules.** If a rogue risks being alone among enemies to assassinate a hobgoblin commander, success means the hobgoblin dies outright—regardless of what sneak attack damage dice say. Failure means the rogue is exposed and alone.
- **Great risk deserves great reward.** A player willing to risk instant character death on a single die roll (like diving off a cliff to spear a dragon) deserves a proportionally massive payoff—not a "+2 charge bonus."
- **Don't touch the odds—change the outcomes.** The dice mechanics stay the same (roughly 66% baseline success). What changes is what success and failure mean in the fiction.
- **Gambling is fun and psychologically healthy (in moderation).** Humans are wired to take risks. The excitement of a high-stakes gamble creates the most memorable moments in gaming—the highest highs and lowest lows.
- **GMs who refuse to let players break the rules are "Lawful Stupid."** Clinging to the safe, balanced, predictable rules area at all times kills excitement and misses half the point of the game.
- **The dice serve multiple purposes:** uncertainty (can't predict everything), fairness (outcomes aren't just GM whim), and agency (players can nudge odds with their strengths). But they're also a necessary evil—losing isn't fun, but the possibility of loss makes winning satisfying.

## Summary

Angry introduces the concept of the "Action Gamble" as the chaotic complement to his established action adjudication system. Standard action adjudication is Lawful—it minimizes unnecessary dice rolls by asking "can it succeed, can it fail, is there a cost?" The Action Gamble goes the other direction: when players propose dramatic actions outside the normal rules, the GM should lean into the chaos. The framework is simple: identify the Stakes (what the player risks on failure) and the Pot (what they gain on success). If the Stakes are sufficient for the Pot, let the gamble happen—and override the normal mechanical outcomes. A rogue who risks being alone among enemies to assassinate a commander gets an instant kill on success, not sneak attack damage. A fighter who dives off a cliff to spear a dragon risks death but deserves a dragon-killing payoff, not a charge bonus. The key insight: don't change the odds (the dice mechanics stay the same), change what the outcomes mean. D&D's baseline 66% success rate still applies. This creates the most memorable moments in gaming—the dramatic gambles that players talk about for years. GMs who refuse to allow this are being "Lawful Stupid," clinging to safe, predictable rules at the expense of excitement.

## Actionable Steps

1. **When a player proposes something dramatic and outside the rules, identify the Stakes and the Pot.** What do they risk on failure? What do they gain on success?
2. **Ask: are the Stakes sufficient for the Pot?** The player doesn't need a "fair" bet—just a sufficient wager. It's ultimately the player's choice whether to take the gamble.
3. **Override normal mechanical outcomes when the gamble warrants it.** Success on the assassination attempt = the target dies. Don't reduce it to normal sneak attack damage.
4. **Don't change the odds—change the outcomes.** Use the same ability checks and DCs the game already provides. Just redefine what success and failure mean for this specific gamble.
5. **Match reward to risk proportionally.** A player risking instant death deserves a proportionally massive payoff. A "+2 bonus" for jumping off a cliff is insulting.
6. **Recognize when to use Law vs. Chaos.** Routine actions with no drama → Lawful adjudication (skip unnecessary rolls). Dramatic, high-stakes moments → lean into the Chaos and let the gamble happen.
7. **Don't be Lawful Stupid.** The rules exist to provide a safe, fun baseline. But the most exciting moments happen when you step outside that baseline. Let your players take dramatic risks.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> When players propose dramatic, high-risk actions, treat them as gambles: identify the Stakes and the Pot, ensure the wager is sufficient, and let a single die roll determine an outcome proportional to the risk. Don't change the odds—change what success and failure mean. The most memorable moments in gaming come from these gambles, not from safe, predictable rule applications.
