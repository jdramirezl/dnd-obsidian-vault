# What Makes a Challenge

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/what-makes-a-challenge/)
**Date:** August 28, 2024
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Capital-C Challenge

A **Challenge** is a gameplay element the players must strive against as part of achieving the game's goal. Monsters, traps, hostage situations, mysteries, resource management — all Challenges.

**Emphasis on players, not characters.** Challenges test the *players'* abilities. Characters are game constructs with statistics; players make the actual choices.

## Challenge ≠ Difficulty ≠ Probability

Three completely separate concepts:

- **Challenge:** An in-game situation players must strive against
- **Difficulty:** How much effort/skill/risk a player must bring to overcome a Challenge
- **Probability:** The likelihood a random event yields a specific result

**Challenge Ratings and Difficulty Classes are just Probabilities.** Rolling a Lockpicking check against DC 15 has no Challenge — the player just picks the obvious skill and rolls. No amount of player skill changes the random outcome.

There is no minimum Difficulty for something to count as a Challenge. If it requires any effort, skill, or knowledge at all, it's a Challenge.

## Challenge Is Choice

TTRPGs can only test mental abilities. Primarily: **the players' ability to choose a good approach to a gameplay situation.**

Two types of choices:
1. **In-the-moment:** What attack? What spell? Sneak or fight? Negotiate or intimidate?
2. **Preparation:** What class? What spells prepared? What equipment? How to spend downtime?

## Meaningful vs. Meaningless Challenges

If literally everyone would make the same choice, it's technically a Challenge but a **Meaningless** one.

**Requirements for Meaningful Challenge:**
1. Options must be substantially different from each other
2. Different players given the same parameters would make different choices

### The Locked Door Example

Pick the Lock vs. Bust Down the Door — in standard D&D, usually meaningless because:
- Players just compare which stat is higher
- No built-in costs or consequences for either approach
- Success = through the door either way
- It's just a **Calculation** (pick the bigger number)

## Ways to Make Challenges Meaningful

### Calculations
Choices where one option is mathematically better. Can be Meaningful if they require real analysis, but "pick the bigger number" isn't enough.

### Obfuscation and Deduction
The correct/better choice is shrouded. Players must reason things out. Mysteries and puzzles.

### Costs and Consequences
Different options require different resource expenditures or lead to different complications. Lockpicking wastes time; breaking the door attracts trouble. Players choose which price to pay.

### Incomplete Information
Players choose without having all the information. What's behind the door? Is it worth the risk? But beware: if there's *zero* information to base a choice on, players just pick randomly — that's Meaningless.

### Incomparables
Options so different they can't be meaningfully compared. Apples and suspension bridges. Common in character creation: +damage in combat vs. +tracking in wilderness.

### Dilemmas
Players must prioritize desires. Sacrifice one good thing for another, or choose which bad outcome to suffer. Save the puppy or the painting?

**Note:** The Trolley Problem is a Calculation, not a Moral Dilemma.

## Why Open-Ended Creativity Tests Suck

"Just create a problem and trust the players to find a solution" is bad design.

**The reality:** Most players aren't genius problem-solvers. They can't reliably pull creative solutions from their collective asses. When they can't, the game stops dead — blank stares, "Well..." and "Uhh..." until the GM starts nudging.

**But:** Most players *think* they're genius problem-solvers. The sense of agency comes from the *experience* of figuring things out, not from actual freedom.

### The True Scenario Designer's Approach

Build at least two visible approaches into every Challenge. Players can always choose "None of the Above" and go creative, but they need built-in options as a baseline.

**Sources of visible options:**
1. **The system's rules** — players know they can attack, pick locks, cast spells. Build Challenges around default options players have internalized.
2. **Deliberate presentation** — use leading language to plant ideas without spelling them out. "A chandelier illuminates the chamber from above where it hangs by a single rotten and frayed rope directly above the head of the slavering monster..."

**The gold standard:** Players figure out an option at the last minute (or don't figure it out until after) and say, "We should have seen that because of this clue and that clue." That's True Scenario Design — if you did it on purpose.

## Macrochallenge (Sidebar)

A protracted, emergent Challenge that arises from multiple other Challenges or gameplay dynamics. Not confronted directly.

**Example:** A dungeon with three fights then a boss. Each fight is a Challenge. The Macrochallenge is managing resources across all fights so you can still beat the boss. Items or information found along the way that help with the boss are part of the Macrochallenge.

This is what separates a True Scenario Designer's boss fight from a simple obstacle course. It's what keeps adventures from being linear sequences of disconnected encounters.

## External Knowledge Problem

How much real-world knowledge can you assume players have? The average person may not know about fording rivers, rope construction, or historical building techniques.

**The trap:** "I don't have to explain 'build a raft' because people should know that." Many players won't think of it.

**Rule of thumb:** Don't rely on external knowledge for critical path options. Use it for bonus/optional approaches that reward knowledgeable players.
