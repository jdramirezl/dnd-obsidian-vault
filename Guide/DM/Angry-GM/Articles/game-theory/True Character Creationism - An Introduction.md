# True Character Creationism: An Introduction

*Part of the True Campaign Managery Course.*

## Definition

> Character Creation is the process by which each player ends up with a complete character for the campaign you're about to run — a character that has everything necessary to start playing and nothing that isn't necessary — as quickly and efficiently and painlessly as possible.

## Core Principles

### 1. Characters Are Made for Specific Campaigns

Every PC is made for a **specific campaign**. The Campaign Vision determines what characters can be made and how. A character is the tool with which a player interacts with the game — if it doesn't fit, it's like playing with the wrong controller.

### 2. Everything Is Subject to GM Approval

You can reject any character — even one made entirely according to your rules — for any reason. You don't have to justify it. Stats, story, eye color, dead sister's name — all of it needs your stamp of approval.

### 3. Characters = Stats + Story

A complete character includes both mechanical stats and narrative elements (description, personality, motivation, backstory, relationships). Both are subject to approval.

### 4. Everything a Character Needs

Determine what constitutes "complete" based on your Campaign Vision:
- **Mechanical**: Everything the system requires, plus anything from optional systems you're using.
- **Story**: Depends on campaign type:
  - Player-driven exploration → personal goals
  - Adventure-of-the-month → broad motivation
  - Save-the-world plot → reason to fight

### 5. Nothing a Character Doesn't Need

Equally important: **don't include extraneous stuff**.

- Every unnecessary pre-filled blank becomes a **psychological constraint** that makes players less adaptable.
- The point of RPGs is to play to find out what happens — players shouldn't know too much about their characters at the start.
- If you're not using Inspiration, skip Traits and Flaws. If you don't track encumbrance, skip gear weights. If you won't mine backstories for plots, don't ask for them.

> **"I Never Need a Psych Profile and Neither Do You"** — Don't ask for personality descriptions. You'll see the character's personality through play. Specific mechanical hooks (motivations, alignment) are fine; a therapist's note is not.

### 6. Nothing You Don't Want

Curate your options. If you know you'll reject something, restrict it in advance. Better to restrict upfront than to reject after a player has invested time.

Examples: No evil characters in heroic campaigns. No monks in medieval European fantasy. No gnomes if they annoy you.

### 7. As Quickly and Painlessly as Possible

Character creation is an **obstacle to gameplay**. Every minute spent on it is a minute not gaming.

- Many players find it tedious, especially new ones.
- Every minute the GM spends on it could be spent on literally anything else.
- Completeness matters more than speed — but don't waste time on extraneous stuff.

> "McDonald's is fast too. Dumbass."

## The Two-Step Process

1. **Determine what constitutes a Complete Character** — What does a character need (and not need) to be playable? What options are available?
2. **Decide how to get Complete Characters into players' hands** — The practical management of the creation process.

## Key Stance: No One Right Method

- Random generation, point buy, heavy restrictions, total freedom, pre-generated characters — all valid depending on the campaign.
- If you have precisely one method you always use, you probably suck at this.
- Don't trust any GM who can tell you the "best" method without asking about your campaign first.
