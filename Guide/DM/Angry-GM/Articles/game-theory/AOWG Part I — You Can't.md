# AOWG Part I: You Can't

**Source:** [The Angry GM — How to Run an Open World Part I](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-run-an-open-world-part-i/)
**Date:** May 5, 2021
**Series:** Angry's Open World Game (AOWG)

---

## Core Thesis

The reason you think you can't run an open-world game is that you think it's your job to make the game interesting, fun, fair, and balanced. It's not — at least not nearly as much as you think. The game makes itself interesting through the dynamics of play. Your job is to make things happen and let the interesting emerge.

---

## The Problem: Standards Kill Creativity

Many GMs hold every idea, encounter, and decision to ridiculously high standards: challenging enough, balanced enough, interesting enough, deep enough, complex enough. This creates a cascade:

1. You shoot down your own ideas before they form
2. The creative flow pinches off — fewer ideas emerge
3. You get stressed and strained — creativity contracts further
4. Eventually nothing comes out at all
5. You conclude you suck at being creative
6. Improvisation seems impossible — if you can't come up with ideas with unlimited time, how can you do it live?

**The irony:** If you only let the very best ideas through, you rarely get anything. If you let ideas flow freely, they may not be the best, but they turn out pretty good in play. You lighten up, ideas flow more freely, and they get better over time.

---

## The Blink Dog Story

Angry's AOWG session example: Party heading to raid tombs for an antique weapon to gift a lord. Random encounter roll → blink dogs. Party befriends them. Blink dogs lead party to a ruined farmhouse (a lair from a regional list). Party charges across 1000 feet of open ground. Archer in the ruin repels them. Blink dog gets hurt and flees. Party is now lost, low on resources, running out of time before the festival.

**Key points:**
- The blink dogs were a random encounter roll
- The farmhouse was a random lair from a list of five in the region
- The charge was entirely unexpected — the GM was racing to keep up
- He handled the archer mechanically by-the-book, no custom rules
- He had no idea what was in the farmhouse or who the bandits were
- None of that mattered — the situation was compelling because of the *dynamics of play*

**The "just bandits" question:** Discord members wanted to know what made the bandits interesting enough. Answer: they don't have to be. The bandits could be just bandits with random treasure. The story is interesting because of what the players did, the choices they face, and how this encounter connects to everything else.

---

## Whatever Happens is an Adventure

The game would have been equally interesting if:
- Blink dogs led to a ghoul pit instead
- They met bandits on the road instead of blink dogs
- They reached the tombs without incident

Even if the farmhouse loot is terrible — "remember the bandits?" becomes a callback that shapes future decisions. Even if the loot is amazing — "remember the bandits?" still shapes future decisions differently. Either way, it's interesting.

> "I don't try to make interesting, fun things happen. I just make things happen. And when things happen and the players interact with those things and with each other, that's interesting."

---

## The Mindset Shift

**What you must give up:**
- The belief that it's your job to make the game interesting
- The sign at the queuing line of your creative brain that says "you must be at least this interesting to ride my game"
- The habit of censoring ideas because they aren't original/deep/complex enough

**What you must trust:**
- Fun and interesting show up as long as you let them
- The dynamics of play — not the quality of individual pieces — make the game good
- If something isn't interesting, it'll pass quickly and something interesting will come along
- Players are easy to please (his words)

**What this is NOT saying:**
- There's no such thing as bad ideas (there is, but they're as rare as great ones)
- You shouldn't plan or prepare anything
- You should act at random without thinking
- Great ideas can't improve a game (they can, but the correlation between idea quality and game quality is weaker than you think)

---

## Key Quotes

> "The game would have been just as interesting if the blink dogs had led them to a ghoul pit at the edge of an ancient battlefield."

> "Standards kill creativity. If you only let the very best ideas come out, you're rarely going to get anything."

> "Of all the complaints you've heard people level against their GMs, have you ever heard anyone complain solely because their ideas weren't great?"

> "Don't try to run a great game, just run a game. And let the players make up their own minds."

---

## Practical Takeaways

- [ ] Stop filtering ideas through quality standards — let them flow
- [ ] Trust that play dynamics will make ordinary elements interesting
- [ ] Use random encounters and random treasure without shame
- [ ] When improvising, grab whatever comes to mind and run with it
- [ ] Note what happens and let callbacks/consequences create depth retroactively
