## Key Concepts

- **Game Design Über Alles.** Whenever you have a choice about how to run or build your game, make the choice based first and foremost on solid game design principles. Every other consideration—narrative, simulation, verisimilitude, self-expression—comes second.
- **Good game design doesn't exclude other motivations—it encompasses them.** Academic gameplay psychology models include collaboration, narrative, immersion, and exploration as intrinsic motivations. Game design is already about delivering the best versions of all of those through gameplay. Focusing on one motivation at the expense of game design wrecks the totality of the experience *and* fails to deliver even that one motivation well.
- **Whatever you run will become a game anyway.** Even in a "true open world" with no planned goals, players will eventually pick a goal, and you'll put obstacles in the way. Now you're running a game. Accept it and do it well.
- **Games smooth out rough social edges.** The best way to mitigate social friction in a group activity is a focused, defined, constrained activity with a shared goal—which is exactly what a game is.
- **There are abundant resources to learn game design.** Between video games, board games, and TTRPGs, the games industry has produced enormous amounts of research and practical knowledge about what makes games work.

## On Encounter Design

- **Don't drop unwinnable fights hoping players figure out to run.** If an encounter looks like a fight, players will assume they can win it. Modern D&D is built on that assumption.
- **Good game design means clear goals and communication.** If you want players to flee a dragon, build the encounter with that as the clear, unambiguous, achievable goal. Give them time to recognize the danger before they're in urgent peril.
- **The world should effectively level with the players.** Unless players deliberately ignore multiple explicit warnings and make a reasoned choice to enter a situation they can't survive, they should never face an encounter that isn't matched to their capabilities.
- **Character death stakes are high in D&D.** Unlike Dark Souls where death is cheap and you respawn, D&D character death is final, permanent, and emotionally costly. This means the game must be designed so players don't end up in one-wrong-move-means-death situations without clear warning.

## The Reveal

This article is the secret introductory preface and goal-setting for the **True Scenario Designery** course. The "Game Design Über Alles" philosophy is the foundation: the GM's real job is building games from the systems and elements they've got—encounters, scenes, adventures, and campaigns. D&D isn't a game; it's an engine. The game is the thing you build and run at the table.

## Summary

Game design must be the GM's top priority. It encompasses narrative, simulation, immersion, and every other motivation players bring to the table. If you make "good game" your first principle, everything else follows; if you put anything else first, you'll likely fail at both that priority and at providing a good experience. This philosophy underpins the True Scenario Designery course: the truest Game Masters are those who can build good games from the tools their system provides.
