# The Anatomy of a Screwjob

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Anatomy of a Screwjob](https://theangrygm.com/the-anatomy-of-a-screwjob/)
**Date:** January 26, 2026

---

## Summary

A **screwjob** is a game design failure where a player gets punished by something they couldn't reasonably have anticipated, avoided, or recovered from. This article dissects what makes a screwjob, how to identify one in your designs, and why they destroy player trust. The concept applies to traps, monster abilities, weapon breakage systems, and any situation where the player feels unfairly hosed.

---

## The Three Screwjob Questions

If a player is asking any of these and you don't have a good answer, you screwed them:

### 1. "What the Hell Could I Have Done to Avoid That?"

- The situation must offer **meaningful choices** that could change the outcome.
- Things that **don't count** as choices:
  - "Come up with some creative alternative" — that's an Open-Ended Creativity Test, not a choice. It relies on luck and in-the-moment inspiration.
  - Rolling dice — "rolling dice isn't gameplay; choosing what dice to roll isn't gameplay."
  - Saving throws — allowing a save doesn't make something a choice.
- Consider the **knock-on effects**: if the "choice" is "you should have searched for traps," the result is players searching every square forever, which isn't gameplay.
- Consider whether the choice is **meaningful and reasonable**: asking a fighter to choose between destroying their signature weapon or not participating isn't a real choice.

### 2. "How the Hell Was I Supposed to Know That?"

- It's unreasonable to expect someone to do something different if they had **no reason to think they should**.
- Information to avoid the screwjob must exist **and** be reasonably accessible before the screwjob situation.
- Hiding critical info behind a random dungeon path doesn't count — random maze navigation isn't a choice.
- **Context clues count**: armored corpses holding disintegrated weapon hilts + acid-sweating demon = fair warning.

#### The Reasonable Player Standard
- Imagine an average player of average skill and attentiveness. What's reasonable to expect of them?
- You can't assume players know anything you didn't teach, tell, or hint at them.
- "You should have known because games have bosses" is bullshit.
- Best evidence you're not screwjobbing: a player says, "You know what? I should have seen that coming."

### 3. "I'm Still Screwed, So Who Gives a Shit?"

- **The more painful the outcome, the higher the screwjob standard.**
- Minor damage from a trap? Players shrug it off — technically a screwjob, but one you can get away with.
- But once the price goes beyond minor damage, **loss aversion** kicks in hard.
- Players especially hate losing:
  - Their character (death or removal from play)
  - Anything earned through play (XP, levels)
  - Anything they invested resources into (signature weapons, researched spells, crafted items)
  - Anything that's part of their character's identity
- Even a technically fair situation can *feel* like a screwjob if the cost is high enough.

---

## Why Screwjobs Matter

- Screwjobs **destroy player trust**.
- Once trust is broken, players stop engaging and start behaving like wounded animals — searching every square for traps, refusing to trust NPCs, never taking risks.
- The Miyazaki principle: he never put a trapped bonfire in *Dark Souls* because "if I did, the players would never trust the bonfires again." Sanctuaries and checkpoints are too important to gamefeel to ever betray.
- **There is no Game Mastering Tribunal.** The only thing that's real is your players' relationship with you and the game. Facts don't care about feelings, but feelings determine whether players come back.

---

## Weapon Breakage as Case Study

- Someone proposed a fumble-based weapon degradation system: fumble once = weapon "at risk," fumble again before repair = weapon breaks.
- Problems identified:
  1. **Statistical reality**: the odds of rolling two fumbles in an adventuring day are surprisingly high.
  2. **No meaningful choice**: weapon degradation is a random crapshoot — players can't do anything to avoid it.
  3. **High screwjob cost**: in modern D&D, a signature weapon is part of a character's identity and mechanical investment. Losing it feels like a screwjob regardless of the math.

---

## Design Takeaways

- It's okay for a player to learn the stove is hot by touching it, **as long as it doesn't burn off their whole hand**.
- There's nothing wrong with monsters that require alternative strategies (gray oozes, rust monsters) — the problem is when players have no warning and no recovery.
- Good design: "We should be careful here because this whole setup smells like a trap." That's thinking. That's engaging. That's playing the game.
- Bad design: players searching every flagstone because they've learned the dungeon is randomly lethal.
