# Winning RPGs: You People Made Me Do This

## Summary

A reactive article defending the claim that **most people play RPGs to win**. Angry defines what a game is (to most people), explains why goals are essential to games, breaks down the layered goal structure unique to RPGs, and argues that while goals are universal, **victory is personal** — each player decides what constitutes winning and how much it matters. He also analyzes Call of Cthulhu as a case study in "unwinnable" games and argues Fiasco is not an RPG due to its fundamentally different player-character relationship.

## What Is a Game?

Most people expect a game to be:

1. **An activity that is satisfying in itself** — a diversion, not done for profit
2. **Constrained by rules or a process** — even Calvinball technically has rules
3. **Has a variable, unpredictable outcome** — shaped by some mix of skill and chance
4. **Has a goal** — some outcomes are more desirable than others

All games are competitive in some sense:
- **Competitive games**: players vs. players for victory only one can achieve
- **Cooperative games**: players vs. the system for shared victory

## Why Goals Matter

Goals serve two critical functions:

1. **Context for decisions** — Without knowing you're supposed to capture the king, chess is just moving statues around. Goals give actions meaning and direction.
2. **Tension and excitement** — The unpredictable outcome only matters if you're invested in a particular result. Goals create that investment.

### "Fun" Is Not a Goal

- "The only goal is to have fun" and "you win if everyone has fun" are meaningless.
- Fun is a *result* of playing a game that tickles the right brain parts. It's outside the game.
- A goal is *part of* the game. It's what makes the game a game and what makes it fun for most people.

## Goals and the Eight Kinds of Fun

Different player types relate to goals differently:

| Player Type | Relationship to Goals |
|---|---|
| **Challenge** | Victory IS the fun — achieving goals is the brain-tickle |
| **Submission** | Goals facilitate losing yourself in a task |
| **Narrative** | Goals give impetus and structure to the story |
| **Fellowship** | Cooperative goals create team feeling |
| **Fantasy** | Goals drive character actions, providing context for immersion |
| **Discovery** | Goals like "explore the sewers" directly appeal |
| **Expression** | Least helped by goals |
| **Sense Pleasure** | Least helped by goals |

No one is purely one type. The same player's different engagement types may need goals for different reasons.

## RPGs: A Game of Many Goals

RPGs are uniquely complex because multiple goal layers coexist:

### Two Default Goals (Handed Down)
1. **Survive** — Keep your character playable (definition varies by game: death, insanity, corruption, etc.)
2. **The adventure/mission goal** — Rescue the dragon, slay the princess, investigate the murder, etc.

### Player-Defined Goals
- Group goals: "Let's conquer this city"
- Strategic sub-goals: "Find a magical sword to defeat the lich"
- Personal character goals: Find my father's killer, become a god, buy an airship

### Goals Are Universal, Victory Is Personal

- Games have goals — that's what makes them winnable/losable.
- But **you, the individual player**, decide which goals matter most and how much victory matters to your enjoyment.
- This isn't unique to RPGs. Video gamers do it constantly: completionists, speedrunners, Nuzlocke challenges, self-imposed restrictions.

## The Social Contract: Don't Be an Asshole

- Even if the adventure goal isn't personally important to you, you owe it to the table to work toward it — other players need it for their fun.
- Personal goals can't sabotage other players' goals.
- This is why RPGs are considered cooperative and why most adventures have built-in goals: many players EXPECT goals but DON'T WANT TO create their own.

## Case Study: Call of Cthulhu (The "Unwinnable" Game)

- CoC is designed so that heroes will eventually lose — investigations drive them mad or kill them.
- But it still has goals. Players are trying their hardest to accomplish them.
- The engagement comes from **seeing how far you can get** — how close to victory before the inevitable loss.
- Goals still provide context and tension. The tension just comes from seeing how far short of the finish line you fall, not from winning.
- This is the opposite end of the spectrum from Kirby's Epic Yarn (where losing is nearly impossible).

## Case Study: Fiasco (Not an RPG)

**Key distinction: asynchronous goals.**

In an RPG:
- The player tries to make decisions **the character would make**
- The character is an **avatar** for the player
- Player and character **share goals** — you want your character to succeed
- You can only choose **what your character does**

In Fiasco:
- Players control **scenes and resolutions**, not just character choices
- You're playing a **story**, not a character
- You're an **author or filmmaker**, not an avatar
- You control the character's **circumstances or fate**, not just their actions
- The mechanics are about **strategically telling an entertaining story**, not resolving character actions

This doesn't make Fiasco bad — it makes it a different genre. The creator and publisher don't even call it an RPG; they call it "a GM-less game."

### Why Genre Distinctions Matter

- Common language enables intelligent critical analysis → academic discussion → improved games
- Genres work as marketing shorthand — fans find games they like
- Genre familiarity lets creators assume things about their audience
- Genre conventions can be consciously broken to explore new design space (Fiasco's creator likely set out to make something *different* from an RPG)
