# The World of D&D According to Rosewater… According to Angry

## Premise

Mark Rosewater (lead designer of Magic: the Gathering) published "[Ten Things Every Game Needs](https://magic.wizards.com/en/articles/archive/making-magic/ten-things-every-game-needs-part-1-part-2-2011-12-19)." Angry applies the first 5 of these criteria to D&D to determine which elements the system provides and which the GM must bring.

**Key framing**: TTRPGs are not complete games — they are **game engines** (encounter-building and resolution engines + character-building systems + resource attrition rules). The GM must finish designing the actual game. Adventures are the real games.

## Rosewater's 10 Elements (First 5 Analyzed)

### 1. A Goal or Goals

Every game needs an attractive goal worth pursuing. D&D provides only two weak implied goals:
- **Survive** (don't lose) — weak, just "don't die"
- **Grow your character** (XP, levels, loot) — provides reason to get through encounters but no broader context; no endpoint

**The GM must bring goals.** Present achievable, attractive adventure goals regularly, or facilitate players choosing their own.

### 2. Rules (as Constraints)

Rosewater's insight: rules exist to make the goal **hard to achieve**. Every die roll is an obstacle. "Game design is unique in that the goal of the design is to actually make the thing hard to do."

Key points:
- **The GM is a game mechanic** — part of the rules system, using rules to build challenges and constrain players.
- Letting players have too much freedom over the rules (e.g., arguing which ability score applies) **breeds creativity out of them** — turns it into a dice game instead of creative problem-solving.
- Restrictions allow creativity to thrive.

### 3. Interaction

Players' actions must interconnect, forcing them to react to each other.

**Stunning realization**: D&D has far less player interaction than it appears.
- In combat, players mostly take individual turns. Swapping out players wouldn't change much.
- Most non-combat challenges = one player steps forward while others watch.
- The "shifting spotlight" model means each character acts alone in sequence.
- Nothing in the system **requires or encourages** teamwork. A party can win every encounter without ever talking to each other.

> "I think TTRPGs like D&D have become very much about groups of players playing solitaire at the table together."

### 4. A Catch-Up Feature

Players who fall behind need a way to turn things around, or they disengage.

- D&D has individual catch-up features (healing, emergency spells) but **no party-scale catch-up mechanic**.
- Once a party member goes down, the action economy disadvantage can snowball (death spiral).
- **At the adventure level**: if the party starts losing the adventure (e.g., running out of time to stop the ritual), what allows them to recover? Almost no GMs design for this.

### 5. Inertia

The game should push inexorably toward completion. Successes should compound; failures should compound.

- **Negative inertia** exists: D&D's attrition system (depleting spells, HP, resources) means overextending makes future encounters harder.
- **Positive inertia** is mostly absent within adventures. Each encounter happens in a bubble; winning one doesn't meaningfully help win the next (beyond XP/leveling, which is long-term).
- The interplay between inertia and catch-up mechanics creates engagement — D&D largely lacks this dynamic.
- **Pacing inertia** (pushing toward completion) falls entirely to the GM.

## The Big Realization

> D&D isn't really a game engine at all. It's an encounter building and resolution engine, a character-building system, and some rules for resource attrition.

There are **no mechanics that deal with the game on a larger scale than individual encounters**. D&D expects adventure designers (publishers or GMs) to handle:
- Goals, interaction, catch-up, inertia, pacing
- Everything that makes a string of encounters into a complete game experience

**Adventure design IS game design.** The DMG implies you just figure out plot elements, slap encounters on a map, add a random encounter table, and go. That's deeply insufficient.

## Notable Examples from Other Games

- **13th Age's Escalation Die**: both an inertia mechanic and a catch-up mechanic (encounter-level).
- **Marvel Heroic RPG's Chaos Pool / Angry's Tension Pool**: inertia mechanics that exist outside individual scenes.
- **Red Hand of Doom's Victory Points** (from comments): campaign-level catch-up mechanic where multiple objectives provide redundancy.
