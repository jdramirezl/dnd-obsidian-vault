# A Trifecta of BS

## Summary

A wide-ranging "BS article" tackling three interconnected issues plaguing modern D&D and RPGs: the overvaluation of encounter balance, the static nature of modern character creation, and how both combine to produce passive, combat-fixated players who treat their character sheets as the totality of what they can do. Angry argues these trends stem from a cultural shift in how players engage with RPGs — away from emergent discovery and toward pre-scripted optimization.

## Key Advice

### Balance Is an Overrated Joke
- Encounter balance in D&D is a **loose educated guess**, not a precise science. Too many variables (dice randomness, party composition, tactical skill, resource expenditure) make pinpoint accuracy impossible.
- The designers deliberately err on the side of making things too easy. GMs who treat CR as gospel are fooling themselves.
- **Older editions** didn't guarantee winnable fights. Players picked their fights, negotiated, fled, set traps, and used creative tactics. The lack of guaranteed victory produced better stories and more engaged players.
- A perfectly balanced game that can be played by a random number generator doesn't need players. Imbalance is where excitement, experimentation, and discovery live.

### Characters Are Defined by What They Do, Not Who They Are at Creation
- Modern character generation (3E onward) front-loads so many choices that characters are essentially complete at level 1-3. Players plan builds, write backstories, and lock in personality traits before play begins.
- This produces **static characters** — a literary no-no. Once you know the character, they never surprise you.
- Older editions and games like DCC encourage discovering your character through play. The most memorable characters come from what happened during the game, not from clever backstory.
- The game now sells the idea that "who you are by birth is more important than what you do with your life" — the wrong message for a game defined by open-ended player choice.

### Players Treat Character Sheets as Everything They Can Do
- The explosion of codified abilities trains players to only look at their sheets for options, ignoring creative problem-solving.
- Players fight everything to the death because the game trains them to expect winnable encounters that yield XP and loot.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop worshipping CR.** If encounters are too easy, scale them up. If too hard, scale down. The system is a rough guide, not scripture.
2. **Include unwinnable encounters** early in a campaign. Teach players that retreat, negotiation, and creative tactics are valid — and sometimes necessary.
3. **Decouple XP from combat.** Award XP for achieving goals, acquiring treasure, or solving problems to reduce the "murder everything" incentive.
4. **Try random character generation** at least once. Roll stats and play what the dice give you. Discover the character through play instead of scripting them.
5. **Challenge players to act beyond their sheets.** Reward creative, narrative-driven actions that aren't codified in class abilities.
6. **Embrace imbalance deliberately.** Vary encounter difficulty wildly. Let the world feel dangerous and unpredictable.

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> Game balance is a vague guess at best. Lean into the imbalance — it's where danger, creativity, and memorable stories live. Characters should be defined by what they do in play, not by the choices locked in at character creation. If your players fight everything and never retreat, the game has trained them to do that. Untrain them.
