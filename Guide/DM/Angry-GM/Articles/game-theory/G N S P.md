## Key Advice

- **Playstyle is how players decide what to do**, not just what they do. It lives in the question "how do you decide what your character does?" and emerges from play experiences and gaming media consumed, not from rulebooks.
- **Playstyle is descriptive, not definitive.** It describes vague patterns of behavior across broad audiences. It is not a rigid category, a value judgment, or a prescription for "the right way to play."
- **Push-Button Play** is when players treat skills and abilities like buttons on a controller—scan the character sheet, pick the right skill for the problem, roll a die, expect the obstacle to go away. The issue isn't any single instance; it's when players expect that to be the *entire* interaction every time.
- **Create-and-Portray Play** is when players build fully realized characters with fixed motivations, personalities, and goals before play begins, then strive to stay true to that vision throughout the game. The alternative is characters whose personality emerges and evolves through play.
- **Performance is a proposed fourth playstyle** alongside Gamist, Narrativist, and Simulationist. Performance players see D&D as putting on a show—they have a role to play and they play it to the hilt.
- **Performance play reframes dice as performance prompts**, not success/failure mechanics. The die result tells the performer *how* to portray the action, not whether they won or lost. This is why such players want to describe their own crits and kills.
- **Push-Button Play and Create-and-Portray are behaviors that emerge from Performance playstyle.** If the game is about performing your character, obstacles are just prompts, and the character must be fully defined before the curtain rises.
- **Performance play has likely always existed** but has become much more prominent recently, driven by cultural shifts and new audiences entering TTRPGs through actual-play streams like Critical Role.
- **Do not confuse playstyle with play aesthetics.** Playstyle (how you play) is about the Dynamics of play. Play aesthetics (why you play) are about the reasons people engage with games. They are related but distinct.
- **Everyone is biased about playstyle.** Your preference is D&D the way you play it. Whatever emotional response you have to hearing about another playstyle is just that—emotional, not objective.

## Summary

Angry proposes adding a fourth letter to the classic GNS (Gamist-Narrativist-Simulationist) framework: P for Performance. Some players see D&D as a Game (overcome obstacles, win), some as a Narrative (tell the best story), some as a Simulation (enter a world and deal with it as-is), and now some as a Performance (put on a show, portray a character to the hilt). This Performance playstyle manifests in two observable behaviors: Push-Button Play (treating skills like controller buttons to press on obstacles) and Create-and-Portray Play (building a fully realized, fixed character before play and staying rigidly true to that vision). Performance players treat dice as performance prompts rather than success/failure mechanics—they want to describe their own dramatic moments. This style has grown dramatically due to actual-play media like Critical Role bringing in audiences who learned D&D by watching performers perform. Angry distinguishes playstyle (how you decide what to do—Dynamics) from play aesthetics (why you play—Aesthetics, per the MDA framework), and emphasizes that playstyle is descriptive, not prescriptive. He acknowledges his own bias against Performance play but argues the style is real, distinct, and here to stay.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Learn to recognize Performance play at your table.** Watch for players who pre-define rigid character visions, treat dice as narration prompts rather than outcome determiners, and want to describe their own crits/kills/failures.
2. **Distinguish Push-Button Play from normal play.** The issue isn't "I pick the lock"—it's when players *expect* every obstacle to resolve with one skill name and one die roll, every time, with no further engagement.
3. **Recognize Create-and-Portray vs. emergent characters.** Some players need a fully formed character before session one; others discover their character through play. Neither is wrong, but they create different table dynamics.
4. **Don't confuse playstyle with play aesthetics.** Why someone plays (fun types/motivations) is different from how they decide what to do at the table. Use the right framework for the right question.
5. **Check your biases before judging another playstyle.** Your emotional reaction to hearing about Performance play (or any style) is personal preference, not objective analysis. See it for what it is.
6. **Have honest conversations about expectations.** If you're a GM who hates Performance play, or a player who craves it, that mismatch needs to be surfaced—not argued about in terms of "right" and "wrong."

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> A fourth playstyle—Performance—has emerged alongside Gamist, Narrativist, and Simulationist. Performance players see D&D as a show to perform, which drives Push-Button Play and Create-and-Portray behaviors. It's real, it's growing, and whether you love it or hate it, you need to recognize it at your table.
