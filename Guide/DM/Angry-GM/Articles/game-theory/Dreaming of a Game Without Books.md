# Dreaming of a Game Without Books

## Summary

Angry articulates his core game design philosophy: minimize the need to reference rules during play by building broad, general, intuitive rules with useful "levers" that can be pulled at lower levels of scope. He uses D&D's spell system as a case study of what NOT to do (too many specific, unreferenceable rules) and the Tension Pool as an example of what TO do (simple, memorable, versatile). The article lays out a layered design approach where general rules are easy to remember, specific rules are small and glanceable, and the system provides enough levers for GMs and designers to create depth without adding complexity.

## Key Advice

### The Dream: No Books at the Table
- The design goal is a game where players and GMs rarely need to stop and look something up. Every rule reference is a speedbump to gameplay.
- This doesn't mean eliminating complexity — it means organizing it into layers where the broadest rules are memorizable and the most specific rules are glanceable.

### General Rules vs. Specific Rules
- Every mechanic has **general rules** (how the class of things works) and **specific rules** (how a particular instance works).
- General rules should be few, intuitive, and memorizable. Specific rules should be brief and referenceable at a glance.
- **D&D spells are a mess** because too much is specific and too little is general. Each spell is a unique snowflake requiring full text reference.
- **Weapon attacks work well** because the general rule is simple (roll attack, roll damage) and specific differences are minimal (damage die, range, a few keywords).

### The Layered Design Approach
1. **Identify what needs broad rules.** If something appears more than once or twice, it needs a general system (magic, pets/companions, action resolution).
2. **Build broad, general rules** that are intuitive and easy to remember.
3. **Build levers into broad rules** that can be pulled at lower levels to add depth without complexity.
4. **Differentiate at lower levels** with increasingly smaller tweaks. The more specific you get, the smaller the rules changes should be.
5. **Pare down to the memorable.** Once a general rule works and has good levers, simplify it to the minimum that still does the job.

### Levers in Action Resolution (D&D 5E Example)
- D&D's action resolution has only ~4 levers: type of check, ability/skill, DC, and advantage/disadvantage. They're all numerical — they just tweak odds.
- **Proposed simplification:** "The GM chooses an ability score; if the player has a relevant proficiency, add proficiency bonus." This is cleaner, more intuitive, and adds a new lever (easy to create new proficiencies).
- **Proposed addition:** Margin of success/failure as a general rule. Simple to remember, provides a powerful lever for class abilities, encounters, and adventures without changing the core system.

### The Tension Pool as Design Example
- The Tension Pool is deliberately simple: visible dice pool, add die for cautious actions, roll for bold actions, complication on a 1.
- It's been simplified over time, not expanded. Bells and whistles were removed to make it broadly usable.
- Its simplicity is a feature: it spurs creativity in others who want to expand and customize it. A good mechanic inspires tinkering.
- The Tension Pool is a system for the **players** to see — it gives them information for decision-making and lets their action choices influence the game.

### DIY Is the Point
- RPGs are a DIY hobby. Creative GMs will always fiddle with mechanics.
- A sign of a good mechanic is that people see many possible ways to use and expand it.
- Design the simplest, most versatile version. Let users customize from there.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Audit your homebrew rules.** If something requires frequent book-checking, it needs broader general rules or simplification.
2. **Look for repeated patterns** in your game. If multiple things work similarly (pets, familiars, summons, hirelings), unify them under one general system.
3. **Build levers, not exceptions.** Instead of writing unique rules for each situation, build general rules with adjustable parameters (proficiency, DC, advantage, margin of success).
4. **Simplify spellcasting** by standardizing ranges (melee/ranged/unlimited), resolution (always ability check), and components (always requires implement).
5. **Use the Tension Pool as-is** for time pressure and recklessness. Don't over-complicate it — its simplicity is the point.
6. **Test the "one paragraph" rule.** If you can't describe a mechanic in one paragraph, it's probably too complex for table use.
7. **Design for the table, not the page.** Ask: "Can a player use this without stopping to read?" If not, simplify.

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> Design broad, general rules that are easy to remember and provide useful levers to pull. Differentiate at lower levels with increasingly smaller tweaks. The goal is a game where you never need to open the rulebook during play. Simplicity isn't the enemy of depth — it's the foundation. The Tension Pool works because it's one paragraph of rules with infinite applications. Aim for that.
