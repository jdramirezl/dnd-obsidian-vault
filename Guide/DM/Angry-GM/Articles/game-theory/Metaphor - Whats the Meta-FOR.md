## Key Concepts

### Metaphor vs. Mechanics

- **The metaphor is the story skin of a game.** It's what the game is supposed to be about—the fictional context. In Pandemic, the metaphor is stopping four diseases from spreading across the world.
- **The mechanics are the rules and procedures.** Moving tokens, placing cubes, resolving dice contests. In theory, you could play many games as pure abstract mechanics without any story.
- **The metaphor adds context and emotional engagement.** It makes rules easier to learn (cubes are "infected populations," not arbitrary tokens) and creates narrative investment. Without it, a game is just a puzzle.

### When Metaphor and Mechanics Mesh

- **Strong metaphor-mechanic coupling means the game *feels* like its story even without the story.** Pandemic stripped of its disease theme would still feel like containing a spreading threat. That's good design.
- **Weak coupling means the story is just a skin.** Splendor's gem-mining theme could be swapped for anything without changing the experience. The metaphor is superficial.
- **The test:** Could you teach the game without any reference to its fiction? If not, the metaphor and mechanics are supporting each other well.

### Metaphor in RPGs

- **RPGs are deeply tied to their metaphors.** Without the fictional world, most RPG mechanics are impossible to use—you can't assign ability scores or determine skill relevance without context.
- **The strongest RPG mechanics can't describe anything other than what they represent.** D&D's Vancian magic system feels like D&D even in other games. The core d20 + ability mod + proficiency mechanic, however, could work in any setting.
- **The weakest mechanics are completely abstract.** D&D 5E's Inspiration mechanic is essentially "specify a condition, fulfill it, get a bonus"—it could work without any reference to the fiction at all.

### Metaphor Runs Deeper Than Surface Details

- **The metaphor isn't just names and descriptions—it's themes and feel.** Pandemic: Reign of Cthulhu uses the same mechanics as Pandemic but with demons instead of diseases. Both are about containing a rapidly spreading force before the world is overwhelmed. Same deep metaphor, different surface.
- **When reskinning, drill down to themes, not surface effects.** A possessed human murder-bot might look like a zombie on the surface, but if the core fantasy is "vicious, savage, unrelenting murder," look at orcs, gnolls, and sahuagin for mechanics—not shambling undead.

### Practical Design Lessons

1. **Design mechanics that fit the metaphor so tightly they couldn't be about anything else.** Ask: "If I change the names, could this describe something different?" If yes, the mechanic isn't working with its metaphor.
2. **Don't reinvent the wheel.** If an existing mechanic fits the deep metaphor you're going for, reskin it rather than building from scratch.
3. **Reskin carefully.** Match the themes and feel, not just the surface description.
4. **Be skeptical of licensed games.** Most just wear the skin of the license without capturing its mechanical essence.

## Summary

In game design, the "metaphor" is the fictional story a game tells, while "mechanics" are the rules and procedures. The best games tightly couple these so the mechanics *feel* like the story even stripped of context. In RPGs, this coupling is especially important because the fiction is the core engagement. When designing or reskinning mechanics, focus on matching the deep themes and feel—not surface details. A possessed murder-bot should use frenzy mechanics (orcs, gnolls), not shambling undead mechanics (zombies), because the metaphor is savage violence, not mindless decay.
