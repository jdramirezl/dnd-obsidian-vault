## Key Advice

- **Engagement = the players are interested and coming back next week.** Don't overthink the difference between "fun," "immersion," "buy-in," and "engagement." They all mean the same thing: the game is working.
- **Core Engagements are the aesthetics your game is designed to satisfy.** Not just aesthetics that happen to be present, but the ones your decisions consistently emphasize. Most games have one to four Core Engagements.
- **Game systems come with built-in Core Engagements, but you're not locked in.** D&D supports Challenge by default, but you can emphasize or de-emphasize any aesthetic through your GMing decisions. Some systems make this easier or harder.
- **Your Core Engagements emerge from your decisions, not from deliberate planning.** You don't sit down and declare "Challenge will be my Core Engagement." It emerges from how you build encounters, apply rules, handle failure, and structure adventures.
- **The MDA Framework: Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics.** Every game element you create (Mechanic) produces player interactions (Dynamics) that generate play experiences (Aesthetics). In RPGs, "Mechanics" includes rules, story elements, house rules, table conduct guidelines, and GM advice—not just stats.
- **Use aesthetics as a tuning tool, not a design blueprint.** The framework is most useful for making smaller decisions and evaluating advice, not for building a game from scratch around target aesthetics.
- **Your players are already telling you what works.** If they keep showing up, your Core Engagements are probably a good match. Focus on the players who seem less engaged.
- **Practical aesthetic triggers:**
  - **Sensation:** Handouts, maps, props, music, terrain, miniatures, fancy character sheets.
  - **Fantasy:** Consistency, internal logic, rich detail, suspension of disbelief, letting players roleplay mundane moments.
  - **Narrative:** Clear goals, direction, logical NPC motivations, story structure (arcs, climaxes, resolution), human drama.
  - **Challenge:** Victory isn't assured, player choices matter, freedom to approach problems in multiple ways, fair consequences for failure.
  - **Fellowship:** Team-oriented play, cooperation mechanics, no intraparty treachery, character bonds and relationships.
  - **Discovery:** Secrets, hidden lore, optional content, strange locations, minor treasures—and critically, things that are *missable*.
  - **Expression:** Options, customization, meaningful choices, player authorship of world details, the ability to be unique.
  - **Submission:** Easy entry, casual play, simple goals, low cognitive load, repetitive but satisfying loops.

## Summary

Part 2 applies the Eight Kinds of Fun to practical GMing through the concept of Core Engagements—the aesthetics your game consistently emphasizes through your decisions. Every GM has a style, and that style is really just "which aesthetics you push hardest." The MDA Framework explains the chain: game elements you create (Mechanics) produce player interactions (Dynamics) that generate experiences (Aesthetics). In RPGs, "Mechanics" means everything—rules, story elements, house rules, table conduct, even racial flavor text. Your Core Engagements emerge naturally from your accumulated decisions, not from deliberate planning. The framework is most useful as a tuning tool: evaluating whether a piece of advice fits your game, diagnosing why a player seems disengaged, or understanding why a new mechanic does or doesn't work. Angry provides practical triggers for each aesthetic and warns against both over-engineering (trying to hit all eight) and under-thinking (ignoring aesthetics entirely). The key insight: if your players keep coming back, you're probably doing fine. Focus your attention on the players who seem less engaged and figure out which aesthetic they're missing.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Identify your Core Engagements by reviewing your GMing Credo or habits.** What patterns emerge? What aesthetics do your decisions consistently support?
2. **Watch your players, don't just ask them.** Most people are bad at introspection. Observe what excites them, bores them, and frustrates them during play.
3. **Experiment to identify player preferences.** Ask players to create an NPC (Expression test). Offer a one-shot with pregens exploring campaign lore (Narrative/Discovery test). Propose removing character death (Challenge vs. Fantasy test).
4. **When evaluating GMing advice, ask which aesthetics it serves.** "Always let players build the world" serves Expression. "Never fudge dice" serves Challenge. Neither is universally correct—it depends on your Core Engagements.
5. **Use the MDA chain to trace your decisions to their aesthetic impact.** Strict racial flavor text → consistent NPC behavior → world feels real → Fantasy aesthetic. Encounter building rules → balanced obstacles → earned victories → Challenge aesthetic.
6. **For Discovery, make things missable.** This is anathema to modern GM instincts, but Discovery seekers need the feeling that they found something others could have overlooked.
7. **Don't try to hit all eight aesthetics equally.** More than three or four Core Engagements feels unfocused. Pick your strengths and lean into them.
8. **Think critically, question everything, dismiss nothing.** Every piece of GMing advice is valid for some aesthetic and harmful for another. Evaluate everything against your specific game and players.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Your GMing style is just your Core Engagements—the aesthetics your decisions consistently emphasize. Use the MDA framework (Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics) to understand why your decisions produce the experiences they do, tune your game for your specific players, and evaluate advice critically. If your players keep coming back, you're probably fine. Focus on whoever seems least engaged.
