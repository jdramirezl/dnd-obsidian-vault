# The Anatomy of a Game

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Anatomy of a Game](https://theangrygm.com/anatomy-of-a-game/)
**Date:** July 26, 2024
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Summary

Part of the True Scenario Designery course, this article defines the four essential components that make a game a game — and therefore what every scenario must provide. Scenario design *is* game design: the Scenario Designer assembles the system's disjointed pieces into an actual, playable experience.

---

## The Four Components of a Game

### 1. Goals
- Every game needs an explicit, in-game Goal — the thing players are working toward.
- "To win, the players must…" — the Goal finishes that sentence.
- **Goals ≠ Motivations** (why players play) **≠ Rewards** (XP, loot, level-ups).
- Goals come from the game, not from the players or their characters.
- Players don't need to know *why* the game is fun, but they must know the Goal to play.

### 2. Rules
- Rules define how players interact with the game: what they can do, can't do, and how outcomes are determined.
- The Scenario Designer doesn't write rules but must have expert-level understanding of them — written *and* unwritten.
- The most important unwritten rule in TTRPGs: the **Declare-Determine-Describe Cycle** — the GM describes what characters perceive, players declare actions, the GM determines and describes outcomes.
- Players' knowledge must be limited to what their characters can perceive.

#### Rules Preprocessing
- Scenario Designers must predict likely player interactions and provide the GM with the mechanics to resolve them.
- Listing mechanical resolutions is *not* defining the possible actions — it's predicting the most likely ones and arming the GM.
- Don't rely on the GM's judgment for interactions you can see coming.

### 3. Challenge
- A game must present one or more in-game Challenges — things players must accomplish or overcome on the way to the Goal.
- **Challenge ≠ Difficulty.** Even easy obstacles are Challenges if they require *any* effort or skill and have *any* uncertainty.
- Rolling dice is not *technically* a Challenge (no player effort or skill), but that's "technically correct, not always right."
- TTRPGs primarily challenge players to **make the best choices** in given situations — implying situations must have wrong/worse/bad choices too.
- Challenge Ratings and Difficulty Classes are not inherently about Challenge or Difficulty in the game-design sense.

### 4. Context
- Context is the framework by which players understand Goals and Challenges.
- In board games (e.g., *Century: Spice Road*), Context is just a coat of paint — it doesn't help you play.
- In TTRPGs, Context is **vital**: understanding the fictional world helps players overcome Challenges, predict outcomes, and discover more gameplay options.
- Goals, Challenges, and actions must be comprehensible within the game's Contextual Framework — the imaginary world and unfolding narrative.

---

## Key Principles

- **"You ain't an Engine Designer."** Scenario Designers work within the system they have. If you need rules that don't exist to make your scenario work, you have a bad scenario, not a bad system.
- **A scenario is a game.** Encounters, adventures, arcs, and campaigns are all fundamentally the same thing — games that need Goals, Rules, Challenge, and Context.
- **Design Statements** (starting point, players' goals, possible outcomes, major challenge) implicitly address all four components.
- **Player Agency** is described as "the Santa Claus of Scenario Design" — a fun fantasy that doesn't exist, but knowing it's a lie and keeping it alive is part of being an adult designer.

---

## The Locked Door Thought Experiment

Angry poses this as homework: a locked door, handled rightly, is a Challenge; handled wrongly, it isn't. The key insight is that TTRPGs challenge players to make choices, so situations must have wrong or bad choices available. A locked door *in and of itself* — without considering what's behind it — can be a genuine Challenge if designed correctly.
