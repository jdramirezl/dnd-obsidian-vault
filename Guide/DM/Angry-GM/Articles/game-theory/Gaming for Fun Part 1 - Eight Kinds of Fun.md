## Key Advice

- **The GM is the real game designer.** D&D is not a game—it's a game engine. The GM assembles it into a game through every decision about how to run, present, and design the experience. Even running a published module involves countless design decisions.
- **"Fun" is not a single thing.** Based on the MDA framework (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek, 2004), there are eight distinct aesthetics of play—eight reasons people engage with games. Everyone prioritizes them differently.
- **The Eight Kinds of Fun:**
  1. **Sensory Pleasure** — Joy from engaging the senses: art, dice, miniatures, terrain, maps, music, physical books.
  2. **Fantasy** — Escapism and immersion. Losing yourself in an imaginary world and being someone you're not. Fragile—broken by inconsistency, abstraction, or out-of-character demands.
  3. **Narrative** — Pleasure from experiencing a well-told story with structure: beginning, middle, end, climax, resolution. Narrative seekers crave closure.
  4. **Challenge** — The fun of overcoming obstacles and earning victory. Not just combat—puzzles, negotiations, investigations all count. Fudging dice and "everybody wins" mentalities are anathema.
  5. **Fellowship** — The game as a framework for social interaction and cooperation. Ruined by group drama, antisocial behavior, and "that one evil character."
  6. **Discovery** — Exploring and uncovering things. Filling in blanks on the map (literal or metaphorical). Includes self-discovery through moral dilemmas. Requires that secrets exist *before* the player finds them—player authorship is not discovery.
  7. **Expression** — Creative self-expression. Making something unique, leaving your mark. The "trending aesthetic" in the RPG community. Powerful but not universal—can conflict with Discovery and Fantasy.
  8. **Submission (Abnegation)** — Turning off your brain and losing yourself in effortless tasks. "Beer and pretzels" play. RPGs handle this poorly due to high cognitive load, but it exists.
- **Everyone has a unique mix of preferences.** No one is about just one aesthetic. Some are essential, others are irrelevant. Most people are unaware of their own aesthetic preferences.
- **Edition wars and style arguments stem from different aesthetic priorities.** People talk past each other because they have different core assumptions about what makes a game fun, and lack the language to express it.
- **Expression and Discovery are often conflated but are fundamentally different.** Asking players to create world details satisfies Expression but destroys Discovery. Discovery requires that secrets existed before the player found them.
- **"Player authorship always increases engagement" is a myth.** It excites Expression seekers but alienates Discovery seekers and can break Fantasy seekers' immersion.

## Summary

Angry adapts the MDA framework's "aesthetics of play" from video game design into a practical tool for RPG GMs. The core insight: the GM is the real game designer—D&D is just a game engine—and understanding *why* things are fun lets you make better design decisions. There are eight distinct types of fun (Sensory Pleasure, Fantasy, Narrative, Challenge, Fellowship, Discovery, Expression, Submission), and every player brings a unique mix of priorities. Most people are unaware of their own aesthetic preferences and can't articulate why they like or dislike certain game elements. This explains edition wars: when D&D 4E required miniatures, Sensory Pleasure seekers loved it while non-seekers resented the overhead. When 4E dropped crafting skills, Expression seekers were upset because expending resources on "useless" skills was a meaningful creative statement. The most important distinction is between Expression and Discovery—they're often conflated but are fundamentally opposed. Asking players to create world details satisfies Expression but destroys Discovery, because Discovery requires that secrets existed before the player found them. The myth that "player authorship always increases engagement" is dangerous because it only works for Expression seekers.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Learn the eight aesthetics and start identifying which ones matter most to you as a GM.** Your preferences shape every decision you make about your game.
2. **Observe your players' reactions to identify their aesthetic priorities.** Watch what excites them, what bores them, and what frustrates them—don't just ask, because most people can't articulate their preferences accurately.
3. **Stop conflating Expression and Discovery.** If your players love exploring, don't ask them to invent what they find. If they love creating, don't hide everything behind a curtain.
4. **Recognize that edition wars and style arguments are aesthetic conflicts.** People aren't irrational—they just have different priorities and lack the language to express them. Use this framework to understand, not to judge.
5. **Don't assume your aesthetic priorities are universal.** What makes the game fun for you may do nothing for (or actively harm) another player's experience.
6. **Question blanket GMing advice through the lens of aesthetics.** "Always let players build the world," "never fudge dice," "miniatures are essential"—each of these serves specific aesthetics and harms others.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Fun isn't one thing—it's eight things, and every player wants a different mix. Learn the eight aesthetics of play, figure out what you and your players actually want, and stop assuming everyone hears the same music. The GM who understands *why* things are fun can make better decisions than the one who just hopes fun happens.
