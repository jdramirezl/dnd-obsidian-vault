# Why Are You Doing This - Fighting Tyler Durden, Game Designer

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/why-are-you-doing-this/)
**Date:** April 28, 2017
**Series:** Hacking Theory

---

## Every Hack Makes the Game Worse

Every new rule you add increases:
- **Complexity** — number of elements to learn and understand
- **Cognitive Load** — number of things to track during play
- **Imbalance** — disconnection between player skill/choices and probable outcomes
- **Unexpected Interactions** — two rules combining in ways you didn't foresee

Professional designers have balanced these against the game experience. You're adding to a system you didn't build and don't fully understand. **Every hack is inherently bad.** It takes something extra to make it worth the cost.

## The Trade-Off

Before you hack, you must justify it. The hack must make the game *better* despite breaking it. "Because it would be cool" or "because the option should exist" is not enough. **More is NOT better.**

## Fight Tyler Durden

Argue with yourself. Out loud or in writing. Start with the assumption your idea is bad and will break the game. Then try to convince yourself it's worth it.

Tyler has three questions:

### Question 1: Why Are You Doing This?

Your hack must do one of three things:
1. **FIX** something broken
2. **ENHANCE** an existing gameplay experience
3. **CREATE** a new gameplay experience

**For fixes:** Prove something is actually broken. Identify what the game IS doing, what it SHOULD be doing, and WHY it should be doing that. Tyler is very suspicious of fixes.

**For enhancements/additions:** Explain which gameplay aesthetics you're targeting. Use the **MDA Framework** (Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics):

| Aesthetic | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Challenge** | Applying skill to overcome obstacles. Relies on fairness and player agency. |
| **Discovery** | Uncovering unknown and unexpected things. |
| **Expression** | Creating things that are unique expressions of imagination. |
| **Fantasy** | Being part of an imaginary world. Escapism. |
| **Fellowship** | Being part of a team or social circle. |
| **Narrative** | Experiencing a well-told, well-structured story. Different from Fantasy. |
| **Sensory Pleasure** | Appreciating things you can see, touch, hear. Art, maps, minis. |
| **Subjugation** | Shutting off your brain in effortless activity. Grinding. (TTRPGs handle this poorly.) |

**Example — Crafting systems appeal to:**
- Expression (unique equipment/loadouts)
- Challenge (resource management, preparation)
- Discovery (seeking resources, discovering recipes)
- Fantasy (doing things your character would do in-world)
- NOT Subjugation (mindless gathering works in video games, not TTRPGs)

Identifying target aesthetics also guides your design later.

### Question 2: Why Didn't the Game Designers Do It?

Assume the designers had the same idea and talked themselves out of it. **Why?** This helps you identify potential problems.

You don't need a perfect answer. If you passed Question 1, Tyler won't beat you up over this one. But thinking through it reveals pitfalls.

**Example — Stealth/infiltration subsystem:** The biggest issue is not all characters can participate equally. Extended infiltration takes lots of table time, and D&D requires all players to participate. That's why the designers stuck with simple single-roll stealth checks.

### Question 3: Why Is This the Best Way to Do It?

**Could you do this smaller?** Don't add a class when a feat works. Don't add a subsystem when rearranging existing systems works. The bigger the hack, the more complexity, cognitive load, imbalance, and interactions.

**Beat yourself up on this one.** Identify alternatives first. Then convince Tyler they won't work — and "it won't be as cool" is a crappy answer.

**Pathfinder's class bloat** is what happens when the designer says "go fuck yourself" to this question. The game became unapproachably complex because they kept adding classes instead of using smaller solutions.

## The Maturity of Design

Some ideas you'll lose the fight with Tyler on. That's how it should be. Those ideas might work someday in a different context, or as cornerstones of a system you design from scratch. But hacking requires the same maturity as designing — confront your ideas and kick the shit out of them so only the strong ones survive.

## Key Insight on Player Feedback

When players say they don't like something, **they are always right that they don't like it.** But their *reasons* and *explanations* are just excuses their brain invented to rationalize an emotional response. Don't dismiss the feeling because the reasoning is wrong. It's the GM's job to figure out the real "why."

Your own reasons and explanations are equally likely to be wrong. That's how brains work.
