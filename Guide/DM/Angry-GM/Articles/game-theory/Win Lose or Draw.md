# Win, Lose, or Draw

## Summary

This article opens a new module in the True Scenario Designery course focused on **Winning and Losing** as game design concepts. Rather than defining them outright, Angry explains why the RPG community struggles so badly to even *discuss* winning and losing. He identifies four root causes: (1) people expect games to be winnable/losable, (2) system designers confuse game engines with games, (3) some community members reject the "game" part of RPGs entirely, and (4) RPGs' continuous, ongoing nature makes final outcomes hard to conceptualize. The article sets up future lessons that will provide a more complete, complex understanding of winning and losing.

## Pop Quiz

> Is it okay for a Scenario Designer to design an adventure the players can't win? Is it okay to design an adventure the players can't lose?

The answer is deferred to future lessons. The point is to expose the cognitive dissonance: your gut gives a quick answer, but serious reasoning ties you in knots.

## Key Concepts

### People Expect Games to Be Games

- When you call something a "game," people expect something they can **win** and **lose**. This is near-universal — it's the first thing people ask about when learning a new game.
- People who want to play without keeping score explicitly call it out: "just a friendly game," "playing for fun," "casual game." The need to specify proves the default expectation.
- Many people don't enjoy games without the win/lose dimension. Without it, there's no challenge, and challenge is a major intrinsic motivation.

### System Designers Are the Problem

- RPG system designers are often bad game designers who don't understand that **RPG systems are game creation engines, not games themselves**. The adventures and scenarios are the actual games.
- When a system designer looks at their "game" and can't find clear win/lose conditions, they conclude RPGs must be special exceptions where winning doesn't apply — rather than recognizing they haven't built a complete game, just the engine.
- This confusion has propagated through generations of gamers, since each generation trains the next and the publishers aren't teaching anyone.
- D&D 2024 PHB notably dropped the traditional "you can't really win D&D" essay and just explains how to play.

### People Who Wish Games Weren't Games

Three groups muddy the waters:
1. **Old-school grognards** — insist they're running simulations, reject modern game design
2. **Performative/storytelling players** — don't care about the "game" part at all
3. Both groups are valid in their preferences, but Angry writes for people who want games to be games

Angry's position: the complete RPG package — storytelling, simulation, performance, AND challenge — is richer than focusing on any single aspect. He's not gatekeeping; he's advocating for the full experience.

### The Game That Keeps On Gaming

RPGs have unique properties that make winning/losing hard to conceptualize:

- **Permanence and continuity** — RPGs are "going concerns." There's always more game to play, and every game follows from the last.
- **Winning feels final** — Most people instinctively think of winning/losing as end-of-game outcomes. If the game never ends, how can you win?
- **Character Death looks like Losing** — It's the only thing in the game engine that resembles a loss state. But it's not necessarily final (resurrection, new characters, campaign continues).
- **If Character Death = Losing, then Not Dead = Winning?** — This tortured logic leads to the nonsensical conclusion that just being alive is winning, which doesn't feel like a real endpoint.

### Resolving the Cognitive Dissonance

The dissonance: you *know* games have goals and challenges (so winning/losing must exist), but everything about RPGs suggests they can't exist.

The wrong conclusion: "RPGs are special; winning and losing don't apply." This is exactly what generations of system designers wrote in their rulebooks.

The right path forward:
- **Winning and Losing DO exist in RPGs.** They are useful, necessary, and central.
- You need a **more complex understanding** of what winning and losing mean.
- RPGs are both **continuous AND discrete** — like light being both particle and wave. They're also fractal.
- This duality exists *precisely because* games need winning and losing, and this is how they fit in.

## Design Implications

- Adventures without win/lose states aren't really games — they're "player fuckaround" sessions with sucky gameplay experiences.
- The continuous nature of RPGs doesn't negate winning and losing; it means they manifest differently than in board games or video games.
- Understanding winning and losing is prerequisite to designing complete, satisfying scenarios.
