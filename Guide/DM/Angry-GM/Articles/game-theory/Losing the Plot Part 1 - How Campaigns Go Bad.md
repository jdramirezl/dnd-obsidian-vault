# Losing the Plot Part 1 - How Campaigns Go Bad

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/what-do-you-do-when-you-lose-the-plot-part-i/)
**Date:** February 27, 2025
**Series:** Losing the Plot (Part 1 of 3)

---

## What Does "Losing the Plot" Mean?

Not about forgetting the in-game story or outline. It's the sudden realization that your campaign isn't where it's supposed to be and nothing you're doing is getting it closer. Your actions and your goals have become disconnected — you're just going through the motions.

**The feeling:** "What the hell am I doing? This is nothing like what it's supposed to be. How did I get here?"

This often gets mistaken for GM Burnout. The difference matters: burnout implies fatigue with no fix except withdrawal. Losing the plot is fixable.

## How It Sneaks Up On You

It catches you off guard because it only happens after you've been "going through the motions" for a while. You keep telling yourself: "If I can just get through tonight's session, I'll sit down this weekend and really clean this up." That weekend never comes.

It's distressingly easy to think you're keeping things running well enough that one good weekend of focused work is all you need. It's depressingly easy to lose track of how many weeks you've been saying that.

## Campaign Vision: The Foundation

Angry's AD&D 2E campaign *Chain of Stars* started with deliberate constraints:

1. **Must have an ending.** Players explicitly asked to actually finish a campaign for once. This is a promise that cannot be broken.
2. **Must wrap in 6-9 months.** External deadline (since expired).

### Building from Random Characters

Used random character generation (old-school AD&D 2E style), then built the quest around whatever characters emerged. This is an **Eigenplot** — the campaign is designed so these specific random characters happen to be exactly the right people for the job. "Bilbo was meant to find the Ring."

Character details that became campaign pillars:
- Cleric with Astrology proficiency → astrology is important
- Peasant fisherwoman with inherited ancient sword → sword/bloodline matters
- Dwarf in exile → backstory hooks
- Elf historian with ancient languages → maps and cartography puzzles

**Key puzzle:** A star chart that's actually a map of terrestrial locations revealing where MacGuffins are hidden. This shaped the kingdom's circular geography.

## Gameplay Structure: The Real Innovation

Beyond plot, Angry focused on **Gameplay Structure** — the shapes adventures and campaigns take. This encompasses dungeon design, exploration loops, critical paths, act structures.

### Three-Part Map

1. **Campaign's overall Gameplay Structure** — the macro shape
2. **Session-by-session play modes** — the Gameplay Loop
3. **Mapping to actual sessions** — the calendar

### Structure Inspired by Zelda: A Link to the Past

Three acts instead of five:
- **Act I:** Linear Gauntlet (2 adventures) — characters come together, learn about the threat
- **Act II:** Open Structure (5 adventures) — MacGuffin hunt across the kingdom, any order
- **Act III:** Linear Gauntlet (1-2 adventures) — confront the threat, serious choices, ending

Transitions marked by major plot revelations. Adventures focused on Labyrinths and Branching Paths.

### The Gameplay Loop

- **Interlude:** Travel to town near adventure site (½–1 session)
- **Interlude:** Explore town, prepare for adventure (½–1 session)
- **Adventure:** Delve adventure site (2–3 sessions)
- **Interlude:** Post-adventure town business (½–1 session)

## The Current State: Lost

Over a year later:
- Players are having fun (they say it's the best game ever)
- The game is stalled in an endless Interlude between Act I and Act II
- No structured adventures are being run
- The campaign looks nothing like the vision
- Angry is treading water and unhappy

**The players don't see the problem. The GM does.** That's the insidious nature of losing the plot — the game can be fun session-to-session while being fundamentally broken at the campaign level.
