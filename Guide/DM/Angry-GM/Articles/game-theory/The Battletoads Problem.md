# The Battletoads Problem

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Battletoads Problem](https://theangrygm.com/the-battletoads-problem/)
**Date:** February 17, 2026

---

## Summary

Every gameplay genre (monster hunts, heists, mysteries, espionage) has an underlying structure — a gameplay loop — that makes it *feel* like that genre. GMs who chase variety by switching genres every session fall prey to **The Battletoads Problem**: players never learn any genre well enough to actually play it, and the GM never gets to run the advanced, interesting version of any genre.

---

## The Core Insight: Gameplay Genres Have Underlying Structures

- A monster hunt *feels* like a monster hunt because of its structure: scout → flush/bait → lead to ambush point → wear down → restrain → subdue.
- A heist *feels* like a heist because of its structure: scout → identify hardpoints/weaknesses → gather experts/assets → plan → execute → adapt → standoff → escape.
- A mystery has its own structure. So does espionage, so does a dungeon crawl.
- If the adventure doesn't capture the underlying structure, it won't feel like the genre, even if the surface elements are there.

---

## Two Parts to Learning Any Game

### 1. Mechanics (the tutorial-textbox stuff)
- What buttons do, what tools do, what buffs do, how environmental hazards work.
- Easy to teach explicitly.

### 2. The Gameplay Loop (learned by doing)
- The phases of a successful play session, the basic strategy, the flow.
- For monster hunts: prepare loadout → explore map → observe prey → note hazards → engage → skirmish-chase-skirmish → capitalize on openings.
- Players learn this by doing, not by being told. Tutorials cover basics; mastery comes from repetition.

## The Battletoads Problem

Named after the 1991 NES game *Battletoads*, which had 12 levels across ~9 different gameplay genres. Players never got comfortable with any one genre because the game kept switching.

**When HBGMs (Homebrewer Game Masters) chase genre novelty:**
- Players can't settle into any gameplay and get good at it.
- The gameplay never realizes its full potential.
- You're essentially running "Baby's First [Genre]" every time — the tutorial level, over and over.

---

## The Three-Adventure Progression

1. **First adventure** = Tutorial. Hold the players' hands, let them do the exciting part, handle the rest for them. (e.g., Bobbert Dulmoon hires the party to play a specific role in his monster hunt plan.)
2. **Second adventure** = Normal play. Let the players run a full [genre] adventure by themselves. See if they learned the structure.
3. **Third adventure** = The interesting one. Now you can throw curveballs because the players understand the underlying game flow. *This* is the adventure GMs actually want to run.

**Capture the Remorhaz is a great *third* monster hunt** — the fire-bleeding, burrowing monster that resists normal capture tools. It's not a first hunt.

---

## The "Current Hotness" Trap

- GMs are prone to **Novelty Bias**: play *Monster Hunter*, want to run a monster hunt; play *Return of Oprah Deen*, want a time-traveling mystery.
- The temptation is to chase whatever's exciting right now.
- But nothing compares to the sixth or tenth adventure in a genre — you can do things with a tenth dungeon you could never do with a third.

---

## Practical Advice

1. **For one-off genre shifts**: Isolate the best part of the genre, make that the players' responsibility, and handle the rest via NPCs/structure. (Tutorial approach.)
2. **Don't switch genres every session.** That's Battletoads.
3. **If you like variety**: Pick 2-3 genres that complement each other and flip between them.
4. **Sprinkle, don't replace**: Instead of running a mystery adventure, add mystery elements to your existing adventure type.
5. **If committed to variety**: Run shorter campaigns or distinct arcs. Give each genre ~3 months (enough to reach the third adventure).
6. **Resist the siren song**: Dance with who you brought. The payoff of depth beats the thrill of novelty.

## The Monster Hunt Structure (Practical Example)

For capturing a large creature:
1. Find the creature's lair or trail.
2. Flush it out or grab its attention (or use bait, possibly drugged).
3. Lead it to a pre-arranged ambush point (terrain that limits escape).
4. Wear it down (deplete to bloodied/staggered).
5. Signal the support team (yeomen with hooks, chains, catchpoles) to restrain it.
6. Continue wearing it down until it stops fighting.
7. Optional padding: sudden reversal (mate shows up, druidic activist attacks, etc.).

**The problem**: Most D&D players will just walk up and hit it until it passes out. The "JK I Meant To Knock It Out LOL" rule enables this.

**The solution**: Don't ask players to invent the whole plan. Assign them the exciting part within a larger plan run by an NPC expert.
