# Losing the Plot Part 2 - Behavior Patterns That Kill Campaigns

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/what-do-you-do-when-you-lose-the-plot-part-ii/)
**Date:** February 28, 2025
**Series:** Losing the Plot (Part 2 of 3)

---

## What "Losing the Plot" Actually Is

Not a specific problem — it's your brain recognizing that *something* has gone wrong without being able to articulate what. Like a Check Engine light on the back of your GM Screen.

When you can't pin down the problem, it feels like the problem is everywhere. Everything's gone wrong. So you get overwhelmed — or you assume the problem is you. "I'm just burnt out." Then you try to fix yourself, which never works, and the game dies.

**The real cause:** Your actions and your goals are no longer connected. You're doing stuff — maybe even fun stuff — but it isn't meaningful because it's not getting you anywhere worth being.

## Three Behavior Patterns That Cause It

Each is totally normal and necessary sometimes. They only become toxic when they turn into habitual patterns.

### 1. Just Getting By

Starts innocently: bad day, stressful week, not enough time. So you do just enough to get through the session and promise to make up for it later.

> "I don't have time to build this encounter right. I'll wing it tonight and do it properly next week."

**The trap:** You're pushing stress into tomorrow. You're demanding next week's you do twice the work. If next week is also bad, you push again. And again.

**It compounds like interest.** You think you're only one week behind, but the debt accumulates invisibly. Then one day you realize you're way off track and can't understand how.

### 2. Firefighting and Flailing

**Firefighting** = taking quick, sensible action in response to an obvious crisis. Games get canceled, notes get lost, players make stupid decisions. You handle it.

Two problems with firefighting:
1. It's not goal-driven — you're reacting, not advancing
2. Most GMs forget the cleanup. You don't just blast the fire and go back to normal. You check for damage, sweep up, repair. If you cancel a month of sessions, you need to re-center yourself, review notes, re-engage players — not just pick up where you left off.

**Flailing** = taking desperate, panicked action to solve a poorly understood or imagined crisis. It's overreaction. It's flooding the house to deal with a knocked-over candle.

Flailing is the natural response to "losing the plot" — you feel something's wrong, grab the biggest thing shaped like a problem, fling a panicked solution at it, and pat yourself on the back. Then a few weeks later you lose the plot again. Cycle repeats.

### 3. Acting on a Whim

The sneakiest one, because it looks like good GMing.

Adding random details, encounters, or hooks because they seem fun or are a good response to players. The shopkeeper's afraid of owls. A roadside encounter hits you mid-session. You drop an unplanned adventure hook.

**Why it's useful:** NPCs taking on life in your head means the world is alive. Inserting encounters off the cuff adjusts pacing dynamically. This is a necessary GM tool.

**Why it's dangerous:** Whims aren't goal-driven. Every random element crowds out something from the Campaign Vision. Even if it doesn't, it distracts you and your players.

**The dopamine trap:** You create engaging detail → players engage → you reward with more detail → they engage more → engagement Skinner Box. It all feels good but the game grinds to a halt. No meaningful progress.

> "That was fun, but what did we actually do for four hours? Are we getting anywhere?"

**The Endless Town Session:** Starts as "I'll let players have a session in town." Six weeks later you're running garden parties and the barbarian's fighting pit racket. Everyone's having fun but you're playing Fantasy Sims instead of Dragons in Dungeons.

**"Always say 'Yes, and…'" is bad advice.** Even trained improv actors know when to stop adding premises and start playing with what's there. Scenes have to progress. They have to end.

## The Heart of It All

Humans crave a meaningful sense of progress toward something. Your Campaign Vision is your Game Mastering Mission Statement — the reason you sit down every week.

- **Just getting by** → filling sessions but not getting anywhere
- **Firefighting/flailing** → keeping the game alive but not advancing toward a goal
- **Acting on whims** → fun moments but no meaningful progress

**Losing the plot = recognizing that your actions and your goals are no longer connected.** You had a dream you cared about and nothing you're doing has anything to do with it anymore.

## Angry's Concrete Examples

- Spent months "just getting by" during the Act I → Act II transition because he hadn't prepped the first MacGuffin dungeon
- Players went to the library instead of following the MacGuffin clues (firefighting moment)
- Didn't properly modify plans to accommodate the side trek (bad firefighting)
- Kept rewarding player engagement with political "second story" content while they were on a side quest he wanted to minimize (acting on whims)
- Campaign got stuck in an Endless Interlude
- Flailed by having an NPC spontaneously warn about an ambush he hadn't planned
- Flailed harder by sending the villain on a murder spree toward the library — essentially putting the final boss encounter before the first dungeon

**Result:** Complete mess. Fun session-to-session, but the campaign is stalled, structureless, and nothing like the vision.
