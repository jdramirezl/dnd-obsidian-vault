# What D&D 5E Does Right

## Context

A deliberately positive analysis of 5E's strengths, written as a critical thinking exercise: identifying the best aspects of things you dislike. Angry considers 5E a "players' game" — made for players (especially new ones), not for creative/tinkering GMs. It's a mixed bag with some things done better than any prior edition and some done worse.

## 1. Pick Up and Play (Approachability)

5E is the most approachable edition of D&D ever, and one of the most approachable RPGs made — **for players** (not GMs).

### The Universal Core Mechanic
- The d20 system (introduced in 3E, refined through editions) reaches its best form in 5E.
- Proficiency modifier is universal across weapons, skills, and tools.
- Better balance between training and ability modifiers.
- Fewer simultaneous modifiers to track.
- Saving throws simplified to just ability scores (no separate Reflex/Dexterity distinction).

### Advantage and Disadvantage
Two benefits:
1. **Simplifies adjudication** — less math, no stacking modifiers, easier for GMs to grant.
2. **Reduces modifier jockeying** — once you have advantage, no point piling more on. Conserves resources.

Weakness: Too many spells/abilities provide numerical bonuses that end-run around the system.

### Strong, Recognizable Archetypes
- Race + Class + **Background** (new in 5E) = three strong, easy-to-grasp archetypes.
- Best mix of core archetypes of any edition (wizard from OD&D, bard from 2E, sorcerer from 3E, warlock from 4E; dragonborn and tiefling kept from 4E).
- Most new players can understand "elf noble wizard" or "human soldier cleric" immediately.

### Two Starting Points
- **Level 1**: Great for new players or humble-beginnings campaigns.
- **Level 3**: End of apprenticeship — every class has signature abilities and a specific build. (Not called out in the rules, but intentional design.)

## 2. Progression Through the Game

### The Sweet Spot
5E hits its stride around level 3 and stays solid through single-digit to preteen levels. The designers **leaned into the sweet spot**:
- Levels 1-2 are rushed through quickly.
- Level 3+ progression slows, letting players linger in the best parts.
- Advancement rate evens out through the rest of the game.

### Encounter-Based Structure
5E leans into D&D's core structure: a string of encounters between players and a goal. It has shed everything that isn't about dealing with obstacles and resolving actions.

### Resource Management Balance
Short/long rests, hit dice, different recharge rates strike a good balance:
- Players **always have enough resources to win any given encounter** (barring stupidity).
- Signature resources make encounters easier but aren't necessary to win.
- Players are never in truly serious danger from a single encounter — they can recognize when to rest.

This solves two big adventure design problems:
- **The Mega Man Problem**: Balancing difficulty when players can tackle challenges in any order.
- **The Boss Fight Problem**: Uncertainty about party state when they reach climactic encounters.

## 3. Behind the Screen (For GMs)

### Gaps in the Rules ("Rulings Over Rules")
- 5E deliberately left rules vague (e.g., stealth) for interpretation and adjudication.
- Scaled back the exacting detail of 3E (exploitable) and the restrictiveness of 4E (chafing).
- Trusts the GM to use their brain — the d20 system already provides a universal resolution tool.

> "By the time 5E came out, I was starting to feel like the great minds behind D&D didn't trust me to run the game anymore."

### Monster and Encounter Building
- **The strongest and most satisfying custom content tools in any edition of D&D.**
- The underlying math is solid despite clumsy presentation and lack of polish.
- Building custom monsters for 5E is the most enjoyable of any edition Angry has run.
- The system errs on the side of easier encounters — this is **good design**:
  - Too-easy encounter → half-hour of lackluster but unnoticed gameplay.
  - Too-hard encounter → dead PC/party → ruined session or campaign.
  - Makes it less likely inexperienced GMs will ruin their games.

## Overall Assessment

5E trades **depth for approachability**. That's not good or bad — it's a tradeoff. Every design decision is a tradeoff. D&D's 50-year success means it does something right, and ignoring its strengths (especially if you dislike it) is foolish for any aspiring game designer.

> "There's a big difference between stuff you don't like and stuff that's actually bad or broken."
