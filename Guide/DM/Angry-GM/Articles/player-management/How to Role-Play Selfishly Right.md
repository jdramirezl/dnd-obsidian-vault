# How to Role-Play Selfishly Right

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-role-play-selfishly-right/)
**Date:** November 24, 2021
**Category:** Player Advice — Social Dynamics

---

## Selfish vs. Selfless: Definitions

**Selfish (Performative) Role-Playing:** Playing as if you're putting on a show. Creating a character with a rich backstory and complex personality, then presenting it to an audience of fellow players. Characters are static — they don't evolve in response to the game or other characters. Changes come from the player's unilateral decisions, not from play.

**Selfless (Interactive) Role-Playing:** Wanting to know the other characters at the table. Letting the world and other characters influence your creation. Starting with a simple blank-slate character and playing to discover who they really are. Showing interest in others so they show interest in you.

**Being selfish is fine.** Everyone plays for their own enjoyment — that's normal. But **playing Selfishly** is self-sabotaging. It doesn't even serve the self-interested gamer.

## Why RPGs Require Other People

RPGs aren't about experiencing stories, losing yourself in fantasies, or expressing creativity. You can do all that alone. RPGs are about doing those things **in a dangerous environment where anything can happen** — where you're not in control and something's really at stake.

That's why there are other players and dice. Role-playing gamers want their creative narrative fantasy challenges **without a safety net.**

But having other players means you must be likable enough that people want to share the table with you. Balance self-interest with being sociable enough to get invited back.

## How Social Media Changed the Rules

Research (NIH meta-analysis) shows social media has fundamentally changed social cognition:

- **Face-to-face:** People spend 30–40% of interaction time talking about themselves
- **Online:** People spend **80%** of interaction time talking about themselves
- People aged 18–35 spent three times as much of their formative years socializing online vs. face-to-face (pre-pandemic numbers)

**Online reciprocity is cheap** — click a like button, costs nothing. **Face-to-face reciprocity is expensive** — building real relationships takes time and investment.

Social media trains people toward large numbers of shallow connections. RPGs require small numbers of deep connections. Even played online, RPGs function like face-to-face interactions.

86% of 18–38 year-olds want to be content creators. Top motives: creativity, fame, self-expression, money, recognition. These are the same things cavemen wanted around the campfire. The desires aren't wrong — the execution matters.

## The Feedback Loop That Makes RPGs Work

RPGs depend on a constantly evolving feedback loop:
1. Your character does a thing
2. That changes the situation
3. Other characters/world respond
4. Situation changes again
5. Your character responds

Without this, it's five people telling five separate stories simultaneously. This loop requires **rich, deep interaction** — not monologues.

## How to Role-Play Selflessly (Even If You're Selfish)

**For players:**
- Drop the "make and portray" mindset. Start simple. Humble beginnings.
- Spend non-combat downtime **finding out about other PCs and the world.** Ask questions:
  - "Where'd you learn that trick?"
  - "Why do you wear that mask?"
  - "Who's that person you mentioned?"
- Before each session: list one thing you'd like to learn about each party member and one thing about the setting. Score yourself after.

**The reciprocity payoff:** When you interact with others, you invite them to interact with you. Players like players who show interest in them. They'll start asking about your character too. You get an audience — but one that's actually invested.

When someone shares something about their character, you get meaty stuff to **react to** in character. Your reactions reveal your own character. That's far better than delivering backstory monologues to a disengaged table.

**For GMs:**
- Show interest in player characters → trains players to show interest in the world and each other
- Identify Selfish Role-Players and have a private conversation using this framework
- If they won't change: they're wrecking the game and can either stop or stop playing

## The Core Truth

If spending time with you isn't pleasant, no one will spend time with you. RPGs are social activities. The rules of face-to-face reciprocity apply, regardless of whether the game is played online or in person.

**You get what you give.** Show interest in others → they show interest in you → deeper connections → better game for everyone, including you.
