# Office Hours — You Ain't a Behavioral Engineer

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Professor Angry's Office Hours: You Ain't a Behavioral Engineer](https://theangrygm.com/professor-angrys-office-hours-you-aint-a-behavioral-engineer/)
**Date:** October 18, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery (Office Hours)

---

## Summary

A corrective rant against the idea that GMs should use game mechanics, systems, and policies to manipulate player behavior. Your job is to create a space where a good game can grow — not to psychologically engineer your players. Player behavior is a player problem. Policies exist to flag issues in advance and minimize intervention, not to reward or punish. And through it all, don't forget to be a human being.

---

## A Space for Gaming (The Garden Metaphor)

- Your job as GM is to run a game for people who want to play. That's it.
- You prepare the garden: fence the space, aerate the soil, set up sprinklers.
- You can't force the plants (players) to grow in neat rows. The more stakes and cages you add, the uglier the garden and the unhappier the plants.
- **You cannot manage player behavior through game mechanics.** The more you try, the crappier and uglier the game becomes.
- Design mechanics and set policies to make a good game — not to drive behavior.

---

## Player Behavior Is a Player Problem

- Every player is responsible for their own behavior.
- Your responsibility is to **communicate** when a behavior creates a problem.
- Once communicated, it's the player's job to change, decide the game isn't worth changing for, or withdraw.
- You're not a psychologist, therapist, social engineer, teacher, or babysitter.

### What Policies Are Actually For
- **Not** for driving behavior or creating a social contract.
- **Not** for being fair and just.
- They're for **avoiding work**: flagging problem behaviors in advance so you hopefully don't have to intervene later.
- They mitigate the impact of minor, incidental problems because those are unavoidable and easier to work around than fight over.

### Conflict Resolution (Preview)
- Not about winning arguments or making players behave.
- About making interventions quick and painless — getting to "thanks for changing" or "get out of my house" with minimum emotional drama.

---

## You Can't Read Minds (And It Doesn't Matter)

- You can never know what's behind anyone's choices or behaviors. Reacting based on assumed motivations is a cognitive distortion called "Mind Reading."
- **Behaviors, actions, and outcomes are all that matter.** They're all you can judge and all you can address.

### Intentions Don't Change Outcomes
- Whether someone missed your game for a medical emergency or because they were gaming online, the session still got wrecked.
- A player with a valid excuse every time who still misses half the sessions is still a problem — the game can't function.
- Forgive trespasses, make room for compassion and compromise, but address the behavior and its impact on the game.

### Two Toxic Extremes
1. **Excusing everything** — Analyzing root psychological causes, refusing to set firm policies because "it's not their fault."
2. **Assuming malice** — Every absence is proof of disrespect, every problem is selfishness.
- Both are wrong. Focus on what people *do*, not what you think they *think*.

---

## Everything Ain't a Reward or a Punishment

- **Punishments and rewards are deliberate actions taken to modify behavior.** That's not what game mechanics do.
- Actions have outcomes, choices have consequences, causes have effects — those aren't rewards or punishments.
- If only characters who participate earn XP, that's not punishment for absence — it's how the system measures growth and accomplishment.
- GMs, Campaign Managers, and System Designers aren't in the business of modifying player behavior. That doesn't enter into design.

### The Danger of Reward/Punishment Thinking
- If you reduce everything to reward vs. punishment, you'll be too afraid to impose any consequence or outcome.
- You'll be unable to hold anyone accountable because everything feels like "punishing" them.
- Your games — and players — will become insufferable, and you'll quit without knowing why.

### The Accountability Conversation
> **GM:** You missed the last three sessions without contacting me…
> **Player:** I didn't *mean* to! It wasn't my *fault*! You can't *blame me*!

- Some people genuinely can't distinguish consequence from punishment, or accountability from blame.
- These conversations are inevitable and sometimes unresolvable. That's the price of running games.

---

## Don't Forget to Be a Human

- All this policy talk doesn't mean being a cold, unfeeling monster.
- Sometimes the right call is to break your own rules out of kindness.

### The Cancellation Example
- A climactic session is planned. A key player has an emergency and can't attend.
- By every written policy, the game can proceed without them.
- But Angry cancels anyway — because it's the kind thing to do. He wants everyone there for the big moment.
- He offers pizza and board games as a backup plan and tries to schedule an extra session.

### Finding the Middle Ground
- GMs need balance between assertiveness and kindness.
- If someone's causing problems, yank on their chain. If someone's willing to meet you partway, compromise.
- If following your written policy genuinely makes you sad, it's okay to make an exception.
- There should be room for forgiveness, second chances, favors, and adapting.

---

## Key Principles

1. **Don't use mechanics to drive behavior** — Use them to make a good game.
2. **Player behavior is a player problem** — Communicate the issue; they decide what to do.
3. **Judge actions, not intentions** — You can't read minds and don't need to.
4. **Consequences aren't punishments** — Stop using that word for everything.
5. **Be a human** — Rules serve the game, but kindness serves the people.
