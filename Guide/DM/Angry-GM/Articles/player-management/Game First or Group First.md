# Game First or Group First

**Source:** [Game First or Group First](https://theangrygm.com/game-first-or-group-first/)
**Series:** True Campaign Managery
**Date:** February 28, 2024

---

## Core Thesis

The "Second Choice" in starting a campaign — after choosing to run one at all — is whether the GM develops the Campaign Vision independently (Game First) or collaboratively with the group (Group First). Game First campaigns tend to succeed more often, but reality requires flexibility and negotiation.

---

## Starting a Campaign: The Choices

### Choice 1: Run a Campaign
- Not trivial. You're choosing to become "president and dictator for life of a social gaming club" with full responsibility for everyone's fun.

### The Campaign Vision
What game you plan to run. Encompasses:
- **Game system** (which also implies genre, setting, and often tone)
- **Campaign structure** (Adventure of the Week, Overarching Plot, Sandbox)
- **Content source** (published adventure, modules stitched together, homebrew)
- Can be as short as a module title or pages of prose

### Rules of the Game
- Variant rules, optional systems, house rules, allowed splatbooks
- Should be deliberate, conscious choices — not "whatever the book says"
- **Character Death** and **Character Advancement** always need special attention — the default rules are usually incomplete or inadequate

### Character Creation
- How involved is the GM? Do players create at home or together at the table?
- How much do players know about the campaign before making characters?
- Which optional builds, races, classes, feats are allowed?

### Clubhouse Rules
- Scheduling: when, where, how long, process for rescheduling/canceling
- Attendance and tardiness policies
- Communication channels and procedures

---

## The Second Choice: Game First vs. Group First

### Game First
- The GM develops the Campaign Vision (alone or with input) and presents it: "Here's what I'm running. You in?"
- Can still involve talking to players, getting a sense of preferences, and incorporating feedback.
- The defining feature: the GM makes the final call on the Vision.

### Group First
- The GM sits down with players and develops the Vision collaboratively (whiteboard session).
- Even so, the GM must still take ownership: "Here's what we built. Now I take full responsibility for it."

### It's a Spectrum, Not Binary
- Few GMs work at the extremes. Most do some back-and-forth.
- Game First GMs often poll players or offer choices between pre-selected options.
- Group First GMs still need a GM who owns the result.

---

## Why Game First Tends to Work Better

- Game First campaigns succeed more often than Group First campaigns.
- A GM who is invested in their own Vision runs a better game than one running something they don't care about.
- "Buy-In" from collaborative creation is a myth — it's shallow and fleeting compared to Investment that grows over time through good GMing.
- Players who help create the game are often selfish creators and not great at game design.

### The Buy-In Myth
- Buy-In is momentary interest from helping create something. It fades unless you keep bribing for more.
- Investment grows over time if you earn it through good play.
- Buy-In is a McDonald's Happy Meal toy. Don't be fooled.

---

## Practical Reality

### You Must Be Open to Negotiation
- Game First doesn't mean "my way or the highway."
- Your leverage depends on your situation:
  - Lots of willing players? You can be firm.
  - Small town, few gamers? You'll compromise more.
  - Established group with trust? More latitude over time.
- Know your starting position. Know what you can bend on and what you can't.

### Feedback Loops
- All the choices (Vision, Rules, Character Creation, Clubhouse Rules) affect each other.
- Scheduling constraints can scuttle a Vision. Character Death rules affect advancement. Attendance policies affect what kind of campaign is viable.
- Expect lots of bouncing around and revising before launch.

### Shifting Visions
- A strong Vision helps, but campaigns can grow beyond it.
- You can start Adventure of the Week and evolve into an Overarching Plot.
- You can start with a weak Vision or none at all. Sometimes the biggest risks lead to the best payoffs.

---

## Pre-Solving Problems

- Most problems (especially social ones) shouldn't be pre-solved. It wastes energy and often makes things worse.
- X-cards, trigger warnings, lines and veils — Angry considers these not only worthless but counterproductive. Better to learn to manage the group and coach through problems as they arise.
- **Exception:** Some problems are so ubiquitous, destabilizing, and easy to prepare for that they're worth pre-solving. Character Death is the prime example.
- Knowing which problems to pre-solve and which to handle in the moment is part of the art.

---

## Summary

Decide whether you're building the game first and inviting players, or building it together. Game First tends to produce better campaigns because the GM is invested in their own vision. But be realistic — negotiate, compromise, and adapt to your situation. All the startup choices (vision, rules, characters, clubhouse rules) interact and loop back on each other. There's no single right way, but there are lots of wrong ways, and success depends more on execution than method.
