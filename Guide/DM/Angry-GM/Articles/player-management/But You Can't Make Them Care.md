# But You Can't Make Them Care

**Source:** [The Angry GM – …But You Can't Make Them Care](https://theangrygm.com/but-you-cant-make-them-care/)
**Date:** April 29, 2020
**Series:** Make Players Care

---

## Summary

An introductory article on emotional investment in TTRPGs — what it is, why it matters, why it's risky, and the general strategy for cultivating it. Emotional investment means players have real emotional responses to fictional events, which deepens roleplaying, raises stakes, and leads to more human (less coldly strategic) decision-making. But it's not for everyone, it can't be forced, and it can backfire. The general approach: fill your world with things people are *likely* to care about, watch for signs of attachment, then cultivate it.

---

## What Is Emotional Investment?

- Replace "caring" with **emotional investment**: players have emotional responses to fictional people, places, things, and events as if they were real.
- The brain's supramarginal gyrus gets tricked into forgetting it's pretend — you project yourself or loved ones onto characters and feel real emotions.
- Examples from Harry Potter: loving Harry, hating Umbridge, feeling triumph when Voldemort dies, or engaging with deeper themes (the "greater good" corrupting well-meaning people, systemic inequality among sentient creatures).

---

## Why Emotional Investment Matters

### Raises Stakes and Deepens Agency
- When players feel emotions, there's more riding on choices than whether a character sheet gets replaced.
- Increased stakes → increased sense of agency.
- Failure carries emotional weight, creating a sense of responsibility.

### Produces More Human Choices
- RPGs are played at emotional distance — it's easy to make cold, strategic, "correct" decisions from 30,000 feet.
- Emotional investment makes it harder to sacrifice a few hundred lives for the greater good when you have to face the sobbing mother.
- It makes it harder to leave a dying ally screaming while you optimize combat actions.
- **Human beings are emotional creatures.** Anything that gets players to feel real emotions improves the roleplaying experience.

### The Warlock Example
- A player's warlock had befriended an abused kid NPC early in the campaign.
- In the Realm of the Dead, the warlock encountered a shade of the kid — an illusion born from guilt and fear.
- The shade told him the kid couldn't stop thinking about the nightmare snake living in the warlock's head. The kid was terrified. The warlock might be *contagious*.
- The player could have walked away (the logical move). Instead, because he *cared* about the NPC, he engaged — and felt like a monstrous, Faustian warlock who'd paid more than he intended.
- Without emotional investment, it would have been just Wisdom saves and psychic damage.

---

## The Risks and Downsides

### Not Everyone Wants It
- Players showed up for fantasy-action-adventure, not emotional drama. Some just want to fight dragons and map dungeons.
- Emotional investment is optional. It doesn't work for every game or every player.

### Emotional Exhaustion
- Emotions burn mental energy. Improperly paced, emotional weight exhausts players.
- *Final Fantasy X* example: the tone never lightened up — every twist made things worse. It became depressing rather than engaging.
- Some players will quit if the emotional weight is heavier or more sustained than expected.

### Unpredictable Reactions
- Once you flip someone's emotional switch, you may get more than you bargained for.
- Some people can't control their emotions. Some have experiences that make certain emotions disproportionately powerful.
- No consent form can cover every possible trigger.
- If players are emotionally mature, you can talk through issues. If they're not, it's a landmine.

---

## Everyone Cares Differently

- Some players attach to any NPC immediately; others need time; others never care about characters but care deeply about themes, causes, or villains.
- Every player cares about different things at different levels for different reasons.
- You can't get people emotionally invested in "the game as a whole" — only in **specific elements**: people, places, things, events, ideas.

---

## The General Strategy

1. **Understand what most people are likely to care about** — what qualities make things likable, hateable, or otherwise "emotionable."
2. **Liberally sprinkle your game with those things.** Fill the world with elements that have care-worthy qualities.
3. **Watch your players.** Notice the moment they show signs of emotional investment in something.
4. **Cultivate and reinforce it.** Once a player latches onto something, nurture that connection.
5. **Then you can start to mess with them.**

> You can't make people care. You can only fill your world with things they *want* to care about and trust them to do the rest.
