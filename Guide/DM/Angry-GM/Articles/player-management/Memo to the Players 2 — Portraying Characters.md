# Memo to the Players #2 — Portraying Characters

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Memo to the Players #2: Portraying Characters](https://theangrygm.com/memo-to-the-players-2/)
**Date:** October 27, 2021
**Series:** Memo to the Players / Up Your GMing Level

---

## Summary

A two-part memo (one for players, one for GMs) on how to portray characters through play. The core thesis: role-playing is about making choices and taking action, not acting, performing, or psychoanalyzing a character. Characters are revealed through gameplay, not backstories. Start with a one-sentence prompt, refer to it often, and let the character grow organically.

---

## For Players: Action, Not Acting

### What Role-Playing Actually Is
- Role-playing is entirely about **making choices and taking action**.
- It's not speaking in character, pretending to be someone else, or putting on a show.
- "It's not who your character is; it's what they do that defines them."

### The Relationship with Your Character
- Your character is like your favorite character in a Netflix series — the longer you spend with them, the more you care.
- The relationship needs time to develop, room to grow, and room to surprise you.
- Everything that happens before the game starts is something you won't experience at the table.

### The Prompt Method

#### Step 1: Come Up with a Prompt
- One short phrase with room for one or two ideas: a motivation, a simple trait, a defining moment.
- Example: *"Bathar is a dishonored ex-soldier trying to redeem himself."*

#### Step 2: Write It Down
- Put it somewhere visible on your character sheet.

#### Step 3: Refer Back to It
- Read it at the start of every session.
- Read it whenever something big happens or you don't know what to do.

#### Step 4: Trust Your Character
- Don't analyze, explain, or change the prompt. Don't write stories about the character.
- Just refer to the prompt and do whatever feels right.
- Gradually, the character grows in your head and starts making choices through you.

### Harsh Truths
1. **No plan survives contact with an RPG** — You can't control events, other players, or perceptions. Your 30-page character analysis is useless after two hours.
2. **No one cares but you** — Everyone is focused on their own character. You're not putting on a show.
3. **Understanding comes after action** — People act first; reasons come later. Neuroscience confirms this. Give your character space and they'll tell you how to act.

## For GMs: Nurturing True Role-Playing

### Encourage the Right Attitude
- During character generation, focus on mechanical choices only.
- Push players to trust their guts and not sweat backstory justifications.
- Show excitement about discovering characters through play: *"I can't wait to find out why your character is so good with animals."*

### Discourage Make-and-Portray
- Discourage complex backstories and pre-existing PC relationships at creation.
- If someone tries to hand you a written backstory, refuse it — say you want to be surprised during gameplay.
- During play, interrupt monologues about thoughts/motivations/history. Ask for actions instead.

### Require Prompts
- Demand a single short sentence. If it's more, hand it back.
- Most prompts naturally include a motivation. If one doesn't, ask the player to add one.

### Nurture Growth
- Early sessions: keep interactions and choices simple. Reinforce each character's prompt and motivation.
- Over time, use in-game events and NPC interactions to foster emerging personality traits.
- If players shy away from character growth, back off — don't push.
- Think of the characters as shy favorites in a Netflix series you want to get to know.

---

## Motivation Required

- Every character needs a motivation — not a goal (goals come and go), but the underlying value that drives all goals.
- Sample motivations: Amass Wealth, Earn Glory, Gain Respect, Accrue Power, Discover the Truth, Do Good, Help Others, Acquire Knowledge, Fulfill a Destiny, Satisfy Wanderlust, Mete-Out Justice, Take Revenge, Test Your Mettle, Become a Master

---

## On Tropes

- Learn to love tropes, clichés, and stereotypes — they're storytelling seeds that resonate deeply.
- Tropes grow well in human brains. Use them with loving respect.
- "Nothing grows from a deconstructed seed."
