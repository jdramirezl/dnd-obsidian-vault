# Dear Players: There's a Better Way to Play

**Source:** [Dear Players: There's a Better Way to Play](https://theangrygm.com/dear-players-a-better-way/)
**Date:** March 27, 2019

---

## Core Thesis

A guide written *for players* (GMs can print and hand it out) covering the fundamental misunderstandings that make players difficult to run for: gonzo behavior, dice-first thinking, vague action declarations, rules-lawyering from the player side, treating the game as a "storytelling experience," and broadcasting character thoughts.

---

## Key Concepts

### Playing a Role
- Role-playing means adopting a persona and pretending the situation is real.
- Complete the sentence: "If this situation were real and I was my character, I would..."
- Don't complete: "Wouldn't it be really funny if my character..."
- Gonzo, silly, or tone-breaking actions short-circuit the GM's brain and ruin the experience for everyone else at the table.
- One silly player at an otherwise serious table gives the GM an ulcer.

### Brains Before Dice
- The GM decides if an action can succeed or fail *before* any dice are rolled.
- If it can't succeed, no roll will make it work. If it can't fail, no roll is needed.
- **Don't roll dice until the GM asks you to.** The dice don't have magical powers to change reality.
- A natural 20 on a skill check doesn't mean automatic success (in most D&D editions, nat 1/nat 20 only matter on attack rolls).

### Dual Wielding Detail and Clarity
- The two things every GM needs to hear: **WHAT** is your character trying to accomplish, and **HOW** are they trying to accomplish it.
- "I persuade the guard" is vague and unhelpful. "I convince the guard the king is in danger and we're here to help" gives the GM something to work with.
- Detail and clarity determine whether you even get to roll, and what happens based on that roll.

### Don't Think About the Rules
- A well-trained GM doesn't think about rules until their brain has evaluated the situation first.
- Players should think the same way. You know what your character is good at without referencing specific skills.
- Don't say "I make an Intimidate check." Say what your character does. The GM picks the mechanic.
- Naming specific skills locks you out of options. Saying "do I recognize this symbol?" lets the GM route it to the right skill (Religion, Arcana, History, etc.).
- Some GMs like to apply different ability modifiers to skill checks situationally (e.g., Strength-based Intimidation). They can't do that if you dictate the rules.

### It's Not a Storytelling Game
- RPGs are not "collaborative storytelling experiences." They are games where you take on a role, make choices, and deal with consequences.
- The story is **emergent** — it comes out of play, not from players trying to craft it.
- Players who deliberately screw things up "for the story" or seek failure because "it's more dramatic" are forcing the narrative and ruining what makes RPG stories great.
- Stories are about people and choices. If your character doesn't behave like a person, the story sucks.
- Making decisions based on anything other than what the character would do wrecks the story for everyone.

### Stop Broadcasting Your Character's Thoughts
- Don't narrate your character's internal monologue, motivations, or backstory to the table.
- Other players (and the GM) learn about your character through **actions and choices**, not exposition dumps.
- Broadcasting thoughts robs everyone of the fun of discovery and makes future revelatory scenes pointless.
- The Snape example: if we knew Snape's full motivations from Chapter 1, the entire series loses its power.
- Show, don't tell. Let your character emerge gradually through play. Use subtlety.

---

## The Action Declaration Formula

When it's your turn to act:
1. **Think about the situation** as if it were real.
2. **Think about your character** and what they would do.
3. **Describe what your character does** — clearly, with detail.
4. **Wait for the GM** to tell you what happens and whether dice are needed.

Don't: grab dice first, name a skill, announce a roll, or narrate your character's inner thoughts.

---

## Summary

Play your character as a person in a real situation. Be clear and detailed about what you're doing and why. Let the GM handle the rules. Don't try to craft a story — let it emerge from genuine choices. And for the love of all that is holy, stop telling everyone what your character is thinking.
