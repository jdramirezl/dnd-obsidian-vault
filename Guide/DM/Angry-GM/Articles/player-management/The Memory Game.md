# The Memory Game

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Memory Game](https://theangrygm.com/the-memory-game/)
**Date:** January 16, 2013
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## Summary

An alternative to traditional backstories: replace pages of narrative with a small set of **memories** — short, impactful vignettes from a character's life. Each memory is a brief event description plus a sentence about its lasting effect on the character. Easier to write, easier to remember, easier to use in play, and more likely to actually come up at the table.

---

## The Problem with Backstories

- Most backstory content is **useless filler** that will never matter in play. The important bits are the ones that explain a skill, personality trait, motivation, or goal.
- Backstory is the part of a character's story that **isn't interesting enough to play at the table.** If it were exciting, you'd play it instead of writing it.
- Good books and movies don't dump backstory upfront — they weave important bits into the narrative when needed.
- Extensive backstories **remove a unique RPG element**: characters are most strongly defined through interaction, and backstories are written without interaction.
- Players get emotionally attached to detailed backstories and resist changing them — even when the character doesn't fit the group.
- Writing a backstory is "the RPG equivalent of narrative masturbation."

---

## What a Memory Is

A memory is a **short vignette** (≤5 sentences) from a character's life that had a lasting effect:

1. **First line:** When the event happened.
2. **Middle:** Brief narrative of what occurred.
3. **Last line:** How the memory affected the character — what lasting impression it left.

### Examples

> *"When Alena was fifteen, her brother Kiefer was killed by goblins during a raid on her village. She insisted on joining the militia, over her parents' objections. Many townsfellows died, but they prevailed. This left Alena tenacious and coldly desensitized to death."*

> *"During Kiryl's apprenticeship to the wizard Borya, Kiryl would cut corners out of laziness. The wizard used pain and fire to discipline students. Kiryl never gained a work ethic, but got much better at making excuses. This left Kiryl with scarred hands and the quick wit of a creative liar."*

> *"When Mara was little, she became deathly ill with a terrible fever. Her father entertained her with stories of great heroes. The fever passed but left scars in her mind. This left Mara with a poor memory and migraines from brain damage, but a strong bond with her father and a starry-eyed view of adventuring."*

---

## What Makes a Good Memory

- Explains things the character sheet can't: personality traits, unusual skills, motivations, goals.
- Fills in details like class origin, training, or who trained them.
- Leads to interesting questions and fuels role-playing at the table.
- **A memory that doesn't shape the character is useless.** The lasting impact is mandatory.

---

## Advantages Over Traditional Backstories

- **Easier to write.** Specific events are simpler than whole-cloth narratives.
- **Easier to remember.** Both player and GM can recall a few key events vs. pages of prose.
- **Easier to skim for plot hooks.**
- **Consistent across players.** Avoids the problem of one player bringing 30 pages while another brings a paragraph.
- **More likely to come up in play.** Each memory defines a lasting impact, making it naturally relevant.

---

## Required Memories (GM Tool)

The GM can require specific types of memories to get useful material:

| GM Need | Required Memory |
|---|---|
| Recent adventure context | A memory of a past adventure |
| Class/training background | An event during class training or education |
| NPC connections | A memory involving a close friend, relative, or rival who is still alive |
| Party relationships | A memory of how the character met another party member |
| Campaign-specific hooks | A memory involving a specific place, organization, or event |
| Campaign starting situation | A memory explaining how the character ended up in the starting scenario |

**Rule of thumb:** Don't require more than half the memories. Leave the rest for the player to explore freely.

---

## Key Principles

- **Backstories are for the GM too.** Required memories ensure the GM gets useful material, not just player self-expression.
- **Focus on impact, not narrative.** The lasting effect on the character is what matters.
- **Players can fill in details during play.** The bare-bones memory is a seed; the full story emerges at the table through interaction.
- **This is an alternative, not a replacement.** Backstories have value too. It's a tradeoff.
