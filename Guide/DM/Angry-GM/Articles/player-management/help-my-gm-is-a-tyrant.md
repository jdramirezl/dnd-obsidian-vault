# Help! My GM is a Tyrant!

## Key Advice

The GM-player relationship is an asymmetric **host-and-guest dynamic**, not a democracy. The GM invests disproportionate effort to make the game happen, and that asymmetry shapes how conflicts must be navigated. Neither side should issue ultimatums — but if communication breaks down, the player's only real options are to compromise, leave, or run their own game.

## Summary

Angry addresses a real conflict between a GM (Allan) and his players who felt "held hostage" by a tyrannical GM. The article establishes foundational social truths: gaming is voluntary, low-stakes, and asymmetric. Using the **Vegan at a Cookout** analogy, Angry explains that the GM (host) controls the supply — there are 4x more players than GMs. Players who threaten to quit are using a losing negotiation tactic. The correct approach is adult conversation with willingness to compromise, recognizing the GM's disproportionate effort. If that fails, the player's only options are to accept the game as-is, leave, find a new game, run their own game, or find a new hobby. GMs, meanwhile, should not be tyrants — they have a responsibility to please their guests and should never run a game they aren't proud of.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Recognize the asymmetry** — The GM invests far more effort than players. Treat the invitation to play as a gift, not an entitlement.
2. **Bring gratitude to the table** — Acknowledge the GM's work. Trust that they are trying to make the game fun. Be open to new experiences.
3. **Never issue ultimatums** — "Do what we want or we quit" closes all lines of communication. It's a losing strategy for the guest.
4. **Talk it out like adults** — If unhappy, approach the GM privately with willingness to compromise. Accept you may need to bend more than the GM.
5. **Accept the Peacemaker's Burden** — Whoever initiates conflict resolution must tolerate some flak with grace.
6. **Know when to walk away** — If the game isn't fun and communication has failed, quit gracefully. Don't stay and be miserable.
7. **Consider running your own game** — If no GM meets your needs, become the host yourself.

> [!tip] The Vegan at a Cookout Rule
> You can't control the menu at someone else's cookout. If you don't like what's being served, you can politely request changes, bring your own dish, or skip the event — but you can't demand the host change the entire meal for you. The same applies to RPGs: the GM sets the game, and players negotiate from a position of less leverage. Gratitude, trust, and patience are your best tools.
