# Coping with Loss

**Source:** [Coping with Loss](https://theangrygm.com/coping-with-loss/)
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building
**Date:** July 17, 2019

---

## Core Thesis

A satisfying game must have a goal the players can win or lose. D&D handles loss terribly — the only failure state is death, and most adventures are inevitable victories until someone gets unlucky. GMs must design better failure conditions using **Macrostructures** and **Bright Lines**.

---

## Key Concepts

### The Adventure Is the Game
- The adventure (not the encounter, not the campaign) is the fundamental unit of "game" in an RPG.
- **Encounters** are mini-games that inherit motivation from the adventure. Most lack standalone motivation.
- **Campaigns** are optional strings of games. A one-shot adventure is a complete, satisfying experience.
- The adventure must have everything a game needs: goal, motivation, the possibility of winning or losing.

### The Problem with D&D's Loss Model
- Most D&D adventures are obstacle courses: overcome challenges sequentially, win if you survive.
- The only way to lose is to die. 99% of the time, winning is inevitable through persistence.
- D&D doesn't know what to do with retreat, partial failure, or losing encounters without dying.
- Designers have made death increasingly unlikely, making most adventures just "inevitable victories until the odds catch up."

### Why Bad Losses Suck
Using the example of an unbreakable vault door:
1. **Unearned loss:** The party didn't do anything wrong. The loss had nothing to do with their choices.
2. **Isolated failure:** The final challenge was disconnected from everything before it. Nothing the party did in earlier encounters mattered.
3. **The adventure that won't end:** Players will never voluntarily admit defeat. They'll keep trying increasingly outlandish solutions. The GM has to declare "you lost" — and most GMs won't.

### Why Boss Fights Work as Final Challenges
- D&D is a resource attrition game. Every fight builds on the ones before.
- If you lose the boss fight, you can look back and say "if I hadn't blown all my spell slots earlier..."
- Resource attrition is the only thing that persists meaningfully from encounter to encounter.

## Macrostructures

A **Macrostructure** is a game mechanic that sits above the whole adventure, tracking progress toward success and failure states outside of individual encounters.

### Examples
- Pursuing squad tracker (how close are the enemies?)
- Town council vote tracker (how many councilors support you?)
- Countdown timer (the sacrifice happens at midnight)

### Properties of Good Macrostructures
- The party can affect the Macrostructure through choices both inside and outside encounters.
- Simple and easy to adjust/fudge when players do something unexpected.
- Creates a feedback loop: Macrostructure state affects encounters, encounter outcomes affect the Macrostructure.

---

## Bright Lines

A **Bright Line** is an unmistakable, obvious loss condition — a clear "game over" that everyone can recognize.

### Examples
- Time runs out (sacrifice at midnight, army arrives in one week)
- Protected NPC dies or flees
- Treasure is destroyed or stolen
- Patron gets fed up and fires the party
- Town turns against the PCs and exiles them
- Someone else beats the party to the goal
- A plague spreads too far to contain

### Requirements for Good Bright Lines
- The party must know (or be able to discover) that a failure state exists.
- They should be able to assess progress toward failure.
- They should have ideas for how to slow the slide toward failure.
- The failure shouldn't catch them by surprise — but they don't need to know all the details.

---

## Does Every Adventure Need This?

No. Some adventures are just obstacle courses with a prize at the end — traditional dungeon crawls. Those are "okay" adventures. Fine for casual play.

But if you want to go beyond beer-and-pretzels gameplay — story-driven adventures, campaigns with weight — you must handle loss better than "keep trying until you die." Especially if you're afraid to kill PCs, you need to write better failures into your game.

**It must be a conscious choice.** Sit down and decide: "Is this adventure just survive-to-win, or does it have meaningful failure?" Once you say that to yourself enough times, you start wanting more.

---

## Summary

Design adventures with failure states beyond death. Use Macrostructures to track progress toward success and failure across the whole adventure. Use Bright Lines to give players clear, recognizable loss conditions. Make losses earned — connected to player choices and built on what came before. The party should be able to see failure coming and have agency in preventing it.
