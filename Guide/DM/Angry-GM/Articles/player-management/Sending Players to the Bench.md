# Sending Players to the Bench

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Sending Players to the Bench](https://theangrygm.com/sending-players-to-the-bench/)
**Date:** August 12, 2020
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## Summary

A comprehensive guide to handling sidelined players — whether from absent players, split parties, or character death. The core message: decide your policies *before* the game starts, communicate them to players, and understand that every solution involves tradeoffs between three real issues. Most GMs waste energy on non-issues instead.

---

## The Three Non-Issues (Ignore These)

1. **"Should sidelining happen?"** — Yes. It's unavoidable. Trying to prevent it removes agency and open-endedness from the game.
2. **"It sucks to be out of the game."** — True, but suckage isn't a measure of what should happen. Players should be able to quietly spectate for 15–20 minutes. That's kindergarten-level social skill.
3. **"What about metagaming?"** — Play split-party scenes in front of everyone. Trust players to handle it like adults. Muddy player/character knowledge doesn't matter.

---

## The Three Real Issues

### 1. Spectator Time
- Players can reasonably spectate for 15–20 minutes. Not an hour. Not a session.
- They signed up to play, not to watch.

### 2. Continuity and Consistency
- Characters disappearing or reappearing without explanation breaks suspension of disbelief.
- Consistency is one of the three pillars of what makes an RPG an RPG (alongside agency and a third from Angry's book).
- Contrivances to explain absences must not strain credulity.

### 3. Lost Resources and Skills
- A missing character means the party loses access to their skills, abilities, and equipment.
- In balance-dependent games like D&D, this can make challenges impossible.

### The Core Tradeoff
- Solving one issue usually makes another worse. Every policy is a compromise based on your table's priorities.

## Scenario 1: Absent Players

### Attendance Policy
- Set attendance and cancellation expectations at game start. Ask for advance warning.
- Angry's rule: never run for fewer than 3 players or fewer than 2/3 of the group.
- Players who miss too many games — even with good reasons — should be asked to withdraw.

### Handling the Absent Character
**Option A — Remove from play:**
- If the party is somewhere a character can leave (town), invent a personal reason (duty, obligation, jail sentence) consistent with the character.
- Hand off vital resources to the party before departure.
- No permanent repercussions from the absence excuse.

**Option B — Background the character:**
- Character follows along quietly, having a bad day. Strains credulity less than disappearing.

**Option C — Someone else drives:**
- A volunteer (or the GM) controls the character in combat and skill checks.
- The character stays quiet otherwise. No role-playing the absent player's character.
- GM vetoes any action the absent player wouldn't approve of.

### Angry's Default Policy
1. Get the character out of the party if at all reasonable.
2. If not possible, ask for a volunteer driver. GM drives if no volunteer.
3. The character participates mechanically but stays quiet narratively.
4. The moment the game reaches a point where the character can leave, write them out.
5. If the party does something that would strand the absent character, write them back in temporarily.

---

## Scenario 2: Split Parties and Personal Scenes

- Splitting the party is fine. It makes sense in many situations and gives players spotlight time.
- **No special policy needed** unless one player hogs too much solo time or becomes a lone wolf.

### Angry's Approach
- Use TV-show pacing: A-Plot / B-Plot. Cut between groups every 10–15 minutes, always at the most exciting moment.
- If a player spends too much time alone, stop playing out their scenes and summarize instead. The camera follows the party.

## Scenario 3: Character Death (and Permanent Removal)

This covers anything that removes a character permanently or semi-permanently: death, petrification, permanent insanity, crippling injury, alignment violations, extended training absences.

### The Two Basic Solutions

1. **Make it reversible:** Resurrection, restoration spells, etc. But D&D's reversals are usually costly and time-consuming, which doesn't actually solve the spectator-time problem.

2. **Allow replacement characters:** Creates massive continuity issues — party cohesion, trust, established relationships all need to be fast-tracked.

### Common GM Mistakes
- Obsessing over making the replacement exactly equal in power to the dead character.
- Agonizing over whether death should carry a penalty vs. being fair.
- These are tiny problems. Do whatever's most expedient.

### Angry's Default Policy (Serious Campaigns)
- **Death is generally not reversible.** Bringing back the dead is mythically difficult.
- **Continuity and party cohesion take high priority.** No stupid contrivances to rush a character back.
- **Players are warned upfront:** if your character dies, you will probably miss at least one full session. The current session may end right there.

### What Happens When a Character Dies
1. Once the immediate danger passes, the game stops for a group discussion.
2. Everyone talks about what to do: head back to town? Press on? How to get the player back in?
3. The dead player's downtime is limited to one session maximum (unless they volunteer for more).
4. **Temporary NPCs** can bridge the gap — the player runs an NPC until a proper replacement can be introduced.
5. The GM forces a compromise if needed. The dead player might settle for a temp character. The survivors might be forced to return to town.
6. Whatever solution emerges, it's a compromise. Like adults.

### The Threat as Motivation
- Knowing you might miss a session if your character dies makes players value their characters' lives more.

---

## Key Principles

- **Set policies before the game starts.** Don't get blindsided. Tell players what to expect so they can find another table if they disagree.
- **Every solution is a tradeoff.** Pick the least painful pain that works for your table.
- **Death should be big and disruptive.** It's rare and extraordinary. It should majorly disrupt the game — that's appropriate.
- **Different tables have different priorities.** There are no universally right answers. Each group decides what matters most.
