# Scheduling and Attendance (Part II)

**Source:** [Clubhouse Rules: Scheduling and Attendance (Part II)](https://theangrygm.com/scheduling-and-attendance-ii/)
**Series:** True Campaign Managery
**Date:** July 22, 2024

---

## Core Thesis

Scheduling enforcement is about discretion, not rules. The GM must judge when attendance issues cross from "shit happens" to "this is gonna kill the game" — and adapt the campaign to the reliability they actually have, not the reliability they wish they had.

---

## Key Concepts

### Responsibility vs. Fault
- People conflate responsibility with blame. Your lateness affects others regardless of whose fault it is.
- Apologize for impact even when it's not your fault. Accountability ≠ guilt.
- GMs are responsible for everything at their table, even things beyond their control.

### The Campaign Manager's Discretion
- Everything in your policy is subject to your personal judgment — not math, not rigid rules.
- Two players can miss the same number of games and only one causes a real problem.
- Trying to codify limits and punishments will break things worse. Use good judgment instead.

### The Gold Standard for Reliability
- Weekly, four-hour sessions with the same group, no more than one absence every 3–4 weeks, no more than one cancellation every couple months.
- The further you deviate, the more you must adjust what you run.

---

## Adjustments for Unreliable Schedules

### Recaps and Reintroductions
- Do a live start-of-session recap every session. Written recaps between sessions are not enough on their own.
- The more unreliable the schedule, the more critical this becomes — even though it "wastes" 15 minutes.

### Pre-Game Social Time
- Players need 15 minutes of social time before the game starts, or they'll socialize during the game.
- Longer gaps between sessions = more social buildup. Budget for it.

### Wasted Table Time
- Less game time = more precious every minute. Master pacing.
- Quality over quantity: "It's better to game well than game lots."

### System and Option Selection
- **Familiar systems:** Unreliable games need systems everyone already knows. Otherwise, players re-learn rules every session.
- **Efficient systems:** Avoid time-sink mechanics (summoned creatures, crafting, complex subsystems).
- **Cut non-adventuring fluff:** Shopping, downtime, random travel encounters — cut them if time is scarce.

### Character-Driven Games Don't Work
- Unreliable schedules kill inter-party investment. Players can't stay invested in each other's characters without regular interaction.
- If a session is built around one character's arc and that player bails, you have a problem.

### Plot Limitations
- Complex mysteries, intrigue, and informational setups require strong continuity that unreliable schedules can't support.
- Best bet for unreliable games: episodic, quest-driven adventures in a system everyone knows.

## Tools of the Trade

### Emergency Contact Info
- Exchange cell numbers (or equivalent) with every player for same-day emergencies.
- This is separate from your group chat. SMS or equivalent that gets immediate attention.

### Campaign Chat Channel
- Permanent private group chat (Discord, Messenger, etc.) for non-emergency communication.
- Name it after the campaign. Keep it focused on the game — if it gets cluttered with off-topic chat, people stop paying attention.

### Reminders and Announcements
- Send session reminders 3–5 days before every game.
- Make official announcements (schedule changes, decisions) look official — bold text, uniform format, pinged/flagged.

### Emergency vs. Non-Emergency
- Emergency = same-day changes. Use direct contact (text message).
- Non-emergency = anything with advance notice. Use the group chat.
- Forcing emergency-level communication for last-minute changes makes people think twice about bailing.

### Polling Tools
- [When2Meet](https://www.when2meet.com/) — free, no account needed, weekly or 4-week availability.
- [WhenAvailable](https://whenavailable.com/) — free, requires email account, cleaner interface, works beyond 4 weeks.

## Having "The Talk"

### Expectations
- You cannot make someone understand, agree, care, or change. You can only make them aware of the problem.
- Go in knowing your only goal is to identify the problem and listen. Not to convince or debate.

### Attribution Bias and the Principle of Charity
- We blame our own bad behavior on external factors and others' bad behavior on internal motivations.
- Always assume the best of people's intentions. Focus on behaviors, not motives.

### How to Do It
1. **Never by text.** Voice-to-voice or face-to-face.
2. **Don't give them time to dread it.** Use the "Good Night Everyone; Oh Hey, Hol' Up a Sec" gambit — grab them casually at the end of a session.
3. **Be direct.** One sentence, two clauses: "You've missed X of the last Y games; that's made it hard for me to [specific impact]."
4. **Stick to facts.** What happened + how it impacted you and the game. No speculation on motives.
5. **Shut up and listen.** Really listen. Repeat what you hear. Don't argue or defend.
6. **If they offer a resolution:** thank them and move on.
7. **If they don't:** reiterate it's causing difficulty, say you wanted them aware, and end it.

### After The Talk
- Even without a promise, players often slowly adjust behavior once aware.
- Acknowledge and thank any effort toward correction, even if slow or partial.
- If things remain a problem: escalate to Conflict Resolution (separate topic).

---

## Summary

A Scheduling and Attendance Policy ensures everyone agrees on what commitment means, treats game time as precious, and respects each other's time. It covers scheduling procedures, cancellation conditions, minimum player counts, absence/tardiness handling, and communication channels. The GM enforces it through discretion, not rigid rules. When the schedule can't support certain game types, the GM adapts what they run rather than fighting reality.
