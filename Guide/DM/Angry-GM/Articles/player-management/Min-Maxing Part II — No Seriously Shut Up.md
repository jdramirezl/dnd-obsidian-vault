# Min-Maxing Part II — No, Seriously, Shut Up

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/just-shut-up-about-min-maxing-part-ii/)
**Date:** June 5, 2025
**Category:** Player Management — Optimization Debate

---

## The Min-Maxing Argument Summarized

"Min-maxer" is a gaming slur. The polite term is **optimizer.** Min-maxers build characters to be as mechanically effective as possible. That's the entire "crime."

**The min-maxing argument:** Players who treat character creation as a challenge to create the most effective character possible are playing RPGs wrong, ruining games for everyone, and must be stopped.

**The xam-nimming argument (the opposite):** Players who treat character creation purely as creative self-expression create ineffective characters that sabotage the party. They're playing wrong and ruining games for everyone.

Both sides claim one extreme is good and the other is evil. Both are equally stupid.

## Why the Whole Argument Is Stupid

Both arguments are predicated on:
1. A giant population of players taking the most extreme approaches possible
2. It being easy to create a character so powerful or so incompetent that it destroys the game for six people

Both premises are **broadly and utterly ridiculous generalizations.** Extreme behaviors exist and should be addressed when they arise — but the argument isn't about addressing extremes. It's about claiming that an entire *approach* is invalid and can't be tolerated.

## Your Preferences Bias What You See as "Extreme"

If you're not into challenge, your sense of what counts as "extreme optimization" is skewed. A feat combination you read about online might seem "broken" to you even though it's perfectly reasonable to a challenge-oriented player.

> **"The extremes aren't the points too far from the reasonable middle ground — they're just the points too far from you. If you're off-center to begin with, the extremes shift with you."**

Your preferences bias you even if you're aware they're preferences and even if you try to be tolerant. That's how brains work.

## Player Motivation Models Explain the Real Issue

Different players have different motivation profiles (see [[Min-Maxing Part I — Understanding Player Motivations]]). The min-maxing argument is really just people saying **"any motives and preferences other than mine are invalid."**

- Anti-min-maxers: "Treating the game as a challenge is wrong"
- Anti-xam-nimmers: "Treating the game as self-expression is wrong"

Both are just taking their own motivation mix and treating it as the objective definition of RPGs. A GM who insists RPGs are about one specific thing is just projecting their preferences.

## What the GM Should Actually Do

### Have No Stance on Min-Maxing

The GM should **not** impose rules, set policies, or make judgments about the right and wrong ways to make a character. If a player makes a legal character using allowed options, they're doing it right. Motives don't matter. The GM should not judge the result against their personal preferences.

### Address Actual Problems When They Actually Arise

If one player's character consistently dominates every encounter before anyone else gets a turn — that's a real problem. If one player's character is dead every fight and the party spends all resources carrying them — that's a real problem.

But the problem isn't min-maxing or xam-nimming. The problem is **an extreme mismatch in player motivations causing a conflict at the table.** The GM resolves that conflict by imposing compromise so everyone can have fun. That's what GMs do — regardless of whether the conflict is about character builds, interpersonal dynamics, or scheduling.

### Hold Yourself to a High Standard of Evidence

Before intervening:
- Point to **actual stuff happening at the actual table**
- Point to **consistently unhappy faces**
- Explain clearly how you link observed behavior to an actual problem
- Do not favor players who share your preferences

### Respect Player Autonomy

Everyone at the table has the right to create any legal character for any reason and play it however they see fit. Everyone must accept that everyone else has the same right.

- If people think Beth is underpowered → tell them to shut up. Beth makes Beth's character.
- If people think Adam is too effective → remind them Adam made a legal character. That's his right.
- Unless there's an **actual, observable problem actively happening,** the GM needs to chill.

## The Bottom Line

GMs' stances on min-maxing are **more likely to cause problems** than extreme player behaviors are to even arise. Don't take a stance. Don't impose policies. Deal with real conflicts when they actually happen, using the same conflict-resolution skills you'd use for any table issue.

> What you think the game should be about? **That's just, like, your opinion, man.**
