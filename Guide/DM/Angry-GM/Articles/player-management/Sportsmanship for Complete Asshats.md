# Sportsmanship for Complete Asshats

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Sportsmanship for Complete Asshats](https://theangrygm.com/sportsmanship-for-complete-asshats/)
**Date:** August 30, 2024
**Series:** Random Bullshit

---

## Summary

A rant about teamwork, sportsmanship, and the recurring table conflict between "Don't tell me how to play" players and "You should do X" players. The core argument: both extremes are wrong, the answer is balance, and the GM is responsible for spotting and fixing the problem. Every player at the table who tolerates a disruptive player is part of the problem.

---

## The Scenario

> **Adam:** Beth, if Beryllia moves there and casts *strike of trueness*, she'll set up Ardrick to finish the ogre.
> **Beth:** Never tell me how to play! I charge the ogre and cast *zapp hands*!

Everyone at that table is the asshat — Adam, Beth, the bystanders, and the GM who lets it happen.

---

## Sportsmanship

- **"It's not whether you win; it's how you play the game."** This isn't a consolation for losers — it's a fundamental social truth.
- Dominant animals in social groups lose about a third of the time on purpose. Animals that never let others win end up without playmates.
- In real-life conversation, people who talk about themselves more than ~1/3 of the time tend to lose social peers. Online, self-performance averages ~90%.
- **Sportsmanship** = a basic social code about being someone others want to play with. Not a firm set of rules — a dynamic that every group navigates case-by-case.

---

## Teamwork

- No human overcomes every challenge alone. Cooperation is why civilization exists.
- When you join a team, you give up some self-interest for the sake of the group's goals.
- If you don't think the goal is worth giving up some autonomy for, you leave the team. That's how it works.
- Children learn this playing team sports: you don't always get to score the goals. You play your position.

---

## TTRPGs Are Team Sports

- Tabletop RPGs are voluntary, cooperative, social games. The rules of human social play apply.
- If you're not an asset to the team, your fellow players should stop inviting you.
- **If there's an asshat at your table and you let them be an asshat, you are part of the problem.**
- If you dread game night because of one player, there's an asshat at your table.

---

## The Two Kinds of "Steve"

### Primadonna Steve — "Don't Tell Me How to Play!"
- Believes any suggestion about their actions is a grievous offense.
- **Wrong.** By joining a team, you tacitly agree to set aside some self-interest. There is nothing offensive about a teammate suggesting a strategy.
- If you find helpful suggestions offensive, you're too far up yourself for cooperative games.

### Commander Steve — "You Go Here, You Do This"
- Believes every player should optimize fully and follow the mathematically best action.
- **Wrong.** Victory at the expense of a satisfying experience is hollow. If one player makes all the choices, no one else is playing.
- Most people would rather play with a group they love and lose every time than play with Commander Steve and win.

---

## The Answer: Balance

- When you join a team: listen politely, give ideas fair consideration, sometimes do things you don't want to for the team's sake.
- When you play with humans: respect others' right to make their own choices, even detrimental ones.
- **Both are costs of playing with other people.** If you can't accept both, play video games alone.
- No two groups settle in the same place. Some lean toward cooperative leadership, others toward personal freedom. Most fall in the vast middle ground.
- Building a successful group means everyone compromises to a comfortable middle. If the group demands too much compromise from you, you're not a good fit — bend more or move on.

---

## The GM's Job

- The GM is overseer, arbiter, and guardian angel of the group's social dynamic.
- **Diagnose the problem:** Is it Commander Steve ordering everyone around? Primadonna Steve refusing all input? Both?
- **Pull Steve aside.** You speak for the collective, not yourself. Stay calm, listen first, de-escalate.
- **First step is always conflict resolution.** Making someone leave is a last resort.
- If Steve won't listen or compromise, show Steve the door.
- **Do not pass this article to your players.** Rephrase the message yourself, less confrontationally.

---

## Key Principles

- **There is literally nothing wrong with** a player politely suggesting a course of action that might help the team.
- **There is literally nothing wrong with** a player choosing their own path so the game is fun for them.
- **There's only a problem when there's a problem.** And when there is, the GM needs to spot it and fix it.
- Social dynamics are *dynamic* — not fixed laws. Every group navigates them differently and continuously.
- Combat communication should be limited to what a character can reasonably bark in one round (~6 seconds).
