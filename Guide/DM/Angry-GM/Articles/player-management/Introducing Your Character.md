# Introducing Your Character

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/introducing-my-character/)
**Date:** September 30, 2022
**Category:** Player Advice — Character Presentation

---

## Why Character Introductions Matter

Players should introduce their character at the start of a campaign and **reintroduce** them at the start of every session. Even if no one listens, the act of describing is what matters — it benefits the describer's brain more than the listeners'.

**Do not use visual aids.** No pictures, no Hero Forge minis. Introduce in words. Here's why:

### It Helps You Visualize
Describing triggers visualization. Visualization and description loop into each other — describe more, visualize better, describe better. Players need this practice.

### It Helps You Parse Narration
Training players to describe their characters also trains them to process the GM's verbal narration better.

### It Helps You Remember
Repetition commits things to memory. Reintroducing your character loads it into working memory and strengthens the mental bonds between all the ideas that make up your character. Connecting words to sensory impressions (via visualization) makes memory even stronger.

### It Helps You Role-Play
The more interconnected your character's details are in your brain — the stronger the neural web — the more quickly and easily you remember it all, and the more effective a role-player you'll be.

---

## The Good Introduction Formula

A good introduction **evokes**, it doesn't describe. Brief, qualitative, impression-based. Follow the natural progression of human perception: size/shape → familiar features → threats → status/role → smaller details.

### Reading a Script Is Fine
Players can write introductions in advance and read them aloud. It's not as effective as improvising, but the benefits still accumulate over time. Players gradually memorize their scripts and start speaking off the cuff.

## The Five Key Elements

Each element gets **one to two sentences max.** Maybe three for non-humans. Only one or two details per element deserve descriptive adjectives.

### 1. Gross Physical Features
Gender, race, build, ethnicity/features, apparent age. Use qualitative descriptors for humans; provide numbers + qualitative descriptors for non-humans.

> *Alain is a tall, broad-shouldered human male barely out of his teens. He has the fair skin and sandy hair of a Sunderlander and a well-kempt growth of beard.*

> *Beryllia is an elf. She's a head shorter than a human, lithe, and graceful. It's impossible to judge her age. She has long, straight bronze hair and alabaster skin.*

### 2. Arms, Armor, and Clothing
Describe **every visible weapon** and where it's carried. Describe armor. Then clothing if not hidden by armor. (If a player can't explain how they carry every weapon, they're over encumbrance.)

> *Alain wears a battered steel breastplate over mail and has a longsword in a scabbard on his hip. A quiver of arrows and a recurved horn bow hang on his shoulder.*

> *Beryllia is decked in elegant noble's attire. She holds a gnarled quarterstaff in one hand and has a dagger tucked into her sash-like belt.*

### 3. Symbols, Decorations, and Their Meaning
Describe meaningful symbols: holy symbols, signet rings, badges, shield heraldry. If the meaning is common knowledge, provide it. If a reasonable person could guess the significance, provide that context.

> *Alain's shield is painted with three interlocked golden rings — a symbol of the church of the Vasaar that represents the King and His Knights.*

> *Beryllia wears a plain silver-blue signet ring marked with a single runic symbol. What it represents isn't known to you.*

### 4. Unique Feature or Personality Signifier
One noticeably unique feature that makes the character stand out. Can be physical, equipment, or a mannerism/gesture. Describe the feature only — not what it means. Let others guess.

> *Alain has a rigid, soldier's posture and never seems to relax.*

If the unique feature fits better with another element, mention it there instead (e.g., Beryllia's striking purple eyes go with gross physical features).

### 5. Recently Revealed Fun Fact (Reintroductions Only)
Remind the table of something that came out during the **last session of play.** Must have actually happened in gameplay — not backstory you wish people would ask about. Just remind, don't give a speech.

> *During your first battle as allies, Alain interposed his shield to protect Beryllia from an orc's attack. He can protect you if you stay close to him.*

> *At camp, Beryllia revealed that she's the daughter of a high elf lord named Vallianthe who rules an enclave deep in the Mearan Forest.*

## NPC Introduction Adaptations (For GMs)

### When to Introduce/Reintroduce
- **Introduce** any NPC the players are about to interact with
- **Reintroduce** an NPC the first time players interact with them in a given session
- Don't reintroduce if they return to the same NPC in the same session

### The Cumulative One Sentence Per Introduction Rule
- **First appearance:** Entire description in one sentence
- **Second reintroduction:** Two sentences
- **Third:** Three sentences
- **Max out at five sentences**

If an NPC joins the party for full sessions, give them a full PC-length introduction. When they leave, back to five sentences max.

### Tips for Brevity
- **Accentuate the important:** Role determines which elements matter most (noble → symbols; soldier → equipment; commoner → clothing)
- **Use high-encumbrance words:** "A bored guard" or "a sneering thug" conjures vivid images in two words
- **The Action Loophole:** Interweave introduction with action to sneak in extra description

### Example: Evolving NPC Description

**First appearance (1 sentence):**
> *The landlord is a barrel-chested thug of a man with a limp and an iron-shod cudgel hanging at his hip.*

**Second reintroduction (2–3 sentences):**
> *When you enter the common room, you see Cudge, the landlord, filling tankards. He's a tall, powerfully-built man well into his middle years, healthy despite his permanent, painful limp. His build, watchful eyes, and iron-shod cudgel tell you he's no one to mess with.*

**As party member (full introduction):**
> *Tall and well-built, Cudge is a human man in his late forties. He has dark brown eyes and a fringe of black hair around his bald scalp. He's stuffed himself into a boiled leather cuirass with stained woolen traveling clothes. He has an iron-shod club at one hip and a crossbow at the other. A copper coin with a hole punched through it hangs from a leather strip around his neck — some kind of keepsake. Cudge walks with a permanent limp, favoring his left leg. Last session, you learned his leg healed badly after being broken by guards at Blackrazor Prison to stop his escape attempts.*
