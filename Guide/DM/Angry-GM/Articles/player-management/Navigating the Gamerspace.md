# Navigating the Gamerspace

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Navigating the Gamerspace](https://theangrygm.com/navigating-gamer-hyperspace/)
**Date:** September 29, 2021
**Series:** Random Bullshit / Open World Game Design

---

## Summary

Explores the concept of "Gaming Stylespace" — a hypothetical multidimensional hyperspace where every GM decision moves the game along countless axes (linear vs. open, player-driven vs. plot-driven, magic level, rule adherence, etc.). Style is emergent, holistic, and descriptive — not prescriptive. Uses the training-facilities debate as a case study to show that most "should I do X?" questions can't be answered without understanding where your game sits in Stylespace.

---

## The Trainer Thing (Clarified)

### What Training Actually Means
- Characters need an external resource to reflect on and practice what they learned adventuring. Not necessarily a higher-level class member.
- The form varies: sparring partners, workbenches, libraries, holy sites, arcane rituals, obstacle courses, etc.
- "Training facilities" = anything appropriate enough to let a character level up. Could be a person, place, resource, or market.

### Why Require Training (Three Purposes)
1. **Pacing** — Forces characters to stop adventuring and return to a non-adventure place. Creates a logical break for bookkeeping.
2. **Narrative space** — Provides off-screen time for characters to plausibly master new abilities (wizards writing spells, sorcerers learning control, etc.).
3. **World interaction** — Forces players to think about and engage with the setting (finding libraries, befriending NPCs, building relationships).

### The Key Insight
- Training ties the mechanical level-up process to narrative and setting. In a role-playing game, that connection matters.
- Don't overthink the specifics. As long as it stops the game, adds narrative time, and forces world interaction, it's doing its job.

## Gaming Stylespace

### The Concept
- Every GM decision moves the game through a hypothetical multidimensional space.
- Regions of this space contain games that feel similar — we label those regions with imprecise terms like "epic fantasy" or "player-driven."
- You can aim for a region but can't plot exact coordinates. Style is fuzzy, emergent, and holistic.

### Why "Should I Do X?" Is the Wrong Question
- There's no universal right answer — it depends on where your game is in Stylespace.
- Both "always available training" and "limited training" are equally valid. Neither is inherently fair or unfair.
- The screaming morons on both sides create false dilemmas from stupid assumptions.

### The Litmus Test Question
> **"Do you want to run the sort of game where the players can and will suddenly stop whatever they're doing and go do something else instead? Possibly to never return?"**

- If **yes** (open-world, player-driven): limit resource availability. This means:
  - Training facilities aren't everywhere → players must travel to find them.
  - Economies are realistic → small towns can't absorb dungeon plunder.
  - Monster difficulty varies by location → some places are too dangerous, others trivial.
  - All of this drives players to make interesting choices about where to go and what to prioritize.

- If **no** (more linear, plot-driven): make resources freely available so they don't distract from the story.

### Related Issues Resolved by the Same Question
- Should the world level up with the characters? (Scaled vs. unscaled encounters)
- Should every settlement have full shopping/services?
- Should training be universally accessible?
- All are facets of the same Stylespace axis.

## Key Principles

- **Style is like genre** — descriptive, not prescriptive. No formula produces "epic fantasy." It's the sum of all decisions.
- **Tone and themes matter more than trappings** — You can get cosmic horror without tentacles if you nail the tone.
- **One decision doesn't define style** — It's the aggregate. Don't obsess over individual choices in isolation.
- **Preferences are personal and subjective** — Stop fighting about which approach is "right."
