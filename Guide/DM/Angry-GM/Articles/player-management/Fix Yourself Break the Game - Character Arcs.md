# Fix Yourself, Break the Game: Character Arcs in D&D

**Source:** [Fix Yourself, Break the Game](https://theangrygm.com/fix-yourself-break-the-game/)
**Series:** Hacking Rules
**Date:** February 19, 2018

---

## Core Thesis

Character arcs — the story of a character overcoming a personal flaw to become empowered — rarely happen in RPGs because the structure doesn't support them. A custom Inspiration system tied to character traits, flaws, and a GM-controlled "transformative moment" can layer genuine character arcs onto D&D.

---

## What Is a Character Arc?

- More than just "the character changes." It's a self-contained sub-story of transformation with a beginning, climax, and resolution.
- Typically: the character must overcome a personal demon to resolve the main story, or resolving the main story empowers them to overcome it.
- **Ghostbusters example:** Each Ghostbuster was a terrible person (con artist, man-child, amoral scientist). The decision to "cross the streams" — sacrificing themselves to save the city — was the moment each overcame their flaw and became a hero. Their transformation enabled the victory.
- Character arcs are about **becoming** a hero through self-empowerment, confronting flaws, and sacrifice.

### Why RPGs Lack Them
1. **Player sovereignty:** Transformations are entirely up to the player. They'd have to script it in advance, which feels contrived and is antithetical to organic role-playing.
2. **Ongoing franchise structure:** RPG campaigns often have no definite end, making it hard to know when the transformation should happen. Repeated relapses (like Iron Man) feel insincere.

## Angry's Inspiration System

### Character Traits (at creation)
Five categories tailored to the campaign. Currently:
- **2 Personality Quirks** — basic behavioral aspects
- **1 Ideal** — a moral code the character will never break ("I can never allow a child to come to harm")
- **1 Motivation** — why they adventure (wealth, glory, boredom, the greater good)
- **1 Flaw** — the thing that gets them in trouble (heavy drinker, cowardly, reckless, know-it-all)

### How Inspiration Works
- **Binary state:** You either have Inspiration or you don't. Characters start with it.
- **Inspired Actions (spend Inspiration):** When an action aligns with a trait, claim advantage on a roll (or other situational bonus). Represents inner strength and mastery.
- **Claiming a Setback (gain Inspiration):** When a trait (especially the Flaw) causes problems, claim a penalty (disadvantage, exhaustion, etc.) to regain Inspiration. Represents coping mechanisms.
- **Major victories** also restore Inspiration (completing an adventure or major milestone).
- Inspiration does NOT refresh once per session. It's entirely driven by the buy/spend cycle.

### Player-Driven, GM-Supported
- Players are primarily responsible for buying and spending Inspiration through Setbacks and Inspired Actions.
- The GM builds scenes around traits during planning and watches for opportunities during play.
- This keeps the GM from having to track 25 traits in real-time while running the game.

## The Character Arc Mechanic

### The Transformation
- When a character **overcomes their Flaw**, they gain Inspiration on every Short or Long Rest — permanently.
- They never have to Claim a Setback again. They are always empowered to be who they are.
- This is deliberately "game-breaking" in a controlled way: roughly one free advantage per 2 encounters. Significant but not overwhelming.

### The Transformative Moment
- **The GM controls when it happens**, not the player. It's a narrative structure element, like initiative order.
- Typically occurs in the lead-up to the campaign's climax (gearing up for the third act).
- The GM writes the character's flaw into the heart of an adventure — forces a head-on confrontation with it.
- The **player controls how** the transformation happens: whether it's a triumphant overcoming or a grim lesson learned too late. But the transformation will happen either way.
- Like Mass Effect or Chrono Trigger: each character gets a spotlight moment to get their act together before the final confrontation.

### For Ongoing/Endless Campaigns
- After the big adventure, characters take a break (time skip).
- They can develop **new flaws** and have **new arcs**.
- They cannot have the same flaw/arc again. Growth is permanent.

---

## On Narrative Mechanics

- **Narrative mechanics** impose story structure on the game, distinct from simulation mechanics (physics) or game mechanics (fun/balance).
- Fate's Aspects are a good example: narrative descriptors with both positive and negative mechanical effects.
- D&D's Inspiration is a half-hearted copy of Fate's Aspects — poorly balanced, easily forgotten, and described as "rewarding good role-playing" (which misunderstands narrative mechanics entirely).
- Narrative mechanics are still in their infancy. Most are small-scope, moment-to-moment. The Character Arc mechanic operates at the macro level — imposing structure on the whole campaign narrative.

---

## Summary

Give characters meaningful traits and a flaw with mechanical weight. Let players buy and spend Inspiration through those traits. When the campaign approaches its climax, the GM creates a spotlight moment for each character to confront their flaw. The player chooses how they respond, but the transformation happens. The reward — permanent Inspiration recovery — represents a character who has mastered themselves. For ongoing campaigns, new flaws and new arcs keep the cycle going without repeating.
