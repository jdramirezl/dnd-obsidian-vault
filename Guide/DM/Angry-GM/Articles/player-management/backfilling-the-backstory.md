# Backfilling the Backstory

## Key Advice

Backstory is the **least important** part of adventure design and should never come first. It exists only to answer two questions: (1) how does solving the problem satisfy the adventure's motivations, and (2) how can the players start solving it. Build backstory backward from resolution and motivation — never forward from a blank page.

## Summary

Angry argues that GMs and players alike are obsessed with backstory to the detriment of actual gameplay. Backstory is everything that happened before the story you're telling — it provides context, not content. Most GMs start adventure design by writing pages of prose history, which is inefficient and often irrelevant. The correct approach is **purpose-driven backstory**: first establish the resolution (how the adventure ends) and the motivation (why the PCs engage), then "figure out" — not "create" — only the backstory needed to connect those two elements and provide a starting point for the players. The process is iterative question-and-answer: ask what needs explaining, answer it, then ask what questions that answer creates. He demonstrates this with two detailed examples — a drug smuggling mystery and a dragon-terrorized village — showing how rich, layered backstory emerges naturally from working backward. Critically, backstory is never final or finished; you develop only what you need and fill in details as the game demands, leaving deliberate blanks for future flexibility.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Never start with backstory** — Begin with the resolution (how the adventure ends) and motivation (why PCs engage).
2. **Use backstory to answer two questions** — How does solving the problem satisfy motivations? How can players start solving it?
3. **"Figure out" backstory, don't "create" it** — Treat it as a puzzle to solve, not prose to write. Ask questions, answer them, repeat.
4. **Don't censor your first ideas** — Even stupid answers lead somewhere. Contradictions can be ironed out with more questions.
5. **Stop when you have enough** — You don't need to know the villain's childhood. Develop only what the current stage of design requires.
6. **Leave deliberate blanks** — Holes in backstory give you room to maneuver, connect to future adventures, and improvise during play.
7. **Add details as needed during play** — Backstory is a living document. Players will dig for details you haven't planned — that's fine.
8. **If you already have a backstory, use it** — The "work backward" advice is for when you're stuck. If inspiration struck first, run with it.

> [!tip] The Backstory Riddle
> Don't write backstory like an author writing a novel. Write it like a detective solving a case — you have the crime scene (resolution) and the motive (motivation), and you're working backward to figure out what happened. Every detail you uncover should serve the investigation. If it doesn't help the players engage with the adventure, it doesn't need to exist yet. You can always add more later, but you can never un-waste the time spent on details no one will ever encounter.
