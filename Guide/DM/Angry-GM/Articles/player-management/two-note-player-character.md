# Angry's Two-Note Player-Character

## Key Advice

Players should create characters with only **two notes** — a vague motivation and a single defining fact — then let the character emerge through play. Elaborate pre-written backstories actively hinder genuine roleplaying by overloading the brain and creating static, inflexible characters.

## Summary

Written directly to players (not GMs), this article argues that the standard character creation process — concept, stats, elaborate backstory, personality traits — produces worse roleplaying outcomes. The brain is naturally wired for social persona-shifting ("you, but..."), and the more rigidly you define a character upfront, the harder it is to roleplay convincingly and the less the character will grow dynamically. Instead, Angry proposes the **Two-Note Method**: write down (1) a general motivation (e.g., "I've sworn an oath of vengeance") and (2) a single defining fact or belief (e.g., "I hate and fear my uncontrollable magic"). Do the mechanical character sheet, skip personality traits, and start playing. The character will emerge organically through play — backstory details will surface naturally, surprising even the player. This produces dynamic characters that evolve in response to the shared story, which is the entire point of RPGs. Don't write anything else down — keeping details unwritten gives your brain permission to change them on the fly.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Fill out the mechanical character sheet** — Race, class, stats, equipment. Pick what looks cool or fun.
2. **Choose a motivation** — A vague, general reason your character adventures (e.g., "bound by honor," "thirst for knowledge," "wanderlust").
3. **Write one defining fact** — A single sentence: a belief, fear, memory, or desire. Write it large where you can see it during play.
4. **Skip personality traits, flaws, and ideals** — Leave them blank. Tell your GM Angry said it was okay.
5. **Do NOT write down backstory** — Even privately. Writing it down removes your brain's permission to change it organically.
6. **Play to find out who your character is** — Let backstory details emerge through play. Your character will surprise you.
7. **Give it several sessions** — The method takes time to kick in. The more you do it, the faster it works.
8. **Share the story** — RPGs are team sports. View other players as co-creators, not an audience for your character's solo adventure.

> [!tip] The Psychological Trick
> Place your single defining fact where your eyes naturally fall during play — the top of your character sheet is ideal. Every time you check your AC or update hit points, you'll see it. This keeps your brain's social persona engine primed without conscious effort, and the character starts to "play itself." Nothing written down stays malleable; everything written down becomes rigid. Use this to your advantage.
