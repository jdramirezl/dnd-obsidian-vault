# Like Players Do — Session Scripting

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Like Players Do](https://theangrygm.com/like-players-do/)
**Date:** July 28, 2021
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## Summary

Introduces the Session Script — a short prose summary of what you think will happen at your next session, written before you prep. It starts from the Player Do list (what players intend to do) and extends it into a narrative prediction. Scripting is a pre-planning tool that organizes thoughts, clarifies goals, highlights problems, prioritizes prep, and lodges the session plan in your brain so you can improvise confidently.

---

## What Is a Session Script?

- A few hundred words of prose describing how you think the next session will play out.
- Written before the session, limited to one session only — never script further ahead.
- Based on the players' current goals, stated intentions, past behaviors, and whatever obstacles or content they'll encounter.
- Uses hedge words throughout: "probably," "likely," "maybe," "hopefully."

---

## Why Script?

### Organize Your Thoughts
- Converts scattered maps, notes, and stat blocks into a single readable summary.
- Separates what's useful now from what's unlikely to come up this session.

### Clarify Goals
- Highlights what the players are trying to accomplish.
- Forces you to articulate your own GM goals for the session (pacing, tone, foreshadowing — not just "advance the plot").

### Highlight Problems
- Reveals where the session might stall, bog down, or derail.
- Shows where players lack information to make decisions, or where meaningless choices will waste time in committee debate.

### Prioritize Prep
- Tells you which maps, stat blocks, rules, and answers you actually need ready.
- Often the script *is* the only prep you need — 15 minutes once you're practiced.

### Get It Stuck in Your Brain
- Writing + visualizing = the session plan lives in your head.
- You feel more confident and in control, even when things go off-script.
- When players deviate, you know the plan exists and can adapt from it rather than feeling lost.

---

## Scripting Does NOT Railroad

- The script starts with what the *players* are trying to do, not what the GM wants.
- Every line is a guess about player behavior, not a mandate.
- When players go off-script, you're aware of consequences to their goals and can communicate them.
- You never force players back on track — you adapt, repurpose, or discard the script as needed.
- Players rarely go truly off the rails permanently; they usually circle back to their goals.

---

## How to Write a Session Script

1. **Start with the Player Do list** — the things players intend to do and the directions they're headed.
2. **Predict what happens next** — given their goals, your notes, and the game world.
3. **At decision points, guess** — use your knowledge of the players to predict their most likely choice.
4. **Acknowledge failure** — note what happens if players fail encounters or make bad choices. Writing it down means you accept those outcomes.
5. **Keep going** until you have a full session's worth, or until you can't continue.
6. **Read it back** — evaluate for problems, pacing, and fun.

### Red Flags
- **Can't guess at all** → Players probably don't have enough information to choose either. Add a lead or clue.
- **Never have to pause and think** → You're probably not offering real choices, or you're writing from your perspective instead of the players'.
- **No hedge words** → Either writing wrong or not offering meaningful choices.
- **Fail states you won't impose** → If you won't kill a PC, plan a non-death failure for every life-threatening encounter.

---

## Building the Habit

- Your first scripts will be wrong — too long, too short, bad predictions. That's normal.
- After each session, review the script and highlight what didn't match reality. Don't analyze deeply — just note it.
- Over time, your subconscious pattern-recognition builds a model of how your players and game behave.
- The process takes months (3–6) of consistent practice to start clicking.
- The goal isn't to understand GMing intellectually — it's to internalize planning and running until it's second nature.
