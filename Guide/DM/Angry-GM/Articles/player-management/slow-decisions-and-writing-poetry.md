# Ask Angry: Slow Decisions and Writing Poetry

## Key Advice

When players take forever to decide, the problem is almost always the GM's fault — not "analysis paralysis." The cure is **exigency** (a sense of urgency that demands action now) combined with **clarity** (one obvious goal and one obvious path forward at all times).

## Summary

Angry tackles two reader questions. The first and primary one addresses players who endlessly deliberate. He rejects the buzzword "analysis paralysis" and identifies two GM-controllable root causes: (1) lack of exigency — players feel no time pressure, so they deliberate endlessly; and (2) lack of clarity — players genuinely don't know what to do because the GM has been too subtle or offered too many options. In urgent situations, the GM must never let up pressure — narrate the consequences of delay in real-time. In non-urgent situations, use escalating flavor interruptions (the waiter clears plates, night falls, time visibly passes) to signal that the world doesn't pause for planning. For clarity, always ensure there is one obvious goal and one obvious (if risky) path to accomplish it, with no more than 2-3 choices at a time. The second question covers writing in-game poetry/chants using a structured brainstorming method.

## Actionable Steps

1. **In urgent situations, maintain pressure** — Narrate ongoing consequences while players deliberate. "Skippy's face is being eaten — what do you do?!"
2. **In non-urgent situations, use flavor interruptions** — Have the world move forward: waiters clear plates, night falls, NPCs leave. Increase frequency the longer players stall.
3. **Never tell players they're taking too long** — Instead, make the passage of in-game time obvious and slightly ominous.
4. **Always provide one obvious goal** — Players should never be confused about what they're trying to accomplish right now.
5. **Always provide one obvious path** — Make the default option clear but risky, so players are motivated to find better alternatives rather than stalling.
6. **Limit choices to 2-3 options** — Too many options cause genuine indecision. If players invent their own options, roll with it.
7. **Be more obvious than you think you need to be** — GMs are never as clear as they think they are. Subtlety is the enemy of pacing.

> [!tip] The Exigency Principle
> "Analysis paralysis" is a symptom, not a diagnosis. The real disease is a world that stands still while players plan. If the game feels like it pauses when players deliberate, you've removed all reason to act quickly. The fix: make the world keep moving — visibly, relentlessly, and with consequences. Players who feel the clock ticking make decisions. Players who feel infinite time available will debate forever.
