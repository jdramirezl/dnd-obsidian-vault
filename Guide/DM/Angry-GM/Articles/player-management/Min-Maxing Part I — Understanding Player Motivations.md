# Min-Maxing Part I — Understanding Player Motivations

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/just-shut-up-about-min-maxing-part-i/)
**Date:** May 27, 2025
**Category:** Player Management — Motivation Theory

---

## The Core Idea

Every gamer plays for different reasons, but those reasons aren't infinitely unique. With enough research, you can group preferences into broad categories — a **Gamer Motivation Model.** But these models are fuzzy and descriptive, not prescriptive. You cannot assign every player a single motivation like an astrological sign.

## Player Motivations Work Like Sims Meters

Think of *The Sims.* Every sim has the same set of needs (Hunger, Energy, Social, Fun, etc.) but each sim's **personality** determines:
- How much weight each meter has on overall mood
- How fast each meter drains
- How easily each meter refills

**Players work the same way.** Every gamer has the same set of motivational meters. The MDA Framework posits eight:

| Meter | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sensation** | Sensory pleasure, aesthetics |
| **Fantasy** | Immersion in an imaginary world |
| **Narrative** | Story, drama, unfolding events |
| **Challenge** | Testing skill, overcoming difficulty |
| **Fellowship** | Social connection, teamwork |
| **Discovery** | Exploration, finding new things |
| **Expression** | Creativity, self-expression |
| **Submission** | Passive engagement, turning off your brain |

Each player's personality determines how these meters are weighted. A player with a fast-draining Challenge meter needs constant testing or they're bored. A player with a slow-draining Submission meter has little patience for grinding.

**You are not a "Challenge guy."** You have weighted meters that go up and down at different rates and affect your mood differently. Motivations also change over time, with mood, and with unmet needs.

## Games Satisfy Multiple Motivations Simultaneously

Very few games provide single-motivation experiences. Games are activities that meet **multiple needs at once** — letting players with different personalities share the same table and each get something out of it.

A challenge-seeker and a social player can enjoy the same game even though they're getting different things from it. One person's favorite part is another person's "thing they put up with to get to the good parts." And that's fine.

**TTRPGs are the most flexible of all games.** They're not distinct because of a specific mix of motivations — they're distinct because they're so **customizable.** A clever GM can satisfy almost any mix of motivations with the right campaign or adventure. And as players' needs change, the GM can adjust dynamically.

## The GM Is Not a Sim

As a GM, you are **not** a player. The games you run do not satisfy your gamer motivations the same way playing does. Your motivations as a GM are more complex and personal — connected to core values, how you connect with others, what you find meaningful.

Key implications:
- You don't have to share your players' preferences to run a game for them
- You don't even need to understand their preferences
- Your players don't need to share preferences with each other
- Your job is to satisfy a table of people with different motivation profiles

**GMs who insist RPGs should only satisfy a narrow set of motives are sucking the magic from the medium.**

## Don't Pigeonhole Players

The biggest mistake: trying to assign each player a single "gamer type" and then adding one element to your game to make each of them happy. That's not how any of this works.

Look at the **individual breakdowns** — the components that add up to a player's profile. The broad "gamer type" label is just more pigeonholing into astrological signs.
