# Clubhouse Rules: Scheduling and Attendance (Part I)

## Key Advice

Every campaign needs a firm, explicit **Scheduling and Attendance Policy** — a social contract that defines each member's obligation to the group and provides contingency plans for when things go wrong. Game time is too valuable to waste on ambiguity, and people need clear rules and accountability to follow through on commitments.

## Summary

Part of the True Campaign Managery series, this article argues that GMs must establish and enforce hard rules around scheduling, attendance, lateness, and cancellation. The core principles: game time is a treasure (every wasted minute costs everyone their opportunity cost), inconsistent scheduling destroys continuity/investment/social cohesion/rules familiarity, and people deserve respect for the time they've committed. A good policy serves two purposes: (1) it defines each member's social obligation to the group, and (2) it provides contingency plans to minimize disruption when problems arise. Angry walks through building a policy step by step: establish a basic schedule (weekly 4-hour sessions is gold standard), define advanced scheduling procedures if regularity is impossible, set cancellation rules (only the Campaign Manager cancels), establish quorum minimums (never below 3 players, never half the group or less), define rescheduling procedures, handle schedule change requests (require unanimity), set expectations for communicating absences and lateness, and establish firm arrive-by and start-of-game times with clear procedures for missing players and late arrivals (wait ~15 min, then start; no arrivals more than 1 hour late).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Establish a regular schedule** — Weekly, same day, same time, 4 hours is the gold standard. If impossible, create a firm scheduling procedure (polls, deadlines, official announcements).
2. **Designate one person to cancel games** — Only the Campaign Manager cancels or reschedules. This prevents confusion and puts responsibility on one set of shoulders.
3. **Define your quorum** — Set a minimum player count below which you won't run. Angry's rule: never fewer than 3 players, never half the group or less.
4. **Set cancellation criteria** — No GM, no play space, act of God, or below quorum. That's it.
5. **Create a rescheduling procedure** — Before proposing a reschedule, confirm the GM can prep and the host is available. Then poll the group with a firm deadline.
6. **Require unanimity for non-emergency schedule changes** — Moving game night to accommodate one player is a favor that requires everyone's agreement.
7. **Set firm arrive-by and start-of-game times** — Players must know when to arrive (before game time) and that showing up at start time means they're late.
8. **Establish a late arrival protocol** — Wait ~15 minutes for warned-late players, then start. For no-call/no-show, send a check-in message 5 minutes before start, wait 15 minutes, then begin.
9. **Cap late arrivals at 1 hour** — Anyone arriving more than an hour late should skip the session entirely to avoid major disruption.
10. **Communicate expectations clearly** — Tell players exactly how to notify you of absences (private message, group chat, SMS) and that you expect notification as early as possible.
11. **Handle departures privately** — If a player must leave the group due to schedule incompatibility, manage it through individual private conversations, not group votes.

> [!tip] The Opportunity Cost Principle
> Every game night that doesn't happen costs more than just the game — it costs everyone the things they turned down to keep that night free. A player who declined overtime, skipped a social event, or arranged childcare for a game that gets canceled without warning has lost twice. Respecting everyone's time means establishing clear rules, communicating early, and treating game night like the promise it is. A simple "sorry I can't make it" is all it takes — because "sorry I'm late" is code for "I respect you."
