# What if Your Players Don't Want To?

**Source:** [The Angry GM – What if Your Players Don't Want To?](https://theangrygm.com/what-if-your-players-dont-want-to/)
**Date:** September 16, 2022
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level / Town Mode

---

## Summary

Addresses the perennial GM question: "What if my players don't want to engage with [mechanic/system/mode]?" — specifically in the context of Town Mode. Provides three answers: don't force it, force it anyway, or realize the question is based on a misunderstanding of what "engagement" actually means. The article also clarifies the critical distinction between engagement, interaction, and roleplaying — and between *playing* and *playacting*.

---

## Part I: Two Answers to "What if They Don't Want To?"

### Answer 1 — Don't Make Them
- You're the GM. You can override any rule, system, or mechanic. That's how TTRPGs work.
- If your players don't want to do something, just cut it. It's always an option.
- This applies to Town Mode, encumbrance, XP tracking, or anything else.

### Answer 2 — Tough Luck
- "My players don't like it" is never, by itself, a good reason to rip out a mechanic.
- Games are complicated; game design is alchemy (part art, part science, part hoodoo). Players can't see the holistic impact of a single system on the whole experience.
- Every game has pain-in-the-ass mechanics that exist for a reason — usually tied to irrational human psychology and long-term satisfaction.
- **Fun is fleeting and momentary; what matters is the long-term play experience.**
- Statistically, most GMs are worse designers than the professionals who built the system. Ripping things out is risky.
- The responsible game-designer move: make players suck it up. Accept initial grumbling. They'll adapt, and the campaign will be better for it.

---

## Part II: The Question Is Based on Wrong Assumptions

### Interaction vs. Engagement vs. Roleplaying
- **Interaction** — the broadest term; just means the players are playing the game (choosing actions, getting responses, reacting). So broad it's nearly useless.
- **Engagement** — implies *with the world as a world*. Players deal with game elements as living things with motivations, fears, and behavior patterns, not just as mechanical obstacles.
- **Roleplaying** — internal engagement. Players think of their characters as living beings with motivations and emotions, not just stat-block avatars.

#### The Goblin Example
- *Interaction:* hostile mobile in room → player uses game actions to destroy it.
- *Engagement:* goblin is chest-thumping → player knows goblins are cowardly → leaves an escape route → goblin flees. The player dealt with the goblin as a *creature*, not a stat block.

### Key Insight
- Engagement and roleplaying are what lead to players *giving a damn* emotionally.
- Town Mode is about making players think of the world as a world — not about forcing in-character conversations for every purchase.

### Playing vs. Playacting
- Not every table interaction needs to be spoken in-character. "My character refuses the dragon's demands with an insult" is functionally identical to speaking the insult in-character.
- GMs can summarize entire conversations in narration, sprinkling in a few in-character lines for flavor.
- The "speak as your character" style is entertaining (and popularized by live streams), but it's not the only valid approach.
- Confusing engagement with mandatory in-character playacting is what makes GMs worry their players won't go for Town Mode.

---

## The Correct Answer

All three answers are valid, but the most responsible one:
1. Don't confuse engagement with in-character playacting.
2. Know how to handle in-game conversation flexibly (narration, summary, occasional in-character lines).
3. Make your players suck it up. Hold your ground. Don't entertain arguments. Call their bluff if they threaten to walk.

> If your players said "we don't like tracking hit points," you'd laugh. Why is Town Mode — or encumbrance, or XP — any different?
