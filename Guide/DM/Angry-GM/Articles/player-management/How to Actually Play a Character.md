# How to Actually Play a Character

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-actually-play-a-character/)
**Date:** April 21, 2021
**Category:** Player Advice — Role-Playing

---

## What Role-Playing Actually Is

Role-playing is **not** pretending to be someone else. You can't take the "you" out of role-playing. Your brain's wiring — genetics, experiences, beliefs — comes along for the ride. You literally cannot think like anyone other than yourself.

The character you play is always **"you, but…"**

The question isn't "what would Angrar do?" It's **"what would I do if I were Angrar?"** Subtle distinction, but critical.

## Role-Playing Is Exploration, Not Portrayal

Role-playing isn't about creating a character and then portraying it. It's about **exploring** a character and gradually coming to understand them. The more time you invest, the deeper and more complex the character becomes.

You can't start with a fully realized character. The best actors in the world don't do that. Characters find their voice over time — through play, through interaction, through discovery.

## You Are Both Actor and Audience

You're not performing for the other players. You're a hybrid — playing a character while simultaneously trying to understand and care about that character. Your character is a deeply personal extension of you.

**No one cares about your character as much as you do.** Not the other players, not the GM. If you're trying to get others to care about your character, you're playing for the wrong reasons. Role-playing is about building a personal relationship with your character.

## Recontextualization Builds Depth

Real understanding comes from **recontextualization** — discovering something that changes your understanding of past events. This forces reflection, which deepens understanding and attachment.

Example: Eddie Valiant in *Who Framed Roger Rabbit* — first appears greedy, humorless, drunk. Later revelations reframe everything: he's struggling, bitter, numbing pain from his brother's murder by a toon. Each discovery forces you to revise your understanding.

## Characters Must Be Shaped by Play

Role-playing is interactive, not solo. You don't play to tell your story — you play to **discover** a story. The game's world, adventures, and other characters do things outside your control. To role-play, you must let those experiences shape your character.

Character is revealed through interaction — and **changed** by interaction. If you can't handle giving up control over your character, write a novel.

## How to Actually Do It

### Step 1: Start with a Simple Seed

You only need two things:
1. **A role** — not a character, a role. An adventuring wizard. A mercenary fighter. A cunning rogue.
2. **A motivation** — the thing that drives your character. Wealth, power, glory, wisdom, being a force for good. Just one.

"Academic wizard who wants power" isn't a character — it's a seed. The less you start with, the deeper and more complex your character will become. A seed in a tiny pot gives a stunted plant. A seed in an empty clearing gives a towering oak.

Don't overthink the character sheet. Don't reject skills because they don't fit your "vision." Leave room to grow.

### Step 2: See What the Character Does

When the GM asks "what do you do?" — ask yourself:

> "What would I do in this situation if I were [role with motivation]?"

Go with your gut. Whatever pops into your head, do it. Don't reason, don't analyze — act, then wonder why later.

At first, there'll be a lot of "you" in the character. Over time, the character takes over. Eventually, answers come without asking the question. Sometimes the character will surprise you. That's the magic.

If you forget to ask the question and just blurt something out — that's fine. Not every moment is a defining moment. But try to catch yourself, especially when stakes are high.

### Step 3: Reflect on Your Character

After each session, ask: **What new thing did I learn about my character?** Did they do anything unexpected or surprising?

- Don't write anything down. Don't ponder deeply. Just note it.
- If nothing new: ask what you're **curious** about. What's mysterious or confusing?
- Never change the seed. Never change the role or motivation in your question. Your brain knows the rest.

### Step 4: Reflect on Other Characters

After reflecting on your own character, do the same for the other PCs:
- Did you learn anything new about an ally?
- What are you curious about regarding another character?

You can pursue this curiosity in-character next session — ask them questions. Most players are delighted when someone shows genuine interest in their character. **You get what you give.**

This trains you to pay attention to others, see them as part of your character's life, and let them shape your character — instead of treating them as your audience.

---

## Key Principles

- **The character is "you, but…"** — you can never fully be someone else
- **Start simple** — a role and a motivation, nothing more
- **Discover, don't define** — let the character emerge through play
- **Reflect periodically** — on your character and on others
- **Role-playing is selfless** — it requires listening, paying attention, and giving up control
- **It takes time** — real relationships don't spring up fully formed
