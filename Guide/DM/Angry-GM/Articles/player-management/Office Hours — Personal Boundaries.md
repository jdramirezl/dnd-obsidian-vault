# Office Hours — Personal Boundaries

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Professor Angry's Office Hours: I Actually Do Respect People's Boundaries](https://theangrygm.com/office-hours-personal-boundaries/)
**Date:** May 1, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery (Office Hours)

---

## Summary

Angry's definitive statement on Lines & Veils surveys and similar "safety tools." He respects individual player boundaries through direct, personal conversation — treating it as a mutual favor built on trust, understanding, and good faith. He argues against surveys specifically because they replace trust-building conversations with impersonal lists of demands, lower the bar for what counts as a boundary, imply promises of perfect compliance that can't be kept, and may be psychologically unhealthy by fostering brittleness.

---

## How Angry Handles Boundaries

### The Conversation Model
- A player approaches privately with a serious issue (e.g., "I have a spider thing — even descriptions cause anxiety").
- The GM listens, considers the cost, and agrees to respect it if possible.
- The GM acknowledges they might slip and proposes a failsafe (e.g., a subtle tap on the table as a reminder).
- Both sides agree: the GM will try their best; the player will assume good faith if there's a slip.

### When You Can't Accommodate
- If a boundary is incompatible with the game (e.g., no violence in D&D), say so honestly and respectfully.
- Respecting, understanding, and agreeing aren't the same as acquiescing.
- Some people just aren't a good fit for the game you're running. That's okay.

---

## Respecting Boundaries Is a Favor

- It requires changing and monitoring your behavior — that takes effort.
- Effort for someone else = a favor. Favors come with mutual agreement, good faith, and gratitude.
- No one is entitled to another person's behavioral changes. Respect is given, not owed.
- The cost might be big or small, but it's never zero. Both sides must take that on faith.

---

## Why Surveys Are Problematic (Four Reasons)

### 1. Surveys Aren't Agreements
- They replace trust-building conversations with impersonal lists.
- No mutual understanding, no failsafe for slip-ups, no assumption of good faith.
- A survey is ultimately just a list of demands to be honored or rejected.

### 2. Surveys Lower the Bar
- People feel compelled to write *something* — they dig for issues they might not actually have.
- Predicting future reactions is unreliable; the list of imagined triggers is much longer than the list of known ones.
- Mixing icks/squicks with genuine traumas devalues the serious issues.
- 5 players × 4 items each = 20 forbidden topics. No human brain can track all of that while running a game.

### 3. Surveys Imply a Promise You Can't Keep
- They promise a completely, totally safe environment — perfect compliance.
- That promise is impossible to fulfill, and the survey removes the good-faith safety net for when you inevitably slip.

### 4. This Isn't Healthy
- Strong evidence suggests that promising safety from all discomfort and framing exposure as harm makes people psychologically brittle.
- Brittle people have extreme emotional reactions to accidental boundary crossings — aimed at the GM or other players.
- Reference: *The Coddling of the American Mind* by Greg Lukianoff and Jonathan Haidt.
- Also linked: [Monte Cook Games — Consent in Gaming](https://www.montecookgames.com/consent-in-gaming/) (presented as the other side's best resource).

---

## The Alternative

- Run a PG/PG-13 game by default — violence yes, graphic/mature content generally no, sex fades to black.
- If a player has a serious issue, they bring it to you privately. You have a conversation.
- During play, if something squicks a player, they say so and you adjust. No survey needed.
- Content warnings via your campaign pitch (tone, themes, genre) give players enough to self-select.

## Key Quotes

- "If you tell me there's an issue, I will break myself helping you if I can."
- "Everyone's got to find a way to carry their baggage. I will absolutely help a person carry their load if I can."
- "Respecting someone's boundaries is a favor you do for someone else. It's a good thing to do — but it's still a favor."
