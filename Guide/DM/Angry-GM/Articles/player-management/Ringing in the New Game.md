# Ringing in the New Game

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Ringing in the New Game](https://theangrygm.com/ringing-in-the-new-game/)
**Date:** January 13, 2021
**Series:** Campaign Management

---

## Summary

A detailed walkthrough of how Angry pitched and set up a new D&D 3.5 campaign called *New Horizons* (or *Year of the Comet*). Covers the full pre-campaign process: gathering player preferences without a formal Session Zero, choosing system and setting, defining playstyle, and structuring character creation — all aimed at starting the game with the least work possible while ensuring player buy-in.

---

## The Goal of Pre-Campaign Work

- **Start the game.** You need player-characters and a first adventure that fit together. Everything else is overhead.
- The pitch must excite the players enough that they approve it. If they reject it, you start over.
- The less work to get there, the better — but not at the cost of a bad game.

---

## Gathering Player Preferences (Without Session Zero)

- A Session Zero is ideal but not required. It just increases the odds of a first-pitch approval.
- **Genre selection:** Throw genres at the players and have them prioritize. Fantasy was the only shared top-three across all players.
- **The Ten-Word Exercise:** Ask each player privately to list ten single words or short phrases describing a great fantasy campaign.
  - Players dash off knee-jerk answers first, then fill with random thoughts — bypassing the conscious brain.
  - This short-circuits the problem of people being bad at predicting what they'll like.

### Common Themes That Emerged
- Exploration, wonder/fantasy, growth through gameplay, progression
- Low fantasy/low magic, player empowerment and player skill
- Humble beginnings/rags-to-riches
- Living, detailed world
- Relationships between characters and with NPCs

## Key Decisions

### System: D&D 3.5
- Fantasy genre + no desire to learn a new system = some edition of D&D.
- Wanted a by-the-book game — no hacks, no house rules documents, no tinkering.
- 3.5 chosen over Pathfinder (left as "self-evident" why).
- Core-only, vanilla. No sourcebooks or supplements. Gnomes and monks removed.

### Setting: The Angryverse (Age of Rediscovery)
- Homebrew setting — a pile of remixable setting elements built over a decade.
- Low-fantasy civilizations in a magical, fantastic world (Tolkien/Martin approach).
- Set in a recovering dark age: travel resuming, trade rebuilding, non-humans re-emerging.

### Playstyle: Adventure-of-the-Week with Travel
- **Not a hex crawl.** Players wanted globe-trotting wonder, not violent cartography.
- **Not an epic overarching plot.** A 13-session time limit meant no dangling storylines.
- PCs follow their fortunes from place to place. Progression comes from traveling farther afield — foot, horseback, passenger ship, maybe their own ship.

### Time Limit: 13 Sessions
- Three months of weekly play. The game must feel complete at session 13.
- Can petition players for renewal, but must be able to walk away with a bow on top.

## Character Creation Approach

### Motivations, Not Goals
- **Motivation** = an ongoing drive that's never finished ("fight evil," "amass wealth").
- **Goal** = a specific endpoint ("kill Satan," "pay off the mortgage").
- Characters need motivations that drive them to adventure *and* to leave home.
- No individual character goals — can't promise to wrap up four player stories in 13 sessions.

### Backstory: Keep It Minimal
- Players wanted growth through gameplay and humble origins. Complex backstories are unnecessary.
- "I was born on a farm, hated it, learned from the local wizard, and headed out." That's enough.

### Establishing Relationships: The Shared-Event Method
- All characters grew up in or near the same hometown.
- Each player writes a 1–2 sentence formative childhood event.
- Events are shuffled to another player who adds 1–2 sentences about their character's involvement.
- Shuffled again to a third player for another 1–2 sentences.
- Returned to the original player. GM curates throughout.
- These are childhood events — characters went separate ways afterward and reunite at campaign start. Relationships have a starting point but are essentially fresh.

## The Pitch

Two elements:

1. **The Hometown:** A small, forgotten kingdom in an isolated corner of the Angryverse. Hardy, self-sufficient people. Roads to neighboring kingdoms and a small port. Details left vague so the GM can fill in whatever the characters need (tribes for a barbarian, a citadel for a paladin, a mad wizard for a wizard).

2. **The Festival of the Harbinger:** Every decade (or half-century), a comet appears in a different constellation, foretelling the coming era. The characters have returned home for this event before heading out into the wider world. The comet marks the beginning of a new chapter — both for the world and for the characters.

## Key Principles

- **Rules and processes are training wheels.** Once you've internalized them, you muddle through without them. The codified advice is for others.
- **Don't ask players to predict what they'll like.** Trust them to tell you when they *do* like something. Use constrained exercises to bypass conscious prediction.
- **The pitch is a contract.** If players rebel later, you can point out they signed off on it.
- **No GM with any sense would create a game that wanted for complex backstories.**
