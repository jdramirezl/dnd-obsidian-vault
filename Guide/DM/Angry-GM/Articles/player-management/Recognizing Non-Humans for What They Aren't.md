# Recognizing Non-Humans for What They Aren't

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Recognizing Non-Humans for What they Aren't](https://theangrygm.com/recognizing-characters/)
**Date:** May 1, 2020
**Series:** Make Players Care

---

## Summary

Part of the "Make Players Care" series, this article explains what makes fictional characters — especially NPCs — feel like real people to players. The key concept is **relatability**: tricking the social parts of the brain into forming human-type bonds with nonexistent characters. Relatability is an illusion, not realism — a stage version of a person that fools people into caring.

---

## Core Concept: Relatability vs. Realism

- **Relatability** means the audience can form some kind of emotional relationship with a character. It's the highest praise for a creator.
- **Realistic characters are paradoxically unreal.** Relatability is an illusion — a shiny, stage version of someone who *could* be real if you squint.
- People are hardwired for social connection. Social isolation is painful and even deadly. Given half a chance, most players will jump at forming pretend relationships with NPCs.
- **Never base a character on a real person.** It always backfires.

---

## The Recognizably Human Qualities

### 1. Personality
- The unique way a character thinks, talks, and acts.
- Personality alone doesn't make something human — it makes a human thing *interesting*.
- Too much personality makes a character unreal. Less is more for emotional engagement.
- One unique quirk or trait is enough to start. Add layers as screen time increases.

### 2. Vulnerability, Fear, and Pain
- Possibly the **most** recognizably human quality of all.
- Humans are vulnerable — a single random event can destroy everything. When we see something else that's vulnerable, we instantly see ourselves in it.
- Every NPC needs something that makes them vulnerable, scares them, or hurts them.

### 3. Hopes and Aspirations
- Humans can imagine a world different from the one they're in. We can picture a bright future and aspire to it.
- When we see something that has hopes and aspirations, we recognize it as human.

### 4. Values, Virtues, and Beliefs
- Every human holds certain ideas sacred — love, justice, family, courage, integrity.
- Living up to values is hard. It takes will, courage, and sacrifice. No one manages it all the time.
- When we see a fictional character make a sacrifice or take a stand, it gives us hope that we can too.

### 5. Flaws and Failings
- We're all flawed. We make mistakes, pursue selfish ends, and fail to live up to our ideals.
- Good people with flaws are kindred spirits. They help us develop a healthy self-image.
- **A strong character isn't flawless.** A strong character has failings but takes responsibility for them and overcomes them. Flawless characters are disempowering lies.

### 6. Self-Awareness
- Humans know they've got thoughts in their heads and are always trying to work them out.
- When a character struggles with inner conflict and tries to figure out what's happening inside, that's recognizably human.
- Self-awareness is rare in real humans, but we recognize it as a quality humans *should* have.

### 7. Challenge, Growth, and Agency
- Humans strive to control their destinies. We face challenges, grow stronger, and get better — by choice.
- **Agency is what separates characters from objects.** Damsels in distress suck not because of inclusion issues, but because they have no agency.
- NPCs we care about are the ones we see doing *something* to master their own fate — even if they need the heroes' help.

---

## Key Principles

- **Recognition is the 'in.'** If players see something familiar in a character — something of themselves or someone they know — the social parts of their brain kick in.
- **Show, don't tell, don't explain.** These qualities are for the GM to know and for the NPC to reveal gradually through play. Never dump backstory.
- **Not every NPC needs all qualities upfront.** Players just need to *believe* there's more under the surface. If they dig, they should find it.
- **Seed one fully-developed NPC early.** After players encounter one deeply human NPC, they'll assume every NPC has that depth — and you just have to not disappoint them when they check.

---

## Running Example: Stedd the Sorcerer

- Introduced as a scrawny, starving teenager threatened by armed soldiers (vulnerability).
- Dreamed of reuniting with an uncle — wanted family and safety (hopes/aspirations).
- Troubled by his uncontrolled magic hurting people — believed causing pain was wrong (values).
- Tried to flee the party to protect them, but was talked down (flaws, self-awareness).
- Was already on the run when the party met him — not waiting for rescue (agency).
