# A Campaign Managery Digression: Changing Character Faces

## Key Advice

**Let your players change characters if they want to.** There is no margin in refusing them. Character swapping is fundamentally different from character death — it's planned, controlled, and far less disruptive. A player trapped in a character they hate is bad for the player, bad for the group, and bad for the game. Your job as Campaign Manager is to facilitate the change with minimal disruption, not to gatekeep it.

## Summary

This article grew out of a Discord debate about character death. Angry discovered that some GMs only allow character changes through character death, which he considers backwards. He presents his near-universal character swap policy and uses it to teach a broader lesson about writing practical policies vs. theoretical ones.

**Angry's Character Swap Policy (two conditions):**
1. The player must work with the GM to make the transition minimally disruptive. The old character gets written out, the new character gets worked into the campaign. This may take a session or two.
2. If a player changes characters too frequently, the GM has a conversation about it. No written rule needed — just managerial judgment.

Key insights:
- **Changing characters ≠ Character death** — Swaps are planned, paced, and don't carry the emotional/mechanical baggage of death.
- **Don't advertise the policy** — Players should ask for it. If you notice an unhappy player, offer it proactively.
- **Always ask why** — Not to gatekeep, but to ensure there isn't an underlying problem that a new character won't fix.
- **Manuals make terrible managers** — Written policies handle common, foreseeable situations. Unusual situations require human judgment. Write policies for when things go bad, then make exceptions when they go well.
- **Sanity-check your theories** — When designing policies away from the table, always ask: "How would this actually play out with real people at my table?"

## Actionable Steps

1. **Adopt a permissive character swap policy** — If a player asks to change characters, say yes. Work together on timing and transition.
2. **Require collaborative transition** — The player plays their old character in good faith until a natural break point. No intentional suicide or rushing.
3. **Ask why, but don't gatekeep** — Understand the reason for the change. Look for underlying issues (frustration with mechanics, interpersonal conflict, etc.) that a new character won't solve.
4. **Don't impose cooldown periods** — Forcing a player to "give it three more sessions" just traps an unhappy player. By the time they ask, they've already decided.
5. **Address frequency as a management issue** — If someone churns through characters, have a direct conversation about what's driving it. Don't write a rule; be a manager.
6. **Write policies for worst cases, not best cases** — Your policy should handle the bad scenario by default. Make exceptions when things go smoothly, not the other way around.

> [!tip] The Real-World Policy Test
> Whenever you're designing a rule or policy away from the table, stop and ask: "What would this actually look like with the real humans at my table? How will they actually feel? What are they actually likely to do?" If your policy only works for spherical chickens in a vacuum, rewrite it.
