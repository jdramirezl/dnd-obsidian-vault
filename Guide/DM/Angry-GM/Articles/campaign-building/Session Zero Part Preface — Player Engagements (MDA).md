# Session Zero Part Preface: Player Engagements (MDA)

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Session Zero Blitz (Part Preface): Engage!](https://theangrygm.com/session-zero-part-preface/)
**Date:** August 29, 2017
**Series:** Campaign Building / Session Zero (Part 2 of 3)

---

## Why This Matters

One key to building a campaign players want to play is understanding **why they want to play**. The MDA (Mechanics, Dynamics, Aesthetics) Approach from video game design provides a far better model than Robin Laws' player types.

### What's Wrong with Robin Laws' Model

- Assigns each player to one and precisely one classification — reality is a mix
- Describes **how** players engage (behavior) not **why** (motivation)
- Two "method actors" might play for completely different reasons (creative expression vs. escapism)

### The MDA Improvement

- Players are combinations of preferences defining why they play
- Preferences can shift with mood
- When you see the group's dominant preferences, build the campaign around them

## The Eight Engagements

Pick 2–3 as **core engagements** that define how it feels to play most of the time.

### 1. Sensory Pleasure

Joy from senses being stimulated — artwork, miniatures, props, maps, music.

- **Not viable as a core engagement** for TTRPGs
- But you can **ruin it** by insisting on "theater of the mind" for players who want maps and minis

### 2. Challenge

Fun of **winning and overcoming obstacles**. Not difficulty — the pleasure of knowing your choices determine the outcome.

- Hard-wired into most RPGs (mechanics resolve success/failure based on choices)
- Not synonymous with combat — puzzles, mysteries, tactical decisions all count
- Challenge-seekers have strong system preferences (D&D = combat; Gumshoe = mysteries)
- To make it core: emphasize that failure is possible and success must be earned

### 3. Discovery

Fun of **combing the world for hidden stuff** — treasure, secrets, lore, Easter eggs.

- Joy from intellectual mastery; must be driven by player actions (could have missed it)
- About persistence and thoroughness
- Requires non-linear gameplay, richly detailed worlds, optional content
- NPCs need secrets; locations need backstories — all discoverable AND missable
- Significant prep for content that might never surface

### 4. Expression

Joy of **creating and sharing things uniquely yours**.

- Push against restrictions, play against stereotypes, complex backstories, weird quirks
- Less common among players — most expression-seekers become GMs
- To make it core: let players choose campaign goals or have their storylines form plot threads
- For a single expression-seeker: ensure unique character options, freedom for unusual solutions

> Fantasy-seeking players thrive under expression-seeking GMs.

### 5. Fantasy

Fun of **escapism** — losing yourself in another world. Not exploring it (Discovery) or creating it (Expression), just **existing in it**.

- Roleplay every shopkeeper, run businesses, gamble in taverns, don't want the game to stop between adventures
- Need a **defined role** in the world beyond powers and backstory
- Key difference from Expression: expression-seekers carve out a unique place; fantasy-seekers want to **fit into** a place
- Requires rich world-building with interaction opportunities beyond combat and plot advancement

### 6. Fellowship

Fun of **belonging to a group** — social pleasure, cooperative gameplay.

- Core engagement of RPGs in general by default
- Can't do much to amplify it, but you can **destroy it** with evil PCs, antisocial loners, or anything that disrupts team dynamics
- **The quiet player:** Shows up every game, drifts to background, barely speaks, chooses actions based on group needs — that's your fellowship-seeker. They just want to belong. **Leave them alone.**

### 7. Narrative

Satisfaction of **experiencing a good story** — one that makes sense, hits the right beats, has tension and resolution.

- Often confused with Fantasy and Expression, but fundamentally different
- Real narrative-seekers love eastern RPGs and visual novels — they want a good story more than freedom
- **What kills them:** Sandbox play, freeform play, collaborative storytelling, goalless dicking around
- To make it core: solid hook, promise of a good ending, adventure-path style, clear motivations, strong plot threads

> Many RPG players are narrative-seekers — they got into the hobby wanting to experience favorite books and movies from the inside.

### 8. Submission

Fun of **dicking around without too much thought** — unwinding, losing track of time.

- Beer-and-pretzels play, murderhobo dungeon crawls, kick-in-the-door gameplay
- Sit back during complex parts, wake up for mindless action
- Works well combined with Challenge and Discovery
- Don't punish fun actions with long-reaching, not-fun consequences
- **Danger combo:** Submission + Expression = wacky hijinks and nothing else
