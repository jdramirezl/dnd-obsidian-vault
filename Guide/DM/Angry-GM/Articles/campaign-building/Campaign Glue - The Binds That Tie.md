# Campaign Glue: The Binds That Tie

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Glue: The Binds that Tie Your Campaign Together](https://theangrygm.com/campaign-glue-the-binds-that-tie/)
**Date:** April 24, 2017
**Topic:** What holds a party together and why it matters

---

## Core Idea

Every campaign needs two things: a **Shape** (how adventures connect) and **Glue** (how characters stick together). Shape is about adventure continuity. Glue is about character continuity. Of all forms of continuity (setting, story, character, system), **character continuity is the most important** — it's what actually defines a campaign.

## The Problem: Conflict Pulls Parties Apart

- RPG groups are made of different characters with different goals, desires, and priorities
- Conflict is what makes stories interesting — without it, no encounters, no meaningful choices, no interesting social interactions
- But conflict is also a force constantly shoving the party apart
- Every time a conflict happens inside a group, the benefit of being in the group must outweigh the negatives — or the group falls apart

## Why RPG Groups Don't Split (And Why That's Worse)

- Players know they can't split the party, don't want to give up their characters, and dismiss conflict as "part of the game"
- So the group becomes like a couple that *should* divorce but won't
- Discussion and compromise turn into stubborn arguments and standoffs
- Bickering spills from characters into players
- The game stops being fun → either "start over" or players drift away

## What Glue Actually Does

1. **Provides a common motivation** around which each player designs their character — common ground for resolving conflicts
2. **Gives the GM enforcement power** — if a character abandons the glue, the GM can say "your character no longer fits; fix it or make a new one"
3. **Acts as an agreement** every player signs AND the GM can enforce

## Glue Must Come Before the Campaign

- Players must create characters with the glue in mind
- Players must agree to the glue
- GM must enforce the glue during character generation
- Can be simple: "This is a campaign about saving the world from evil. Make a character willing to risk everything for that."

## Types of Glue (Weakest → Strongest)

### 1. Personal Relationships (Weakest)
- "You're all old friends / family / a pre-existing party"
- Emotional connections are backstory, not future-facing motivations
- Past connections are inorganic — decreed but untested by actual play
- Easy to say characters like each other; hard to maintain when play reveals friction
- **Verdict:** Not really glue. It's backstory.

### 2. Common Means
- Each character has different motivations, but adventuring gets them all what they want
- Weakest *real* glue — each character is only tied to the group as long as every adventure serves their individual goal
- Falls apart the moment someone has to sacrifice their goal for someone else's
- This is what you get with **no forethought** — accidental glue
- **Verdict:** Works if you strengthen it with compatible motives and conflict prevention

### 3. Common Motivation
- Every character individually wants the same thing (money, heroism, fame, serving the greater good)
- Great for loose, adventure-of-the-week meatball campaigns
- Conflicts resolve naturally: "which choice gets us more of the thing we all want?"
- Remaining conflicts are about what lines can/can't be crossed — which is healthy
- **Verdict:** Solid for episodic campaigns

### 4. Employers and Organizations
- External version of common motivation or common means
- Provides a framework and code of conduct for resolving disagreements
- Creates a feeling that you HAVE TO resolve conflicts because you HAVE TO work together
- GM has a way to intervene before conflict spills from characters to players
- **Verdict:** Second-best glue. External enforcement makes it stronger than internal motivation alone

### 5. Common Goal (Strongest)
- Like common motivation but with a **definite endpoint** — progress is quantifiable
- Easier to make decisions and compromises when you can measure what you're giving up
- External and tangible — everyone can point to it and say "I agree to pursue that"
- Examples: overthrow a tyrant, save the world, buy an airship
- **Verdict:** Strongest glue. External beats internal, goals beat motivations

## Strength Hierarchy

> External beats internal. Goals beat motivations. Motivations beat means. Means beats relationships.

## Surface Preparation (Enhancing the Glue)

- "No evil alignment" rule
- Be wary of classes that skew evil-ish (warlocks, necromancers, assassins)
- Shared connections: same hometown, same faith, same school
- These aren't strong enough to be glue alone, but they enhance whatever glue you're using

## Key Takeaway

There is no *best* glue and no *perfect* glue. There's just *strong* glue. Pick the right type for your campaign shape, establish it before play begins, and enforce it.
