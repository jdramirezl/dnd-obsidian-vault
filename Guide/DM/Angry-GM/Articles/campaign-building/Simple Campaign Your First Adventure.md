# Simple Campaign: Your First Adventure

## Key Advice

**The first adventure launches the campaign.** It should be short (aim for 2 sessions), simple (one goal, no side objectives), and perfectly fit the campaign premise. Start with action, give players space to introduce characters afterward, and lay groundwork for the future by seeding random details (names, symbols, organizations) you can build on later. The premise's third paragraph — describing what's happening the moment before the adventure starts — is the key to making characters and the first adventure slot together.

## Summary

Part of the *Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign* series. This lesson covers step 3: writing the first adventure. Character generation (step 2) and adventure-building can happen in either order or simultaneously.

**Finish the premise first:**
- The third paragraph of the premise describes the starting situation: where are the characters, why are they there, and what's about to happen?
- This paragraph tells players what backstory their characters are allowed to have AND tells the GM where the adventure starts.
- Example: "The would-be adventurers meet for the first time at a roadside inn on their way to Acrea's Hold... the heroes are called upon to resolve some trouble on the road."

**What makes a good first adventure:**

- **Keep it short and simple** — aim for 2 sessions (you'll probably need 3). One objective, no side quests. One climactic encounter. 5–7 encounters of varying difficulty, mostly combat with 1–2 non-combat challenges. A few non-encounter spaces for interaction.
- **Keep eyes on the prize** — simple goal, revealed at the start, the whole adventure is about achieving it. Save complex, evolving, or mysterious goals for later adventures.
- **Show off the campaign** — the first adventure should perfectly fit the premise. If it's a dungeon-plundering campaign, the first adventure is a dungeon plunder.
- **Start with action** — the very first scene should demand mechanical action (ideally "roll initiative"). Players can't just start *being* their characters from the word go. Action first, roleplay second.
- **Give players space** — after the opening action, back off and let players introduce their characters to each other. Don't force it, but don't interrupt it either. Once they're done, transition to the next scene via NPC interaction.
- **Lay groundwork** — seed random details for the future: give goblins a tribal symbol, mention a mysterious organization, name an NPC's guild. You don't need to know what any of it means yet.

**What won't ruin your first adventure:**
- **Simplicity** — don't overthink. Depth emerges from gameplay, not complicated plots.
- **Clichés and coincidences** — meeting at an inn by accident is fine. Players barely remember how campaigns start.
- **Published material** — use monster stats, traps, and treasures from the rulebooks you paid for.

**Motivation and the first adventure:**
- Every character needs a motivation (covered in the character generation lesson).
- You don't need to know the characters' motivations before building the first adventure — the premise ensures they'll fit.
- Hit the "four Gs": Gold, Glory, the Gods, and the Greater Good. If the adventure offers a mix of these, most characters will go along.
- Worst case: remind players this is the first adventure and ask them to just go with it.

**Appendix A — Adventure-building steps:**
1. **Premise** — a goal and an obstacle. Keep it simple. ("Stolen medicine → orcs' lair in ruins → get it back.")
2. **Summary** — imagine the adventure playing out. Write it down. Rewrite until it includes all the good-first-adventure qualities.
3. **Break into acts** — 2–3 broad parts, each with a specific sub-goal.
4. **Distribute scenes and encounters** — spread 5–7 challenges across the acts. Include non-encounter interaction scenes.
5. **Design the encounters** — brainstorm ideas, note difficulty. Mostly easy/moderate, end on a hard climax. Include 1–2 non-combat challenges.
6. **Finish the horse** — stat blocks, maps, treasure, flavor text, NPC descriptions.

**Appendix B — The Meet Cute (first 5 minutes):**
- Plan the first 5 minutes in detail. Write it down. Overwrite. This is the one time you should script flavor text.
- Pre-roll initiative secretly. Introduce each PC one at a time as their turn comes up. First PC arrives at the scene, sees danger, acts. Second PC arrives, sees the fight, joins in. And so on.
- This creates a cinematic, energizing opening that settles GM nerves and hooks players immediately.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Finish your premise's third paragraph** — define exactly where the characters are and what's happening the moment before the adventure starts.
2. **Build a short, simple adventure** — one goal, 5–7 encounters, 2–3 acts. Use the step-by-step process in Appendix A if you're stuck.
3. **Start with action** — make the first scene a fight or urgent challenge. Introduce characters one at a time via initiative order.
4. **Leave space after the action** — let players roleplay introductions, then transition via NPC interaction.
5. **Seed future content** — add names, symbols, organizations, and rumors you can develop later. Don't worry about what they mean yet.
6. **Script your first 5 minutes** — write detailed flavor text for the opening. Read it aloud to jumpstart the session.
