# Session Zero Part Zero — What The Hell is Session Zero For?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/session-zero-part-zero/)
**Date:** August 28, 2017
**Series:** Session Zero Blitz (Part Zero)

---

## Overview

Session Zero is a pre-campaign meeting where the GM gathers information to develop a campaign premise the players will want to say yes to. It is **not** a collaborative committee exercise — it's an information-gathering tool the GM uses. The GM listens, then the GM builds. This article defines the purpose of Session Zero and lays out the 8 key decisions a GM needs to make (or deliberately not make) to turn a campaign idea into a campaign premise.

## The Right Mindset

- Session Zero fits **after** you decide to start a campaign but **before** you have a premise. It's part of developing the premise.
- It empowers you to make decisions that give the campaign the best chance to succeed.
- **You** are still building the game. **You** still own it. The players provide input; you make the decisions.
- Before Session Zero, **everything is optional** — your ideas, your players, even the rule system. Walk in prepared to compromise, dump ideas, or dump players if needed.

## What You Bring

Write down whatever ideas you already have and decide how important each one is to you. Also decide how important this specific group of players is to you. If the ideas matter more than the players (e.g., Internet strangers you can replace), hold firm on ideas. If the players matter more (friends, established group), be prepared to kiss unworkable ideas goodbye.

## The 8 Decisions

You don't have to make every decision for every campaign. Deliberately choosing to leave something undecided is itself a purposeful choice. But know what the decisions are.

### Decision 1: Scheduling *(Required)*

How often are you running games? This cannot be left undecided — it's the single biggest factor that will determine whether you can run the game you want.

**When frequency drops below ~3 times/month:**
- Players forget what's going on between sessions. Complex multi-adventure plots become hard to follow.
- Game time becomes extremely valuable. Every session must feel productive.
- You must keep pace up, stay focused, cut banter, and focus on player actions.
- You're probably limited to adventure-of-the-week style "plate of meatballs" games.

### Decision 2: Commitment and Reliability *(Required)*

Even with a frequent game, unreliable attendance creates the same problems as infrequent play — but worse, because players also miss chunks of the story.

**Ask honestly:** "How many games are each of you actually going to be able to show up for?" Attendance problems are only problems if you don't know they're coming. If you know, you can build a game everyone enjoys at that level of commitment.

### Decision 3: Lifespan *(Optional)*

How long will the campaign last? Three months? A year? Two years?

- **For campaigns with ongoing plots:** Not starting with the end in mind is one of the biggest mistakes GMs make. Leads to abandoned, unfinished campaigns.
- **For campaigns without ongoing plots:** You can still set limits — a target experience level (e.g., 10th level), a specific goal (buy the starship, retake the homeland), or just "we'll see."
- **Endless campaigns** work well with adventure-of-the-week styles. Don't do it with complex plots — you need to know how much time your story threads have to fill.

### Decision 4: Rules System *(Usually Pre-decided)*

- **Choose the system designed for the game you want to run.** Every system has strengths and weaknesses. Don't fight your rule system.
- **Choose a system you know well.** Learning a new system means discovering things that don't work in practice the way they seem on paper.
- **If learning a new system:** Run something short and simple first (3 months). Don't commit to a long, complex campaign in an unfamiliar system.
- **Maintain veto power** on the system decision. It matters more to the GM than to the players.

### Decision 5: Genre and Tone *(Usually Implicit)*

- **Genre** is usually implied by the system, setting, and premise. Rarely an explicit decision.
- **Tone** — the general mood and feel — should be chosen more deliberately. It determines what will be tolerated and what will break the game.
- **Benchmarks help.** Lord of the Rings novels vs. LotR movies vs. Hobbit movies are three very different tones within the same genre. Game of Thrones is a completely different tone. Have a reference point in your head.

### Decision 6: Setting *(Variable)*

Key sub-decisions:
- **Size:** Where does the game start? How far will it eventually go?
- **Importance:** Is the setting just a container for adventures, or is it central to the premise?
- **Type:**
  - **Published setting** — less work, setting details already exist.
  - **Homebrew setting** — you invent it (lots of up-front work if the setting is important).
  - **Non-setting** — the default implied by the core rules. Assume the rulebook's races/classes/gods/magic are true, invent a starting town, and go.

The real point: provide enough information for players to generate characters that fit.

### Decision 7: Options and Restrictions *(Can Be a Non-choice)*

What character creation options are allowed? Two key factors:

- **Complexity:** Every option you allow is one you must understand. Players referencing six different books at the table is awful. Obscure feat + class ability interactions that halt play are awful.
- **Tone:** Every option affects genre and tone. Gunslingers and artificers turn fantasy into steampunk. Psionics add a sci-fi feel.

**Critical advice:** Always **opt in** rather than **opt out.** Choose a few specific supplements that work well with your game and allow those — don't allow everything except a few known problems. No RPG was meant to run with all mods installed.

### Decision 8: Shape and Glue *(The Premise)*

How do the adventures fit together (shape) and what keeps the party together (glue)? These are central to the premise and affected by every other decision. Getting to a solid premise the players will buy into is the whole point of Session Zero.
