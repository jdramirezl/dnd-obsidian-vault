# Conflicted and Misaligned: Storytelling, Conflicts, and Morality

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/conflicted-and-misaligned/)
**Date:** July 12, 2017
**Series:** Start Your Own Campaign / World-Building

---

## What Conflict Actually Is

Every story is about resolving a conflict. No conflict = no story. At its simplest, conflict is what happens when two forces are in opposition.

But the key insight: **the essence of any conflict is two ideas at odds with each other.** Normally we balance opposing ideas. A conflict becomes a confrontation when a side must be chosen, a priority set, or a compromise negotiated.

## Internal vs. External Conflicts

- **Internal ("Innie")** — Two ideas you hold are in opposition and you must choose. Example: valuing your time vs. respecting social order when stuck behind a slow person in line.
- **External ("Outie")** — Each idea has champions who confront each other. Example: Batman (social order) vs. the Joker (freedom/anarchy).

Most stories have both. Before the hero resolves the external conflict, they usually resolve their internal one first.

## Conflicts in Disguise

Specific confrontations between characters are usually about **general, underlying conflicts**:
- Batman vs. Joker → social order vs. freedom/anarchy
- The Dark Knight's surveillance subplot → individual rights vs. security
- Ferris Bueller vs. Principal Skinner → freedom/experience vs. rules/structure

The same essential conflict (order vs. freedom) reaches **different conclusions** in different stories:
- *The Dark Knight*: order wins (Batman keeps his rules)
- *Demolition Man*: freedom wins (autocracy is the villain)
- *Ferris Bueller*: freedom wins (rules are stifling)

## Good vs. Evil is NOT a Conflict

**Good and evil are value judgments applied to conflicts, not forces in conflict themselves.**

- In *The Dark Knight*, social order = good, anarchy = evil
- In *Demolition Man*, social order = evil, freedom = good
- In *Ferris Bueller*, rules = bad, freedom = good

The conflict is always between ideas (order vs. freedom). The author decides which side is "good" or "evil" based on the story they want to tell. Neither side is inherently good or evil.

## D&D's Alignment Problem

D&D presents a world with **objective morality** — good and evil are real, detectable forces. This creates several problems:

### The Fundamental Incoherence
- The **Law/Chaos axis** is a genuine conflict (social order vs. freedom) with no value judgment.
- The **Good/Evil axis** is a set of value judgments with no underlying conflict.
- Asking players to choose one of each is nonsensical — one is a conflict, the other is a judgment.

### The Overlap Problem
- D&D's definitions of "good" include elements of "lawful" (putting others first, greater good).
- D&D's definitions of "evil" include elements of "chaotic" (selfishness, putting desires above others).
- Result: Chaotic Good ≈ "mostly good" and Lawful Evil ≈ "sort of evil."
- 4th Edition was almost honest enough to admit the system was really: Very Good → Good → Neutral → Evil → Very Evil.

### The GM's Impossible Task
- Alignment requires the GM to declare what is objectively good and evil in complex moral situations.
- No two people will ever see alignment the same way.
- 5E half-assed it — alignment exists but does nothing. It's written on character sheets and forgotten.

## What Should Replace Alignment

Two options:
1. **Go all-in** — Develop and communicate clear moral rules for your world. Lots of work, but can create fantastic campaigns.
2. **Remove it completely** — Pull alignments off gods, planes, societies, monsters, magic, everything. Decide what devils and demons mean in a world without objective evil. Also lots of work.

The worst option is what 5E does: leave it in but ignore it.

## Practical Takeaway

Understanding dramatic conflict — what it is, how it drives stories, and how value judgments differ from the conflicts themselves — is the foundation for building settings and stories around thematic tensions rather than simplistic good-vs-evil frameworks. This is the prerequisite for the conflict-driven mythology system in the "Conflicted Beliefs" articles.
