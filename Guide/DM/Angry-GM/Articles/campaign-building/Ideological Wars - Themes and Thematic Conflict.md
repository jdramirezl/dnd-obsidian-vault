# Ideological Wars: Themes, Conflict, and RPG Settings

## Key Advice

**Good vs. Evil is not a thematic conflict — it's the author picking sides.** Real thematic conflicts are between two ideas that are both valid but incompatible (fate vs. free will, progress vs. tradition, freedom vs. security). These conflicts generate endless story material because neither side is automatically right, forcing players into genuinely interesting choices.

## Summary

This article explains thematic conflict as a worldbuilding and story-generation tool. A theme is a big idea that keeps recurring in a story (fate, free will, industrialization, mortality). A **thematic conflict** occurs when two themes are individually valid but incompatible — like fate vs. free will in Lord of the Rings. These conflicts act as narrative forces that swirl through the setting and collide to create encounters, adventures, and campaigns. They provide a "color palette" that ties disparate story elements together and makes the campaign feel cohesive. The article argues that Good vs. Evil is not a thematic conflict but a value judgment the narrator applies *to* themes — and that the most interesting stories play both sides of their thematic conflicts across the moral spectrum. Building 2-3 strong thematic conflicts into your setting gives you infinite fodder for factions, religions, philosophies, wars, and moral dilemmas.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Identify 2-3 Thematic Conflicts for Your Setting** — Pick pairs of ideas that are individually valid but incompatible when taken to extremes. Examples:
   - Fate vs. Free Will
   - Progress vs. Tradition
   - Freedom vs. Security
   - Individual vs. Community
   - Reason vs. Passion
   - Order vs. Chaos (with nuance, not just labels)
2. **Don't Assign Good/Evil to Themes** — Resist making one side of a conflict "the good side." Industrialization isn't evil; unchecked industrialization is destructive. Tradition isn't good; rigid tradition causes stagnation. Let both sides have merits and dangers.
3. **Use Thematic Conflicts to Generate Content** — Each conflict gives you three themes to play with: each idea in isolation, plus the conflict between them. Use them to create factions, religions, NPCs, quests, and moral dilemmas.
4. **Build Conflicts Into the Setting's Fabric** — Embed thematic conflicts in your gods, races, magic systems, organizations, and geography. When players encounter these elements, they unconsciously absorb the themes without you having to exposit them.
5. **Create Situations, Not Sermons** — Present scenarios where the thematic conflict forces a choice with no clear right answer. "The impoverished farmers can start a coal mine — some favor the prosperity, some oppose the environmental cost, and no one knows how it'll turn out." Let players wrestle with it.
6. **Use Themes as a Cohesion Tool** — When you need a new adventure or encounter, ask which of your thematic conflicts could drive it. This ensures new content feels like it belongs to the same campaign, even if the specific plot is unrelated to previous adventures.

> [!tip] Three Themes for the Price of One
> Every thematic conflict gives you three themes: Idea A, Idea B, and the conflict between them. Two thematic conflicts give you effectively seven themes (4 individual + 2 conflicts + the interplay). That's more than enough to sustain a multi-year campaign and make every adventure feel like it belongs to the same story.
