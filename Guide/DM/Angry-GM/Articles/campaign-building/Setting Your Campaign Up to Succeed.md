# Setting Your Campaign Up to Succeed

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Setting Your Campaign Up to Succeed](https://theangrygm.com/setting-your-campaign-up/)
**Date:** September 10, 2018
**Series:** Campaign Building / Worldbuilding

---

## What IS a Setting?

A setting is **not** just a time and place. It's **time, place, and mood** — the circumstances under which the story takes place. It defines the tone of the story.

**Example:** *You've Got Mail* and *Teenage Mutant Ninja Turtles* are both set in 1990s New York City. But the settings are completely different — one is cheerful and magical, the other is dark, dirty, and dangerous.

### Setting = Premise in the Shape of a World

A campaign setting is all the details needed to justify the characters' existence and the plots they experience. It provides the excuses necessary for the game to happen — from the rules of the world to the nature of its inhabitants to the backstory for every adventure.

## Worldbuilding Is Exceptions-Based

You never build a world from nothing. You start with a set of common assumptions (a genre, a known reference point) and then highlight the exceptions.

- **"My world is like medieval fantasy, but…"**
- Pop culture and reality provide common-ground assumptions
- The more unique your setting, the longer the list of "buts"

### How Published Settings Work

This is exactly what core rulebooks do. The PHB, DMG, and Monster Manual establish: "We're doing medieval fantasy. Here's how OUR dwarves, elves, wizards, and dragons work." The homebrewing GM then only needs to define: "Here's what's different about MINE."

### Examples of Exceptions-Based Worldbuilding

- **Ghostbusters:** Opens in normal NYC, then shows a ghost. "Everything is normal. Except ghosts are real."
- **Game of Thrones:** Starts with knights on horseback (medieval). References fictional places (not Earth). Mysterious magical Others (magic exists but is rare). Dragons are extinct. Seasons last years. That's the whole foundation.

## Setting vs. Worldbuilding

- **Setting creation:** Building the details that justify your specific stories — purposeful and campaign-driven
- **Worldbuilding:** Often amounts to "I don't know what I'm trying to accomplish, so I'll draw a map, name kingdoms, and write a history textbook"

There's no one right way to create a setting, but there are wrong ways. The right approach depends on what your setting needs to do.

## How Much Detail Do You Need?

### The Fundamental Rule

> Stop creating details once you have sufficient detail to do whatever you're currently doing in the game without inviting further questions from an audience that has already agreed to reasonably suspend their disbelief.

- "This area is prone to earthquakes" — most people accept that. You don't need plate tectonics.
- Nobody questioned the Force until midichlorians ruined it.
- Don't explain things past the point where people stop asking questions.

### "Start Small and Build" Is Bad Advice

This common advice fails because:

1. It ignores that different campaigns need different amounts of macro-level planning
2. It doesn't recognize that core rulebooks have already done the first step for you
3. It treats all settings as the same when they serve fundamentally different purposes

**A political drama** (like Game of Thrones) requires knowing the political history before you start. You can't "start small and build" your way into a complex web of alliances and betrayals.

**A dungeon-of-the-week game** barely needs mythology beyond "what color are the cleric's spells."

## The Three Layers of Setting

### Layer 1: Common Assumptions (Genre)

The broad strokes everyone already knows — medieval fantasy, sci-fi, steampunk, etc. Core rulebooks handle most of this.

### Layer 2: Exceptions to the Genre

What makes YOUR world different from the default. This is the real creative work of setting design.

### Layer 3: Specific Details

Kingdoms, towns, NPCs, historical events — added on top of the foundation as needed to drive specific stories.

## Key Takeaways

- A setting is time, place, AND mood/circumstances
- The setting exists to justify the characters and their adventures
- Build by starting with known assumptions and defining exceptions
- The amount of detail needed depends entirely on what kind of campaign you're running
- Stop adding detail when reasonable suspension of disbelief covers the gap
- "Start small and build" is incomplete advice — sometimes you need the big picture first
