# Session Zero Part Preface — Engage!

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/session-zero-part-preface/)
**Date:** August 29, 2017
**Series:** Session Zero Blitz (Part Preface)

---

## Overview

Understanding *why* your players play is essential to building a campaign they'll enjoy. This article adapts the MDA (Mechanics, Dynamics, Aesthetics) framework from video game design to tabletop RPGs. Instead of labeling players as one fixed "type" (Robin Laws style), the MDA approach recognizes that every player is a mix of preferences — **engagements** — that explain *why* they play, not just *how* they behave. Identifying 2–3 core engagements for your group lets you design a campaign that hits the right emotional notes.

## Why MDA Over Robin Laws

Robin Laws' player classifications (power gamer, method actor, etc.) have two problems:
1. **One label per player.** Reality is messier — most players are a mix of preferences that shift with mood.
2. **Describes behavior, not motivation.** A "method actor" might be acting out of a desire for creative expression *or* a desire to lose themselves in a fantasy world. Those motivations are subtly different and require different GM responses.

The MDA approach looks at *combinations of preferences* that explain *why* people play. When you see that most of your group plays for a particular reason, you can build your game to cater to that reason.

## Core Engagements

Every campaign has core engagements — the brain tickles that define how it feels to play the game most of the time. Most GMs never choose these deliberately. Deliberate choice is better.

## The Eight Engagements

### 1. Sensory Pleasure

The joy of having your senses stimulated — artwork, miniatures, props, maps, music.

- **Not viable as a core engagement** for a tabletop RPG campaign. It's supplementary.
- **Can be ruined** by GMs who insist on "theater of the mind" when players genuinely enjoy maps and minis.
- **Takeaway:** If players prefer sensory pleasure, don't fight it. Provide visual and tactile aids.

### 2. Challenge

The fun of winning — overcoming obstacles through your own skill and choices. Central to challenge: knowing that success and failure are both possible and that *your actions* determine the outcome.

- **Hard-wired into most RPGs** via success/failure mechanics. Almost always at least a background engagement.
- **Not synonymous with combat.** Puzzles, mysteries, tactical decisions all count.
- **Not synonymous with difficulty.** An action-focused D&D game has challenge as a core engagement regardless of brutality level — as long as failure is possible and players must earn success.
- **Challenge-seekers have strong system preferences.** D&D/Pathfinder players expect combat challenges; Gumshoe/Call of Cthulhu players expect mysteries.

### 3. Discovery

The fun of combing the world for hidden things — treasure, Easter eggs, information, secrets, lore. Joy comes from intellectual mastery of the world through persistence and thoroughness.

- **Requires non-linear gameplay** in richly detailed worlds with optional, missable content.
- **Lore-seekers** want hidden backstories and secrets. **Scavenger hunters** want to map every corner and find every treasure.
- **Takes real GM work.** NPCs need secrets, locations need backstories, and all of it must be discoverable *and* missable. Much of it may never come out in play.

### 4. Expression

The joy of creating and sharing things that are uniquely yours. Expression-seekers push against restrictions, build unusual characters, write complex backstories, play against stereotypes, and enjoy pushing the game in unusual directions.

- **Harder than it looks as a core engagement.** Beyond character creation, opportunities for creative expression are limited and can be disruptive.
- **Less common among players** than you'd think — many expression-seekers gravitate to GMing.
- **Works as core engagement when:** players choose campaign goals, player storylines form the plot threads, or the system gives players narrative control.
- **For a single expression-seeker:** ensure interesting/unique character creation options, let them create a backstory that fits the world, work their plots in occasionally, and offer encounters with freedom for unusual solutions.

### 5. Fantasy

The fun of escapism — losing yourself in another world. Not exploring it (Discovery) or creating it (Expression), just *existing* in it. Fantasy-seekers role-play every shopkeeper interaction, want to run businesses, gamble in taverns, and don't want the game to stop between adventures.

- **Requires a rich, detailed world** and a defined role within it. The character's identity is defined by the role they fill in daily life, not just powers or backstory.
- **Key distinction from Expression:** Expression-seekers want to carve out a *unique* place. Fantasy-seekers want to *fit into* a place.
- **Fantasy-seekers thrive under expression-seeking GMs.**
- **Works as core engagement when:** the world is alive enough to interact with in ways beyond killing things and advancing the plot.

### 6. Fellowship

The fun of belonging to a group — social pleasure, cooperative gameplay, being part of a team.

- **Already a default core engagement** of RPGs by virtue of cooperative group play. Hard to make it *more* central.
- **Can be ruined** by allowing evil PCs, antisocial loners, secretive characters with dark pasts, or anything that disrupts team dynamics.
- **The quiet player mystery:** The player who shows up every session, drifts into the background, barely speaks, and chooses actions based on what the group needs? That's your fellowship-seeker. They just want to belong. **Leave them alone.** They're perfectly happy.

### 7. Narrative

The satisfaction of experiencing a good story — one with tension, climaxes, proper beats, clear motivations, and the promise of a good ending.

- **Often misidentified.** People see fantasy-seekers and expression-seekers role-playing and call them "story gamers." Real narrative-seekers are the ones who love eastern RPGs and visual novels — games about piloting a character between cutscenes and making limited choices.
- **Sandbox play, freeform play, collaborative storytelling, and goalless dicking around KILL the narrative-seeker.** A well-structured dungeon-of-the-week campaign with clear villains and adventure hooks is *better* for them than a freeform sandbox.
- **Good improvisation helps** — it guarantees the story always has direction. But it must be grounded in solid story structure.
- **Works as core engagement with:** a solid hook, the promise of a good ending, and an iconic story appropriate to the genre (heroes saving the world, rebels against the empire, etc.).
- **More common than you'd think.** Many players got into RPGs wanting to experience their favorite books and movies from the inside.

### 8. Submission

The fun of dicking around without too much thought — unwinding, chillaxing, losing track of time. In RPG terms: beer-and-pretzels play, the murderhobo dungeon crawl, kick-in-the-door-kill-the-thing-take-its-stuff.

- **Submission-seekers** sit back during complex parts and wake up for mindless action. They act at random to make something happen if the group talks too long.
- **Can seem like fellowship-seekers** (drifting into background) but wake up for the fun parts.
- **Core engagement of casual dungeon-crawler games.** Works well combined with Challenge and Discovery.
- **What kills submission-seekers:** fun actions having long-reaching, not-fun consequences. Complex story-building or world-building is out.
- **Warning:** A mix of submission-seekers and expression-seekers produces wacky hijinks and nothing else.

## Mixing and Matching

No player is purely one engagement. Most are a mix. Your job during Session Zero is to identify which 2–3 engagements the majority of your players prefer, then make those the core engagements of your campaign. The core engagements define how it *feels* to play the game most of the time — other engagements will still appear, just less prominently.
