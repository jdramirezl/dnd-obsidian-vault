# Conflicted Beliefs: Building a Perfect Five God Mythology for D&D

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/conflicted-beliefs/)
**Date:** July 18, 2018
**Series:** Start Your Own Campaign / World-Building

---

## Core Idea

Build your D&D mythology around **thematic conflicts** — the same dramatic tensions that drive your stories. Gods become embodiments of themes, and the conflicts between gods become the engine for every story in your world.

## Why Gods Should Embody Themes

- Classical mythology was about explaining the world through forces and ideas, not just providing moral frameworks.
- Gods as theme-embodiments means their influence is felt in every story without them needing to act directly. When war breaks out, people blame the war god. When families struggle, the hearth goddess is invoked.
- This makes gods **story seeds** — you can create adventures around any god's themes and they'll resonate with deeper ideas.

## Why Five is the Perfect Number

1. **Memorability** — People remember 3-5 things easily. A hundred gods (Forgotten Realms) means nobody remembers any of them.
2. **Shifting alliances** — With five, each god has two allies and two enemies. Alliances constantly shift, preventing permanent stalemates. (Three doesn't work — creates unbreakable stalemates. Seven works but alliances get too large before balance kicks in.)
3. **Minor gods** can fill out the mythology without increasing core complexity.

## The Three-Step Process

### Step 1: Choose Five Thematic Conflicts

Pick five pairs of opposing themes you want to explore. Both sides must be defensible — avoid simple good-vs-evil pairings.

**Example conflicts:**
- Justice vs. Compassion
- Tradition vs. Progress
- Freedom vs. Safety
- Idealism vs. Pragmatism
- Determinism vs. Free Will

### Step 2: Pair Ideals into a Star Diagram

You have 10 ideals (5 pairs). Each god embodies **two ideals from different conflicts**. Arrange them in a pentagram where:
- Opposite points are in conflict (the original pairs)
- Adjacent points are allies (share thematic proximity)

Pair ideals that are thematically close but not identical:
- Justice + Idealism
- Pragmatism + Free Will
- Freedom + Progress
- Compassion + Tradition
- Determinism + Safety

### Step 3: Build Gods from the Pairs

Take each pair of ideals and build a deity around them:
- Give them names, archetypes, domains, associated races, elements, or biomes
- **Erase explicit mention of the themes** — replace with a broader archetype. "Compassion + Tradition" becomes "the Mother Goddess" of home, hearth, and family.
- Connect to cleric domains, paladin oaths, and other game mechanics.

## Versatility

This system works for more than gods:
- Five power centers in a city
- Five schools of magic
- Five guilds or factions
- Five major races
- Five planes of existence
- Five NPCs in a short adventure

## Key Principles

- **No alignment on gods.** Gods are beyond good and evil — they represent fundamental forces. Each can be both good and evil depending on context.
- **Themes don't drive stories; conflicts do.** Set up gods so they're constantly tugging on the world in different directions.
- **Subtlety matters.** The dramatic conflicts should be lumps under the carpet, not labels on the gods.
