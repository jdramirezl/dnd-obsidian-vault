# Sitting Through Character Generation

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Sitting Through Character Generation with Angry](https://theangrygm.com/sitting-through-character-generation/)
**Date:** January 21, 2019
**Series:** Campaign Building / Starting a Campaign (Practical Journal)

---

## Angry's Character Generation Philosophy

### What the GM Needs from Each Character

Only three things matter at character creation:

1. **Archetype** — Race and class (the mechanical identity)
2. **Tie to the premise** — How does this character connect to the campaign's starting hook?
3. **Personal goal or motivation** — What drives them to engage in the campaign?

Everything else is math, statistics, and useless details. Backstory is just rationalization and motivation — keep it to an elevator pitch. Details should emerge **through play**, not in a pre-written document.

### Don't Request Written Backgrounds

- Give the GM the same level of detail as the campaign pitch: one short paragraph
- If you have deeper ideas, reveal them in-character during play
- Backstory that nobody witnesses is just rationalization — play the selfish character, and when someone asks why you gave a present to a poor kid, THEN share the story organically

## The Character Generation Process

### Angry's Standard Process

1. Meet players, figure out what game they want
2. Come up with a premise, send for approval
3. Work out character generation details, tell players to generate on their own
4. Ask each player for brief character details
5. Fill in world details to flesh out their backgrounds
6. Start running the game

### When You Need a Character Generation Session

Sometimes you can't just turn players loose — new players need guidance, experienced players may need oversight for game balance, and mixed-experience groups need the GM present.

## Running the Session: Step by Step

### Step 1: Nail Down Archetypes First

Go around the table and get everyone's race and class before anything else. Don't let anyone dive into details until all archetypes are set.

- **Spell out available options** in your campaign document with one paragraph each explaining what that choice means in YOUR world
- Keep options countable: races on one hand, classes on both hands
- Be clear that playing an elf means playing LIKE an elf — no "playing against type"

### Step 2: Help New Players Refine Vague Ideas

New players often fixate on a vague gameplay type ("good fighter with a little magic"). Pay attention to every word — they don't know the right terms but they know what they want.

**Example from Angry's game:**
- New player wanted "good fighter who can use a little magic" and wanted half-elf
- "A little magic" = supplementary, not primary → Cleric? No, didn't like being shackled to a god → Ranger fit perfectly (badass survivalist + eventual nature magic)
- Half-elf wasn't available in the setting → offered elf or human → she chose human
- Listen carefully to distinguish "gish" from "fighter with magic tricks"

### Step 3: Get the Story Hook from Each Player

Before anyone fills in numbers, find out how each character connects to the premise and what drives them. This guides mechanical choices and prevents surprises.

**Examples from Angry's party:**

| Player | Archetype | Story Hook | Motivation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Frack | Human Paladin (swashbuckler) | Member of ancient knightly order, trained by a hermit | Revive the order, earn glory and renown |
| Fourth Man | Human Rogue (crossbow) | Urchin/scoundrel with a good heart, on the run | Survive → eventually become Robin Hood |
| Frick | Human Fighter (dual-wield) | Noble house in disgrace, trained by alcoholic uncle | Cleanse the family name |
| Greenhorn | Human Ranger (archer) | Isolated frontier upbringing, goblin-hunter | Wandering, came for the festival (details TBD) |

### Step 4: Turn Experienced Players Loose, Focus on New Players

- Experienced players can handle the math on their own
- Spend your time walking new players through the process
- Use the mechanical choices as opportunities to plant story seeds (favored enemy → goblins because of frontier upbringing near elven lands)

### Step 5: Let New Players Be Thin

Don't force new players to have a complete backstory. Give them enough to start and let the character develop through play. They may even change characters once they understand the game better.

## Key Lessons

### On "Problem Players"

- Don't prejudge players based on war stories or self-descriptions
- The concept of "problem player" classifications (Power Gamer, Spotlight Hog, etc.) is terrible GMing — it hamstrings your ability to run for anyone
- Judge players by how they behave at YOUR table
- The only real problem player type: asshole

### On Party Composition

- An all-human party is fine (and can be great for a dark-age frontier setting)
- Don't panic about missing arcane magic or other "holes" — design encounters for YOUR party, not a theoretical one
- Players who wait to see what others pick before choosing are normal and helpful

### On Keeping Experienced Players in Check

- Mixed optimization levels (veterans + newbies) can wreck game balance
- Having oversight during character creation helps you know what's coming
- Core rules only is a reasonable constraint for Pathfinder/3.5 to prevent runaway optimization

## The Three-Part Archetype

D&D 5E's smartest design choice (copied by PF2): **Race + Background + Class** = Birth + Upbringing + Training. This gives players a complete identity framework before any numbers go on the sheet.
