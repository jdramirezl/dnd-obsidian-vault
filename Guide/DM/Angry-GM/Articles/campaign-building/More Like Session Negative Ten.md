# More Like Session Negative Ten

**Source:** [The Angry GM — More Like Session Negative Ten](https://theangrygm.com/more-like-session-negative-ten/)
**Date:** November 2, 2018
**Series:** A Very Angry Campaign (Campaign Diary)

---

## Overview

A real-world campaign diary showing how theory collides with reality. Covers a messy Session Zero with a mixed group of strangers, how to pitch a campaign when you have nothing prepared, and practical techniques for getting a game off the ground under imperfect conditions.

---

## The Normal Process

1. **Session Zero** — Figure out admin (schedule, system), pump players for preferences (past games, books, movies, video games), then craft a Pitch.
2. **The Pitch** — "This is where the game starts, how you end up together, and what I see happening." Outlines the premise, gives a glimpse of adventure types, helps players think about characters.
3. **Character Generation** — Iron out details, let players make characters while GM figures out how to start.

### What a Pitch Does
- Outlines the premise
- Gives a glimpse into adventure types
- Ensures players make characters that fit the game
- Is as much a design statement for the GM as a promise to players
- Doesn't need to be super focused or detailed — you can figure out specifics later

### Pitch Examples

**Focused:** "You're mercenaries who joined a trade caravan to a new kingdom. Dark things are afoot — the kingdom's leaders are in thrall to a dark force, poised to start an expansionist campaign. You'll stymie their efforts, form a resistance, and destroy whoever is pulling strings."

**Open-ended:** "A recently reclaimed frontier city lies on the edge of vast wilderness filled with ancient ruins. Adventuring guilds have established chapters. The city faces growing pains. You've been invited to join a guild. Adventures in ruins, frontier control, and city problems — no two alike."

## When Session Zero Fails

### The Situation
- 4 players: 2 experienced buddies, 1 complete newbie, 1 casual lapsed gamer
- The buddies were boisterous, excited, testing the GM, dominating conversation
- The newbie's head was spinning from random game jargon
- The lapsed gamer was shy and an outsider to the friend group
- Result: Session Zero gave almost nothing to build a pitch around

### Player Assessment
- **The Buddies:** Challenge-seekers, Expression-seekers, Narrative-seekers. Big presences, play off each other, good at system optimization but focused on cool stories and characters. Identified as "Non-Munchkin Power Gamers."
- **The Newbie:** Wanted creative expression — fixated on character creation, wanted to make her own character rather than use a pre-gen.
- **The Lapsed Gamer:** Genuinely happy to play but hard to engage. Got very little info from him.

### The "Random Helper" Problem
The buddies tried to help the newbie by flinging random game facts with no context or system — like someone explaining Grey's Anatomy seven seasons in by sharing random fun facts.

## Pitching Nothing (But Making It Sound Like Something)

### The Angryverse Approach
Fall back on a standard set of world assumptions — a generic world with well-detailed regions that each represent a "type" of game:
- **Zethinia** — Holy Roman Empire / Byzantium (theocratic, expansionist)
- **Alqaad** — Desert kingdom of magic
- **The Sunderlund** — Norse-Germanic-Pagan wildlands
- **Free Cities of the Broken Coast** — Pre-unification Italy (political intrigue)
- **The Western Kingdoms** — Generic western fantasy (semi-feudal, dark age, castles and farming villages)

The world is vague in specifics but well-detailed in general — history and mythology known in broad strokes, regions known vaguely but consistently.

### The Non-Pitch

> In western Aerth, beyond the Serpent's Teeth mountains and the wild Sunderlands, lie the Kingdoms of the Westerlands. These small, isolated kingdoms have kept to themselves since the rise and fall of the Zethinian Empire four centuries ago.
>
> After a cruel winter, the people prepare to celebrate the Festival of Springtide. The Festival in Red Bridge in the Kingdom of Caelon promises to be the best in the Westerlands. People from all over gather.
>
> But hope is muted. Seers mutter about bad signs — a wandering red star, dark of the moon on Soulsnight, frontier raids, soldiers on the road, strange shadows, rivers running high. In Red Bridge, there is undeniable tension.

**What this actually says:** "There's a festival, and something is probably going to happen." The rest is a horoscope. But it sounds like there are big plans.

## Practical Decisions

### "Core Only" Rule
With mixed experience levels, restrict options to core rulebook only:
- Prevents less experienced players from being overwhelmed
- Prevents experienced players from bringing crazy combos
- Reduces GM's need to know every supplement

### World Assumptions Document
Used D&D 4E's "list of explained assumptions" technique — 10 assumptions that define the world, each with a short paragraph. Establishes:
- Ancient world, ruins of fallen empires, dark age
- Civilization vs. dangerous wilderness
- Humanity of the world, isolation of non-human races
- Magic as rare and special
- Player roles in the world
- Two primary religions (Old Gods and New — primal spirits vs. actual gods)

### Race/Class Options List
One paragraph per option explaining how it fits the world. Serves multiple purposes:
- Gives newer players a simple, non-game-jargon list of choices
- Gives experienced players firm limits
- Hooks every concept directly into the world

## Key Takeaways

- Session Zero can fail. Have a fallback plan.
- You can pitch a campaign with almost nothing — a vague setting and an inciting event is enough to start
- A generic but personally well-known world is invaluable for improvising campaigns
- Restrict options (core only) when dealing with mixed experience levels
- Use a world assumptions document to set tone and feel
- Character generation can be used to figure out what the campaign is actually about
- The pitch is as much a design statement for the GM as a promise to players — you don't need to know the answers yet
