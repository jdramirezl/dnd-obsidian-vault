## Summary

The surface topic is absurd — should dwarven bear cavalry exist in every setting? — but the real lesson is one of the most important worldbuilding principles Angry teaches: **every element in your game should be opt-in, not opt-out.**

Angry dismantles bear cavalry through real-world domestication criteria (six factors: breeds in captivity, matures quickly, efficient diet, calm disposition, not panicky, social/hierarchical). Bears fail virtually all of them. He then examines whether cavalry even makes sense for dwarves (underground/mountain fighters who need low-profile skirmish tactics, not mounted charges). The conclusion: if you're going to use your imagination, imagine something *better* than something demonstrably stupid.

But the real payload is the worldbuilding philosophy. Most GMs treat the core rulebook as the default — everything is *in* unless you can justify taking it *out*. Angry flips this: **nothing is in unless it can justify being included.** Every race, class, background, spell, and monster must earn its place in *this specific campaign*. This is why Angry "bans" things — not because he hates them, but because he starts from zero and only adds what fits.

His players have never complained about this. Only the internet complains.

## Key Advice

- **Opt-in, not opt-out.** Don't start with "everything in the PHB is available unless I remove it." Start with "nothing is available unless I add it." Every element must justify its inclusion in *this* campaign.
- **Verisimilitude matters.** Cool ideas should also make sense within the world's internal logic. If normal animals behave normally but bears are magically different for no reason, that's arbitrary and lazy.
- **Use your imagination for something better.** If you're willing to invent fantasy elements, don't stop at something that falls apart under scrutiny. Invent something that's cool *and* coherent (e.g., armored cave lizards for dwarves instead of bears).
- **No element belongs in every setting.** No matter how awesome something is, it doesn't automatically fit every campaign. Not elves, not dwarves, not wizards, not anything.
- **"Banning" is the wrong framing.** You're not banning things — you're simply not including things that don't fit. The default state is exclusion, not inclusion.
- **Your players won't care.** In Angry's extensive experience, players never complain about what's excluded. Only internet strangers do.
- **The six criteria for animal domestication** (useful for worldbuilding mounts/beasts):
  1. Breeds in captivity
  2. Matures quickly
  3. Efficient diet (herbivores preferred)
  4. Calm disposition
  5. Not prone to panic
  6. Social with dominance hierarchy

## Actionable Steps

1. **For your next campaign, start from zero.** Open the PHB and go through every race, class, subclass, and background. For each one, ask: "Does this fit the campaign I'm building? What role does it play in this world?"
2. **If it can't answer, it's out.** Don't feel guilty. You're curating, not censoring.
3. **Apply the same logic to monsters, magic items, and spells.** Does this monster exist in your world? Why? What ecological or magical niche does it fill?
4. **When inventing fantasy elements (mounts, creatures, cultures), apply internal logic.** Use real-world principles as a starting point. Domestication criteria, terrain, ecology, and military tactics should inform your choices.
5. **Don't default to tropes.** Ask why dwarves live in mountains in *your* world. If you have a good answer, great. If not, maybe they don't.
6. **Communicate your choices to players without apology.** "Here's what's available in this campaign" is all you need to say. You don't owe justifications for exclusions.

> [!tip] The Opt-In Litmus Test
> For every element you're tempted to include in your setting, ask: "If this didn't already exist in the rulebook, would I invent it for this campaign?" If the answer is no, leave it out. Your setting will be tighter, more coherent, and more memorable for it.
