# Why So Many NPCs: Companion and NPC Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Why Are There So Many People in the Angryverse?](https://theangrygm.com/so-many-people/)
**Date:** June 22, 2022

---

## Why NPCs Matter

### Interaction is Everything

Humans are social creatures. We learn about characters — and connect with them — through interaction. All stories people care about are stories about people. RPGs must include plenty of interaction to deliver a satisfying narrative experience.

### A World Outside the Party

NPCs create the illusion that a world exists beyond the PCs' story. Well-crafted NPCs represent stories that intersect the PCs' stories — stories that started before the PCs arrived and will continue after they leave.

**The shopkeeper test:** A shopkeeper who just says "what can I do for ya" and "thank you, come again" is a game construct that pops into existence when needed. A shopkeeper who snaps at you, then apologizes because he's having a bad day? That NPC tricks the players' brains into thinking he's a real person with a life outside this transaction.

### Different Perspectives

NPCs who aren't adventurers show the lives of non-protagonists — the people the PCs make better or worse. This makes PCs feel special and shows players their actions have impact.

> "Lots of GMs piss and moan that their player-characters are a bunch of amoral murderhobos. Well, one thing to consider is how many nice, normal, real-seeming, non-fantastical, real human people the PCs stumble across."

## Companion NPCs

NPCs that travel with the party are especially valuable because adventure settings (wilderness, dungeons) offer few natural interaction opportunities. Companions bring a bit of the world into the adventure.

A companion doesn't have to be an adventuring ally — just an interactable bit of the world that exists beyond the PCs' adventures. Oarsmen, guides, rescued prisoners, fellow travelers.

### Ignore the "GMPC" Stigma

The attitude that companion NPCs are inherently bad is wrong. If someone uses the term "GMPC" as a criticism, stop listening. The real issue is always an overbearing GM, not the existence of companion NPCs.

## Good NPCs Need Good Reasons

### Why Should the Players Want Them?

Every companion NPC needs an in-game reason for the players to want them around:

1. The NPC has a useful skill or ability
2. The NPC's goal aligns with the PCs' goals
3. The players feel responsible for the NPC
4. The adventure goal demands the NPC come along
5. The players like the NPC and want to keep them around
6. They're going the same direction — strength in numbers

### Why is it Worth the GM's Effort?

Have something planned to do with the NPC:

- Provide a specific asset or resource useful on the adventure
- Add an extra element of challenge or difficulty
- Add a conflict or dilemma
- Tell a side story related to the main adventure

**Exception:** If the party seeks out or invites an NPC on their own initiative, that's all the reason you need. Pack their bag.

## Building Good NPCs: The Two-Note Method

Every NPC starts with two notes:

### Note 1: Goal or Motivation

- **Goals** work for short-term companions ("I want that specific gem")
- **Motivations** work for long-term buddies ("I want to prove I'm the best treasure hunter")
- If a short-term companion becomes long-term, turn their goal into a motivation

### Note 2: A Humanizing Trait

Pick one from this non-exhaustive list:

- Vulnerabilities
- Fears
- Pains
- Dreams
- Values
- Virtues
- Beliefs
- Flaws
- Shortcomings
- Self-Awareness
- Growth Potential
- Agency

## Running NPCs Well

### Don't Play, Portray

The GM doesn't play characters — they portray game constructs. Game mechanics make the choices; the GM describes the results. NPCs don't decide; they respond to morale checks, resist Bluff checks, etc.

**Track every companion's Reaction Stat** — a 1-to-10 scale (or Hostile/Unfriendly/Indifferent/Friendly/Helpful). Use it to:
- Determine the NPC's actions during interactions and events
- Set DCs for player rolls against the NPC
- Adjust based on how the party treats the NPC

### Speak When Spoken To (Usually)

NPC companions should generally not initiate interactions or join conversations uninvited. They speak when spoken to.

**Exception:** NPCs can speak up when their defined purpose demands it — volunteering information they're there to provide, using a skill they're there to use, creating a conflict they're there to create. But only for their specific, planned purpose.

### Don't Let Anyone Forget the NPC Exists

1. **Use a token/mini** for the NPC — keep it on the table with the party
2. **Keep NPC notes visible** — stats, personality, purpose
3. **Mention the NPC in scene-setting** — "The oarsman gathers firewood," "He chews thoughtfully on his stew"
4. **Inject small, useless remarks** — "I'm glad we survived that" after a fight, agreeing with a PC's plan after others have already agreed

## The Four Dimensions of NPCs

(From Writing Excuses podcast, endorsed by Angry GM)

| Dimension | Description | Tracking |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **1. Spear-carrier** | Extras with no speaking parts. Raw archetypes. | Name on location list |
| **2. Secondary NPC** | Speaking parts. Two-note NPCs. | One-sentence on master NPC sheet |
| **3. Full NPC** | Has real motivations, connected to the story. Goal becomes motivation. | Own page/spreadsheet |
| **4. Character Arc** | Makes progress toward or away from their goals over time. | Own page with development notes |

Only promote NPCs to higher dimensions as the game demands. Don't waste time fleshing out people who never get used.
