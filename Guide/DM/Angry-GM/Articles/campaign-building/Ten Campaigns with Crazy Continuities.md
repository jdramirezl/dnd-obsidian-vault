# Ten Campaigns with Crazy Continuities

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Ten Campaigns with Crazy Continuities You Should Never Ever Run](https://theangrygm.com/ten-campaigns-with-crazy-continuities/)
**Date:** March 2, 2022

---

## Core Concept: Continuity

A long-running campaign needs a **common thread** (continuity) tying adventures together — something that makes them feel like part of the same bigger thing and primes players' brains to connect everything.

### Multiple Continuities Run Side-by-Side

Most D&D campaigns have **all of these at once**:
- **Party continuity** — same group of heroes
- **Character continuity** — same individual characters
- **Setting continuity** — same world
- **Narrative continuity** — ongoing plot
- **Thematic continuity** — same core emotional experiences
- **System continuity** — same game rules

You can break any one (or most) of these and the campaign still holds together. The default assumption in D&D is *party continuity* — the party persists even if membership changes.

### Black-and-White Thinking Warning

Continuity isn't all-or-nothing. These ideas don't truly "break" continuity — they **play with it**, relying on non-default continuities to hold things together while the usual ones are disrupted.

## The Nine Campaign Ideas

### 1. Character Continuity: The Chosen Ones
- PCs are **demigods** — the only beings who gain levels. Everyone else is 1st level.
- **Permadeath** (or costly resurrection via level drain from allies).
- Dead PC's player can watch, stay home, or play a disposable 1st-level hanger-on.
- Goal: get enough demigod survivors through the campaign to fulfill the destiny.

### 2. MacGuffin Continuity: The One Thing
- Continuity follows a **single artifact** through the world's history.
- Players don't know the campaign premise at first — they discover it.
- When the artifact is secured, time skips forward; new PCs pick up the story decades later.
- Each generation of PCs shepherds the artifact, learning more about it.
- Campaign ends when the artifact fulfills its purpose (or the bad guys win).

### 3. Setting Continuity: Legacy of Adventure
- **Generational campaign** — play in a small frontier village, adventure, retire the party.
- Skip ahead a couple of generations; the village has grown (or been destroyed) based on prior outcomes.
- New 1st-level PCs may be descendants of previous heroes.
- Players can write retirement stories and pass down durable magic items as macro-advancement.

### 4. Setting + System Discontinuity: Legacy of Gygax
- Same generational setup as above, but **each generation uses a successive edition of D&D**.
- Start with OD&D or AD&D 1E → AD&D 2E → D&D 3.5 → D&D 4E → D&D 5E.

### 5. Player Continuity: The World That Never Was
- PCs are spat out by a magical artifact with **amnesia** (one memory each).
- Death causes the artifact to resurrect them — sometimes in a **completely different body** (new character).
- Memories persist across incarnations; players are effectively playing the characters' **souls**.
- The truth: PCs are reincarnations of ancient heroes bound to the artifact. Each death unravels the real reality's history.
- Ran for over two years (never finished).

### 6. Organizational Continuity: GIMF Joe
- PCs belong to an **organization** (government agency, rebel alliance, adventurer's guild).
- Each player creates a **roster of three specialist characters**.
- Before each mission, players pick which character to send based on skills, preference, and XP needs.
- Dead characters get replaced; players can retire characters to make new ones.
- Angry ran a version with ~24 players, each with one character, signing up for different guild missions.

### 7. Ship Continuity: Upper and Lower Decks
- Each player has **two characters**: one bridge crew member, one away team/mission crew member.
- Half the game is starship operations, half is ground missions.
- Inspired by *Star Trek: TNG* episode "Lower Decks" and the *Alternity* RPG system.
- Works for any setting with a vehicle/base that needs a crew and also sends teams on missions.

### 8. Negative Continuity: Marmota Majora
- A **time loop** campaign.
- Play normally for a month, then the world ends. GM hands out original character sheets and restarts.
- Players must figure out how to prevent the calamity across multiple loops.
- A Time Wizard NPC provides limited clues and resets; his power weakens each loop.
- Campaign ends when players prevent the catastrophe (or the Time Wizard runs out of power).

### 9. Multiverse Burp: The Day the Multiverse Burped
- Use a **generic, multi-genre system** (Savage Worlds, etc.).
- Each player makes a character from **any setting and era** — Napoleonic soldier, cyberpunk hacker, space pilot, Weird West gunslinger.
- A multiverse accident sets them adrift across realities.
- They drift from adventure to adventure, portal to portal, hoping to find their way home.
- Inspired by *Tales from the Floating Vagabond* and *Quantum Leap*.

*(Note: Angry promised ten but only delivered nine. Intentionally.)*

## Key Takeaways

- **Party-and-character continuity is so default that most people don't realize it's a choice**, not a requirement.
- These ideas are **not for everyone** — some players can't handle losing their character or playing something non-standard. Test for ~12 sessions before deciding.
- Narrative continuity and player continuity (the players themselves remembering things) are the hardest to break and still have a functional campaign.
- Players **will** remember things their characters don't — lean into it rather than fighting "metagame knowledge."
- Any of these can be adapted to D&D or other systems with creative GM work.
