## Summary

Angry introduces **Curated Character Generation (CCG)** — his method for getting a party of characters built quickly without letting players overthink, over-backstory, or over-communicate before the game starts. The core philosophy: characters should be at the *start* of their stories, not the middle. A complete character has exactly three parts — a filled character sheet, a single-word motivation, and a two-sentence physical description. No backstory, no personality traits, no bonds/flaws. The premise *is* the backstory. The GM controls the process by writing a **Campaign Primer** (premise + culled option lists), requiring each player to submit two different character concepts (race/class/background/motivation), secretly choosing which one each player plays, and then running a fast, boot-camp-style character generation session. Players never get time to overthink or coordinate.

---

## Key Advice

1. **Character generation is NOT roleplaying.** The more "done" a character is before play, the less it's shaped by in-game experiences. Start characters at the beginning of their stories.
2. **A complete character has exactly 3 parts:**
   - A completed character sheet (no Personality Traits/Ideals/Bonds/Flaws)
   - A motivation (one word or short phrase — a drive, not a goal)
   - A two-sentence physical description
3. **The premise IS the backstory.** Every character was a normal person who decided to become an adventurer and headed to the starter town. That's it.
4. **Enforce the premise rigidly.** Reject characters that don't fit. No escaped Feywild minstrels. No lost-kingdom-reclaiming princes. Normal people who want to adventure.
5. **Motivation ≠ Goal.** Motivation is an inner drive that can never be fulfilled: *amass wealth*, *explore the world*, *help others*. Goals are temporary; motivations explain why characters accept goals.
6. **Build a Campaign Primer** containing:
   - The campaign premise
   - Culled lists of allowed races, classes, and backgrounds (always fewer than the core book offers)
   - A list of sample motivations
   - Do NOT include character generation rules (ability scores, equipment methods)
7. **Cull options, then replace.** Start with core options, remove at least one from each category. To add a supplement option, you must remove a core option to make room. Minimum: 4 races, 8 classes, 8 backgrounds.
8. **The two-concept submission process:**
   - Each player submits two *different* character concepts (race, class, background, motivation)
   - The GM secretly picks which one each player plays
   - Players don't learn their approved character until CG-Day
   - This prevents attachment, overthinking, and inter-player coordination
9. **CG-Day is boot camp.** Move fast. Don't let players discuss choices, backstories, or relationships. Fill out sheets mechanically. Take the sheets home afterward.
10. **Start the game ASAP after CG-Day.** Momentum matters.

### Sample Motivations List
- Amass wealth, Earn glory, Gain respect, Accrue power, Achieve status
- Earn recognition, Obey duty, Discover truth, Do good, Help others
- Perfect self, Gain knowledge, Indulge pleasure, Fulfill destiny, Stave off boredom
- Satisfy wanderlust, Instill chaos, Slake anger, Mete out justice, Spread the faith
- Take revenge, Climb the ranks, Build a following, Prove your mettle, Master a skill

## Actionable Steps

1. **Write your Campaign Primer** — paste in your premise, then add culled option lists for race, class, background, and motivation.
2. **Decide generation rules** — pick one method for ability scores (standard array recommended), HP (max at 1st, average thereafter), and equipment (starting gear by class/background). Write these down but do NOT share them yet.
3. **Hand out the Primer and set a CG date.** Tell players to submit two different concepts (race/class/background/motivation only) by a deadline before CG-Day.
4. **Review submissions** — reject anything off-list, flag duplicates or overly similar pairs, refuse extra backstory material.
5. **Secretly pick one concept per player.** Aim for variety in roles and motivations, but don't overthink it. If you can't decide, pick each player's second submission.
6. **Run CG-Day like boot camp** — reveal choices, march through sheet completion step by step, don't let players slow down to discuss or plan.
7. **Collect sheets** (or make copies). Prepare a master reference of AC, passive scores, proficiencies, saves, and initiative modifiers.
8. **Set the first session date.** Tell players to prepare their two-sentence physical descriptions. Launch the game.

---

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> The best characters aren't the ones with the most elaborate backstories — they're the ones that get shaped by actual play. **Start blank, play forward.** Your job as GM is to get characters made fast and get the game running. Everything interesting about a character should happen *at the table*, not in a Google Doc before Session 1.
