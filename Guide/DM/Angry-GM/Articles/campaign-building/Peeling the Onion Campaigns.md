# Peeling the Onion Campaigns

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Parfaits Ain't Easy: Peeling the Onion Campaigns](https://theangrygm.com/peeling-the-onion-campaigns/)
**Date:** May 16, 2018
**Series:** Campaign Building / Campaign Types

---

## What Is a Peeling the Onion Campaign?

A campaign whose major plotline involves solving a deep, layered mystery. A single dark truth is buried under layers of other mysteries. As the heroes solve each mystery (or get close), they discover it was superficial — the real mystery is hidden underneath.

**Not just a mystery campaign.** A normal mystery poses a question and ends with its resolution. A Peeling the Onion campaign stacks mysteries on top of each other, each revelation peeling back a layer to expose something deeper.

### Example (Angry's Icerazor Glacier Campaign)

1. Investigate a queen's murder → discover someone was trying to start a war
2. Investigate the war → discover both sides have unexplained regicides, both orchestrated by a dwarven weaponsmith guild
3. Investigate the guild → discover its leader is a strange cultist
4. Investigate the cult → discover it worships a fabricated ancient figure; the founder belongs to a different, real order bound to Levistus (a Duke of Hell)
5. Confront Levistus → discover Asmodeus manipulated everything so the heroes would kill Levistus for him

## Who It's For

Best for players who prioritize **Discovery** and **Narrative** engagements. These campaigns promise:

- The ultimate Discovery experience — constantly pushing back darkness to find the Truth
- Strong, well-structured, well-paced narrative

**Not for everyone.** Players who don't respond to Discovery and Narrative elements will not enjoy it. Confirm fit during Session Zero.

## Warning: Easy to Mess Up

These campaigns seem easy and fun but are treacherous:

- Problems are subtle and slow-building, not big and obvious
- They dig at you over time like a lump in a mattress
- Easy to do wrong, hard to spot until the campaign falls apart

## How to Build One

### 1. Start from the End — Know the Truth

You **must** know the ultimate, final revelation before you begin. You cannot improvise it, fit it together later, or half-ass it.

- Plan the Truth (the deepest secret)
- Plan the Resolution (the final confrontation/climax)
- Plan the major plot points that connect the Truth to the starting mystery
- Use those to plan minor plot points and adventures as needed

**Why planning is non-negotiable:**

- Every mystery must be solvable and fair — players want to figure it out themselves
- Each layer must make sense individually AND as part of the stack
- Inconsistencies lead players to wrong conclusions unfairly
- The less you know about the Truth, the more you risk plot holes

### 2. Avoid Plot Plague

**Plot Plague** (aka the Chris Carter Effect / Kudzu Plot): When improvisation causes the plot to grow and spread uncontrollably, adding questions faster than they're answered.

- Stories must contract toward an ending — mysteries start with infinite possibilities and narrow to a single resolution
- Without knowing the ending, you don't know when to start contracting
- Uncontrolled growth leaves plot threads unresolved or forces ham-fisted answers
- Once Plot Plague starts, there is NO getting it back under control

### 3. Build Revelations That Don't Steal Victories

The core engagement is the **Moment of Revelation** — two types:

1. **"Things are deeper than you thought"** — discovering a cultist behind the dwarves
2. **"Things actually make sense now"** — realizing the devilish weapon designs match Stygia's frozen, poisonous landscape

**Critical rule: Revelations must NOT move the goalposts.**

- Don't pull a "your princess is in another castle" — that steals the players' victory
- Each mystery's solution must genuinely fix or resolve something
- The revelation reframes the victory as part of a larger war, not as a non-victory
- Good revelations make previous victories MORE victorious in retrospect

### Bad Revelation (Goalpost Moving)

> "The villain you defeated was just a puppet. The real villain is someone else." → Players feel their accomplishment was pointless.

### Good Revelation (Deepening)

> "You stopped the war and saved thousands of lives. Now you discover the war was designed to corrupt soldiers' souls for Hell — you didn't just save lives, you saved immortal souls." → Previous victory becomes even more meaningful.

## Checklist for Each Layer

- [ ] Does solving this mystery genuinely resolve something? (Real victory, not a fake one)
- [ ] Does the revelation make previous victories feel MORE significant?
- [ ] Is the mystery solvable and fair with the clues provided?
- [ ] Are there no inconsistencies that could mislead players unfairly?
- [ ] Does the revelation raise stakes without invalidating accomplishments?

## Key Takeaways

- Know the Truth before you start
- Plan major plot points; improvise minor ones carefully
- Every layer must deliver a real victory
- Revelations deepen meaning, never steal it
- Keep improvisation controlled to prevent Plot Plague
- The ending must answer every remaining question definitively
