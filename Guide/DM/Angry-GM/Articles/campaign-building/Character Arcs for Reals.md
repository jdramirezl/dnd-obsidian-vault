# Character Arcs for Reals

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/character-arcs-for-reals/)
**Date:** April 3, 2024
**Series:** Campaign Building / Game Theory

---

## What Character Arcs Actually Are

A **Character Arc** is an inner transformation a character undergoes throughout a narrative, often as a result of the events of that narrative. The **inner** part is critical — changes in belief, personality, mindset, perspective, or morality.

### Three Types of Character Arcs

1. **Positive Arcs** — Character becomes a better person. Overcoming a flaw (Eddie Valiant in *Who Framed Roger Rabbit?*) or complete transformation (Scrooge in *A Christmas Carol*).
2. **Negative Arcs** — Character becomes worse. Cautionary tales about people broken or corrupted by hardship (Anakin Skywalker).
3. **Flat Arcs** — Character's beliefs are challenged and temporarily broken before being reaffirmed. Debated whether these are true arcs.

## Why Character Arcs Matter

- They make characters more **relatable** — audiences invest more emotion in characters that undergo transformative journeys.
- They lie at the heart of the **Quest Narrative** (the Hero's Journey) — the template for basically every adventure RPG.
- The external quest is usually less important, thematically, than the hero's inner transformation. Luke Skywalker's victory matters less than his becoming a Jedi.

## Why They Don't Work in TTRPGs

### No Real Audience
- TTRPGs have no audience the way movies or books do. Either arcs are meaningless because there's no audience to care, or they're invisible because each player can already read their own character's thoughts.

### Player Dependency
- GMs control events, not characters. Players control characters, not events. Neither side has full control over both halves needed for an arc.
- RPGs implicitly tell players to **stay true to their character**, which at best allows for Flat Arcs.

### Three Ways Arcs Happen at the Table
1. **Accidentally** — Everything aligns by chance. Can't be counted on.
2. **Overplanning** — GM and player plot it out in advance. Stops being a roleplaying game; becomes scripted improv.
3. **Opportunism** — Player is committed to growth and seizes on whatever opportunities arise. The only viable approach, but requires exceptional player skill and awareness.

## Possible Mechanical Solutions

If you want to encourage arcs, you need **system-level design**, not GM techniques.

**Example: Marvel Heroic Roleplaying (Cortex Plus)**
- Characters had **Milestone Paths** — lists of in-game events to spot and capitalize on, spelling out rudimentary transformative arcs.
- Spider-Man's path: declare an old foe (1 XP) → take trauma from that foe (3 XP) → forgive or be forgiven (10 XP).

### Design Considerations
- You need a mechanic to **encourage** arcs AND a mechanic to **pace** them (prevent instant completion).
- A **Character Flaw** mechanic paired with an **Overcoming the Flaw** mechanic could work — the flaw causes problems but also gives benefits, creating a meaningful tradeoff.
- Designing a general system for all possible arcs is nearly impossible without heavy bounding constraints.

## Practical Takeaway

Don't sweat character arcs. Focus on running the most engaging game you can. Build investment through doing a thousand tiny things right. If players care emotionally, some will undergo inner journeys naturally — and often won't even notice it's happening.
