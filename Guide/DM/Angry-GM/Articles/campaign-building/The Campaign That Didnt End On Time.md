# The Campaign That Didn't End on Time

**Source:** [The Angry GM — The Campaign That Didn't End On Time](https://theangrygm.com/the-campaign-that-didnt-end-on-time/)
**Date:** April 21, 2026

---

## The Core Question

> How long, in real play time (assuming weekly sessions), is a campaign?

The community consensus wasn't about a number — it was that **campaigns always last way longer than planned**. Unless they die.

## Definition Refresher

> A campaign is a continuous roleplaying game that lasts for **more than three sessions**.

If your game hits session four, you're running a campaign.

---

## Three Approaches to Campaigns

1. **The Neverending Fuckabout** (doesn't count) — Slice-of-life, no goals, no real game. Not a campaign.
2. **String of Next Adventures** — Run actual adventures with goals, string them together with minimal structure. Valid but simple.
3. **Actual Scenario Design** (the best approach) — Start with a goal, have a beginning, middle, and **planned ending**. This is where the best campaigns live: save the world, defeat the empire, reclaim the birthright, etc.

The planned ending doesn't require rigid linearity or railroading. It just means the game starts with a vision for how it concludes.

---

## The Value of Endings

### Endings Are Psychologically Powerful

- **Brains love endings.** Closure, completed patterns, resolved tension.
- **Endings recontextualize the entire experience.** Your brain evaluates whether the investment was "worth it" based on the payoff. The value of every session is undefined until the ending assigns a final value.
- **Bad endings ruin everything** — not because endings are risky, but because endings are so important that a bad one has outsized impact.
- **Without an ending, your brain can't fully process emotion or extract meaning.** Open, unfinished things resist deep reflection.

### Evidence: Avengers Endgame

- Outperformed every other Marvel film. *Infinity War* was thought to be the ceiling; *Endgame* blasted past it by $700M.
- For millions, Marvel effectively "ended" at *Endgame*. The franchise continues but is no longer the cultural phenomenon it was.

### Evidence: Star Trek TNG Finale

- "All Good Things…" remains one of the highest-rated Trek episodes ever, despite being a mostly episodic show.
- The finale was structurally just another episode — but the ending gave it enormous weight.

### The Ending Doesn't Have to Be Final for the Characters

> "And so the heroes rode off into the sunset, and they probably had many more cool adventures, but this is where we bid them goodbye." That still lets your brain do its ending thing.

## The Problem: Campaigns That Don't End on Time

The typical pattern:
1. Plan a 6-month or 12-month campaign with a clear ending.
2. Eight months in, you're only in act two.
3. Everyone's having fun, so you give the speech: "I screwed up my planning, but we're all still having fun, right? Let's just keep going."
4. Players agree enthusiastically.

This seems fine. But...

### Every Campaign Has an Expiration Date

- Interest wanes. People burn out. Life intervenes. Players move away. New shiny things appear.
- **Every campaign ends.** The question is whether it ends on your terms with a proper goodbye, or just dies.
- Dead campaigns leave bittersweet memories — fun gaming tainted by the lack of closure.
- Once a campaign passes its planned end date into "no end in sight," **you lose knowledge of when the expiration date will come.**
- By the time you spot a dying campaign, it's often been dying for a while. Your window to pivot to a worthy ending is small.
- If *you're* the one who's burnt out, you're in no state to craft a good ending.

---

## Why Are You Still Bad at Estimating?

The standard excuse: "I'm bad at estimating how long things take."

**Angry's pushback:** If you're not new at this, you don't get to be surprised anymore.

- You know how long combats take at your table.
- You know your players spend 20 minutes debating at every intersection.
- You know a dungeon with three decision points loses an hour to committee discussion.

> You're not bad at estimating. You're designing based on how you *wish* the game played instead of how you *know* it plays. You won't accept reality, so you won't plan for it.

---

## The Real Culprit: Scope Creep

**Creep** — adding things to a project that weren't part of the original plan. Each addition seems small and innocuous, but they compound.

### How Creep Kills Campaigns

- You come up with a clever subplot that wasn't in the plan.
- You start building a two-session dungeon and expand it to five sessions because of a cool idea.
- You justify it: "The campaign's already past its expiration date, everyone agreed to keep going, and this idea is awesome."
- Repeat indefinitely.

> Creep is impossible to spot from the inside. It's an emergency.

Game Mastering is game design. GMs are creatives working on passion projects. Creatives are especially prone to creep because they love their ideas and can't say no to them.

---

## The Uncomfortable Truth

> Did you ever actually decide to change the plan? Did you ever say, "This plan needs changing because X, Y, and Z"? Or did you just stop respecting the plan and start dicking around because it seemed more fun?

You had a plan. You believed in it enough to commit months and sell your friends on it. Every element was a deliberate choice for a reason.

If you're nowhere near that plan today, ask yourself honestly:
- Was it necessary adjustments and improvisation?
- Or was it undisciplined creep?

### The Stakes

- If the only question is "Are we having fun?" — then fine, ignore all this.
- If the question is "Am I running my best game?" — then the Campaign That Didn't End on Time deserves scrutiny.
- "Everyone's still having fun" is not proof that you're doing your best work.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Plan for an ending.** Endings are enormously valuable — they retroactively increase the value of every session.
- **Respect the plan.** Deviation should be deliberate and justified, not unconscious drift.
- **Accept your table's reality** when estimating time. Plan for how the game actually plays, not how you wish it played.
- **Watch for creep.** Every cool new idea has a cost. Ask whether it serves the campaign or just your creative impulse.
- **Every campaign will end.** The only question is whether you control the ending or it controls you.
- **Bad endings taint everything.** But no ending at all might be worse — it leaves the whole experience unresolved.
