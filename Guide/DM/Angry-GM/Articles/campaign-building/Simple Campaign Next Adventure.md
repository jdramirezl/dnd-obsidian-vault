# Simple Campaign: Building a Next Adventure

## Key Advice

**Every adventure after the first must handle motivation, hooks, lures, preparation, travel, and interwovenness** — things the first adventure got to skip. Use a **big prep / little prep** cycle: plan the adventure's premise, conflict, and structure in advance (big prep), then build out individual sessions as needed (little prep). Plant **setups** — both planned and improvisational — to make the campaign feel like a connected, living world rather than a series of disconnected events.

## Summary

Part of the *Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign* series. This lesson covers step 6: building the second (and every subsequent) adventure. The next adventure's big prep must be done before the party reaches Town.

**Big prep vs. little prep:**
- **Big prep** — premise, major conflict, act structure. Done when you realize a new adventure is needed soon.
- **Little prep** — scenes, encounters, maps, stat blocks. Done session-by-session as needed.
- Sessions and adventures don't line up — most adventures span 2–3 sessions. You can't build a multi-session adventure one session at a time because the beginning must set up the ending.
- Do big prep first, then little prep for the next session. If you have extra time, prep further ahead.

**The Art of the Setup:**
- Makes the campaign feel planned and the world feel real, even when you're making it up as you go.
- **Planned Setups** — deliberately insert details that set up future adventures. Requires knowing the next adventure before the players reach Town.
- **Improvisational Setups** — take random details you already planted (Town fun facts, rumors) and build future events around them. Looks like genius planning.
- Your Town's fun facts and rumors are a **Rumormill** — they serve three purposes: adventure inspiration, improvisational setups, and player-driven adventure selection (players who engage with a rumor are telling you what interests them).

**What makes a "next adventure" different from the first:**

1. **Motivation** — the first adventure got a free pass. From now on, adventures must serve at least some characters' motivations. Check each character's written motivation plus four preinstalled motivations: survival (threats, money for needs), advancement (XP, magic items, power), protection (threatening things they care about), and group loyalty (going along with friends).
   - You don't need to serve every motivation every adventure — aim for about half the party. Rotate who gets payoffs.

2. **Hook** — an in-game scene where players learn the goal and where to start. NPC interaction, a mysterious location, a treasure map, etc.

3. **Lure** — what catches the players' attention and draws them to the hook. Can be obvious (NPC approaches with a job) or subtle (overheard rumors, visible smoke, a crying widow). More subtle = more player agency felt.
   - Use your Town's essential services list — plant lures where players will naturally go.

4. **Preparation** — reward players who prepare. Come up with three useful things (information, resources, allies) that give an edge but aren't required. Plant them in Town:
   - One obvious and easy to find.
   - One harder — requires asking questions.
   - One hidden — requires hunting or overcoming a challenge.

5. **Travel** — now that you have a map, make travel matter. Plot the adventure site, look at the route and terrain.
   - **Planned encounters** — at least one for trips of 2+ days. More for longer trips.
   - **Complications** — 3+ per trip (wandering monsters, hazards, strangers, weather, equipment damage). Connect them to terrain types. Use instead of random encounter tables. Ideally, build lists of 3–5 complications per terrain region for reuse.

6. **Interwovenness** — every adventure must feel like another chapter in the same story, happening in a real world. This is what setups and the rumormill accomplish.

**Optional side quest:**
- An extra objective with its own payoff that adds challenge (a detour, search, resource expenditure, or increased difficulty).
- Examples: protecting a vulnerable NPC, searching for a rare plant, capturing a monster alive.
- Plant it in Town the same way you plant hooks and prep resources.

**Four questions for designing the hook:**
1. How do the characters know their goal?
2. How do the characters know where to start?
3. How do the characters know the adventure exists?
4. How do the characters know the adventure serves their motivations?

**Running example (Angry's Acrea's Hold campaign):**
- Premise: flooded coastal ruins with a manticore. Built from a Town fun fact (the "graverobber" with the bronze medallion — flagged as untrue, actually an explorer who died of manticore poison).
- Goal: recover Mavel's backpack for his girlfriend.
- Motivations served: help others (goody-goody), acquire wealth (greedy guts), explore (explorer).
- Lure: girlfriend trying to sell the medallion in the market, causing a scene.
- Hook: when a PC touches the medallion, Mavel's ghost whispers about ruins on the sea.
- Prep resources: (1) learn about the manticore → healer reveals poison barb → scholar identifies it → buy antivenom; (2) hire a guide for the swamp; (3) take a boat downriver. GM suggests one, players must think of the other.
- Side quest: potion maker asks for rare swamp plants if players reveal their destination.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Do big prep before the party reaches Town** — premise, conflict, act structure, and motivations for the next adventure.
2. **Check motivations** — ensure at least half the party has a reason to pursue the adventure. Sweeten the deal for those who don't by adding explicit payoffs.
3. **Design hook and lure using the four questions** — plant the lure where players will naturally go (essential services, market, temple).
4. **Plant three prep resources in Town** — one obvious, one requires questions, one hidden.
5. **Consider a side quest** — an optional objective that adds challenge and reward.
6. **Make travel matter** — plan at least one encounter per 2-day trip. Build 3–5 terrain-specific complications for reuse.
7. **Use your rumormill** — build adventures from fun facts you already planted. Players who engage with rumors are telling you what they want.
