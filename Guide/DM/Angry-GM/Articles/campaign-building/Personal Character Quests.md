# Personal Character Quests

**Source:** [The Angry GM — You Meant to Ask About Personal Character Quests](https://theangrygm.com/personal-character-quests/)
**Date:** March 26, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery (Feature)

---

## Overview

A reluctant but thorough guide to planning and executing Personal Character Quests — in-game goals that come from individual players and are specific to their characters. Angry considers them a fundamentally flawed idea that works against what tabletop RPGs do best, but provides practical frameworks for GMs who want to use them anyway.

---

## What Are Personal Character Quests?

Player-driven, character-specific goals: "I want to avenge my master's death," "I want to reclaim my family's throne," "I want to free my brother from prison," "I want to lead the Jade Order."

- Typically devised during character generation
- Provide the impetus for the character to adventure
- Implied that when the quest is finished, the character's story is over
- Many variations exist — GM-assisted creation, choosing new goals after completion, etc.

---

## Goals Are Not Motivations

This distinction is critical.

**Goals** — things a character can DO. Concrete finish lines. You know when they're accomplished.
- Is the killer dead? Quest complete.
- Are you sitting in the fancy chair with the hat? You're the Grand Poohbear.

**Motivations** — things a character VALUES. Abstract drives that can never truly be finished: wealth, justice, respect, glory, love, duty, faith, knowledge, mastery.
- Motivations *explain* goals — WHY are you chasing the killer? Duty or revenge?
- A character with a Motivation can chase a thousand Goals
- A character with a set Goal can only chase that one Goal
- Motivations empower GMs to create goals players want to pursue; Goals limit the GM to one track

**Prefer Motivations over Goals for character creation.** Motivations are flexible tools; Goals are rigid constraints.

---

## Why Personal Character Quests Are Problematic

The core issues everyone cites:
1. **Disengagement** — other players check out when one character's personal story takes center stage
2. **Endpoint problem** — what happens when a character finishes, fails, or dies mid-quest?
3. **Motivation conflict** — why would a character pause their personal quest to help someone else with theirs?

The fundamental game design problem: tabletop RPGs need shared Goals to provide direction and context for choices. Without a shared Goal, each player is playing their own separate game. You end up running five games on crisscrossing tracks that keep stopping, starting, and crashing into each other.

---

## Designing Personal Character Quests

Design them the same way you design any quest:
1. Determine scope — is it a side thing, a single adventure, a campaign chapter, or the whole campaign?
2. Define the Goal
3. Break pursuit into Plot Points
4. Connect Plot Points to each other
5. Expand Plot Points into Encounters/Scenes/Adventures as needed

No special technique required beyond normal adventure design. How you plan to execute them determines how you break them into pieces.

---

## Executing Personal Character Quests

### Step 1: How Many Quests?

- **One or two** — easier to manage, can weave into shared goals
- **Every character** — requires deliberate campaign structure; this IS your campaign now

### Step 2: Entrees or Side Dishes?

Two options, both with tradeoffs:

| Approach | Upside | Downside |
|---|---|---|
| **Side dishes** (shared goal is primary) | Everyone plays the same base game | Players will naturally treat their quest as the main event anyway, leading to disengagement |
| **Entrees** (quests ARE the campaign) | Honest about what the game is | Running five separate games on crisscrossing tracks |

Pick your poison: potentially disengaged players OR a potentially disconnected campaign.

## Integration Methods (by Scope)

### Meanwhile, Back in Town
**For:** Small quests, many characters have them
- Slot quest advancement into between-adventure downtime
- Design around town exploration, investigation, interaction
- Never demand urgency — breadcrumbs picked up by happenstance
- Occasional encounter or reward woven into an adventure for variety

### Questing with a Side of Quests
**For:** Small quests, not too numerous, don't fit town mode
- Expand each Plot Point into an encounter or two as a diversion during another adventure
- Require deliberate party choice but don't charge too much
- Example: "While we're in the forest, can we detour to this camp? I need to ask someone something."

### Tonight… In a Very Special Episode
**For:** One or two extensive quests, or quests that integrate with shared goals
- Turn Plot Points into full adventures, dropped into the campaign as standalone sessions
- **Caveats:**
  - No more than 1 out of every 4–5 adventures
  - No character's quest should need more than 3 adventures to finish
  - Keep personal adventures shorter than normal (2 sessions if normal is 3–4)
  - Ensure the adventure plays into other party members' Motivations
  - Write for the whole group, not just the quest owner

### It's My Turn to Pick the Adventure (Interwoven Campaign)
**For:** Extensive quests for every character, can't work as side content
- Accept that the Personal Character Quests ARE the campaign
- Turn each Plot Point into a standalone adventure
- Keep them short, incorporate multiple Motivations
- Rotate through characters — jumble the order to avoid formulaic patterns
- Weave in GM-originated standalone adventures or campaign-level plots between rotations
- Those GM adventures can be longer and more involved

**This is actually the best (least worst) approach if you're committed to Personal Character Quests.**

## Practical Advice

- **Be deliberate** — decide up front that Personal Character Quests are a thing in your game, whether they're optional or required, and exactly how you'll integrate them
- **Help struggling players** — many players have a hard time inventing a destiny; be ready to assist
- **Don't pre-solve problems** — keep your eyes and ears open and pivot on the fly when issues actually arise, rather than wasting energy on hypothetical problems
- **Trust your players** — if adventures are good and fun, most players won't check out just because it's not "their" quest; if someone does, that's a selfish-player problem, not a quest-design problem
- **Motivations over Goals** — whenever possible, push players toward Motivations rather than fixed Goals; Motivations give you flexibility to create engaging content

---

## Key Takeaways

- Personal Character Quests are character-specific Goals, not Motivations — and Motivations are almost always the better tool
- The core problem is that they fragment the shared game into parallel solo games
- If you use them, be intentional about scope and integration method
- The Interwoven Campaign (rotating spotlight) is the strongest structure for quest-heavy games
- Most players are mature enough to enjoy adventures that aren't "theirs" — the problems are real but manageable with awareness
