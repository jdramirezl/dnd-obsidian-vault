# Campaign Startup Dilemmas — Wishing and Stuff

## Key Advice

When starting a new campaign, GMs face three recurring dilemmas that have **no universal right answer**. They are situational, personal, and must be resolved case-by-case using judgment — not absolute rules. Trying to answer them with "always" or "never" is a false dilemma. This article is a prerequisite to understanding Session Zero.

## Summary

Angry identifies three fundamental dilemmas every GM faces when launching a campaign. None have pat answers — the point is to recognize them clearly so you can make informed, personal decisions rather than seeking universal rules.

**Context: Session Zero and false dilemmas**
- Session Zero is a pre-campaign meeting between GM and players to align goals, expectations, and desires.
- Most campaign-startup questions are posed as binary either-or choices, but stripped of context they become false dilemmas demanding absolute answers to situational problems.
- The GM exists as a game mechanic precisely because the game needs human judgment over absolute rules.

### Dilemma 1: Me vs. Them

**The question:** When your preferences as GM conflict with your players' preferences, whose win?

- Every person at the table has preferences — favorites they crave and game-breakers that ruin the experience. Most preferences are broad enough that compromise is easy.
- When two players conflict, you ask each to compromise, look for creative solutions, or make a call and tell someone to live with it. That's reality.
- The real dilemma is when the **GM's** preferences conflict with the players'. GMs invest more time and effort than players. If the GM isn't enjoying the game, it becomes an unpaid job, the GM's mood poisons the table, and burnout follows.
- But some GMs run games primarily for social reasons and have high tolerance for games that don't match their other preferences. For them, any game beats no game.
- **There is no axiom.** "The GM's needs always come first" is wrong. "The GM's job is to serve the players" is also wrong. It's personal and situational every time.

### Dilemma 2: The Dream Game

**The question:** What do you do when reality prevents you from running the game you want?

- Add up all your preferences and you get a vision of a perfect game. But time, money, skill, player reliability, and other real-world constraints may make that game impossible.
- Creative workarounds exist — marathon prep sessions, limiting scope, simplifying mechanics — but they all require deciding which dreams to sacrifice.
- Resolving this dilemma means accepting external limitations, figuring out which you can work around, and which you can't. Run the game you **can** run, not the one you **want** to run — or decide the gap is too large and don't run a game at all.
- Two guiding aphorisms: the Serenity Prayer ("grant me the strength to change what I can, the serenity to accept what I can't, and the wisdom to know the difference") and "wish in one hand, shit in the other, and see which fills up first."

### Dilemma 3: The Comfort Dilemma

**The question:** Do you go with the familiar, proven, easy thing — or push for something new, risky, and innovative?

- Familiar elements (tavern openings, standard races, default settings) are clichés because they **work**. They're approachable, require no homework, and let you start playing fast.
- Every new, unique element carries three costs: increased cognitive load for players, increased prep/teaching effort, and the risk it simply doesn't work.
- Many GMs overvalue innovation and treat "cliché" as a slur out of snooty artistic elitism. But clichés are reliable workhorses. Racehorses can win prizes — but most racehorses lose.
- Angry's personal approach: start most campaigns in a tavern using the default setting. Once the campaign is running smoothly, **then** start taking risks and innovating.
- The same dilemma applies to players — pushing players out of their comfort zones with character creation is often counterproductive. Players stick with familiar classes/races because they're investing months in that character.
- **Decide on a case-by-case basis.** Weigh the risk vs. reward rationally instead of reflexively chasing novelty or clinging to safety.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Recognize false dilemmas** — when you catch yourself asking "which is always better, X or Y," stop. The answer depends on context. Use judgment, not axioms.
2. **Resolve Me vs. Them honestly** — acknowledge that GM enjoyment matters (burnout kills campaigns), but also that some GMs thrive on social satisfaction alone. Know which GM you are.
3. **Accept reality for the Dream Game** — identify your real constraints (time, money, skill, player reliability), work around what you can, and sacrifice what you must. Don't waste energy wishing the world were different.
4. **Evaluate innovation rationally** — before replacing a cliché, ask: does the new thing justify its cognitive load, teaching cost, and risk of failure? Start with the reliable and layer in the risky once the game is stable.
5. **Apply the same thinking to players** — don't force players out of comfort zones for its own sake. Their character investment is real.
