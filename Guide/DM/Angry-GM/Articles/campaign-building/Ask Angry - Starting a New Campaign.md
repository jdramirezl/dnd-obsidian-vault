# Ask Angry: Starting a New Campaign

## Key Advice

**Tell your players the campaign premise before they make characters.** The number one reason campaigns start poorly is that the GM sends players off to generate characters in a vacuum, then scrambles to bring them together and make a story happen at them. If you communicate the premise upfront, players will create characters that fit the game, and the opening scene writes itself.

## Summary

A reader asks how to get past the cliché of starting in a tavern and hooking players into the campaign. Angry's answer is blunt: the problem isn't the tavern — it's that most GMs don't know what their campaign is about before they start it.

Key points:
- **Know your premise first** — Before session zero, decide: Is this dungeon-of-the-week? An overarching plot? Political intrigue? What is the actual starting hook?
- **Tell the players the premise** — Give them enough to build characters that fit. "The campaign is about an undead plague. Make a character who would want to save the world. The first scene is a funeral in a small town."
- **Tavern starts are fine** — The cliché only feels bad when it's a symptom of having no plan. If you have a premise and a hook, starting in a tavern is perfectly functional.
- **Don't blindside players with the premise** — You can try to surprise them, but you risk the entire hook failing if the players don't bite. It's safer and more effective to be upfront.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Define your campaign premise in one or two sentences** — Before anything else, know what the campaign is about and what the players will be doing.
2. **Communicate the premise to players before character creation** — Give them the genre, the central conflict, and the opening situation. Let them build characters that fit.
3. **Set the opening scene explicitly** — Tell players where the first scene takes place and ask them to decide why their character is there. This eliminates the awkward "you all meet in a tavern" problem.
4. **Don't fear clichés** — A tavern start with a clear premise and hook is infinitely better than a clever, surprising opening that fails to engage the players.
5. **Ensure player buy-in through transparency** — The more players know about what's coming, the better they can invest in characters who care about the story.

> [!tip] The Campaign Kickoff Formula
> Before session one, give your players this: (1) what the campaign is about, (2) what kind of characters would fit, and (3) where and how the first scene begins. That's it. Players who know the premise will build characters who care about it, and you'll never need to trick them into engaging with your story.
