# Why So Many NPCs — Companion NPCs and World-Building

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Why Are There So Many People in the Angryverse?](https://theangrygm.com/so-many-people/)
**Date:** June 22, 2022

---

## Why NPCs Matter

### Interaction is Everything
- Humans are social creatures who learn about themselves and others through interaction — including fictional characters.
- Interaction helps us understand, connect with, like, dislike, love, hate, and cheer for characters.
- All stories people remember are about people. Stories are how we pass life skills, and human lives are 90% social.
- If you want a satisfying narrative, you need plenty of interaction.

### A World Outside the Party
- RPGs present the illusion of a whole imaginary world. NPCs are the manifestation of stories that intersect the PCs' stories — stories that started before the PCs came along and will continue after they move on.
- A shopkeeper who just says "what can I do for ya" is a game construct that pops into existence and ceases to exist.
- A shopkeeper who snaps at you, then apologizes because he's having a bad day — that suggests a real person with a life beyond the PCs. Even if players ignore it, their brains are tricked into having emotions.
- NPCs provide different perspectives. PCs are exceptional — powerful, lucky, rich. Meeting normal people shows the lives the protagonists make better or worse. This makes PCs feel special and shows players their actions have impact.

**Murderhobo problem:** Consider how many nice, normal, real-seeming, non-silly human NPCs the PCs encounter. That has a lot to do with whether players care about the world.

### Why Companion NPCs Specifically
- Fantasy adventure RPGs have a problem: heroes spend all their time in wilderness and dungeons where interaction opportunities are thin.
- Companion NPCs solve this — any NPC that travels with the party on adventures (or part of them).
- They don't have to be adventuring allies. Oarsmen, guides, prisoners, fellow travelers — just interactable bits of the world that exist beyond the PCs' adventures.

### The GMPC Non-Issue
- The attitude that "GMPCs" are bad is complete nonsense. If someone uses the term, stop listening and think about dinosaurs.
- The real issue is overbearing GMs, not companion NPCs.

## Good NPCs Need Good Reasons

### Why Should the Players Want Them Around?
Non-exhaustive list of reasons PCs might welcome an NPC:
1. The NPC has a useful skill or ability
2. The NPC's goal aligns with the PCs' goals
3. The players feel responsible for the NPC
4. The adventure goal demands the NPC come along
5. The players like the NPC
6. Going the same direction — strength in numbers

### Why is the NPC Worth Your Prep Time?
NPCs need a purpose beyond just "interaction is good." Non-exhaustive list:
- Provide a specific asset or resource useful on the adventure
- Add an extra element of challenge or difficulty
- Add a conflict or dilemma
- Tell a side story related to the main story

**Exception:** If the party seeks, recruits, or invites an NPC on its own initiative, that's all the reason you need. Pack their bag. (But a smart GM sees the opportunity and comes up with something to do with them.)

## Building Good Companion NPCs

### The Two-Note NPC
Every companion starts with two qualities:
1. **First note — Goal or Motivation:**
   - Goals work for short-term companions
   - Motivations work for long-term buddies
   - If a short-term companion becomes long-term, turn their goal into a motivation
2. **Second note — A humanizing trait** (pick one):
   - Vulnerabilities, Fears, Pains, Dreams, Values, Virtues, Beliefs, Flaws, Shortcomings, Self-Awareness, Growth Potential, Agency

### Four Dimensions of NPC Depth (from comments — Writing Excuses framework)
1. **Spear-carrier:** Extras with no speaking parts. Raw archetypes. "Jim the Butcher."
2. **Secondary NPC:** Speaking parts. Two-note NPCs. The shopkeep having a bad day.
3. **Full NPC:** Gets another dimension on repeat interaction. Goal turns into motivation.
4. **Character Arc:** Makes progress toward or away from their goals over time.

Only 3rd and 4th dimension NPCs need their own detailed records.

## Running Companion NPCs Well

### Don't Play, Portray
- You're not a player. NPCs are game constructs you portray and describe.
- Game mechanics make the choices — morale checks, Bluff resistance, etc. You describe the results.
- Track every companion's **Reaction Stat** (1–10 scale or Hostile/Unfriendly/Indifferent/Friendly/Helpful). Use it to determine actions and set DCs. Adjust after interactions and events.
- **Never** roll randomly to determine what an NPC does. Outcomes of player actions are determined by mechanics. NPC decisions are not random.

### Speak When Spoken To (Unless You Have a Good Reason)
- NPCs should not initiate interactions or join conversations without invitation from a PC.
- **Exception:** When the plan demands it — volunteering planned information, using their designated skill, telling their story, creating their conflict.
- This must be specific and purposeful. Not blanket permission to volunteer any information or create any conflict.
- You can improvise new purposes mid-adventure, but they should be specific plans, not carte blanche to join the party as a player.

### Don't Let Anyone Forget the NPC Exists
1. **Use tokens/minis** for companion NPCs. Keep them on the table/VTT with the party.
2. **Keep NPC notes visible** — stats, personality, purpose. If you keep overlooking them, you need better notes.
3. **Mention the NPC when setting scenes:** "The oarsman is gathering firewood." "He's chewing thoughtfully on his stew."
4. **Inject small, useless remarks** after scenes or during downtime: "I'm glad we survived that." "That sounds like a good idea." These don't count as interacting — they just remind everyone the NPC exists.

The story, world, and interaction benefits are wasted if everyone forgets the NPC is there.
