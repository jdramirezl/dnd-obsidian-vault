# AOWG Lesson 3 Part 2: Smoke on the Horizon

## Key Advice

**Smoke on the Horizon** is a cue that implies something worth investigating without revealing what it is. It triggers the exploration cycle ("Huh! What's that? I'm gonna find out") by requiring deliberate action to even assess the value of the thing. Don't show players the goblin camp — show them a thin tendril of smoke rising from behind a ridge.

## Summary

This article is the practical toolkit for making exploration happen at the table. "Smoke on the Horizon" is the core technique: present incomplete sensory cues that imply something exists without revealing what. The article covers how to generate smoke from locations, encounters, NPCs, lore, and quests, then how to place that smoke along the players' current path using the Party-Do List. It introduces **The Runaround** — complicating the Party-Do List with fetch quests and errands that carry players past smoke signals. Finally, it presents five advanced narration techniques: The Pronoun Game (NPCs omitting shared knowledge), Signal-to-Noise (1/3 of cues lead to nothing), Bad Narration (burying cues in flavor text), Inexplicable Dots (unexplained map details), and Broken Patterns (one element that doesn't match the rest).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Use Smoke, Not Reveals** — Don't place encounters directly in the party's path. Instead, show signs of the encounter (tracks, sounds, smells, distant light) and let players choose to investigate. This transforms a random encounter into an act of exploration.
2. **Match Smoke to the Party-Do List** — Look at what the players are currently doing and figure out which smoke signals they'd naturally pass on the way. Place your cues along their current trajectory.
3. **Give Players Runarounds** — When you can't find a natural place for smoke, add intermediate steps to Party-Do items. Send players to collect debts, deliver messages, or fetch supplies — errands that carry them past new smoke signals.
4. **Use The Pronoun Game** — Have NPCs reference things without explaining them. "Did the Plumcloak send you?" or "Stop by the shrine and drop off Wellyt's gift." Players who are curious will ask follow-up questions. Those who aren't will move on.
5. **Maintain Signal-to-Noise** — 1/3 of your cues should lead to nothing interesting. This makes the world feel real and makes genuine discoveries feel rewarding. If every cue pays off, exploration becomes a chore.
6. **Try Advanced Techniques:**
   - **Bad Narration** — Bury a cue in the middle of a flavor text paragraph. Attentive players catch it; others don't.
   - **Inexplicable Dots** — Put unexplained details on maps (a weird symbol, an unlabeled building). Let players ask about them.
   - **Broken Patterns** — Establish a pattern (all buildings are old wood), then violate it (one new brick house). Don't call attention to it.
   - **The Unfinished Set** — Leave gaps in sets (4 of 5 council members met, unexplored hallway on the map). Brains hate incomplete things.

> [!tip] The Smoke Formula
> For every game element you want players to discover: (1) identify a sensory cue that implies its existence, (2) make sure the cue doesn't reveal the full picture, (3) ensure following up requires deliberate action with a cost, and (4) place the cue along the players' current path. That's it. That's the whole technique.
