# How to Start Starting a Campaign: Preplanning the Premise

## Key Advice

The campaign you will run is **the campaign you are able to run**, not the one you want to run. Before you dream up epic stories, assess your practical constraints — player reliability, your available prep time, group cohesion — and build a premise that fits reality.

## Summary

Starting a campaign is a multi-phase process: Preplanning → Premise → Players → Planning (Setting, Plot, Starting Point) → Character Generation → First Adventure. This article focuses on the first phase: developing a **Premise**. A premise answers WHO the characters are, WHY they're together, and WHAT they'll do. It's built by choosing a campaign shape (meatballs, noodles, sausage, spaghetti) and glue (common goal, employer, organization, personal bonds) based on entirely practical concerns: player reliability, GM effort capacity, group stability, and experience level. The article also covers setting decisions (non-setting vs. published vs. homebrew) and the Unique Selling Point — the one cool thing that makes players want to play your specific campaign.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Assess Your Practical Constraints First** — Before choosing a campaign type, honestly evaluate: How reliable are your players? How much prep time do you have? How experienced are you? How well does the group know each other?
2. **Choose Campaign Shape Based on Reality:**
   - **Unreliable players / low effort** → Plate of Meatballs (episodic, adventure-of-the-week)
   - **Mostly reliable / moderate effort** → Noodle or Sausage (single overarching plot)
   - **Very reliable / high effort / experienced** → Plate of Spaghetti (multiple interwoven plot threads)
3. **Choose Glue Based on Group Stability:**
   - **Strangers / unstable groups** → External glue (employer, organization, shared obligation)
   - **Established groups** → Can rely on personal bonds or common means
   - **Any group** → Common goal works if the campaign shape supports it
4. **Write the Premise** — Answer three questions in 1-2 paragraphs: WHO are the characters? WHY are they together? WHAT will they do? Optionally add WHERE (setting) and a Unique Selling Point.
5. **Choose Your Setting Approach:**
   - **Non-setting** (default rules, blank slate) — Lowest effort, zero shame
   - **Published setting** — More work than non-setting but rich in detail and hooks
   - **Homebrew** — Only if you need it and know it; heaviest investment for everyone
6. **Sell the Premise in 1-2 Sentences** — Find the single coolest thing about your campaign and pitch that. Don't write a 3-page email. Get players interested enough to want to know more.

> [!tip] You Can Always Add, Rarely Subtract
> A meatball campaign can evolve into noodles, then sausage, then spaghetti. It's much harder to remove plot threads and simplify. When in doubt, start simple. You can always layer complexity on later as the group proves it can handle it.
