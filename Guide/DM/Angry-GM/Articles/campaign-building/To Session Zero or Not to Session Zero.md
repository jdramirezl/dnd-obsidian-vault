# To Session Zero or Not to Session Zero

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/to-session-zero-or-not-to-session-zero/)
**Date:** April 24, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery
**Tags:** #angry-gm #session-zero #campaign-management #campaign-building

---

## Core Thesis

> True Campaign Managers host a Session Zero when they need to; they don't when they don't.

Session Zeroes are the **exception**, not the rule. Hosting an unnecessary Session Zero wastes the most precious commodity adult gamers have: game time.

## What Session Zero Actually Is

A meeting with your players before the first actual play session. You gather the same way you would for a game, but instead of playing, you talk and work things out.

**Session Zero is NOT for character generation.** Any meeting where characters get made is a "Session CG." These are separate discussions. If you're ready to make characters, you don't need a Session Zero. If you need a Session Zero, you're not ready to make characters.

## The Only Three Good Reasons for Session Zero

### 1. Meeting Strangers
- Players who have never played at your table before
- Players who have never played in the same party together
- You've never presided over these players as GM
- Basically a meet-and-greet to get everyone used to interacting

### 2. Settling Clubhouse Rules
- Figuring out when/how often you play, who hosts, snack responsibilities
- Establishing lateness and attendance policies
- **Only needed when** other channels (group text, etc.) have failed or raised conflicts
- If you distribute rules and get anything but unanimous consent → call a Session Zero
- Conflict resolution demands dialogue — actual back-and-forth

### 3. Group First Vision-Crafting
- If the *group* (not just the GM) is developing the initial Campaign Vision
- This is the "Group First" vs "Game First" campaign distinction

## Reasons That Are NOT Good Enough

- Setting expectations
- Getting buy-in
- Establishing a "social contract"
- Gathering info about what players want or how they prefer to play
- Establishing personal boundaries / lines and veils (Angry argues this actually *damages* the game)

## Practical Tips

- **Host it the same way you host games** (in-person → in-person Session Zero, etc.)
- **If meeting strangers IRL**, use a neutral public space (restaurant, game shop, library)
- **Keep it short**: ~1 hour for clubhouse issues, ~2 hours for meet-and-greet, full session for Campaign Vision development
- **Don't tack on character generation** — when Session Zero is done, send everyone home
- **Communication hierarchy** (most to least effective): In-person → Video chat → Voice chat → Text chat → Written document

## Key Quote

> If you can actually get all your players in the same room or virtual space, it's a crime to waste that time on anything that isn't gaming.
