# Session Zero Part One: How to Run It

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Session Zero Blitz (Part One of One)](https://theangrygm.com/session-zero-part-one/)
**Date:** August 30, 2017
**Series:** Campaign Building / Session Zero (Part 3 of 3)

---

## The Goal

Gather information to come up with a **campaign premise** — a two-or-three sentence description of the game. Session Zero is NOT about presenting your pitch. You walk away with information, then build the pitch later.

## The Core Principle: Conversation, Not Interrogation

A good Session Zero is a **casual conversation with a secret agenda**.

### The Movie Sequel Analogy

Imagine you've secretly been hired to make a sequel to a movie you just saw with friends. You can't tell them. So you steer a casual post-movie dinner conversation, listen carefully, note everything useful, and only intervene when the conversation drifts too far.

## Why This Works: People's Brains Are Stupid

- People are **good at knowing what they like** (emotional response)
- People are **bad at knowing what they want** (prediction based on guesswork)
- People are **terrible at explaining any of it** (reasoning about emotions)

You want their **emotions**, not their reasons. If you discover something that made someone happy and imitate it, you'll make them happy — without needing to know why.

## Practical Setup

- **All players must attend** — reschedule if needed
- Schedule ~90 useful minutes (book ~2 hours)
- Dinner **before** the session (not during, not after)
- Phones away, no tablets/computers at the table
- **Notepad** for story/setting ideas + **separate sheet** with player names for engagement notes
- Pen, not keyboard

### Your Three Goals

1. Fill in campaign decisions: genre/tone, setting, options/restrictions, shape and glue
2. Identify 2–3 **core engagements** (Challenge, Discovery, Expression, Fantasy, Narrative, Submission)
3. Collect story elements players will latch onto

## The Process

### Phase 1: Calling to Order (First Slice of Bread)

Handle practical stuff explicitly:
- Scheduling (frequency, duration)
- Commitment and reliability (honest attendance expectations)
- Campaign duration
- Review settled decisions (system, setting basics)
- State deal-breakers ("No evil characters," etc.)
- Tell players character generation comes later

### Phase 2: The Peanut Butter (Secret Reconnaissance)

Get players talking to each other so you can **shut up and listen**.

**Getting started:** Ask the person to your left about their favorite moment from a past RPG. For new players: favorite part of a video game, book, or movie.

**The Conversation Ball Technique:**
- Initially players toss the ball back to you — you need them tossing it to **each other**
- Go around the table; watch for opportunities to connect two players
- Example: Alice talks about a heist, Dave talks about his rogue → "You both seem to like crazy plans — did your rogue ever do anything like Dave's?"
- After lateralling: raise your pad, lean back, visibly withdraw
- If conversation falters, stay withdrawn — let people fill the silence

**What to listen for:**
- Passionate excitement → story element seed
- Group excitement about themes (evil empires, exploration, horror) → setting/genre clue
- Someone forgiving bad gameplay for good story → Narrative engagement
- Pop culture references people fixate on → write down names, look up later

**Steering:** Don't let conversation stay too long on one topic. Nudge toward different media/genres. Use open-ended questions to specific people, then withdraw. Confirm engagement theories with specific examples ("You're the sort who found every collectible in Doom, aren't you?"), never abstract labels.

### Phase 3: Enough Peanut Butter (Second Slice of Bread)

It's over when: you have enough info, OR conversation is increasingly unfocused, OR people are checking phones.

1. Consult notes — circle standout ideas
2. Throw combinations at the wall: "You know what would be cool? An evil empire of undead like in Warcraft III"
3. See what resonates (muted assent is fine — people are tired)
4. Don't finalize the premise — just test promising combinations

**Wrap-up:** Review scheduling decisions, recap promising ideas, avoid character discussion, set a deadline for the finalized premise, gather notes, go home.

## Key Reminders

- **Shut up and listen**
- **Be honest through lies** — tell players you're building the game with their input (true), while secretly gathering intel (also true)
- **Pop culture is your best friend** for clues to preferences
- **Never discuss engagements directly** — use specific examples
- **Your fun counts too**
