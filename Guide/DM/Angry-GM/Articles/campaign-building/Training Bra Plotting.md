# Training Bra Plotting: How to Plan a Campaign (Or Adventure)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/training-bra-plotting/)
**Date:** November 21, 2017
**Series:** Start Your Own Campaign
**Tags:** #angry-gm #campaign-planning #plot-structure #adventure-design #campaign-building

---

## Core Insight

The only real difference between an encounter, an adventure, and a campaign is the amount of **padding**. Any seed idea could serve equally well as any of the three. The design process is the same: start with the end, then inject plot points between the beginning and the end.

## The Same Seed, Different Scales

| Scale | Seed Example | Plot Points Are... |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Encounter** | "Can the party kill the dragon?" (dramatic question) | Sources of conflict |
| **Adventure** | "Kill the dragon" (resolution/goal) | Encounters |
| **Campaign** | "Kill the dragon" (plot thread endpoint) | Adventures |

## The Three Types of Plot Points

### 1. Checkpoints
Intermediate steps the plot *must* pass through to succeed. They bring the party closer to the goal.

**Example:** Shrub Niggurath has 6 phylacteries hidden throughout the world. The party literally cannot win until all 6 are destroyed. Each phylactery is a checkpoint.

- Can feel like chokepoints/failure points
- But well-run campaigns offer alternate paths if players fail one

### 2. Obstacles
Things that get in the party's way but don't advance the goal. Success = continue as before. Failure = setback or consequence.

**Example:** The King in Saffron lures the party into his Tesseractical Hedge Maze. Escaping doesn't bring them closer to their goal, but failing means becoming lawn ornaments forever.

- Must still have consequences that ripple through the plot
- The longer they're stuck, the stronger the villain grows / more towns fall / etc.

### 3. Diversions
Side quests. Departures from the main story. Impact is tangential to the primary goal.

**Example:** Exploring the Inverted Pyramid to obtain the Sword of Winning. Makes the party more powerful generally, which helps against the final boss, but only indirectly.

- Must still impact the plot somehow
- Time spent on diversions may allow the villain to advance

## How to Build a Campaign

1. **Start with the end** — what's the final goal/resolution?
2. **Figure out the beginning** — how does the story start?
3. **Squirt plot points into the middle** — checkpoints, obstacles, diversions
4. **Don't add more detail than you must** — it's just an outline
5. **Add more plot points as needed** — during play, on the fly

### Example: Shrub Niggurath Campaign
- 6 phylactery adventures (checkpoints)
- Hedge Maze of the King in Saffron (obstacle)
- Cult of Kidnapping rescue (diversion)
- Inverted Pyramid exploration (diversion)
- Introductory adventure discovering the prophecy
- Research adventure learning about the phylacteries
- **= ~12 adventures from one basic idea**

## Padding on the Fly

The real power of this approach: plot points are just notes until you run them.

- **Party fails an adventure?** → Insert a new plot point (recover the lost phylactery from the enemy)
- **Party does something unexpected?** → Add a plot point
- **Cool idea mid-campaign?** → Add a plot point
- **Party wants to chase a side thread?** → Add a plot point

### The Key Difference from Other Approaches

| Approach | Problem |
|----------|---------|
| Plan beginning-to-end (paving a road) | Rigid, hard to adapt |
| Plan only one step ahead | No roadmap, blank-page paralysis |
| **Angry's approach: End → Beginning → Plot points in the middle** | Flexible outline, never lost, always free to change |

> You have a plan. You're never struggling with a blank piece of paper. But you're not tied to anything and you're always free to add, remove, or change ideas.

## Connections

- Plot points at the campaign level = adventures
- Plot points at the adventure level = encounters
- Plot points at the encounter level = sources of conflict
