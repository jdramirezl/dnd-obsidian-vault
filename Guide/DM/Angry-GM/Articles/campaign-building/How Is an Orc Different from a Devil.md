# How Is an Orc Different from a Devil

**Source:** [The Angry GM – How is an Orc Different from a Devil](https://theangrygm.com/how-is-an-orc-different-from-a-devil/)
**Date:** January 31, 2024
**Topic:** Alignment, morality, racial monocultures, and the nature of mortal vs. supernatural evil

---

## Core Idea

"Always evil" means fundamentally different things for mortal creatures (orcs) and supernatural creatures (devils). Understanding the distinction is essential for portraying both well and for making fantasy worlds feel fantastic.

## Part I: Why Orcs Are Evil (Mortal Evil)

### Racial Monocultures Matter
- If every member of every race is a unique individual, everyone's just people with different skin colors and stat bonuses
- Monocultures are what make fantasy worlds feel *fantastic* — they make non-human races genuinely alien and interesting
- Your elf isn't special if he's a unique individual; he's just like everyone else

### Evil by Deed, Not by Identity
- Orcs make evil *choices* and take evil *actions* — they don't do evil for evil's sake
- They're motivated by the same things as everyone: survival, comfort, happiness, freedom from pain
- It's the choices they make in pursuit of those things that are evil
- They don't wake up saying "time to do evil!" — they steal because they want things, kill because people resist, destroy because they're angry

### Nature and Nurture
- Orcs are **predisposed** to evil — it's an inborn trait (from Gruumsh, in D&D terms)
- Elves are predisposed to good (from Corellon)
- Humans are predisposed to evil too, just less strongly — that's why we teach kids to share and be polite
- Humans build societies to check evil tendencies; orcs build societies that *run on* evil tendencies
- The feedback loop: predisposition → society that reinforces predisposition → more evil

### You Still Can't Kill Baby Orcs
- Orcs have **free will** — they are sentient and mortal
- Until they've made evil choices, they are guilty of nothing
- You cannot punish a free-willed creature for a crime you *think* they may commit
- Life is inherently sacred and cannot be destroyed without the best of reasons (like self-defense)
- Aragorn never went on an orc genocide — he only killed orcs posing direct, imminent threats
- The pivotal event of LotR was Bilbo choosing NOT to kill Gollum

### What "Always Evil" Races Represent
- They show what happens when humans don't rise above their base instincts on a societal level
- Orcs = societies governed by anger, bitterness, hatred
- Hobgoblins = military conquest and enslavement
- Goblins = greed, cowardice, laziness
- This lets you explore dark human tendencies without using caricatures of real historical societies

### Are Orcs *Really* Always Evil?
- No. Free will means any orc *could* choose good — it's just billion-to-one odds
- One good orc (like Drizzt) is special and meaningful precisely because it's against all odds
- If every dark elf is a good-aligned rebel, it means nothing
- Redemption must come against all odds and require constant struggle to mean anything

## Part II: Why Devils Are Evil (Supernatural Evil)

### Fundamentally Different
- Devils are evil because they ARE evil — full stop
- Not mortal, no free will, no survival instincts, no aspirations, no pleasure or pain
- They're just evil in the shape of a creature
- "Always evil" for a devil is like "always flesh" for a human

### Corruption vs. Ruination
| | Devils | Demons |
|---|---|---|
| **Method** | Corruption | Ruination |
| **Goal** | Instill evil in mortal souls | Put evil into the world |
| **Specifics** | Make mortals sin (pride, avarice, wrath, lust, envy, gluttony, sloth) | Spread terror, decay, suffering, death, misery, violence |
| **Summary** | Want mortals to be terrible people | Want the world to be a terrible place |

And the celestial mirror:
| | Angels | Guardinals |
|---|---|---|
| **Method** | Elevation | Beautification |
| **Goal** | Make mortals good people | Make the world a good place |

### Outsiders Are Alien
- Single-minded but not stupid — they can use elaborate deceptions, pretend to be redeemable, pretend they're not what they are
- Beyond reason — if a devil is negotiating, it's not willing to compromise; it's tricking you into thinking you can come out ahead
- You have nothing to offer a devil except your own corruption
- Social interactions with devils don't work as challenges — they're inherently one-sided and defensive

### Practical GM Advice
- Assign each fiend/celestial a **specific motive**: a sin, a virtue, a ruin, or a beauty
- Play as if that one trait is the only reason the creature exists
- A devil of greed, an angel of compassion, a demon of terror, a guardinal of harmony

### Can Devils Be Redeemed?
- Fallen angels must exist (good can always fall — that's why we must remain vigilant)
- If angels can fall, they must be able to rise again (nothing falls so far it can't be redeemed)
- BUT: this is a cosmological event, not a current event — it's something that "can have happened" but not something that "can happen"
- Exception: **once** in a campaign, heroes might redeem a fallen angel or bring a devil to light — it must be the rarest, most amazing thing ever
- Beyond that, "always" means **always**

## Key Takeaway for Campaign Building

- Mortal evil is about choices, predisposition, and societal failure — judge by actions, not nature
- Supernatural evil is about pure moral force — alien, single-minded, beyond reason
- Racial monocultures make fantasy feel fantastic; stripping them makes everyone just people
- Non-human races are extrapolations of human nature — they exist to explore aspects of the human experience
