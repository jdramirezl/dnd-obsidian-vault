# My World Has a Sky — Grounding Players

**Source:** [The Angry GM – My World Has a Sky… And Yours Should Too](https://theangrygm.com/my-world-has-a-sky/)
**Date:** November 30, 2022
**Series:** Campaign Building / Running the Game

---

## Core Concept: "The Sky"

"The Sky" is a collective term for time, date, weather, season, lunar cycles, and daylight — everything the characters perceive when they're outside. It's scene-setting data you track and weave into narration constantly.

## Why The Sky Matters

Most GMs cite two reasons for tracking weather and time:

1. **Gamey GMs:** Adds mechanical challenge and unpredictability (rain obscures vision, wind deflects arrows, cold kills the unprotected).
2. **Narrative GMs:** Sets tone (good weather = optimism, fog = spooky, storms = ominous).

Both are valid but secondary. The real reason is **grounding**.

## Grounding Your Players

**Grounding** means never letting the players forget they're interacting with an actual world — not just moving tokens on a map. It keeps them from floating into the abstract space of game mechanics and resource management.

- The real world has a sky that constantly affects you, consciously or subliminally.
- Living under a sky is part of the human experience.
- If you want players to think of characters as people in a world (not avatars on a chessboard), reminding them there's a sky — constantly — is a great habit.

Grounding is holistic and emergent. It grows out of many small things that individually have zero apparent impact on the game but collectively make the world feel real.

**If your players are murderhobos who don't care about the world, that's on you.** You don't "get" grounding.

## Angry's Top Five Sky-Tracking Tips

### Tip 1: Don't Create Anything You Have to Explain More Than Once

Keep your calendar, celestial mechanics, and weather system simple and intuitive. Everything should be inferable from context alone.

- **Example:** 12 months of 28 days each. Seasons named intuitively: Springswax, Springstide, Springsebb.
- Two moons: "Azura the Silver Moon" (good) and "Krisma the Red Moon" (evil). Always say the full name so players remember there are two.
- Add one fantastical element to remind players it's not our sky (a second moon, a ring, weird seasons). But keep it simple — Eberron's 12 moons are too many for anyone to casually describe.

### Tip 2: Never Shut Up About Your Damned Sky

Repetition is your best friend. Players barely notice anything you say — the only way to get it into their brains is to say it a dozen times.

- Start each session with the date.
- First time characters are under the sky, describe weather, daylight, everything.
- Mention the sky in every outdoor scene-setting narration and transition.
- At night, always talk about the moons.

### Tip 3: Mechanics Don't Matter

Don't waste time on detailed weather mechanics. A simple +1 or +2 bonus/penalty for fog or wind is enough, and even that can be inconsistent.

- **Do** reward players who use the sky creatively (waiting for a cloud to cover the moon before sneaking).
- Weather determination: Use the 3.5 DMG tables, a simple one-page generator, or just make it up.
- Track weather with a three-phrase description: temperature, cloud cover/precipitation, wind. Example: "Mild, partly cloudy, breezy."

### Tip 4: Use The Right Sky for the Right Job — Sparingly

Occasionally pick tonally appropriate weather or impose mechanical weather challenges. But don't do it every time or it becomes contrived and predictable.

- If every adventure has matching weather, players will metagame: "Oh crap, he said it's foggy — break out the holy water."
- Use it as spice, not the main course.

### Tip 5: Trust Your Gut

- If something starts to leak out of your mouth — sudden fog, surprise eclipse — go with it. Your brain detected a pattern you didn't consciously notice.
- Keep the sky's current status visible to you at all times so you can infuse every narration with it.
- Games are experiential and emotional. Your conscious, rational brain will often mess that up — don't let it interrupt a good instinctive call.

## Practical Summary

| Element | What to Track | How |
|---|---|---|
| Time | Time of day, date, season | Simple calendar, notes on paper |
| Weather | Temperature, clouds/precipitation, wind | Three-phrase description |
| Celestial | Moon phases, daylight hours | Optional but adds fantasy flavor |
| Mechanics | Almost nothing | Rare +1/+2 adjustments at most |

The Sky doesn't need to *do* anything. It just needs to *be there* — and you need to actually describe it.
