# Open World Lesson 5: A Living World of Stuff to Do

**Source:** [The Angry GM – AOWG Lesson Five](https://theangrygm.com/angry-open-world-lesson-five/)
**Date:** September 1, 2021
**Topic:** Seeding an open-world game with content and making the world feel alive

---

## Core Idea

An open world must feel **dynamic** — like it keeps spinning whether the players are watching or not. You achieve this by filling the world with things to interact with (Stories, Tasks, and Problems) and by being relentlessly specific in your narration.

## The Reactively Proactive

- Open-world gameplay isn't as proactive as you think — most players need an invitation to act
- You always have to give players something to do; open-world gaming just also gives them the chance to do something *else* instead
- Players on rails who see side paths feel like they're exploring; players with no direction just stare at their character sheets

## A Dynamic World

- In most D&D campaigns, the world only exists to serve the current adventure — everything comes alive when players enter and goes back to sleep when they leave
- An open world must feel like it exists independently of the players
- **Randomness helps:** when players see you making rolls and pulling things out of thin air, it proves they're not just connecting pre-planned quest dots
- A dynamic world keeps changing whether the players act or not — but player action can nudge it in a different direction (that's agency)
- For heroic games: the world always evolves in a crappy direction until the heroes get involved

## Three Categories of Content

### 1. Stories (Interactive Flavor Text)
- Just things happening in the world that don't involve the players
- No rewards, no XP, no quests — narrative equivalent of empty rooms
- Examples: two washerwomen arguing about their kids, indecipherable graffiti on a dungeon wall, an empty treasure chest with a broken lock, a pile of rocks with an offering bowl
- Purpose: show the players the world has a life outside their adventures
- Can be simple and incomplete, or complicated and interactive

### 2. Tasks (Roll a Die, Get a Prize)
- Require a single action or decision from one player
- May cost a minor resource (time, money) and require a single die roll or small risk
- Reward: a little treasure, access to a resource, a minor bonus, a small discovery, or a lead to further adventure
- Town examples: deliver a message, collect a debt, shadow someone, watch someone's stuff
- Wilderness/dungeon examples: climb an obstacle, make an offering at a shrine, decipher a script, pick a lock on a chest
- **Purpose for players:** teaches them that poking and prodding at the world pays off
- **Purpose for GM:** lets you send players in a direction so they "discover" things you wanted them to find — and they think it was their own idea

### 3. Problems (The Real Adventures)
- Something that demands a solution — a goal requiring a series of actions, choices, risks, costs, and consequences
- Takes the skills of an entire adventuring party
- NOT quests with glowing arrows — present a *situation*, not a solution
- Example: instead of "go to the tomb and get the sword," present "undead roaming the road + nearby tomb of an undead-slaying paladin" — let players connect the dots (or find a different solution entirely)
- Players can reject and redefine problems — they set their own goals from the situations you present

### Quest vs. Problem
> A quest happens when the GM hands the players a goal. An open-world quest happens when the GM points out a problem and the players make it a goal and decide how to solve it.

## Planting Seeds: The Art of Specificity

### Where Content Comes From
- You make it up — either in prep or during play
- Players will also invent content by deciding some random detail must be a Story, Task, or Problem
- Content enters the game through narration — while setting scenes, describing outcomes, transitioning

### The Key Technique: Be Specific
- Never describe anything in general without adding something specific
- A treasure chest is just a container — but one marred with scratches and burns is a Story
- Mention the thronging market crowd, but call out the performing wizard, the street preacher, the half-orc beggar
- Trees and plants press close on the path — but is there a waymarker? A ranger's mark? A rabbit caught in a snare?
- Debris covers the ruined abbey floor — but what about the broken gnomon from a smashed sundial? A crumpled pewter cup? A jagged bit of serrated bone dagger?

### Why Specificity Works
- At worst, specific details are just signs the world is lived in — background texture
- At best, they become foreshadowing, hooks, or player-driven adventures
- Between sessions, you might figure out what that random detail means and build on it
- Once you make a habit of specificity, you can weave Tasks and Problems seamlessly into narration

### It's Okay to Be Obvious Sometimes
- Some Stories, Tasks, and Problems can drop themselves in the players' laps
- A duel interrupting dinner, screams from a monster attack — these are fine
- Not everything needs to be subtle background detail

## Key Takeaway

Fill your world with specific, clickable details. Most will just be texture. Some will become adventures. The players won't know which is which — and that's exactly what makes the world feel alive and open.
