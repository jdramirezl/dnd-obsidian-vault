# From Zero to Pitch in 24 Hours

**Source:** [The Angry GM – From Zero to Pitch in 24 Hours](https://theangrygm.com/from-zero-to-pitch-in-24-hours/)
**Date:** December 29, 2017
**Topic:** The practical process of going from Session Zero to a campaign pitch

---

## Core Idea

A worked example of the entire campaign-starting process: from having nothing but "I want to run a game" to delivering a written pitch to players within 24 hours of Session Zero. The creative process is shown step-by-step, including the messy, intuitive parts.

## The Process (Summary)

1. **Note firm decisions** — schedule, system, any pre-existing ideas
2. **Recruit players** — with at least the time commitment defined
3. **Session Zero** — 1–2 hour casual conversation; get players talking about what they enjoy
4. **Lob ideas, take notes** — see what players respond to emotionally
5. **Wait 24–48 hours** — a campaign idea "magically appears" in your head
6. **Write the Pitch** — 1–2 page description of why the campaign will be awesome
7. **Send to players** — if they agree, develop details while they create characters

## Pre-Session Zero Decisions

### Schedule First
- Schedule is the BIGGEST factor in starting a new game — not system, not player compatibility
- Weekly games are best for immersion and emotional investment
- 4–5 hour sessions hit the sweet spot (3 hours too short, 5 pushes endurance)
- Set a campaign end date if you know one (e.g., 6–7 months)

### System Shortlist
- Don't go in with a blank sheet — constrain the group decision
- Prepare a shortlist of systems you'd be happy running
- Be willing to accept the group's preference even if it's not your first choice

### Know Yourself
- Know what you like to run: world-building, exploration, action, epic quests, etc.
- Your preferences inform the system list and will shape the pitch

## Session Zero Insights

### Getting Natural Responses
- People are good at knowing what they like when they see it, but terrible at thinking about what they like in the abstract
- You want their emotional lizard-brain responses, not pre-planned answers
- Ask about past gaming experiences — what was fun, what was missing

### What Emerged (Example)
- **Two players** wanted attachment to the world — recurring characters, a home base, relationships
- **One player** wanted old-school frontier exploration and adventure
- **Three players** lit up about mystery adventures they'd played before
- **One player** casually asked about genasi (noted!)
- **One player** mentioned a favorite cleric of Kord (god of storms/sea — noted!)
- **Doorknob question:** "Can there be a dragon?"

### Synthesizing Conflicting Desires
- Homebodies wanting a small kingdom vs. GM wanting wide exploration → **island archipelago** (home port city + wild islands to explore)
- Mystery lovers + epic quest lover → **"Layers of the Onion" campaign** (deep mystery underlying the adventures)
- Emotional attachment + external threat → **kingdom in danger from hostile force**
- Travel progression idea: rowboats → ship → airship (scope grows with level)

## The Pitch

### Key Selling Points
1. Refugees who forged a kingdom after fleeing invasion
2. Powerful ancient magic tied to the islands
3. Discovering the truth of the people's past
4. Progression from accidental heroes to saviors of the kingdom

### What a Good Pitch Does
- Shows off a world the players want to explore
- Leaves questions the players want to answer
- Sells the *feeling* of the campaign, not the plot details
- You don't need all the answers — the pitch represents almost everything you know at that point

## Key Lessons

- The pitch sells the game you *want* to run, not the one you've already written
- Lucky creative breaks happen when you let ideas collide (Wind Waker + Kord + genasi = island archipelago)
- Players' offhand comments and preferences are gold — note everything
- Conflicting player desires aren't problems; they're creative constraints that produce better campaigns
