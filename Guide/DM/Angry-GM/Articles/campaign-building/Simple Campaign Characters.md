# Simple Campaign Characters (Curated Character Generation)

## Key Advice

**Character generation should be fast, minimal, and GM-controlled.** Players don't need backstories, personality traits, or goals to start playing — they need a completed character sheet, a single motivation (not a goal), and a two-sentence physical description. The GM drives the process using **Curated Character Generation (CCG)**: players submit two character concepts, the GM picks one for each player, and then everyone fills out sheets together in a fast, boot-camp-style session. This prevents overthinking, discourages pre-play attachment, and gets the game started quickly.

## Summary

Part of the *Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign* series. This lesson covers step 2: generating the party's characters.

**Philosophy — less is more:**
- Character generation is NOT roleplaying. The more "done" a character is before play, the less it's shaped by in-game experiences.
- Players should create characters at the *start* of their stories — not interesting yet, but about to become interesting.
- The campaign premise already provides every character's backstory: normal people who decided to become adventurers and are heading to the starter town.
- Enforce the premise rigidly. No epic backstories, no prior adventures, no exotic origins that contradict "humble beginnings."

**A complete character has exactly three parts:**
1. A completed character sheet (without Personality Traits, Ideals, Bonds, and Flaws)
2. A motivation — a single word or short phrase describing their inner drive (not a goal)
3. A two-sentence physical description

**Motivation vs. goals:**
- Motivation is a nebulous inner need that can be fed but never fulfilled: amass wealth, help others, explore the world, prove your mettle, etc.
- Goals are specific and completable. RPGs already provide plenty of goals. Motivation explains *why* characters accept certain goals.
- Sample motivations: amass wealth, earn glory, gain respect, accrue power, discover truth, do good, help others, perfect self, satisfy wanderlust, mete out justice, spread the faith, prove your mettle, master a skill.

**The Curated Character Generation process:**
1. **Write a Campaign Primer** — the premise plus a curated list of allowed races, classes, backgrounds, and motivations.
2. **Cull options** — always offer *fewer* options than the core rulebook. Remove at least one race, one class, one background. This defines your world and prevents decision paralysis.
3. **Replacement rule** — supplements are fine, but every addition must replace a core option. No net additions.
4. **Players submit two different character concepts** — race, class, background, motivation only. No more than 8 words each. Two genuinely different concepts, not variations.
5. **GM secretly picks one concept per player** — aim for variety and complementary motivations, but don't overthink it. Party composition barely matters.
6. **Character generation session** — fast, boot-camp style. Announce which concept each player is building. March them through the sheet step by step. Don't give them time to discuss, backstory-build, or overthink.
7. **Take the sheets home** — mumble about "updating notes." Keep them until the first session.
8. **Start the game ASAP.**

**Why withhold information and control the process:**
- Prevents players from getting attached to or overthinking concepts before play.
- Stops players from coordinating backstories and relationships prematurely.
- Ensures the GM gets a functional, varied party.
- Players discover their characters through play, not through pre-game writing.

**Character generation rules (for D&D 5E):**
- Standard array for ability scores (15, 14, 13, 12, 10, 8)
- Max HP at first level, average thereafter
- Starting equipment from class and background (no shopping)
- No feats (ability score increases only)
- Alignment: yes. Personality Traits/Ideals/Bonds/Flaws: no.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Write your Campaign Primer** — paste in your premise, then list the allowed races, classes, backgrounds, and motivations. Include brief glossary definitions for unfamiliar options.
2. **Cull aggressively** — aim for ~4 races, ~8 classes, ~8 backgrounds. Remove anything that doesn't fit, feels mechanically problematic, or you just don't like.
3. **Ask each player for two different concepts** — race, class, background, motivation only. Set a deadline. Don't approve anything until you have all submissions.
4. **Pick one concept per player** — look for variety and complementary motivations. Don't overthink it.
5. **Run character generation as a fast, controlled session** — march players through the sheet. Don't let them slow down to discuss backstory or relationships.
6. **Have players prepare a two-sentence physical description** for the first session.
