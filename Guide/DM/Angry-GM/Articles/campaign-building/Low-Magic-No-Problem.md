## Summary

There are two approaches to "low magic" in D&D. The **easy way**: magic is rare in the *world*, but PCs are the exception. Role-play the world's reaction — NPCs fear magic, there are no magic shops, wizards are legends. This works fine and doesn't require rule changes. The **hard way**: actually removing magic from the *players'* hands. This is where D&D breaks.

The problem is structural. In 5e, almost every class has explicit magic. Only fighter, barbarian, and rogue are truly non-magical — and two of those fill the same party role. You literally cannot build a diverse, functional party without magic. Even racial abilities are magical. The system is saturated.

Angry's deeper insight: the real motivation behind the "low magic" desire isn't nostalgia for Conan-style swords-and-sorcery. It's that **magic in D&D doesn't feel magical anymore.** Every character sneezes magic. Spells are shallow, numerous, and follow different rules for no good reason (why isn't Lay on Hands a spell?). There's no sense of wonder, risk, or sacrifice. Magic is just another resource to spend. The "kitchen sink" design philosophy — throw in every magical option for every class — has made magic feel rote and mechanical rather than fantastic and impossible.

The honest conclusion: you can't run a truly low-magic game in D&D 5e without breaking the system so badly it's no longer D&D. If that's what you want, use a different system (Conan RPG, Dungeon Crawl Classics, Warhammer 2e, etc.). What D&D actually needs is fewer, deeper, more meaningful magical options — but that's a design problem, not a GM problem.

## Key Advice

- **The easy low-magic approach works fine.** Keep magic rare in the world but let PCs be the exceptions. Role-play NPC reactions: fear, awe, suspicion, torch-and-pitchfork mobs.
- **Decide what the world knows about magic** — whether true or not — and play that consistently. Warn players that using magic will make them pariahs.
- **You don't need systems for this.** Just role-play the world. When someone casts a spell in public, decide how the NPCs react based on the world's relationship with magic.
- **The hard approach (removing player magic) breaks D&D.** Almost every class is magical. Fighter, barbarian, and rogue are your only truly non-magical options, and that's not enough for a diverse party.
- **The real problem isn't "too much magic in the world."** It's that magic doesn't *feel* magical. It's shallow, abundant, and mechanical. There's no wonder, no risk, no sacrifice.
- **D&D has become fantasy superheroes.** Every character is bursting with magical abilities, none of which feel special or defining. Players are jaded by their own power.
- **If you truly want low magic, use a different system.** D&D 5e cannot support it without becoming something else entirely.
- **The Angryverse approach:** No one remembers how to make magic items (all are ancient relics). Arcane colleges no longer exist. Priests and clerics are distinct — priests pray and hope, clerics are chosen individuals with divine agency. Magic exists but is rare, respected, and not commercially available.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Decide which kind of "low magic" you want.** Rare-in-the-world (easy) or rare-in-the-party (hard/impossible in 5e)?
2. **For rare-in-the-world:** Define the world's relationship with magic. Is it feared? Respected? Illegal? Unknown? Write 2-3 sentences about how average people perceive magic.
3. **Role-play the consequences.** When PCs cast spells publicly, have NPCs react according to your world's magic culture. No system needed — just consistent role-play.
4. **Remove magic item shops.** Make all magic items ancient relics or quest rewards. This alone dramatically changes the feel.
5. **Distinguish priests from clerics.** Priests are religious leaders without magical power. Clerics are rare, chosen individuals. This makes divine magic feel special.
6. **Warn players at session zero.** If magic use will have social consequences, players need to know before they build characters.
7. **If you want magic out of player hands, switch systems.** Consider Dungeon Crawl Classics, Warhammer Fantasy 2e, Conan RPG, or Zweihander. Don't torture D&D into something it's not.

> [!tip] The Angryverse Magic Framework
> A simple way to make magic feel rare without changing rules: (1) No magic item shops — all items are ancient relics. (2) No arcane colleges — wizards are self-taught or apprenticed to rare mentors. (3) Priests ≠ Clerics — most religious figures have no magical power. (4) NPCs react to magic with appropriate awe, fear, or suspicion. This preserves full player options while making the *world* feel low-magic.
