# Time is an Illusion, Downtime Doubly So

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/time-is-an-illusion/)
**Date:** October 1, 2018
**Series:** Random BS / Game Design Theory
**Tags:** #angry-gm #downtime #time #game-design #crafting

---

## Core Problem

Time doesn't function as a meaningful resource in TTRPGs the way it does in other games. This makes downtime systems fundamentally broken because there's no real cost to spending time.

## The Design Problem

- **In board games**, time is limited by turns. Wasting a turn has real cost because competitors advance.
- **In video games**, time is a *player* resource — grinding takes real-world hours away from fun activities (opportunity cost).
- **In RPGs**, any amount of in-game time can be compressed to a single narrative sentence: "Several days pass and then…" There is no inherent opportunity cost.

## Why "The GM Can Fix It" Isn't an Answer

> "Well, the GM can fix it by designing adventures the right way."

This is not a valid game design answer. If time is important, the *game designers* have to make it important mechanically. Asking GMs to cover for broken design is a cop-out.

## The Downtime Definition Problem

No two people agree on what "downtime" means. It can include:
- Buying equipment
- Leveling up characters
- Healing from injuries
- Crafting items
- Running businesses
- Building castles
- Using Profession/Craft skills

## The Crafting Spiral

Angry's thought process on why crafting breaks down:

1. Money and ingredients come from adventuring (it's a game about adventuring).
2. But characters with skills *can* earn money/ingredients without adventuring.
3. If ingredients are mundane enough to find during adventures, they're mundane enough to buy or forage.
4. The only limiting factor is *time* — but time isn't a real cost in RPGs.
5. Players will always say "we'll just wait" because there's no mechanical consequence.

## The Opportunity Cost Framework

Time works as a resource when it has **opportunity cost** — choosing one thing means giving up another. Requirements for time to work as a resource in RPGs:

1. **Limited** — you can only have so much of it
2. **Versatile** — there's more than one thing you want to spend it on
3. **Consequential** — wasting time must have mechanical consequences
4. **Not arbitrary** — can't just say "you can only have X downtime hours"
5. **Not more lucrative than adventuring** — adventurers shouldn't make a living by NOT adventuring
6. **Simple** — can't put undue burden on the game
7. **Optional** — only players who want to engage need to
8. **Fair** — players who use it can't tyrannize those who don't
9. **Scalable** — GM can decide how much complexity to include

## Key Insight

> Any game that allows the PCs to do ANYTHING that affects the game while off camera — whether it's crafting or training or building contacts or earning money — NEEDS a robust, mechanical system that somehow governs time as a resource.
