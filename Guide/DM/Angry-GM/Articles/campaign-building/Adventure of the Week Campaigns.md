# Adventure of the Week Campaigns

**Source:** [The Angry GM – These Are the Voyages: Adventure of the Week Campaigns](https://theangrygm.com/these-are-the-voyages/)
**Date:** February 21, 2018
**Series:** Campaign Building / Campaign Descriptors

---

## What Is an Adventure of the Week Campaign?

A campaign where each adventure is a **self-contained story** with a setup, climax, and resolution. Pluck any adventure out and you have a complete, satisfying story. The world begins when the players sit down (inciting incident) and ends when the adventure is resolved. Between adventures, the party is just lounging in a tavern or traveling — the world only exists during adventures.

This is a **Campaign Descriptor** (can be mixed with other descriptors and applied to different campaign shapes), not a Campaign Shape.

## Adventure of the Week vs. Plate of Meatballs

These look similar but are fundamentally different:

| Plate of Meatballs (Shape) | Adventure of the Week (Descriptor) |
|---|---|
| Adventures are truly self-contained with no unifying plotline | Adventures are self-contained but **can** have connecting plot threads |
| Could be played in any order | Can have an order that matters for ongoing threads |
| No through-line at all | Through-line possible via recurring villains, organizations, or themes |

Most Adventure of the Week campaigns naturally settle into a Plate of Meatballs shape, but they don't have to. You can have an AotW campaign shaped like a Noodle or even Spaghetti.

**Star Trek TNG** = Plate of Meatballs (truly episodic, no continuity)
**Star Trek DS9** = Adventure of the Week Noodle (self-contained episodes with threaded plot arcs)

## Why Run One?

- **Variety:** Formulaic structure makes it easier to make every adventure different (ruins one week, murder mystery the next, volcano evacuation after that)
- **Action-focused:** Only the exciting parts matter — show up, get a goal, accomplish it
- **Satisfying narratives:** Self-contained stories can be more satisfying than a 3-year campaign that takes forever to resolve
- **Schedule-friendly:** Great for erratic attendance or infrequent sessions, especially if adventures fit into single sessions
- **Easy to maintain:** GM can run whatever inspires them without fitting it into a larger puzzle
- **Organized play:** This is why organized play programs use this format

## Weaknesses

- Players can feel **disconnected** from the larger world
- Lack of overarching plot can weigh on players who need more than endless unrelated problems
- Adventures can start to feel **formulaic and repetitive** despite variety
- Like a high-fiber diet: keeps things moving, but too much movement isn't always good

## Running an AotW Campaign

### Issue 1: Writing Self-Contained Adventures

Each adventure must provide **everything it needs** from beginning to end:

- **Motivation must be built into the adventure itself** — you can't draw on past events without reintroducing them
- You can reference a past that never happened or off-screen events — players in AotW campaigns quickly learn to roll with sudden revelations
- **Resolution must be complete** — no loose ends, no sequel hooks, no setting up the next adventure
- Every adventure must wrap up all plotlines and have its celebratory cake party

> Assume every adventure includes a completely new group of players and that the players will never come back. Get that in your head and you've got a good AotW campaign.

### Issue 2: Continuity

If you erase ALL continuity, you're not running a campaign anymore. Something must tie adventures together — it's just not the adventures themselves.

**Sources of continuity:**
- **Characters:** Same protagonists who remember their adventures and grow
- **Setting:** The Ongoing Adventures of the Adventuring Adventurers in the World of Adventure
- **A unifying game/setting feature** (most important for drop-in/drop-out groups):
  - Crew of a starship flying planet to planet
  - Heroes for hire with a home base
  - Members of an organization briefed by a superior

The unifying element becomes the **formula opener** — the stand-in for "everything is perfectly normal, and then one day…" It anchors players, glosses over between-adventure time, and gets the game started. It should be **stable** — it can be endangered during an adventure but must be safe again by the end.

## Noodles of the Week: Adding Plot Threads

You CAN have ongoing plot threads and still run an AotW campaign. The key difference: **players don't advance the plot — they hammer it back down**.

### The Whack-a-Mole Approach

Instead of the party constantly moving toward destroying the cult and killing Orcus (Epic Quest), each adventure involves discovering a particular cultist's particular plot in a particular location, countering it, and moving on. Until next time.

**Think Sailor Moon:** Each week, a villain comes up with a new scheme. Sailor Moon discovers it, thwarts it, fights the monster. The villain escapes to try again.

### Options for Plot Thread Integration

| Approach | Example |
|----------|---------|
| Single recurring thread every adventure | "The Negaverse is at it again, Sailor Scouts" |
| Alternate between thread adventures and stand-alones | X-Files: alien conspiracy one week, monster-of-the-week the next |
| Multiple threads, flit between them | DS9: Bajor, Dominion, Ferengi, stand-alones |

### Occasional Big Victories

You can loosen the format occasionally:
- Wipe out a lieutenant → new lieutenant fills the vacuum → new routine
- Give players a sense of permanent impact, then settle into a new formula
- When the campaign feels done → one big adventure that ties up the major thread
- These big episodes aren't things players built toward — they're **opportunities that present themselves**

> "They are just really big moles that need to be whacked down. And the biggest one of all will break the machine."
