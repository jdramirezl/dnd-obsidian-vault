# Angry Gods

## Key Advice

**D&D doesn't have religion — it has gods as game mechanics.** If you want religion to feel meaningful in your world, build religions as worldviews that shape cultures, values, and societies — not as spreadsheets of deities with stat blocks. Deliberately leave the ultimate truth unknowable, even to yourself. The mystery and imperfection is what makes religion feel real.

## Summary

This article introduces the Angryverse's approach to religion and serves as both worldbuilding lore and a masterclass in designing compelling fantasy religions. Angry argues that D&D's default approach — gods as class features on a spreadsheet — produces "religion for atheists" that feels hollow and mechanical.

**Core Design Principles:**

- **Religions are worldviews, not god-lists** — A religion is an understanding of how the cosmos works and how to live in it. The gods are secondary to the moral and philosophical framework.
- **Naturalistic vs. Moralistic religions** — Naturalistic religions (paganism, shamanism) explain natural forces. Moralistic religions teach civilization how to function. In the Angryverse, naturalistic "gods" are actually manifestations of a pantheistic force (druids, not clerics). Moralistic religions power divine magic.
- **The truth is deliberately unknowable** — Angry refuses to define the objective truth behind his world's religions, even to himself. Multiple contradictory faiths can all be "right" because they're imperfect interpretations of something too vast for mortals to comprehend.
- **Gods don't have stat blocks** — They exist above the game world. Players can never meet them, fight them, or speak to them directly. Their agents (angels, etc.) are fair game.
- **Story-first, not mechanics-first** — The Vasaari Faith is built around a compelling narrative (The Books of the Prophets) that shapes culture, not around a list of domains and alignments.

**The Vasaari Faith (example):**
- Based on the teachings of angelic beings (Vasaar) who took mortal form as Prophets
- Five Books of the Prophets, each with distinct themes: duty and justice (The King), acceptance and fate (The Watchers), diligence and mastery (The Twins), good-hearted heroism (The Wanderer), and mystery/doubt (The Stranger)
- Clergy structure: ordained priests vs. lay priests, with divine magic granted rarely and by divine will alone
- Knightly orders tied to specific virtues: Hospitallers (mercy), Justiciars (truth), Crusaders (courage)

## Actionable Steps

1. **Build religions around stories, not stat blocks** — Write a founding narrative for each faith. What story do the faithful tell about who they are and how the world works?
2. **Distinguish naturalistic from moralistic faiths** — Nature spirits and elemental forces can be one tradition (druidic/primal). Moral philosophy and civilization-building can be another (clerical/divine).
3. **Leave the ultimate truth unknowable** — Don't define which religion is "really right." Make them compatible enough to coexist in the same world but contradictory enough to be distinct. The mystery is the point.
4. **Keep gods above the game** — No stat blocks, no direct appearances, no killing gods at level 20. Gods should be forces that shape the world through faith, agents, and institutions.
5. **Make clergy titles and structures faith-specific** — A priest of one faith should have a different title, training path, and role than a priest of another. This makes religions feel culturally distinct.
6. **Include doubt and imperfection** — Even within a faith's own scriptures, include moments of doubt, contradiction, and humanization. The Book of the Stranger is a brilliant example — it questions the other Prophets and adds complexity.

> [!tip] The Religion Reality Check
> If your world's religion can be fully described by a table of deity names, domains, and alignments, it's not a religion — it's a spreadsheet. Real religions shape how people live, what they value, how they treat strangers, and what they fear. Build the culture first, then figure out which class features it supports.
