# What's Really In a Campaign

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Supplemental Bulls$%&: What's Really In a Campaign](https://theangrygm.com/whats-really-in-a-campaign/)
**Date:** February 9, 2022

---

## The Problem: Nobody Knows What "Campaign" Means Anymore

- The word *campaign* has become fuzzy. Many newer players conflate it with *setting*, *adventure*, *adventure path*, or *module*.
- WotC and Paizo share blame (calling campaigns "adventures," inventing "adventure path"), but TSR did it too — the terminology has always been messy.
- This isn't just pedantic — it signals a **failure to understand a fundamental concept** about how RPGs are structured.

## The Never-Ending Adventure

D&D's core gameplay loop is immediate and moment-to-moment:
1. GM describes an immediate situation.
2. Player declares an immediate action.
3. GM determines the outcome.

This works great for dungeon crawling but **breaks down** for travel, town exploration, downtime, and anything that isn't moment-to-moment decision-making. GMs intuitively know this but have no alternative tools.

**The result:** Adventures never end. One goal leads to another without pause. There's no space between adventures. Shopping, selling loot, carousing, leveling up — it all happens mid-adventure because nothing is allowed to stop the game.

### Why Modern D&D Makes This Worse

- Healing is instant (no recovery downtime forcing breaks).
- Leveling happens anywhere, anytime.
- GMs drop new quests before old ones finish.
- Optional between-adventure activities (carousing, businesses, crafting) have no mechanical purpose.
- Supply management, trip planning, and equipment upkeep have been stripped away.

All these things once **sneakily injected structure** into campaigns: *start in town → get quest → do quest → return to town to rest/recover/advance*. That structure is gone.

## Structure Is the Real Issue

**Structure** is what makes a campaign feel like more than an endless sequence of events. It's the invisible framework that:
- Molds the story (gameplay structure provides narrative structure).
- Tells players what to do and when.
- Empowers player agency by creating distinct modes of play.
- Creates differentiation — different "games within the game."

### Structure in Other Games

- **4X strategy games** (Civilization, Starcraft): Explore → Expand → Exploit → Exterminate. The systems enforce the structure.
- **Open-world video games**: Resource gathering, quest completion, exploration, and town activities are distinct modes with clear boundaries. Players choose which "game" to play at any moment.
- **JRPGs** (Final Fantasy VII): Even within a single city, the game alternates between "explore town and talk" and "fight through dungeon and kill boss."

### Structure in Stories

- *Fellowship of the Ring*: Clear adventures (Escaping the Shire, Mines of Moria, Flight down the Anduin) interspersed with rest periods (Rivendell, Lothlórien) and travel.
- Without structure, a campaign feels like listening to that friend who sucks at telling stories — an endless sequence of "and then" with no point.

## Adventures Must Begin and End

For a campaign to have structure:
- Adventures must **begin and end**. Players must **feel** when each begins and ends.
- There must be **space between adventures** — time that feels different from adventuring.
- Things done *on adventures* must be distinct from things done *between adventures*.

### The "How Much Would It Suck If the GM Died?" Test

Pick a spot in the story. If the GM died and there was never another session:
- **Not at all?** → The campaign could end there.
- **A bit, but survivable?** → End of a major arc. Campaign could end but you'd want a sequel.
- **Kind of a lot?** → End of an adventure.
- **Unbearable?** → Middle of an adventure.

## The Secret Definition

> **A campaign** is an ongoing game of D&D that goes on for **four or more** gameplay sessions.
> **An adventure** is a single, **one-to-three session** chunk of that game.

The practical rule: **No more than three sessions of adventure between breaks the players can feel.** However you structure your game — dungeons of the week, chapters in an epic, megadungeon delves — insert a felt break every three sessions at most.

## Key Takeaways

- The GM is responsible for providing structure. The system won't do it for you anymore.
- GMs with a natural sense of gameplay flow create structure intuitively. Those without it produce Never-Ending Adventures and eventually burn out.
- The words *campaign* and *adventure* are arbitrary — the GM defines them through gameplay and story divisions.
- Without structure, campaigns lose their luster over time, leading to constant restarts, character swaps, and burnout cycles.
- **Bingeable series** are the modern equivalent of the Never-Ending Adventure — addictive but unmemorable, because they withhold the structural elements brains need to form lasting memories.
