# A Campaign Manager's Guide to Selfishness

## Key Advice

The GM is the de facto president of a social gaming club. Before you can manage your players' interactions, you must manage your own needs. **Healthy self-interest is the prerequisite for sustainable campaign management.** Your leisure time is a finite treasure — no one is entitled to it, and you owe no one a seat at your table. A disinvested GM runs a terrible game, so protecting your own enjoyment is not selfish — it's the foundation of a good campaign.

## Summary

This article is a prerequisite lesson for the True Campaign Managery course. Angry argues that most GMs fail at campaign management because they can't manage their own needs first. He introduces the concept of **Social Calculus** — every participant carries an internal cost-benefit scale that determines whether the game is worth their time. The GM's scale carries the most weight because they invest the most effort (prep, hosting, social management). When costs outweigh benefits, burnout and game death follow.

Key concepts include:
- **The Invisible Hand of Self-Interest**: GMs are a rare commodity (1 in 5 gamers). Keeping yourself happy is the most selfless thing you can do because you're hard to replace.
- **Social Calculus**: Everyone unconsciously weighs costs vs. benefits. If the game isn't the best use of your 4 hours, it will eventually fall apart.
- **Emotions Know No Reason**: You don't need a "good reason" to be unhappy. Feelings are valid even when irrational.
- **Negotiation Over Prevention**: Preemptive measures (lines, veils, social contracts) can't predict the future. Open communication, compromise, and conflict resolution are the only real solutions.
- **Assertiveness ≠ Selfishness**: Stating your wants and needs honestly is the starting point for any productive social negotiation.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Acknowledge your own needs first** — Before session zero, identify what you want out of the campaign. Write it down. Your needs are valid and non-negotiable as a starting point.
2. **Run your internal cost-benefit check regularly** — After each session, ask yourself: "Is this still worth my time and effort?" If the answer trends negative, act before burnout sets in.
3. **State preferences clearly during character creation** — Don't let players sit in polite silence. Model assertiveness by declaring your campaign vision first, then ask each player to state their honest preferences.
4. **Don't burden new players with costs before showing benefits** — Minimize restrictions and rules-lawyering at the start. Present positive options instead of negative restrictions.
5. **Accept that hard decisions will come** — You may someday need to remove a player for the health of the game. Prepare yourself mentally for that possibility.
6. **Treat your game like a club you run** — You are the president. Accept responsibility for the game as a shared social activity, but remember that the club exists for everyone's enjoyment, including yours.

> [!tip] The GM's Selfishness Principle
> Your fun is not optional — it's the engine that powers the entire campaign. A GM who sacrifices their own enjoyment "for the players" will eventually burn out, and then *everyone* loses. Protect your leisure time like the rare treasure it is. The only proper starting place for social interaction is honest self-interest.
