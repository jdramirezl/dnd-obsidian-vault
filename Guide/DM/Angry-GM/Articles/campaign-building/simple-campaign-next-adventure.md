## Summary

The second adventure — and every adventure after it — is fundamentally different from the first. The first adventure got a free pass: players made characters knowing it was coming, and everyone tolerates a railroad to start. From now on, every "next adventure" must handle **motivation** (why do the PCs care?), **hooks** (how do they learn the goal?), **lures** (what catches their attention?), **preparation** (rewarding players who plan ahead), **travel** (making the journey matter), and **interwovenness** (connecting adventures to each other and the world). Angry introduces the **Big Prep / Little Prep** cycle — do broad adventure design (premise, conflict, structure) as "big prep" when you know a new adventure is needed, then fill in session-by-session details as "little prep." He also explains the **Art of the Setup** (both Planned and Improvisational) and the **Rumormill** as tools for campaign continuity. A detailed worked example shows how to derive a next adventure from Town's Fun Facts.

## Key Advice

### Big Prep / Little Prep
1. **Big Prep** = premise, major conflict, act structure, motivations, hook, lure, prep opportunities, side quest, travel plan. Done when you know a new adventure is needed.
2. **Little Prep** = individual scenes, encounters, maps, stat blocks, session-by-session details. Done as needed, one session at a time.
3. **Do big prep before the party reaches Town** after their current adventure. The hook and lure need to be planted in Town.

### The Art of the Setup
4. **Planned Setups** — deliberately insert details that foreshadow future adventures.
5. **Improvisational Setups** — take random details you already invented (Fun Facts, rumors) and build future events around them so it *looks* like you planned it.
6. **The Rumormill** serves three purposes: adventure inspiration, improvisational setups, and player-driven adventure selection (players show interest → you build that adventure).

### What Next Adventures Need (That First Adventures Don't)

7. **Motivation** — check that at least half the party has a reason to pursue the adventure. Use written motivations + four preinstalled motivations:
   - **Survival** (threats, need for money/food/healing)
   - **Advancement** (XP, magic items, new abilities)
   - **Protection** (threats to things they care about)
   - **Camaraderie** (going along because their friends are going)
8. **Hook** — a scene that tells PCs what to do and where to start. Answer: *How do they know the goal? How do they know where to start?*
9. **Lure** — what catches their attention and draws them to the hook. Answer: *How do they know the adventure exists? How do they know it serves their motivations?* Use Town's Essential Services list to place lures where players will naturally go.
10. **Preparation** — come up with 3 useful-but-not-necessary things (info, resources, allies) the party could acquire in Town before departing. Plant them:
    - One obvious and easy to find
    - One harder, requiring questions
    - One hidden, requiring hunting or overcoming a challenge
11. **Side Quest** — an optional extra objective with its own payoff that adds challenge (a detour, a search, a resource cost, or an increased difficulty). Drop it into Town the same way you plant hooks and prep resources.
12. **Travel** — plot the adventure site on your map. Plan at least one **Planned Encounter** for trips of 2+ days. Come up with 3+ **Complications** (wandering monsters, hazards, strangers, bad luck) tied to terrain types. Use these instead of random encounter tables.

### Four Questions for Baiting and Setting the Hook

| # | Question | Purpose |
|---|----------|---------|
| 1 | How do the characters know their goal? | Defines the hook |
| 2 | How do the characters know where to start? | Defines the hook |
| 3 | How do the characters know the adventure exists? | Defines the lure |
| 4 | How do the characters know it serves their motivations? | Defines the lure's appeal |

## Actionable Steps

1. **Check your timing** — have big prep done before the party finishes their current adventure and returns to Town.
2. **Write a premise and summary** for the next adventure. Build it from Town's Fun Facts or Regional Facts if you need inspiration.
3. **Check motivations** — verify that at least half the party has a reason to pursue this adventure. Sweeten the deal for specific motivations if needed (add treasure for the greedy, add a challenge for the glory-seeker, add exploration for the wanderer).
4. **Answer the four hook/lure questions.** Design the hook scene and the lure that leads to it. Place the lure where players will naturally encounter it (near Essential Services).
5. **Design 3 preparation resources** — information, tools, allies, or potions that give the party an edge. Plant them in Town at varying difficulty levels (obvious / requires asking / hidden).
6. **Design a side quest** — an optional objective with its own reward and its own added challenge. Plant it in Town.
7. **Plan travel** — plot the destination on your map. Add at least one planned encounter for the journey. Write 3–5 terrain-appropriate complications for wandering-monster-style rolls.
8. **Break the adventure into acts** and do little prep for the first session's worth of content.
9. **Seed future setups** — as you build this adventure, plant at least one detail that could become a future adventure hook (Planned Setup). Note any existing details you can retroactively connect (Improvisational Setup).

---

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> The first adventure was easy mode — you handed players a mission and they took it. Every adventure after that has to **earn** the players' engagement. Use motivation to make them care, lures to catch their eye, hooks to give them direction, and preparation to reward their cleverness. And always, always have your big prep done before the party walks through the Town gates. **The Art of the Setup is what makes a campaign feel like a campaign instead of a series of disconnected one-shots.**
