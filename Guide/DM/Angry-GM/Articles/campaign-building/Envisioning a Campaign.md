# Envisioning a Campaign

**Source:** [Envisioning a Campaign – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/envisioning-a-campaign/)
**Date:** March 13, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery

---

## Summary

A Campaign Vision is a written description of the game you intend to run — not the plot, but the gameplay, structure, tone, and constraints. It's the third step in starting a campaign (after deciding to run one and identifying Needs and Wants). Writing it down forces you to refine raw ideas into something actionable. The Vision then guides every subsequent decision — mechanics, character options, session zero, clubhouse rules — without necessarily dictating them.

---

## Key Concepts

### Campaign Vision Defined
- A written description of the campaign you plan to run.
- Describes the **destination**, not turn-by-turn directions.
- Describes **gameplay**, not plot. If you spend more than three sentences on plot, you've gone wrong.
- Sets criteria, constraints, and conditions that limit your decisions going forward.
- You write it yourself, alone, even if you took a Group First approach to gathering input.

### Needs vs. Wants
Before writing the Vision, identify your Needs and Wants (and guess at your players'):

- **Needs** = dealbreakers. Things the campaign must provide or must avoid, or the game doesn't happen. Test: "Would I cancel the game over this?"
  - Example: "No evil characters." "Flexible scheduling."
- **Wants** = high priorities worth shaping the game around and bargaining for, but not worth killing the game over.
  - Example: "Anything but D&D 5E" — painful to give up, but survivable.

Write the list generously, then **challenge every item** to knock off anything that isn't truly a Need or a strong Want. Walk away for a day, then revisit. The shorter the list, the more freedom you have to build a good game.

### Your Needs Trump Player Needs
- If the game doesn't meet a player's Needs, that player walks. If it doesn't meet yours, there is no game.
- Don't ask players for formal Need/Want lists — they're unreliable. Use your best judgment from experience and conversation.
- Trust your gut. Negotiate and compromise rather than trying to talk players out of their stated Needs.

### The Writing Process
1. **Free-write** — address yourself like a letter: "Dear Me, I want to run a game. Here's what I'm thinking…" Let your intuition talk.
2. **Walk away** for at least a day.
3. **Red-pen it** — read as if you're a clueless replacement GM. Mark anything unclear, contradictory, impossible, or plot-heavy. Check against your Needs/Wants list.
4. **Rewrite** — incorporate the red-pen feedback into a cleaner draft.
5. **Final check** — one more read. Minor notes are fine; don't rewrite again unless you truly feel unready.

The **process** of writing, rejecting, and rewriting matters more than the final document. It forces you to reach internal consensus on where you're going and why.

### Thinking Isn't Planning
- Ideas that feel complete in your head are unrefined ore, not gold.
- Writing is how you separate dross from value and beat ideas into useful shapes.
- You are not special enough to skip this step. Nobody is.

---

## Homework

Write a letter to yourself describing the campaign you're **currently running** — not the plot, but how you're running the game. Imagine you're handing it off to a clueless replacement GM. Red-pen it, then rewrite it. The goal is to practice communicating effectively with yourself before trying to pull a fresh campaign out of thin air.
