# The Personal Character Quest Plus One Campaign

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Personal Character Quest Plus One Campaign](https://theangrygm.com/the-personal-character-quest-plus-one-campaign/)
**Date:** March 28, 2024
**Series:** Campaign Building / Campaign Types

---

## What Is This?

A framework for the one way to run a campaign built around Personal Character Quests (PCQs) that might not be a total disaster. Called the **PCQ+1** — each player gets a Personal Character Quest, and the GM adds a **Plus One Quest** as the shared campaign backbone.

> This is explicitly presented as a bad idea you can mitigate, not a good idea. Personal Character Quests are inherently destabilizing to cooperative games.

## Why Personal Character Quest Campaigns Are Hard

### Games Need Shared Goals

- Players need something to work toward to give actions meaning
- Cooperative games require the Goal to unify team-based choices as well as individual ones
- TTRPGs are **legacy games** — choices have permanent consequences, raising the stakes
- Without a Shared Goal at the heart of every adventure, player investment erodes

### The Metagame Helps (But Not Enough)

- The unwritten rule that "the game goes where the party goes" forces players to accept each other's quests as temporary Shared Goals
- But the Metagame won't make players **care** about someone else's quest
- Every player has a voice whispering: "I'd rather spend game time on MY quest"
- Small compromises add up — missed sessions, hoarded resources, growing disinterest

### The Core Problem

- Inviting each player to invent a PCQ **amplifies natural self-interest**
- Game time becomes a limited resource each player wants for their own story
- Roleplaying forces character compromises that are inherently disengaging
- Failed campaigns die not with a bang but with a slow whimper of growing disinterest

## Prerequisites

- **Must be done from the start** — you cannot pivot an existing campaign into this
- **GM must be deeply involved in character creation** — collaborative, not player-dictated
- **GM must be willing to say "No"** and enforce constraints
- **More work, not less** — letting players set goals means more planning
- **Fragile by nature** — even done right, it might crash and burn

## The PCQ+1 Framework

You need five components: **Glue**, **Personal Character Quests**, **Two Motivations per player**, a **Plus One Quest**, and a **Matrix**.

### 1. Campaign Glue

Before anyone makes characters, figure out what keeps the party stuck together. The answer **cannot be "personal relationships"** — those are too weak and fragile.

The Glue must be external, structural, durable, hard to break away from, and permissive enough to allow personal pursuits.

**Good examples:** Shared ship ownership (Farscape, Firefly), indentured to a patron, members of a loose organization, fugitives on the run together

> The key question: "Why are these people stuck helping each other on their worst days when they don't want to and they're kind of sick of each other?"

### 2. One Quest and Two Motivations Per Player

Work with each player individually (interview-style, not written submissions):

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Personal Character Quest** | The player's chosen goal for their character |
| **Motivation 1** | Explains dedication to the PCQ (keep it broad) |
| **Motivation 2** | Connects the character to the campaign Glue/Premise |

**Example — Ardrick:**
- **PCQ:** Recover his family's ancestral sword
- **Motivation 1:** Sense of Duty (broader than just "duty to family")
- **Motivation 2:** Need to Belong (orphaned, patron took him in)

Err on the side of harmony. If one Motivation is selfish, counterbalance with a group-oriented second Motivation.

### 3. The Plus One Quest

A GM-created quest that resolves or finishes the Premise. When complete, it unglues the party — if they stay together after, it's by choice.

### 4. Mining Quests for Plot Points

Break each quest (Personal and Plus One) into:
- **Major Plot Points** (3 per quest typical) — become full adventures
- **Minor Plot Points** (2 per quest) — side quests, complications, diversions

### 5. The Matrix

Arrange all Plot Points in columns (one per quest). When it's time for the next adventure, pick a column and turn the next Major Plot Point into an adventure. Optionally add a Minor Plot Point from a different column.

## Matrix Rules

1. **First and last adventures must come from the Plus One Quest**
2. **Don't move to a new row without finishing the current one**
3. **Be ready to split Plus One Major Plot Points** for extra adventures if needed
4. **Never let the players pick what adventure comes next**
5. **Motivate as many characters as possible in every adventure** — never go more than one adventure without a Motivational hit for any character
6. **In the final adventure**, try to make each PCQ pay off somehow (gravy, not required)

## Ongoing Vigilance

**Losing one player?** Move their next Major Plot Point up the queue.

**Whole party disconnecting?** Run a Plus One Quest adventure to reignite group spirit.

> "It is a complete myth that spotlight time needs balancing. Some people need more than others. That's totally okay."

### The Cardinal Rule

**Intraparty conflict kills Personal Character Quest Campaigns.**

Never: let players choose whose Goal to chase next, ask a player to push off their Goal, set up competing adventure choices, interrupt one PCQ with another, put Motivations into conflict, or force a character to act against their Motivations.

If you find yourself doing any of these: **abort and rewrite**.

## On Psychological Reactance

Players who say "I don't want the GM to control the pace" are experiencing **Psychological Reactance** — the desire to feel free, not to actually be free. The structure still makes for a better game. An artful GM ensures players never feel control is being taken away — through **Guided Nonlinearity** or an **illusion of an Open World**.
