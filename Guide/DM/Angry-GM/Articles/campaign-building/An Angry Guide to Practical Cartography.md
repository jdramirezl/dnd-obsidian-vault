# An Angry Guide to Practical Cartography

## Key Advice

**Draw the maps you need, not the ones you want.** Most maps in RPG products are unnecessary — they're glorified lists of locations that convey no information a simple text list couldn't handle better. A good map displays only the minimum information needed to run the game, and only information that is most efficiently shown visually. Understand the difference between Practical Maps, Tactical Maps, and Artistic Maps, and use each appropriately.

## Summary

Angry breaks down RPG mapping into three distinct categories and argues that most GMs waste enormous time on the wrong kind:

**1. Practical Maps** — The most basic and most useful. They show the relationship between locations using simple rectangles and lines. A dungeon practical map is just boxes (rooms) connected by lines (passages). That's literally all you need for most dungeons. No scale, no detail, no art — just topology.

**2. Tactical Maps** — Required only for locations where combat will occur. These must show distances (5-foot grid for D&D/Pathfinder), walls, doors, and anything that affects movement, line of sight, or line of effect. They should NOT include decorative details that don't have mechanical impact (carpet, scattered debris, etc.). Only map tactically the rooms where fights actually happen.

**3. Artistic Maps** — The pretty maps you see in published modules. They establish tone and atmosphere but are decorative, not functional. They set an unrealistic standard for home GMs and are mostly unnecessary for actual play. They're "gamer candy" — delicious but empty calories.

Key principles:
- A map is a diagram. It exists to convey useful information that is most easily conveyed visually.
- Most city maps are pointless because players travel point-to-point, not intersection-by-intersection. Relative location rarely matters.
- A map only becomes useful when spatial relationships have mechanical consequences (faction territories, neighborhood conditions, chase scenes with actual rules).
- More detail on a map can actually hurt play — players interact with artwork instead of asking questions and using imagination.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Start every map as a Practical Map** — Rectangles and lines. Show rooms and connections. Nothing more. This takes 5 minutes.
2. **Only create Tactical Maps for combat rooms** — If a fight will happen there, draw it on graph paper with 5-foot squares. Show walls, doors, and mechanically relevant features only.
3. **Ask "does this information affect gameplay?"** — Before adding any detail to a map, ask if it has mechanical consequences. If not, leave it out or put it in the room description text.
4. **Don't map cities unless spatial relationships matter** — If your campaign doesn't track faction control, neighborhood conditions, or travel time mechanically, a simple list of locations is more useful than a map.
5. **Use simple, consistent symbols** — Pick symbols for doors, pillars, difficult terrain, water, etc. and stick with them. The map is supported by text descriptions anyway.
6. **Separate your hobby mapping from your prep mapping** — If you enjoy drawing beautiful maps, do it as a relaxing hobby. Don't confuse it with game prep.

> [!tip] The Mapping Litmus Test
> Before drawing any map, ask: "What information does this map need to convey that can't be presented more easily as text?" If the answer is just "a list of locations," skip the map. If the answer is "how rooms connect" or "where combat features are relative to each other," draw only that — rectangles, lines, and a grid where needed.
