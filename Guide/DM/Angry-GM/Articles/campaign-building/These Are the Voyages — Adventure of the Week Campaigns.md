# These Are the Voyages — Adventure of the Week Campaigns

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/these-are-the-voyages/)
**Date:** February 21, 2018
**Series:** Start Your Own Campaign / Campaign Descriptors

---

## Overview

Adventure of the Week is a **campaign descriptor** (not a shape) for campaigns where each adventure is a self-contained story with its own setup, climax, and resolution. Think Star Trek: TNG — every episode begins with normalcy, a crisis happens, the crew resolves it, everything returns to normal. The key distinction from a Plate of Meatballs (a campaign shape): Adventure of the Week describes a *formula for how adventures connect*, while Plate of Meatballs describes a *structure for the whole campaign*. You can have an Adventure of the Week campaign in the shape of a Noodle or even Spaghetti — not just Meatballs.

## The Formula

Every adventure follows this pattern:
1. **Everything is perfectly normal.**
2. **A problem appears** with a reason why the heroes should care.
3. **The heroes resolve the problem.**
4. **Everything returns to normal.** Roll credits. See you next week.

The world only exists between inciting incident and resolution. There's a start-and-stop motion — no mushiness, no flow from one adventure to the next, no dominoes.

## Adventure of the Week vs. Plate of Meatballs

| | Plate of Meatballs (Shape) | Adventure of the Week (Descriptor) |
|---|---|---|
| **What it defines** | Structure of the whole campaign | Formula for how individual adventures connect |
| **Adventures** | Truly self-contained; could be played in any order | Self-contained; each provides its own motivation and resolution |
| **Continuity** | None between adventures | Minimal, but *some* continuity element exists |
| **Through-line** | No unifying plot-line | Can have plot threads running through adventures |
| **Analogy** | Star Trek: TNG (pure episodic) | Star Trek: DS9 (episodic with continuity) |

A Plate of Meatballs is almost always an Adventure of the Week campaign. But an Adventure of the Week campaign doesn't have to be a Plate of Meatballs.

## Why Run Adventure of the Week?

- **Variety.** The formulaic nature makes it *easier* to make every adventure different — ancient ruins one week, murder mystery the next, volcano evacuation after that.
- **Action-focused.** Players show up, get a goal, and kill it. Great for players who don't care about elaborate plots.
- **Easy to maintain.** Watch a disaster movie, decide to run a volcano adventure — no need to fit it into a bigger jigsaw.
- **Satisfying narratives.** Self-contained, well-structured stories can be more satisfying than a three-year campaign that never hits a real resolution.
- **Handles erratic attendance.** Especially if each adventure fits into one session.
- **Organized play standard.** There's a reason organized play programs use this format.

## The Downsides

- **Disconnection.** Players can feel disconnected from the larger world and events outside their adventures.
- **Repetition.** Without overarching plot lines, the endless string of unrelated problems can wear on players who need more.
- **Formula fatigue.** Eventually players pick up on the structure. Some thrive on regularity; others get restless.

## Two Big Issues to Manage

### Issue 1: Writing Self-Contained Adventures

You must get good at writing one-shots that have everything they need:
- **Provide all motivation within the adventure itself.** Don't draw on anything from the past without reintroducing and explaining it. When the old friend shows up, tell the players who he is and why they owe him.
- **Completely resolve everything at the end.** No loose ends, no sequel hooks, no setting up the next adventure. Every plotline wraps up with a celebratory cake party.
- **Mental model:** Assume every adventure includes a completely new group of players and that the players will never come back. Even though neither is true.

### Issue 2: Continuity (The Unifying Element)

If you erase *all* continuity, you're not running a campaign anymore. Something must tie the adventures together — it's just not the adventures themselves.

**What provides continuity:**
- **The characters** — same protagonists whose relationships grow and change.
- **The setting** — "The Ongoing Adventures of the Adventuring Adventurers in the World of Adventure."
- **A game/setting feature** (especially useful with attendance problems):
  - Crew of a starship flying planet to planet.
  - Heroes for hire with a home base in a city.
  - Members of an organization.

The unifying element establishes the formula for how adventures begin: detect a new planet and beam down, get a call from a client, get briefed by a superior officer. It glosses over what happened between adventures, anchors the players, and gets the game started.

**The unifying element must be stable.** It can be endangered during an adventure but must be out of danger by the end. Everything goes back to normal.

## Noodles of the Week — Adding Plot Threads

You *can* have ongoing plot lines in an Adventure of the Week campaign. The key: **Whack-a-Mole, not Chess.**

Instead of the party constantly advancing toward destroying the cult and killing Orcus (Epic Quest), each adventure involves discovering a *particular* plot by a *particular* cultist in a *particular* location, countering that plot, defeating that cultist, and moving on. Until next time.

**Think Sailor Moon:** Each week, a villain comes up with a new scheme. Sailor Moon discovers it, thwarts it, fights the monster. The villain escapes to try again.

**The players don't advance the plot — they hammer it back down.** Adventure of the Week is about constantly restoring peace and normality. Hence, Whack-a-Mole.

### Options for Plot Threads

- **Single thread every adventure:** "The Negaverse is at it again, Sailor Scouts."
- **Alternate thread and stand-alone adventures:** "Now that we've dealt with the Cigarette Smoking Man again, let's go hunt this werewolf in Pennsylvania, Scully."
- **Multiple threads, flit between them:** Ferengi episode, stand-alone, stand-alone, Dominion war episode.

Plot threads can involve specific villains, organizations, locations, or character traits.

### Occasional Big Victories

You can loosen the format occasionally for significant developments:
- Wipe out a villain's lieutenant → new lieutenant fills the vacuum → new routine.
- Shake up the formula, give the players a sense of permanent impact, then settle into a new pattern.
- When the campaign feels like it's run its course, have a big adventure that ties up the major plot thread — the biggest mole that breaks the machine.

These big episodes aren't things the players have been building toward. They're opportunities that present themselves in the moment.
