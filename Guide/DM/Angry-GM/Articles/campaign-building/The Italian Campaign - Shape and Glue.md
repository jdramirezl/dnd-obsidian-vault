# The Italian Campaign: On Shape and Glue

**Source:** [The Angry GM — The Italian Campaign: On Shape and Glue](https://theangrygm.com/the-italian-campaign/)
**Date:** April 5, 2017

---

## The Two Things Every Campaign Defines

Every campaign — planned or not — defines exactly two things:

1. **Shape** (aka Continuity) — How adventures relate to each other.
2. **Glue** — What unifies the characters and keeps the party together.

Shape is optional to plan (one will emerge). **Glue is mandatory.** Without Glue, the campaign will die.

---

## Shape: The Italian Model

Campaign shapes are named after Italian food.

### The Four Basic Shapes

#### 1. Plate of Meatballs
- Each adventure is a standalone meatball. Minimal connections between them.
- Drop any adventure and the campaign still works.
- If a player can miss a session and jump back in without a detailed recap, it's probably Meatballs.
- Connections emerge naturally over time through recurring characters and persistent memory.

#### 2. Noodle (Adventure Path)
- A single long strand — one overarching story with a defined goal.
- Each adventure is a "slurp" advancing toward that goal.
- Adventures have their own structure but are chapters in the bigger story.

#### 3. Sausage (Linked Adventure Paths)
- A chain of sausage links — multiple adventure paths in succession.
- Each sausage is an adventure path with its own overarching goal.
- **Linked sausages:** Each path builds on the previous, possibly with a grand unifying goal.
- **Separated sausages:** Paths are unrelated; new players/characters can join at sausage boundaries.

#### 4. Plate of Spaghetti (Interwoven Adventure Paths)
- Multiple noodles (adventure paths) piled together.
- Each session might advance one or more of several different overarching goals.
- Classic example: Each PC has a personal long-term goal; each adventure advances one or two of them.

### Combination Plates (Tour of Italy)

You can mix and match:
- **Spaghetti and Meatballs** — Interwoven paths + standalone adventures.
- **Sausage and Spaghetti** — One major path series + interwoven minor paths.
- **Any combination** — Add meatballs to anything.

### Shape Can Change Mid-Campaign

- A Meatballs campaign can grow a Noodle when a recurring villain develops a master plan.
- Spaghetti can reduce to a single Noodle as goals resolve.
- Meatballs can become Spaghetti as players adopt personal goals.
- **Unlike adventures, campaigns can develop goals at any time** — deliberately or accidentally.

### Planned vs. Emergent Shape

- If you don't choose a shape, you get **Meatballs by default**.
- Anything beyond Meatballs requires a deliberate choice **at some point**.
- You can plan for shape changes: start with Meatballs while a background plot waits to emerge as a Noodle or Sausage.

## Glue: The Critical Element

> **The single most important aspect of campaign design:** The characters NEED a reason to adventure together and to STICK together.

### Why Glue Matters

- **Fiction must make sense.** Real people stay in incomprehensible relationships; fictional characters can't. We hold fiction to a higher standard than reality.
- **Stories provide psychological needs:** analyzing patterns and imagining a world that makes sense. When a story stops making sense, emotional engagement drops.
- **The Metagame traps parties together.** Players almost never consider having their character leave the party — it's an unwritten rule that the game follows the party. If the party breaks up, the game breaks.
- **Result:** When relationships are borked, the fiction demands a breakup but the metagame prevents it. The game becomes an incomprehensible relationship that drags everyone down.

### What Counts as a "Borked" Relationship

It doesn't take much:
- Internal strife, hatred, or betrayal.
- Moral or ethical disagreements.
- Different problem-solving approaches (impulsive barbarian vs. careful wizard).
- Simply not having a common goal.

Any of these can bog the game down with arguments, side-taking, and awkward silences.

### Characters Don't Grow on Their Own

- In movies, characters undergo arcs and learn to work together (buddy cops).
- In RPGs, **players don't tend to build character arcs.** They settle into who they want to play and stay there.
- You can't rely on natural character growth to fix party friction.

---

## Two Huge Mistakes GMs Make

### Mistake 1: The Accidental Party

- Strangers meet during Something Terrible™ and are forced to work together.
- GMs assume bonds of trust and friendship will form naturally.
- **They won't.** Or at least, you can't count on it.
- The Accidental Party is a way of **getting** the party together, not **keeping** them together.

### Mistake 2: The Old Friends

- Making players create characters with pre-existing relationships.
- GMs assume players won't create characters that would split up.
- **They will.** The character a player creates is **never** the character they end up playing.
- Personalities emerge through play. Group dynamics cause people to settle into unexpected roles. Traits appear as responses to situations no one planned for.
- Pre-existing relationships are a way of **getting** the party together, not **keeping** them together.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **Shape is useful but optional.** If you don't plan one, Meatballs will emerge. That's fine.
- **Glue is mandatory.** If you don't plan it, the campaign is at serious risk.
- Build an **in-game reason** for characters to be stuck together despite conflicts.
- Use that forced togetherness + adventure design to push characters toward growth and compromise.
- **Focus on how the campaign starts** (Glue), not just where it's going (Shape).
- Most GMs over-invest in Shape and under-invest in Glue. Flip that priority.
