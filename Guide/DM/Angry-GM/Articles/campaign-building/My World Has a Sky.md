# My World Has a Sky… And Yours Should Too

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/my-world-has-a-sky/)
**Date:** November 30, 2022
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## Overview

Track time, date, weather, seasons, and lunar cycles — collectively called "The Sky" — not for mechanical challenge or narrative tone, but to **ground** the players in the world. The Sky reminds players they're interacting with an actual world, not just moving tokens on a board. It's the single most effective ambient detail a GM can maintain.

## What is "The Sky"?

The Sky is everything the characters perceive when they're outside: time of day, date, weather, temperature, wind, cloud cover, precipitation, moon phases, and daylight hours. Taken together, it tells you what the world looks and feels like at any given moment.

## Why The Sky Matters: Grounding

- **Grounding** means never letting players forget they're interacting with an actual world, not just playing a game of resource management
- It's the GM's job to keep players from floating into abstract game-mechanics space
- If your players are murderhobos who don't care about the world, that's on **you** — you don't get grounding
- Grounding is holistic and emergent — it grows from many small, seemingly zero-impact details done constantly and habitually
- The real world has a sky that always affects you (consciously or not). Fantasy worlds need the same

## The Two Common (But Secondary) Reasons

1. **Gamey GMs:** Weather adds mechanical challenge — rain obscures vision, wind deflects arrows, cold kills the unprotected
2. **Narrative GMs:** Weather sets tone — fog is spooky, sunshine is hopeful, storms are ominous

Both are valid but are just gravy. The primary purpose is grounding.

## Angry's Top 5 Sky-Tracking Tips

### Tip 1: Don't Create Anything You Have to Explain More Than Once

Keep calendars and celestial mechanics simple and intuitive. Everything should be inferable from context.

- **Angryverse calendar:** 12 months × 28 days. Seasons: Springswax → Springstide → Springsebb (wax = grow, ebb = recede)
- **Two moons:** Azura the Silver Moon (good) and Krisma the Red Moon (evil). Always say "the Silver Moon" so players remember there are two
- Add one fantastical element to your sky (a ring, a second moon, weird seasons) to inject fantasy — but keep it simple. Eberron's 12 moons are too many for anyone to casually describe

### Tip 2: Never Shut Up About Your Damned Sky

Repetition is your best friend. Players barely notice anything you say — the only way to get it into their brains is to say it a dozen times.

- Start each session with the date
- First time characters are under the sky, describe weather and daylight
- Mention the sky in every outdoor scene-setting narration and transition
- If it's raining, remind them they're in the rain. If it's foggy, every outdoor scene has fog
- At night, always mention the moons

### Tip 3: Mechanics Don't Matter

Don't waste time on detailed weather tables with mechanical effects. At most, apply a +1/+2 bonus or penalty to checks in extreme conditions. But **do** reward players who use the sky creatively (waiting for clouds to cover the moon before sneaking).

For determining weather, use the D&D 3.5 DMG tables, a simple one-page generator, or just make it up. Track with a three-phrase description:

> Mild, partly cloudy, breezy
> Warm, foggy, calm
> Frigid, heavy snow, high wind

### Tip 4: Use The Right Sky for the Right Job… Sparingly

Occasionally pick tonally appropriate weather or impose mechanical weather challenges — but don't do it every time or it becomes contrived and predictable. If every dungeon crawl starts with fog, players will metagame the weather.

### Tip 5: Trust Your Gut

- If something starts to leak out of your mouth — sudden fog, surprise eclipse — go with it. Your brain detected a pattern you didn't consciously notice
- Always have the Sky's current status visible to you (notes, tracker, etc.)
- Games are experiential and emotional — your conscious brain will often mess that up, so don't let it interrupt a good instinctive call

## Practical Implementation

1. Create a simple calendar (real-world analog is fine)
2. Decide on celestial features (one or two moons, any rings, etc.)
3. Keep a weather note visible during play: temperature, cloud cover/precipitation, wind
4. Work the sky into every scene-setting narration
5. Don't overthink mechanics — the sky just needs to **exist**
