# Low Magic, No Problem — Oh Wait, Problem

**Source:** [Fanservice BS: Low Magic, No Problem. Oh, Wait, Problem – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/fanservice-bs-low-magic/)
**Date:** April 25, 2018
**Series:** Fanservice BS / Random BS

---

## Summary

Can you run a low-magic game in D&D? The short answer is no — not without breaking D&D so badly it's no longer D&D. But the deeper insight is about *why* people want low magic in the first place: magic in D&D has become so ubiquitous and mechanical that it no longer feels fantastic, wonderful, or impossible. The real desire isn't for less magic — it's for magic that *matters*.

---

## Key Concepts

### Two Kinds of "Low Magic"

**1. Low magic in the world, not in the party:**
- Magic is rare in the setting, but the PCs are special exceptions.
- Role-play the world's reaction: people fear, respect, or are awed by magic.
- No magic item shops, no arcane colleges, no readily available healing.
- This is achievable without rule changes — just role-play the world consistently.
- The Angryverse does this: no one remembers how to make magic items; arcane colleges are gone; clerics are rare individuals chosen by gods, not seminary graduates.

**2. Low magic in the party (limiting player options):**
- This is what most people actually mean when they ask about "low magic D&D."
- The problem: D&D 5E is saturated with magic. Nearly every class has explicit magical abilities.
- Without magic, you're left with fighter, barbarian, and rogue — and two of those fill the same role.
- You can't build a diverse party without magic. The game breaks.

### The Kitchen Sink Problem
- D&D creates variety by piling on isolated magical effects, one at a time.
- Every class gets spells or spell-like abilities. Lay on hands, turn undead, ki powers — these are mechanically spells but aren't treated as such.
- The result: magic doesn't feel special. It's just something everyone does, all the time.
- "There is never a moment in D&D when there isn't something magical happening on screen."

### The Real Motivation Behind "Low Magic"
The desire for low magic is really a desire for:
- **Magic that feels fantastic** — rare, special, impossible, wonderful.
- **Magic that feels dangerous** — with real costs and consequences.
- **Characters that feel like underdogs** — scrappy heroes in a world bigger and stranger than them, not superheroes farting magic from every orifice.
- **Fewer, more meaningful options** — instead of a thousand shallow tricks, a few defining abilities that feel special.

### Why D&D Can't Do It
- The game's math assumes magic. Monsters above CR 5 resist non-magical weapons.
- Only 3 classes (fighter, barbarian, rogue) avoid explicit magic, and they can't cover all party roles.
- Removing magic doesn't leave interesting non-magical options behind — D&D hasn't invested in building those up.
- The Battle Master fighter shows it's *possible* to build variety without magic, but D&D is "half-assed about its non-magical options."
- If you want low magic, use a different system: Conan RPG, Warhammer 2E, Dungeon Crawl Classics, Zweihander, Burning Wheel, etc.

### What You Can Do in D&D
- Run a world where magic is rare and feared, but let PCs be the exceptions.
- Decide what the world **knows** about magic (whether true or not) and how people react.
- Warn players that using magic will make them pariahs.
- Accept that the party will still be magical — and that's okay if the *world* treats magic as extraordinary.
