## Summary

A **Campaign Vision** is a written description of the game you intend to run. It is not a plot outline — it describes the *gameplay*, the *feel*, the *constraints*, and the *goals* of the campaign. The process of writing, challenging, and rewriting the Vision is more important than the final document itself, because it forces you to reach internal consensus on what you're building and why.

Before writing the Vision, you must identify your **Needs** (dealbreakers that would kill the game) and **Wants** (strong priorities worth fighting for but not dying on). You also estimate your players' Needs and Wants. Then you challenge every item on those lists ruthlessly to knock off anything that isn't truly essential — fewer constraints mean more creative freedom.

The Vision is written alone, by the GM, even if player input was gathered. It goes through a cycle: free-write → walk away → red-pen it as if you're a different person reading a bad memo → rewrite. This iterative process refines raw ideas into actionable direction.

## Key Advice

- **Write it down.** Thinking is idea generation, not planning. Ideas must be refined on paper. You are not the exception.
- **Destination, not directions.** The Vision tells you *where you're going*, not every turn-by-turn decision. It helps you make those decisions quickly later.
- **Describe the game, not the plot.** If you spend more than three sentences on plot, your Vision is bad. Focus on gameplay, structure, tone, and feel.
- **You write it alone.** Even with Group First input, the GM must take ownership and internalize the goals by writing the Vision themselves.
- **Needs vs. Wants.** Needs are true dealbreakers ("Would I cancel the game over this?"). Wants are strong priorities but survivable compromises. Be brutally honest about which is which.
- **Your Needs come first.** A player's unmet Need means they leave. Your unmet Need means there is no game at all.
- **Challenge everything.** Write your Needs/Wants list generously, then try to knock every item off. Whatever survives is real.
- **Trust your gut.** If you can't cross something off even without a logical reason, your intuition knows something you don't.
- **Free-write → Red-pen → Rewrite.** Let your visionary self draft, let your practical self critique, then reconcile. This is a conversation with yourself.
- **The process matters more than the product.** The act of writing, rejecting, and rewriting builds internal consensus. The final document's format is secondary.

## Actionable Steps

1. **List your Needs and Wants.** Separate yours from your players'. Label each clearly.
2. **Walk away for a day.** Then return and challenge every single item. Justify each one. Try to eliminate as many as possible.
3. **Free-write your Campaign Vision.** Open a document and describe the game you want to run. Address it like a letter to yourself if you're stuck. Don't self-edit yet.
4. **Walk away again.** At least an hour, ideally a day.
5. **Red-pen it.** Read it as if you're the GM who has to execute this mess. Mark contradictions, gaps, unclear points, plot-heavy sections, and conflicts with your Needs/Wants list.
6. **Rewrite.** Incorporate the red-pen feedback into a cleaner second draft. Organize if it helps.
7. **Final review.** Read the rewrite. If only minor issues remain, note them and move on. Don't rewrite again unless you genuinely feel unprepared.
8. **Use the Vision going forward.** When making campaign decisions (character generation, rules, setting details), consult the Vision. It should make the right answer obvious or at least eliminate wrong ones.

> [!tip] Angry's Homework
> Before writing a fresh Campaign Vision, write one for a campaign you're *currently running*. Describe it as if briefing a clueless replacement GM. Then red-pen and rewrite it. This teaches you to communicate effectively with yourself before you try to pull a new campaign out of thin air.
