## Two Types of Problem Behavior

- **Disruptive behavior:** Impedes the game itself (arriving late, derailing sessions, hogging spotlight).
- **Disturbing behavior:** Adversely affects others' enjoyment or emotional comfort (talking over people, inappropriate jokes, making others uncomfortable).
- These often overlap and blur together.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. The Goal Is to Resolve the Conflict, Not Correct Behavior

> **The problem is not the behavior — the problem is the conflict** between the behavior and the group's shared goals or individual comfort.

Any resolution that ends the conflict is valid:
- The player changes the behavior.
- A compromise mitigates the impact.
- The group adjusts expectations.
- The player leaves the table.

Going in with "I need to fix this bad behavior" creates an adversarial prosecutor-vs-accused dynamic and limits your options.

### 2. Conflict Over = Problem Solved

Your job ends when the conflict ends. You don't need:
- Agreement or understanding from the player.
- The player to see things your way.
- The player to like you afterward.

You **cannot control what people think**. Aim for mutual understanding, but accept that sometimes the best you can get is the conflict being over.

### 3. Facts vs. Feelings

> External events do not *cause* feelings. Your feelings arise from your **beliefs about** external events.

- You're not mad because the player is late. You're mad because the player is late **and** you believe punctuality = respect.
- If you learned the player had cancer, the same lateness would feel completely different.
- **Disruption** is factual (late is late, lost time is lost time). **Disturbance** comes from interpretation and perspective.
- Feelings are valid and real, but they don't come from the behavior — they come from the stories you tell yourself about the behavior.
- Campaign Managers must distinguish where disruption ends and disturbance begins.

### 4. The Peacemaker's Burden

> Blessed are the peacemakers; they have to take shit from every side.

Once you learn these skills, you'll be the only one using them. Your players will never extend you the same courtesy. Your job is to resolve conflicts, not to coach players in conflict resolution. If you need to feel heard and validated, get a therapist or a friend — not your players.

## Technique 1: Talk About Facts or Your Own Feelings

**The Game Masterly "We" — Don't Use It.** Never speak for anyone but yourself, even when you know you're representing others. If Beth complains about Adam, you say to Adam: "Last week, you did X. **I** feel angry about that."

### The Three Things You're Allowed to Discuss

1. **Specific, tangible, objective facts** — "You've been late to three out of the last four games."
2. **Your own personal choices** (flagged as such) — "I held the start of the game waiting for you."
3. **Your own personal feelings** (flagged as such, owned as yours) — "I feel disrespected." Not "You're disrespecting me."

**Never** discuss the player's motivations, intentions, thoughts, or opinions. You don't know what's in their head. Guessing wrong triggers defensive anger and anger spirals.

---

## Technique 2: Listen Actively, Reflect, and Validate

> People do not listen if they don't feel listened to.

### The Listening Problem

Humans only hear about half of what's said. The moment you hear something you can respond to, your brain starts formulating a response and ignores the rest. This is catastrophic in conflict resolution — you miss critical context.

### Reflective Listening

**Before you respond to anything, summarize what you think they said and invite correction:**

> "It sounds like there's a lot going on. Let me make sure I got all that. [Summary of their points]. Do I understand correctly?"

- Validate their feelings in the process where possible.
- This works even when the player is being hostile — reflect their hostility back neutrally, confirm you understood, then respond.
- **Listening ≠ agreeing.** You can reflect someone's position perfectly and then still show them the door.

---

## The Process (Summary)

1. **Approach privately**, away from the table.
2. **Present the conflict** clearly and briefly — specific behaviors + your personal feelings/choices.
3. **Let the player respond.**
4. **Reflect back** their response without judgment; ask them to confirm.
5. **Propose a solution** or invite them to help find one.
6. **Negotiate** a final agreement.
7. Repeat steps 3–6 as needed. Keep following the rules (facts/feelings only, reflect before responding).

---

## Setting Realistic Expectations

- This approach has the **best odds** of working, but it's not guaranteed.
- Sometimes players dig in, get defensive, explode, or refuse to acknowledge a conflict.
- The worst case: you do everything right and it still fails. All you can do is conduct yourself well enough to walk away saying, "I did everything I could."
- **You always have the right to walk away** — to end the game, remove a player, or leave a table. That is your ultimate and only true power.

## Personal Anecdote: The Conflict Resolution from Hell

- Angry confronted a close personal friend and professional collaborator ("Alan") about disruptive play in a one-shot.
- Followed the textbook approach perfectly: specific behaviors, personal feelings, active listening, reflection, validation.
- Alan kept re-explaining his preferred playstyle. Angry never called it wrong — just said "my table, my preferences."
- After nearly an hour, Alan insisted he had a right to keep talking until they agreed.
- Angry ended the chat. They never spoke again.
