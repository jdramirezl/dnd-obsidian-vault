# Wishing and Stuff — Campaign Startup Dilemmas

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Wishing and S$%&ing: Campaign Startup Dilemmas](https://theangrygm.com/wishing-and-stuff/)
**Date:** July 30, 2017
**Series:** How to Build a Campaign

---

## Overview

A prerequisite to the Session Zero discussion. Before you can talk about how to run a Session Zero, you need to understand three recurring dilemmas that arise when starting a new campaign. None of them have universal right answers — they are situational, personal, and must be resolved case-by-case.

---

## Session Zero — Quick Definition

A pre-campaign meeting between the GM and players to hammer out goals, expectations, and desires for the upcoming campaign. Definitions vary (collaborative setting design, character generation, campaign pre-planning), but the core idea is alignment before play begins.

---

## False Dilemmas and Binary Thinking

People love either-or questions: "Should I run the game I want or the game the players want?" "Should I let players bring pre-made characters or build around my premise?" These are false dilemmas when posed as absolutes.

- Either-or questions need context — the answer changes with the situation
- GMs don't have to make absolute pronouncements; the GM exists as a game mechanic precisely because the game needs human judgment over rigid rules
- GMs have leanings and patterns, but that doesn't mean they aren't deciding case-by-case
- Anyone arguing "rule of cool ALWAYS" or "rules as written ALWAYS" is missing the point

## The Three Campaign Startup Dilemmas

### 1. The Me vs. Them Dilemma

**Question:** What's more important — what the players want from the game or what the GM wants?

Every person at the table comes with preferences about tone, themes, mechanics, and content. A good game satisfies everyone's preferences at least a little. Preferences have degrees:

- **Favorites** — hungry for these, can rarely get enough
- **Game-breakers** — these ruin the game
- **In between** — things liked but livable without, things disliked but tolerable, things with no strong feeling

**When players conflict with each other:**
1. Ask each if they're willing to compromise
2. Look for creative solutions that satisfy both
3. If neither works, make a call — someone has to suck it up or find another game

**When the GM conflicts with the players:**
- GMs put in more time and effort than players — that's just true
- If the GM isn't enjoying the game, it becomes an unpaid job
- A consistently unhappy GM poisons the table's mood
- GM burnout is real — diminishing returns will eventually kill the game
- If the GM quits, the game is over for everyone

**But that doesn't mean the GM always wins.** Different GMs run games for different reasons. Some run primarily for social enjoyment and have high tolerance for games that don't match their other preferences. For them, any game beats no game.

**Resolution:** There is no universal rule. It's personal and situational. Neither "the GM's needs always come first" nor "the GM's job is to serve the players" is correct as an absolute.

### 2. The Dream Game Dilemma

**Question:** What happens when reality prevents you from running the game you want?

Every GM has an ideal game — the sum of all their preferences. But that game may not be possible due to external constraints:

- **Money/time** — can't buy or read every sourcebook
- **Unreliable players** — a long political intrigue campaign falls apart if players miss every third session
- **Limited prep time** — full-time job and family means two hours a week for game prep
- **Skill limitations** — complex homebrew faction rules may be beyond your design ability

**Resolution:** Accept external limitations. Figure out which ones you can work around and which you can't. Run the game you CAN run, not the one you WANT to run. Or decide the gap is too large and don't run a game at all.

Two guiding principles:
- The Serenity Prayer — strength to change what you can, serenity to accept what you can't, wisdom to know the difference
- "Wish in one hand, shit in the other, see which fills up first"

### 3. The Comfort Dilemma

**Question:** Do you run the familiar and proven, or push for something new and risky?

**Advantages of the familiar (clichés, tropes, default settings):**
- Tried and tested — you know they work
- Everyone understands them — approachable, no homework needed
- Fast to get into — game can start immediately

**Advantages of the new and unique:**
- Potential for greatness — memorable, impressive, special
- Personal creative satisfaction

**Risks of innovation:**
- Each new element can fail
- Each adds cognitive load — new rules must be taught, new concepts explained
- Increased complexity for both GM and players
- Time and effort wasted if it doesn't land

**The player's Comfort Dilemma:** Players who always play the same race/class/archetype aren't broken — they're making a rational choice. They'll be stuck with that character for months or years, and every option has its own rules to learn.

**Many GMs overvalue innovation.** Using "cliché" as a slur comes from snooty artistic elitism. Clichés are clichés because they work.

**Angry's approach:** Start most campaigns in a tavern using the default core world. Get the game running smoothly first. Once it's purring, THEN start taking risks and introducing unique elements.

**Alternative:** Open with the big unique setpiece. If it explodes, at least it explodes in session one and is easier to clean up.

---

## Key Takeaways

- All three dilemmas are situational — there are no universal right answers
- Strip away idealism and face the actual choice: weigh costs vs. benefits for your specific situation
- The GM's enjoyment matters — an unhappy GM kills the game eventually
- External constraints are real; accept them and design around them
- Don't treat "cliché" as a dirty word — familiar elements reduce risk and cognitive load
- Innovation is a gamble; take it deliberately, not out of snobbery
- These dilemmas are prerequisites for a productive Session Zero discussion
