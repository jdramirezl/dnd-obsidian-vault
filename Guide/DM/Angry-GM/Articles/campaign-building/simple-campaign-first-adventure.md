## Summary

Angry walks through how to build the first adventure for a simple homebrew campaign. The first adventure and character generation are independent, simultaneous tasks — do them in whichever order life allows. The article covers what makes a good first adventure (short, simple, action-first, premise-fitting, with groundwork for the future), what won't ruin it (simplicity, clichés, published material), and how character motivation connects to adventure goals via the "Four Gs" (Gold, Glory, Gods, Greater Good). Two appendices provide a concrete 5-step adventure-building process (premise → summary → acts → encounters → finish) with a full worked example, plus advice on scripting the critical first five minutes of Session 1 — the "Meet Cute."

---

## Key Advice

1. **Adventures ≠ Sessions.** An adventure spans 2–3 sessions ideally. The first adventure will probably take 3–4 if you're new.
2. **The premise's third paragraph defines the starting point.** It tells you where the PCs are and what's happening when the game begins. Finish your premise before building the adventure.
3. **Qualities of a good first adventure:**
   - **Short and simple** — one objective, no side quests, 5–7 encounters, one climactic encounter
   - **Eyes on the prize** — simple goal revealed at the start, whole adventure about achieving it
   - **Shows off the campaign** — perfectly fits the premise's gameplay loop
   - **Starts with action** — first scene is a mechanical challenge (ideally "roll initiative"), not a roleplay scene
   - **Gives players space** — second scene is a campfire/debrief where players introduce characters at their own pace
   - **Lays groundwork** — sprinkle in names, symbols, organizations, NPCs, and rumors that can become something later
4. **Don't be afraid of:**
   - **Simplicity** — depth emerges from gameplay, not complicated plots
   - **Clichés and coincidences** — meeting at an inn by accident is fine; players don't care and won't remember
   - **Published material** — use the monster stats, traps, and treasures you paid for
5. **Motivation drives adventure acceptance.** You don't need to know specific PC motivations for the first adventure — just hit the Four Gs: Gold, Glory, Gods, Greater Good. Any non-evil character will bite.
6. **The Meet Cute** — script the first 5 minutes in detail. Write actual flavor text. Pre-roll initiative. Introduce each PC cinematically as they arrive into the action. This is the one time over-preparation pays off.

### The 5-Step Adventure-Building Process

| Step | Action | Output |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Come up with a premise | A goal + an obstacle |
| 2 | Summarize the adventure | A narrative walkthrough |
| 3 | Break into acts | 2–3 broad parts |
| 4 | Distribute scenes/encounters | 5–7 challenges placed in acts |
| 5 | Finish the horse | Stats, maps, treasure, flavor text |

---

## Actionable Steps

1. **Finish your premise's third paragraph** — define where the PCs are, why they're there, and what's about to happen.
2. **Write a one-sentence adventure premise** — a goal and an obstacle. Keep it dead simple.
3. **Summarize the adventure** — write a narrative walkthrough including the action opening, the space-for-introductions scene, the journey/obstacles, and the climax.
4. **Break it into 2–3 acts** — each act has a specific sub-goal in pursuit of the main objective.
5. **Distribute 5–7 encounters** — mostly combat, 1–2 non-combat challenges, plus non-encounter interaction scenes. End on a hard climactic encounter.
6. **Lay groundwork seeds** — give enemies a tribal name/symbol, mention an organization, name an NPC who connects to the wider world, include a mysterious artifact or inscription.
7. **Script the first 5 minutes** — write detailed flavor text for the opening. Pre-roll initiative. Introduce each PC cinematically into the action one at a time.
8. **Build the mechanical content** — stat blocks, maps, treasure, trap DCs, NPC descriptions. Use published material freely.
9. **Don't over-prepare** — if you can improvise it at the table, don't write it down. Focus prep on things you can't wing.

---

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> Your first adventure's job is to **start the game**, not to be a masterpiece. Keep it simple, start with action, give players space to breathe after the first fight, and sprinkle in seeds for the future. The campaign gets interesting over time — the first adventure just has to get the engine running. And for the love of all that is holy, **script those first five minutes.**
