# Making Premises — Simple Homebrew Campaign

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Making Premises](https://theangrygm.com/start-a-campaign-2-premise/)
**Date:** February 23, 2022
**Series:** Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign

---

## Overview

Covers the Campaign Pre-Launch Checklist, Just-in-Time game mastering, and how to write a campaign premise — the foundational document that drives everything else.

---

## Campaign Pre-Launch Checklist

1. Write a Premise
2. Make Some Characters
3. Write Your First Adventure
4. Build a Starter Town
5. Build a Region Around the Town
6. Write Your Second Adventure
7. Run the Campaign Until You're Done

Not strictly sequential — steps overlap. You start running games before finishing the build. Character-building and adventure-building happen simultaneously. While running the first adventure (2–3 sessions), build the hometown and region, then plan the second adventure.

---

## Just-in-Time Game Mastering

Only ever work on the **next thing you actually need.**

- You don't need world history to run the first adventure
- You don't need a continent map for a 3-mile trek
- Each step exists only to get you to the next step
- After the second adventure, the campaign is self-sustaining

> Smart GMs plan even their most deeply complex campaigns just-in-time.

Session Zero is optional. It's fine to build a game *for* your players rather than *with* them.

## What is a Premise?

A short prose description — part roadmap, part gameplan, part promise, part advertisement, part instruction. Shared with players so they can make appropriate characters.

A good premise answers **three questions** in roughly **three paragraphs:**

### 1. What is the Campaign About? (Elevator Pitch)

One to three sentences. Who are the characters, what are they doing, and why?

Examples:
- "A group of perfectly normal people must safeguard and destroy a magical artifact to prevent an ancient evil from conquering the world."
- "Leaving their provincial lives behind, would-be adventurers team up to seek their fates and fortunes in a fantastic world."

### 2. How Does the Campaign Play Out?

What will players actually be doing session to session? What kinds of situations, modes of play, and gameplay patterns?

### 3. How Does the Campaign Start?

Where are the characters when the first adventure begins? What are they doing or about to do?

### What a Premise is NOT

- Not a campaign outline or story summary
- Not a detailed world history
- Not a 60-page document of house rules and lore

---

## How to Write a Premise

1. **Free-write** a crappy first version answering the three questions. Don't censor or correct.
2. **Walk away** for at least a day.
3. **Rewrite from scratch** without looking at the first version. Aim for under one page.
4. **Walk away** again for at least a day.
5. **Rewrite again** — copy good sentences fresh, rewrite bad ones, cut 10% of the words.
6. **Check:** Under a page? Three paragraphs? Readable? If not, rewrite and cut 10% again.

## The Secret Premise

When you write a premise, you unconsciously establish three hidden elements:

### Setting
A proto-world hides in every premise through word choices and tropes. Review and make sure the implied setting matches your intent.

### Structures
- **Narrative structure** — Overarching plot? Interwoven narratives? Episodic adventures?
- **Gameplay structure** — Modes of play (dungeon delving, traveling, downtime, interaction, exploration). How adventures are separated from each other.
- The premise should suggest episodic adventure chunks, matching modes of play to adventure vs. between-adventure time.

### Party Dynamic
How and why characters come together and what keeps them together. Keep it simple. Pre-existing relationships not required.

> Most of my games start with strangers who come together by chance. All of them start in inns or taverns. And my games are great.

## The Default Premise

**"The Adventuring Adventurers Who Have Adventures"**

> In a fantasy world littered with the ruins of ancient empires and fallen kingdoms, in a dark age of history, a group of humble individuals leave the simple work-a-day lives of their kin to make their way as traveling adventurers. Though they start as strangers, they soon learn to work together and eventually trust each other as each seeks their own fate and fortune.
>
> After a chance event gives the would-be heroes their first taste of adventure, the party discovers further opportunities in the world's scattered villages, towns, and cities. They prepare in town, travel to the adventure site dealing with wilderness challenges, overcome obstacles, resolve the major conflict, then return to town for rewards, recovery, restocking, upgrading, and training. Town interactions lead to new adventure opportunities. Eventually, they outgrow the town and move on to start the cycle over.
>
> As the campaign opens… [you fill this in]

This is the purest D&D premise — the oldest RPG premise and the one most GMs cut their teeth on.
