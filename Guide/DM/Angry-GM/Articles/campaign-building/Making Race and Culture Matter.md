# Making Race and Culture Matter

**Source:** [Fanservice BS: Making Race and Culture Matter in RPGs – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/making-race-and-culture-matter/)
**Date:** October 30, 2017
**Series:** Fanservice BS / Random BS

---

## Summary

If you want race and culture to matter in your game — to be more than stat bonuses players forget about — you must build them around strong, consistent archetypes. Verbal storytelling has no visuals; the only way to make non-human races feel distinct from humans is through reinforced behavioral patterns. Archetypes aren't straightjackets — they're foundations on which individual variation is built. And they're powerful storytelling tools, but they're delicate and easily broken.

---

## Key Concepts

### The Problem with Verbal Storytelling
- RPGs are entirely spoken narrative. Players see and hear *you*, not your characters.
- Details like NPC race, gender, and even name sink quickly in players' brains. What sticks is the **archetype**: "the innkeeper," "the soldier," "the wizard."
- Without constant reinforcement, the world becomes populated by nonspecific humans with vague roles.
- Even player characters suffer: players remember each other's *classes* more easily than their *races*, because class is constantly reinforced through gameplay.

### Building Feedback Loops
- Connect details together so they reinforce each other. Haughty attitude + breathy voice = elf. After a few encounters, players won't even need to be told.
- This is the narrative equivalent of visual character design in a movie. If elves looked and behaved exactly like humans on screen, you couldn't tell the difference. Same principle applies in spoken narrative.
- **Humans are defined by their diversity.** The only way to make other races stand out is to make them *less* diverse — built around strong archetypes.

### Non-Humans Have Race, Humans Have Culture
- Non-human races should be **monolithic** — all members share the same core archetype.
- When a race has two distinct archetypes (e.g., high elves and wood elves), the game handles this by making them **two subraces** — effectively two distinct races.
- Humans are the diverse, adaptable, varied race. Human cultures can have their own archetypes, but the "default" human culture is cosmopolitan and varied.
- Races and cultures are defined **purely by what makes them different** from the default cosmopolitan human baseline. Too many differences or too few, and they blend into the default.

### Archetypes as Storytelling Tools
- A race = a package of story themes and personality traits.
- When you need a story about honor, sacrifice, or xenophobia, use a member of the "honorable warrior" race. They come pre-loaded with assumptions.
- Each use reinforces the archetype, and the archetype makes each use easier. That's the feedback loop.
- If you include a member of a race without their archetype, players will forget the race within seconds — or stop seeing that race as meaningful at all.

### Layers of Complexity
- A racial archetype should include only **a few distinct traits** (e.g., dwarves: gruff, materialistic, diligent, proud).
- Any individual should exhibit at least two of those traits and rarely contradict any of them.
- On top of the archetype, add unique individual traits.
- Variations on a theme are the key: a craftsman is materialistic about making things; a thief is materialistic about stealing them; a mercenary is materialistic about getting paid. Same foundation, different expression.

### Playing Against Type
- Subverting an archetype is one of the most powerful storytelling moves — but it's **delicate**.
- It only works if the archetype is already strong and well-established.
- The subversive character must be clearly **unique and exceptional** — not just different, but remarkable even by the world's standards.
- Even characters who break the archetype should **reinforce it** by existing in contrast to it. Drizzt only works because drow are evil. The runty dragonborn wizard only works because dragonborn are honorable warriors.
- Do this too often and you break the archetype permanently. (See: the erosion of the drow archetype by too many Drizzt clones.)

### The Conditional Advice
All of the above applies **IF** you want race and culture to matter in your game. If you don't care, that's fine — don't do any of this. But if you want race to be more than a stat bonus, this is how.

## Practical Tips
- Use distinct voices or speech patterns for different races — even silly ones help players remember who's who.
- Keep index cards with racial cues on your side of the DM screen.
- Channel economic determinism into racial archetypes for extra flavor (e.g., mountain dwarves near a lake become skilled sailors and shrewd merchants — on top of the usual dwarf stuff).
- Reuse well-established NPCs across campaigns to build recognition.
