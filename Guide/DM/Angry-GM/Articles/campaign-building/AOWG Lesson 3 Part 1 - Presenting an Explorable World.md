# AOWG Lesson 3 Part 1: Presenting an Explorable World of Adventure

## Key Advice

Exploration is **the satisfaction of curiosity** — not wandering around looking for stuff. The entire exploration cycle is: **"Huh! What's that? I'm gonna find out."** Players must always have a goal so they can *choose to walk away from it* — that choice is what makes exploration feel real.

## Summary

This article defines what exploration actually is and why most GMs get it wrong. Exploration isn't map-filling or hex-crawling — it's a player noticing something, forming a question, and choosing to investigate. The "Narrative Flashlight Problem" explains why TTRPGs struggle with exploration: the GM controls all perception, so players can never truly discover things on their own. The solution is twofold: (1) train players to explore by rewarding it randomly (~2/3 of the time), and (2) always give players a goal they're moving toward so they can *choose* to deviate from it. The article introduces the **Party-Do List** — a pre-session list of everything the party intends to do — as the single most important prep tool for open-world games.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Understand the Exploration Cycle** — Every act of exploration follows: "Huh!" (notice something) → "What's that?" (form a question) → "I'm gonna find out" (choose to act). If any step is missing, it's not exploration.
2. **Reward Exploration Randomly** — About 2/3 of exploration should turn up something. About 2/3 of those somethings should be useful. The remaining 1/3 of the time, exploration leads to nothing. This is why good dungeons have ~33% empty rooms.
3. **Always Give Players a Goal** — Players need a direction, a task, a purpose. Not so they'll complete it, but so they can *choose to abandon it* when something more interesting catches their eye. That choice is what makes exploration feel like exploration.
4. **Build the Party-Do List** — Before every session, write down your best guesses about everything the party wants to accomplish. Include how they'll likely pursue each item. If the list is thin or trails off into "I don't know," you need to fix that before the session.
5. **Seed Goals During Play** — Use adventures to generate future Party-Do items: bounties to claim, items to repair, mysteries to investigate, NPCs to visit. The richer the list, the more directions players can choose from.

> [!tip] The Party-Do List Test
> If your Party-Do List looks like "Travel to City → Find an Inn → ??? → Profit," you're in trouble. A good list has 8-12 specific, actionable items with notes about where the players will likely go to accomplish them. Build those items *into* your adventures so they accumulate naturally.
