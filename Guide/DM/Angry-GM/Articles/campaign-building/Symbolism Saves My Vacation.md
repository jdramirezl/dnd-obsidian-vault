# Symbolism Saves My Vacation

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Symbolism Saves My Vacation](https://theangrygm.com/symbolism-saves-my-vacation/)
**Date:** December 20, 2017
**Topic:** Using themes and symbolism in RPG campaigns

---

## Core Idea

Symbolism is a narrative tool that connects the surface-level elements of a story (explosions, laser swords, dungeon crawls) with its underlying themes (faith vs. technology, the nature of power, etc.). Every story has themes, even simple ones. The themes are what the story is *really* about.

## Key Takeaways

### What Symbolism Is
- A symbol is a thing in the story that connects to one of the story's themes
- Symbols exploit connections that already exist in the audience's brains
- Example: lightsabers as "elegant weapons for a more civilized age" symbolize tradition, piety, and faith in a sci-fi setting

### The Subtlety Problem
- Symbolism must be subtle enough that the audience isn't consciously aware of it, but prominent enough that their brain registers it
- If the audience notices the symbolism overtly, they'll think you're a hack
- Heavy-handed symbolism is called "anvilicious" — themes hitting with the subtlety of an anvil dropped on your head
- Artiste GMs who need applause for their brilliance create a paradox: if they do it well, no one will notice

### Theme vs. Message
- A **theme** is an idea underlying a work of fiction — subtle, incidental, and the audience doesn't have to agree with it to enjoy the story
- A **message** is when the author has something specific to say and makes sure you know it — turns the game into a soapbox
- Games that scream their message only work for people who already agree; everyone else resents being preached to

### The Secret: Don't Try
- "Trust the tale, not the teller" — most themes are accidental or mostly accidental
- Authors write stories they feel, and their feelings run along thematic lines because that's how brains work
- George Lucas wasn't *trying* to write about faith vs. technology; he was trying to write a B-grade sci-fi movie. The themes emerged naturally
- **The practical advice:** Know about themes and symbolism, then ignore them. Build the game that *feels* right. Trust your gut. The themes will build themselves and the symbolism will inject itself
- If your gut says the innkeeper should secretly love the resistance contact, or the city banner should be crimson and black — go with it. That's your trained brain nudging the story

### Can Lack of Symbolism Wreck Your Game?
- No. You don't need to consciously add symbolism. Just tell the story

## The Rule

> Good themes and good symbolism should catch you by surprise. If you're doing it on purpose, you're doing it wrong.

## Application to Campaign Building

- Don't start with a theme and build a campaign around it — that produces heavy-handed, preachy games
- Build the world and adventures that feel right, then notice the themes that emerge
- Once you notice a theme, you can lean into it subtly, but never force it
- The GM who understands narrative structure unconsciously will produce better-themed games than the GM who consciously engineers every symbol
