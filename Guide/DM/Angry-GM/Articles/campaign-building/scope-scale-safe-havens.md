## Summary

This article tackles the foundational question of how much geographic and narrative ground your campaign will cover, and how that decision shapes everything from world-building workload to narrative structure. Angry defines **scale** (size/duration) and **scope** (variety) but then collapses them into a single practical concept: "how much ground will the PCs cover?" He identifies two poles — **large campaigns** (epic travel, exploration, isolated adventures) and **small campaigns** (familiar locations, recurring NPCs, immersion and evolution) — and stresses they are *different*, not *opposite*. The article then introduces **safe havens and home bases** as the key structural element that makes small campaigns work, and shows how to hybridize the two approaches using the **Beatbox Campaign** (hub-and-spokes-and-hub) and the **Turtle Campaign** (traveling home base).

---

## Key Advice

1. **Scale = size/duration; Scope = variety** — but in practice, think of them as one concept: how much ground do the PCs cover over the campaign's life?
2. **Large campaigns** emphasize epic travel, exploration, and character growth through distance. Adventures tend to be isolated and disconnected. Think *Lord of the Rings*, *Star Trek: TNG*, *Final Fantasy*.
3. **Small campaigns** emphasize familiarity, recurring characters, immersion, and consequence. The world evolves around the players. Think *Deep Space Nine*, *Ankh-Morpork*, *Diablo*, *GTA*.
4. **They are different, not opposite.** Wanting to explore a world is not the opposite of wanting to immerse in one — they're just different desires.
5. **Every small campaign needs a safe haven/home base** — a central location that is not just a rest stop but an active part of gameplay where scenes happen, hooks are found, and relationships develop.
6. **You don't have to build the whole world upfront** — even if the campaign will eventually span every inch of it.
7. **A disaster at the home base** can shift a small campaign into a large one — a powerful structural tool when used carefully.
8. **Hybrid structures exist:**
   - **Beatbox Campaign** — players travel from hub to hub, settling into each for a while before moving on (like *World of Warcraft* zones).
   - **Turtle Campaign** — players carry their home base with them (a ship, a caravan, a flying castle, a village on a sea turtle's back).

---

## Actionable Steps

1. **Decide your campaign's scope/scale position** — Are your players travelers or homebodies? Do they want epic exploration or deep immersion? Or a mix?
2. **If small:** Design a central location (town, fortress, city) that will serve as the home base. Plan for it to be an active gameplay space, not just a rest stop.
3. **If large:** Accept that adventures will be more isolated. Plan ongoing plot threads (a quest, a chase, a collection) to provide travel impetus.
4. **If hybrid (Beatbox):** Plan 2–4 hub locations the party will progress through. Each hub gets its own mini-arc and cast of recurring NPCs. Transition between hubs when the party outgrows the current one.
5. **If hybrid (Turtle):** Design the traveling home base (ship, caravan, camp) and its cast of recurring characters. Make the base itself a source of scenes and relationships.
6. **Match world-building effort to scope** — large campaigns need breadth over time; small campaigns need depth in one place.
7. **Consider player mix** — if your group is split between explorers and homebodies, a hybrid structure lets you serve both.

---

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> The size of your campaign world matters less than how familiar the players become with it. A city can feel as vast as a continent if the players never revisit the same neighborhood. A continent can feel as cozy as a village if the players keep coming home to the same tavern. **Design for the feeling you want, not the map size.**
