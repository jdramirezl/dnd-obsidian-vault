# Simple Campaign: Building a Starter Town

## Key Advice

**Town provides resources, interaction, information, context, and opportunities.** You need far less prep than you think — one paragraph vision, five quick questions, and three lists. The real challenge isn't building Town; it's running it. Players don't know what to do in Town, so you must actively prompt them toward the things their characters would naturally seek out. Build Town *after* the game starts so you can tailor it to the actual party.

## Summary

Part of the *Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign* series. This lesson covers step 4: building the starter town. You have about 3 weeks (while players are in the first adventure) to build Town, the region, and the second adventure.

**What Town is for (bare minimum):**
- **Resources** — equipment, supplies, leveling up.
- **Interaction** — NPCs, relationships, the world as a living place.
- **Information** — learning about the world and its history.
- **Context** — seeing the world as a world, not just a game construct.
- **Opportunities** — leads on the next adventure.

**The gap between ideal and reality:**
- GMs imagine players will explore Town organically — wandering, discovering, building relationships.
- In reality, players sit in awkward silence until someone says "we go to the inn," then ask "is there an armor shop?" until the GM gives up and throws a quest-giver at them.
- **Bridge the gap by prompting players explicitly.** Narrate what their characters would naturally know: "you realize you need a place to sleep," "you can sell trinkets by wandering the market," "as a cleric, it's customary to visit the local temple."
- While players follow prompts to things they need, throw interesting things in their path — NPCs, events, rumors, points of interest.

**How to build Town — one paragraph, five questions, three lists:**

### The Vision Paragraph
- Close your eyes, imagine standing in Town as fog lifts. Write what you see — one paragraph, half a page max.
- Stop the moment you start describing specific buildings or people. This is just priming your imagination.

### Five Questions
1. **How big?** — Outpost, village, town, or city. That's it. No census numbers.
2. **How is it structured?** — Two sentences max. Most settlements radiate from a key feature. Note any neighborhood divisions.
3. **What's at the Heart?** — The defining feature that represents the town's character (a river, a castle, a market, a temple). This will unconsciously flavor everything.
4. **Who runs it?** — One person, one name, one title. Keep it simple. Whoever gets the final say.
5. **Who keeps the peace?** — One person or organization. Who polices the town and confronts troublemakers?

### Three Lists

**List 1: Essential Services** — things adventurers will actually seek out:
- Inn, tavern, market, smith, bowyer, leatherworker, reagents/potions.
- Tailor the list to your party: temples for clerics, fighting pits for fighters, black markets for rogues, libraries for scholars.
- Name the places and the people who run them. Players interact with *whos*, not *wheres*.

**List 2: Fun Facts About Town** — 3–5 interesting things to overhear or encounter:
- Fuel for interactions, encounters, and interruptions while players are doing other things.
- Be specific — use proper names even if you don't know what they mean yet.
- Keep it grounded and human, not wacky or fantastical.
- **Flag one fact as totally untrue** (bonus Angry Point).

**List 3: Facts About the Region** — 3–5 things about the world beyond Town's walls:
- Great for foreshadowing and planting adventure seeds.
- Include mildly fantastical elements — let the sense of fantasy grow over time.
- **Flag one fact as a false flag** (bonus Angry Point).

**Maps:**
- You don't strictly need a town map, but players appreciate even a crappy sketch.
- Do the map AFTER the region and second adventure are done. Priorities first.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Write your vision paragraph** — close your eyes, imagine the fog lifting, write what you see. Stop at half a page.
2. **Answer the five questions** — size, structure, heart, ruler, peacekeeper. One or two lines each.
3. **List essential services** — cover the basics (inn, tavern, market, smith) then add party-specific services. Name every place and person.
4. **Write 3–5 fun facts about Town** — specific, grounded, with proper names. Flag one as false.
5. **Write 3–5 facts about the region** — mildly fantastical, with proper names. Flag one as false.
6. **Prompt your players in-game** — narrate what their characters would naturally know and do. Throw interesting things in their path while they're seeking services.
