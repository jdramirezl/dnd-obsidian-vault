## Summary

Angry explains what Town is for (resources, interaction, information, context, and opportunities), why it never works the way GMs imagine (players don't know what to do there and need heavy prompting), and then provides a dead-simple Town-building method: write one vision paragraph, answer five questions, and create three lists. That's it. The article emphasizes building *just enough* Town to run the game — no more — and using tutorial-style narration to guide players through Town Business until they learn to navigate it themselves. Town is built *after* the first adventure starts, because you need to know the party's composition to tailor services to their needs.

---

## Key Advice

1. **Town provides 5 things at minimum:** Resources, Interaction, Information, Context, and Opportunities. (Someday also: respite, safety, development, and agency.)
2. **Players don't know what to do in Town.** They'll sit there and say "we go to the inn" and then stare at you. You must prompt them — hard — using tutorial-style narration that tells them what their characters would know.
3. **Don't be wishy-washy with Town content.** Don't say "there's a fight across the street." Have the fight spill into the PC's path. Remind players constantly they can take action.
4. **Build Town AFTER the game starts** — you need to know the party's classes, backgrounds, and races to tailor essential services.

### The Town-Building Method

#### One Paragraph: Your Vision of Town
- Close your eyes, imagine standing in Town as fog lifts. Write what you see.
- Stop when you start naming specific buildings or people. One paragraph, half a page max.
- This primes your imagination. You'll never show it to anyone.

#### Five Questions

| # | Question | What It Means |
|---|----------|---------------|
| 1 | How big is your Town? | Outpost, village, town, or city — that's it |
| 2 | How is it structured? | Layout from above: radiates from what? Walls? Neighborhoods? |
| 3 | What's at the Heart? | The defining feature that represents the town's character (market, river, castle, temple) |
| 4 | Who runs it? | One name, one title. Whoever gets the final say. |
| 5 | Who keeps the peace? | One name or organization. Who polices and responds to trouble? |

#### Three Lists

| List | Content | Size |
|------|---------|------|
| **Essential Services** | Places PCs will seek out: inn, tavern, market, smith, temple, etc. Include who provides each service. Tailor to party composition. | 7–12 entries |
| **Fun Facts (Town Rumors)** | Interesting things to overhear or encounter in Town. Specific names, grounded, not wacky. Flag one as untrue. | 3–5 items |
| **Regional Facts (Outside Town)** | Things to learn about the world beyond the walls. Specific names, mildly fantastical. Flag one as untrue. | 3–5 items |

5. **Tailor Essential Services to your party.** Clerics need temples, rogues need criminal contacts, guild artisans need guild halls. Anticipate what each PC will look for.
6. **Keep it grounded and human.** Resist the urge to fill Town with wacky, fantastical weirdness. Simple and real is more engaging. Let fantasy grow over time.
7. **Fun Facts and Regional Facts are seeds.** They might become adventures, they might go nowhere. Be specific (use names) but don't define what they mean yet.
8. **Flag one fact per list as false.** This creates mystery and prevents players from treating rumors as gospel.
9. **Don't draw a map yet.** Do the region and second adventure first. Map Town later if you have time.

---

## Actionable Steps

1. **Write your vision paragraph** — fog-lifting exercise, one paragraph, stop at specific details.
2. **Answer the five questions** — one or two sentences each. Keep it simple. Start with a village or town.
3. **Build the Essential Services list** — start with universal needs (inn, tavern, market, smith, bowyer, leatherworker, reagents/potions), then add party-specific services (temple, fighting pit, black market, library, shrine, guild hall, etc.). Name the providers.
4. **Write 3–5 Fun Facts about Town** — specific, grounded, named. Flag one as untrue.
5. **Write 3–5 Regional Facts** — specific, named, mildly fantastical. Flag one as untrue.
6. **Prepare tutorial prompts** — write out the narration you'll use to guide players through their first Town visit (finding the inn, selling treasure, shopping, visiting class-relevant locations).
7. **Resist the urge to over-build.** Town will grow in depth and complexity as the campaign continues. You're just setting the minimum in stone.

---

> [!tip] The Angry Takeaway
> Town is the most important recurring location in your campaign, but players will flounder there unless you guide them. **Build just enough to run it** — one paragraph, five questions, three lists — and then **prompt your players like it's a tutorial level.** The Town will grow organically as you play. Don't front-load the work; front-load the guidance.
