# The Italian Campaign: On Shape and Glue

## Key Advice

Every campaign defines two things: the **Shape** (how adventures relate to each other) and the **Glue** (what keeps the party together). Shape is optional — if you don't pick one, one will emerge. Glue is **mandatory** — if you don't plan it, your campaign will crack and die. The single most important aspect of campaign design is giving the characters a reason to stick together despite whatever conflicts arise.

## Summary

Angry introduces his "Italian Model" for campaign shapes and argues that party cohesion (Glue) is far more important than narrative structure (Shape).

**Campaign Shape — The Italian Model:**

Four basic shapes, named after Italian food:

1. **Plate of Meatballs:** Each adventure is standalone. No overarching connections. Players can miss an adventure and jump back in easily. The default shape if you don't plan one.

2. **Noodle (Adventure Path):** A single overarching story with a defined goal. Each adventure is a "slurp" advancing toward that goal. What people usually mean by "adventure path."

3. **Sausage Links:** Multiple adventure paths in succession. Each "sausage" is its own adventure path with its own overarching goal. Can be linked (building on each other toward a grand goal) or unlinked (completely independent paths).

4. **Plate of Spaghetti:** Multiple adventure paths interwoven simultaneously. Several overarching goals, each advanced by different adventures. Best example: each player has a personal long-term goal, and adventures advance one or two at a time.

**Combination plates (Tour of Italy):**
- **Spaghetti and Meatballs:** Interwoven adventure paths plus standalone adventures.
- **Sausage and Spaghetti:** One major adventure path series plus interwoven minor paths.
- Campaigns can change shape mid-course — a Plate of Meatballs can grow a Noodle when a recurring villain develops into an overarching threat.
- You can plan for this: start with Meatballs while a background plot waits to emerge as a Noodle.

**Campaign Glue — The critical element:**

- Characters NEED a reason to adventure together and STAY together.
- Fiction must make sense. Unlike real life, stories need logical relationships. When a party "should" split up but can't (because of the metagame assumption that the party stays together), the game breaks.
- The **metagame** dictates that D&D is about a party having adventures together. Players almost never consider having their character leave. This creates a trap: characters who should split up are stuck together by unwritten rules.
- Even mild conflicts (different problem-solving approaches, ethical disagreements) can poison a campaign over time by hogging spotlight and creating resentment.

**Two huge mistakes GMs make:**

1. **The Accidental Party:** Strangers meet during a crisis, work together, and the GM assumes bonds will form naturally. Sometimes they do. Often they don't. The Accidental Party is a way of *getting* the party together, not *keeping* them together.

2. **The Old Friends:** Making players create characters with pre-existing relationships. The problem: the character a player *creates* is never the character they *play*. Personalities emerge through interaction. Traits develop in response to situations. Pre-existing relationships can be poisoned by emergent personality conflicts. Like the Accidental Party, this *gets* the party together but doesn't *keep* them together.

**Character discovery vs. character growth:**
- Characters' personalities *emerge* through play (players discover how their character reacts to new situations).
- But characters don't *grow or change* in the way fictional characters do in movies (undergoing meaningful arcs). Players settle into who they want to be and stay there.
- This means you can't rely on characters naturally "learning to work together" like buddy cops in a movie.

**The GM's job:**
- Build an in-game reason for characters to be stuck together.
- Use that forced proximity plus adventure design to push characters toward growth and compromise.
- If you don't plan Glue, you're leaving everything to chance.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Shape is optional; Glue is mandatory.** If you don't pick a Shape, one emerges. If you don't plan Glue, the campaign may die.
2. **The Italian Model** (Meatballs, Noodle, Sausage, Spaghetti) provides a useful vocabulary for thinking about campaign structure.
3. **Campaigns can change shape** — deliberately or accidentally. A Meatballs campaign can grow a Noodle. Plan for this possibility.
4. **The Accidental Party and Old Friends are ways to START a party, not KEEP one.** Neither provides lasting Glue.
5. **Characters are discovered through play**, not created on paper. Pre-planned relationships will be tested by emergent personalities.
6. **The metagame traps characters together** even when the fiction says they should split. Without Glue, this creates a game that feels wrong but can't fix itself.
