# Session Zero Part One — How to Even Session Zero

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/session-zero-part-one/)
**Date:** August 30, 2017
**Series:** Session Zero Blitz (Part One of One)

---

## Overview

The practical, step-by-step guide to actually running a Session Zero. The goal is to gather information to build a campaign premise — not to run a committee meeting. A good Session Zero is a casual conversation with a secret agenda: you listen to your players' emotions about what they love and hate, take furious notes, and use that to build a game they'll enjoy. The GM runs it like a secret reconnaissance mission disguised as a friendly chat.

## Core Principle: People's Brains Are Stupid

- People are **good at knowing what they like** (emotional response).
- People are **bad at knowing what they want** (predicting future enjoyment).
- People are **terrible at explaining any of it** (reasoning about emotions is guesswork).

Don't ask players to predict what they'll enjoy. Instead, get them talking about what they've loved in the past and observe their emotions. If you can discover what made someone happy before and imitate it, you'll make them happy again.

## The Secret Agent Metaphor

Imagine you've been secretly hired to make a movie sequel. You're at dinner with friends who are the perfect audience. You want to pump them for information about what they loved and hated — but you can't let them know what you're doing. So you mostly let the conversation happen, listen carefully, note everything useful, and only steer when the conversation drifts too far off topic.

**That's a good Session Zero.**

## Preparation

- **Schedule ~2 hours** (90 useful minutes minimum). Include dinner beforehand as a bribe and socialization period.
- **All players must attend.** Reschedule if necessary. Players who try to blow it off are often the ones whose input matters most.
- **Eliminate distractions.** No phones, tablets, or computers at the table. No alcohol beyond a beer or two.
- **Bring a pad and a separate sheet of paper.** The sheet has each player's name with space for engagement/preference notes. The pad is for story ideas, setting notes, and anything cool.

## The Peanut Butter Sandwich Structure

### Slice 1: Calling to Order

Start officially. Discuss the practical stuff that requires explicit conversation:
- **Scheduling:** When, how often, how long.
- **Commitment and reliability:** Ask honestly — how many sessions can each person actually attend?
- **Campaign duration:** Any sense of how long this should run?
- **Rules system:** Already decided? Confirm it.
- **Deal-breakers:** State yours upfront (e.g., "no evil PCs, no overly antisocial characters").
- **Review anything already settled** from prior discussions.

Forestall all story/setting discussion until later.

### The Peanut Butter: The Casual Conversation

**Your goals while listening:**
1. Fill in campaign decisions: genre/tone, setting, options/restrictions, shape and glue.
2. Identify 2–3 core engagements (Challenge, Discovery, Expression, Fantasy, Narrative, Submission).
3. Collect story elements that excite the players.

**How to do it:**

1. **Start with open-ended questions to specific people.** Ask the person to your left about their favorite moment from a past RPG. For non-RPG players, ask about a favorite video game, book, or movie moment.

2. **Get rid of the conversation ball.** You don't want a Q&A cycle (you ask, they answer, repeat). You want players talking *to each other*. Keep going around the table until you spot an opportunity to lateral — "Alice, you both seem to like pulling off crazy plans. Did your rogue ever do anything like Dave's?" Then withdraw.

3. **Use body language to withdraw.** Raise your pad, lean back, make a show of taking notes. Give silence a moment to fill itself.

4. **Listen and note everything.** If something gets everyone excited (evil empires, Cthulhu horror, ships and exploration), write it down. If someone shows a leaning toward a particular engagement, note it by their name. Write down pop culture references people love — you can look them up later.

5. **Steer when needed.** Don't let conversation stay too long on one topic. Use topic changes to confirm patterns you're seeing. If Alice and Bob both seem like discovery-seekers, ask one of them about exploring in Fallout 4.

6. **Never discuss engagements directly.** Don't say "you seem to dig discovery." Instead say "you're the sort of person who found every collectible in the Doom reboot, aren't you?" Give them something specific to react to.

7. **If the ball comes back to you,** change topics and ask open-ended questions until you can lateral again.

### Slice 2: Enough Peanut Butter

The conversation is over when:
- You've identified 2–3 core engagements and some story elements, OR
- The conversation keeps stalling, veering off topic, or people are checking phones.

Whatever you have is all you're going to get.

**Wrap-up process:**
1. Consult your notes. Circle standout ideas.
2. Throw combinations at the wall: "You know what would be cool? An evil empire of undead and necromancers like in Warcraft III." See what resonates.
3. Bounce a few ideas. Don't stress if assent is muted — people are tired by now.
4. Review scheduling decisions.
5. Recap the most promising ideas.
6. Tell players you'll develop a firm premise and send it in a few days. Set a deadline.
7. Caution against thinking about specific characters until the premise is finalized.

**Example wrap-up:**
> "Okay, so we're doing D&D. Weekly game every Saturday, about four hours. I'm going to work up some specifics, but it sounds like it would be fun to have a campaign about fighting some sort of invasion from a secret world that involves undead with a good mix of investigation and action. How does that sound?"

## Key Techniques Summary

| Technique | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Open-ended questions to specific people | Gets individuals talking emotionally |
| Lateraling the conversation ball | Gets players talking to each other so you can listen |
| Body language withdrawal | Signals you're out of the conversation |
| Furious note-taking | Captures emotions, preferences, and story seeds |
| Pop culture references | Reveals engagements and preferences indirectly |
| Throwing ideas at the wall | Tests which combinations resonate |
