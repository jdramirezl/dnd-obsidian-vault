# Scope and Scale and Safe Havens

## Key Advice

**Scope and scale** describe how much ground a campaign covers — not just geographically, but in terms of variety and familiarity. **Scale** is about size; **scope** is about variety. Together, they define whether your campaign is a globe-trotting epic or a deeply rooted hometown story. The choice directly shapes your narrative structure, world-building workload, and what your players get out of the game. **Safe havens and home bases** are the key structural tool for bridging the gap between large and small campaigns.

## Summary

Angry explores one axis of campaign design: how much ground the PCs cover and how familiar they become with their surroundings. This produces three basic campaign structures.

**Large scope and scale campaigns:**
- PCs travel constantly, rarely return to the same place, and only backtrack briefly for specific reasons.
- Examples: Lord of the Rings, Star Trek: TNG, Final Fantasy, Dragon Quest.
- Selling points: epic feel, exploration, world-building showcase.
- Adventures tend to be isolated and disconnected — arrive somewhere new, have an adventure, move on.
- Ongoing plot threads provide the impetus for travel (reaching a destination, chasing someone, collecting artifact pieces, or just wandering).

**Small scope and scale campaigns:**
- PCs keep returning to the same locations, build familiarity, and develop relationships with recurring characters and factions.
- Examples: Star Trek: DS9, Ankh-Morpork Discworld books, Diablo, GTA.
- Selling points: immersion, relationship-building, watching the world evolve as a consequence of player actions.
- Structure is hub-and-spoke: a central location where adventures start and end, with actual game time spent in familiar places with familiar characters.
- The central location can be a fortress, village, town, city, or small region — size doesn't matter, familiarity does.
- The location evolves over time; change comes from the setting growing, not from the players moving on.

**Small ≠ opposite of large — they're different:**
- Large campaigns emphasize exploration and epic distance. Small campaigns emphasize immersion and consequence.
- A city campaign can be either large or small depending on whether players revisit neighborhoods and build familiarity.

**Safe havens and home bases:**
- Every small campaign has a safe haven at its heart — a location that's relatively safe, familiar, and serves as the anchor for the campaign.
- "Safe" is relative — temporary, localized danger is fine, and a looming threat can be a plot thread. But the haven is generally a place players can retreat to.
- A disaster destroying the home base can shift a small campaign to large — a powerful structural tool if it doesn't feel like a screw job.

**Hybrid structures (middle ground):**
- **Beatbox campaign** ("hub-and-spokes-and-hub-and-spokes"): PCs travel from one home base to the next. At each, they settle in, do adventures, get attached, then move on. Each location has its own plot arc. This is how World of Warcraft is structured. Great for sandbox feel with good challenge progression.
- **Turtle campaign**: PCs carry their home base with them — a ship and crew, a band of wandering exiles, a flying castle, a village on a giant sea turtle. Recurring characters and relationships travel with the party while the world around them changes.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Decide your campaign's scope and scale early** — ask whether your players want epic exploration or deep immersion. This shapes everything else.
2. **For small campaigns, define your home base** — make it a place where actual game time happens, not just a rest stop. Fill it with recurring characters and evolving situations.
3. **For large campaigns, consider adding safe havens** — use the beatbox structure (sequential home bases) or the turtle structure (traveling home base) to give players the benefits of familiarity without sacrificing the epic feel.
4. **Don't build the whole world upfront** — even large campaigns only need the world built by the end, not at the start.
5. **Use home base disruption as a campaign pivot** — destroying or threatening the safe haven can dramatically shift the campaign's feel, but only if it doesn't feel like a punishment.
