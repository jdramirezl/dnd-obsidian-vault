# What's in a Campaign — Introduction

**Source:** [The Angry GM — What's in a Campaign](https://theangrygm.com/start-a-campaign-1-introduction/)
**Date:** February 2, 2022
**Series:** Let's Start a Simple Homebrew Campaign (Introduction)

---

## Overview

The introductory article for the Simple Homebrew Campaign series. Defines what a campaign is, explains published vs. homebrew paths, and introduces the three critical elements: continuity, narrative structure, and the gameplay loop.

---

## What is a Campaign?

> A campaign is an ongoing series of contiguous, tabletop roleplaying game adventures.

Simplified:

> A campaign is what happens when you get to a place where you could stop playing but, instead, you don't.

---

## Two Paths: Published vs. Homebrew

- **Published** — Buy a module/adventure path and run it.
- **Homebrew** — Create and run your own adventures.

### Homebrewing Basics

- Most homebrewers use a "Lego approach" — assemble from premade bits, occasionally create custom content
- Stealing content for personal use is expected. Call it "homage" or "building a content library."
- Build a folder of stolen game elements: maps, encounters, monsters, names
- Coming up with names is hard. Steal them from everywhere.

---

## Three Critical Campaign Elements

### 1. Continuity

Something that stays the same from one adventure/session to the next.

**Types:**
- **Character continuity** — Same characters session to session (most common)
- **Party continuity** — The party concept survives even as members change (Party of Theseus). Only breaks if everyone dies at once or the party breaks up.
- **Narrative continuity** — The ongoing story persists even if characters change
- **Setting continuity** — Same world, even if characters and stories change

**Key insight:** When one continuity breaks (e.g., TPK), switch to another. The campaign doesn't have to end.

> RPGs don't really rely on character continuity. They survive mostly on party continuity.

### 2. Narrative Structure

How the story is put together. Not collaborative storytelling — RPGs produce stories because everything does. Good structure makes them engaging.

- Least important of the three elements and easiest to simplify
- This is what "simple" in Simple Homebrew Campaign refers to

### 3. The Gameplay Loop

How the game actually plays out session to session. **Not part of the rules** — it's how you personally run your game.

**The Skyrim Example:**
> Town → talk to people → pick quest → do it → return to town → sell/upgrade/restock → next quest → run out → move to next town → repeat

**Why loops matter:**
- Varied enough to stay fresh, consistent enough that players know what to do
- Easier to run, fewer surprises
- Established expectations can be subverted for tension
- Most TTRPGs have implied loops but don't explain or support them mechanically

**The Never-Ending Adventure problem:** Running everything the same way with no variety in modes of play.

> It's on you to figure out how your game plays out. What are the modes of play? How do players move between them?

---

## Key Takeaways

- A campaign = keep playing instead of stopping
- Homebrewing = making your own adventures. Steal freely.
- Three elements: continuity, narrative structure, gameplay loop
- Party continuity (not character) is what really keeps campaigns alive
- The gameplay loop is the most important and most overlooked element
- Narrative structure is least important and easiest to simplify
- All three get established when you write a premise
