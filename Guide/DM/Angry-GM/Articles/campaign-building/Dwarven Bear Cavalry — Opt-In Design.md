# Dwarven Bear Cavalry — Opt-In Design

**Source:** [Fanservice BS: Dwarven Bear Cavalry… Yes, Really – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dwarf-bear-cavalry/)
**Date:** March 23, 2018
**Series:** Fanservice BS / Random BS

---

## Summary

Ostensibly about why dwarven bear cavalry is a stupid idea, this article's real lesson is about **opt-in vs. opt-out design** when building a campaign setting. Most GMs start with everything in the rulebook and only remove what doesn't fit. The better approach: start with nothing and only include what earns its place. If you're not doing that, you might as well let the dwarven bear cavalry in — you're already most of the way there.

---

## Key Concepts

### Why Bear Cavalry Doesn't Work (The Surface Argument)
- Bears fail nearly all six criteria for domestication: they don't breed easily in captivity, mature slowly, require enormous amounts of food (omnivores/carnivores), are temperamental, panicky, and solitary.
- Bears aren't fast enough for cavalry — short bursts only, slower than horses, can't sustain speed.
- Dwarves live underground or in mountains. Cavalry (flanking, mobility) is useless in tunnels and narrow passes. Dwarves are built for skirmish fighting in cramped spaces, not mounted charges.
- If you're going to use your imagination, imagine something that actually makes sense — armored cave lizards, underdark predator cats, giant mountain goats — not something that falls apart the moment anyone thinks about it.
- "My imagination is better than that. And yours should be too."

### The Real Lesson: Opt-In vs. Opt-Out

**The question:** Does any hypothetically awesome idea belong in every setting?

**The answer:** Of course not. No matter how awesome something is, it doesn't belong in every setting just because you can find an excuse to shove it in.

Most GMs use an **opt-out** approach:
- Start with everything in the core rules.
- Remove things that obviously don't fit.
- Default assumption: everything belongs unless you can make a case for removing it.

The better approach is **opt-in**:
- Start from the assumption that **nothing** belongs in the game.
- Each race, class, background, spell, and monster must make a case for its inclusion.
- "Everything is banned unless it isn't."

### Practical Application
- In Angry's latest campaign: no wood elves or mountain dwarves (they exist in the world but aren't part of the game). Wild magic sorcerers are in — because they fit this specific game's themes.
- This isn't about "banning" things. There's no reason needed for exclusion. The reason is needed for **inclusion**.
- Players at the actual table have never complained about this approach. The only complaints come from the internet.

### The Analogy
If you're not making game elements opt into your game, you might as well let the dwarven bear cavalry in. You're already defaulting to "everything is included unless I specifically object." That's the same logic that leads to bear cavalry in every setting.
