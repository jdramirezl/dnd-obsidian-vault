# There's No Such Thing as a Skill Challenge

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/no-skill-challenges/)
**Date:** August 3, 2022
**Category:** Random Bulls$&%

---

## Summary

Skill challenges — the mechanic from D&D 4E where players accumulate successes before failures — are bad design. Even though 4E killed them off, GMs keep reinventing them in 5E without realizing it. The real problem isn't that failed rolls punish participation. The real problem is that skill challenges reduce every non-combat encounter to picking skills from a list and rolling dice, regardless of the fictional situation.

## Description-First vs. Action-First Design

The fundamental distinction:

- **Action-first design** (bad): You design a problem, then define a series of if-then actions to overcome it. "If the players use this skill, they score a success or failure." This is a **puzzle** — a thing with a correct answer.
- **Description-first design** (good): You describe the challenge using the minimum number of game mechanics needed. Then you let the GM adjudicate whatever the players try. This creates a **problem** — a thing with no correct answers, only possible solutions.

A skill challenge is a bunch of if-then conditionals. It doesn't matter what the challenge describes — if the players roll the right skills, they win or lose. The fictional situation is irrelevant to the mechanical resolution.

## The Whatever Stat Is Different

Angry's "Whatever Stat" (a 1-to-10 scale tracking an in-world state) looks similar but is fundamentally different:

- It **must measure something that exists in the world** — the NPC's patience, the guard's alertness, the ritual's closeness to completion
- There's **no if-then relationship** between player actions and the score — it's "when something happens to change the score, the score changes"
- You **can't run it without knowing what it describes**, because the GM has to decide what impact any event has on the actual in-world thing being measured

## D&D 3E's Descriptive Stat Blocks

D&D 3E stat blocks were **description-based** rather than action-based. An NPC stat block told you how strong they were, what they knew, how they'd been trained. A trap stat block described how it was triggered, reset, repaired, and bypassed. This gave the GM a complete mechanical picture of the situation, enabling them to handle whatever the players did.

D&D 5E's language and mechanics are designed around handling what the players do, not describing the world.

## The Quicksand Principle

GMs today think designing encounters means figuring out how the players can overcome a challenge. But the job is to **describe the challenge** — how big is the quicksand patch, how deep, what does it do, how hard is it to spot — and then see what happens.

> "When you design a challenge, your job's to describe the challenge. Using the bare minimum number of descriptive game mechanics you need. And nothing else."

## How to Design Non-Combat Encounters

1. Describe the challenge using the minimum number of descriptive game mechanics needed
2. Don't define how players overcome it
3. When running it, describe the challenge, listen to the players, and use the mechanics you've got to work out how things play out
4. You can pull an ability check and a DC out of thin air whenever you need to

### Homework Exercise

Design three non-combat encounters (one trap, one social encounter, one hazard/obstacle) following two rules:

1. **Use third-person, omniscient narrator voice.** No talking to the GM about the players.
2. **Design system-agnostic challenges.** No mechanics at all. The closest you can come is saying something is "easy" or "difficult."

This forces description-first design rather than action-first design.
