# Here Comes Tagalong — Henchmen, Allies, and DMPCs

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/here-comes-tagalong/)
**Date:** July 27, 2016
**Series:** Give Non Players Character

---

A **Tagalong NPC** is ANY NPC who travels with the party during their adventures. That includes NPC allies, companions, cohorts, and even pets. Because anything is an NPC if it makes decisions based on consistent motivations.

## What Makes Tagalongs Great

Tagalong NPCs offer tremendous possibilities:

- **World-building proof:** Because PCs interact with them regularly, a well-developed Tagalong with strong motivations, hopes, dreams, flaws, and fears proves how alive the world really is.
- **Character arcs:** Tagalongs can grow, develop, and change — driven by the PCs' actions. Most NPCs can't do this because they don't get enough screen time.
- **Source of conflict:** A Tagalong with their own goals and personality creates organic, non-violent conflict within the party.
- **Mechanical utility:** They allow the GM to plant extra skills, tools, and information in the party.
- **Exposition delivery:** A Tagalong provides an in-game way of sharing information, providing hints, and warning players of unforeseen consequences so they can make informed choices.

Good storytellers weave exposition into the narrative. Tagalongs are great tools for that — far better than direct narration.

## Henchbeings vs. Allies

### Henchbeings

A henchbeing is subservient to the party — pets, hirelings, cohorts, apprentices, assistants, bodyguards, escort NPCs. Two defining traits:
1. They will **generally follow orders** or won't act independently in most situations.
2. The party generally **chooses** to bring them along (hired, conscripted, or accepted).

### Allies

An ally works with the party because of common goals but is **not subservient**. They act independently. They might take suggestions but will ignore, argue with, or countermand orders that conflict with their goals. The most common form is the dreaded DMPC.

**Power level does not define the distinction.** A ranger's animal companion can be equal to the ranger. A bodyguard might be more skilled than some party members. What matters is the relationship dynamic, not the stat block.

## The Dreaded DMPC

There is nothing inherently wrong with a DMPC. The complaint that the GM is "just including the GMPC to have someone to play on the side of the PCs" is a stupid complaint. GMs are allowed to have fun too.

A GMPC done right is great fun for the GM and the players. They give PCs the chance to bring along a piece of the world to interact with. They give the GM organic ways to provide information. In moral arguments, the GMPC can represent the view of the world.

Past players remember ally NPCs in the same breath that they remember the PCs — wistfully and fondly.

## A Tagalong is an NPC

A Tagalong is going to have a LOT of screen time — in ALL the scenes. You can get away with a shopkeeper with one fun quirk, but a Tagalong better be a human being. You need to know:
- What they want
- Why they are in the party
- What lines they won't cross
- What they fear and desire

**You have to role-play them.** They can't act like preprogrammed combat robots. They have to make decisions based on what they want and fear.

For **henchbeings**, this is actually harder than allies. You tend to just make them mindless slaves of the party, which dehumanizes them. You need to know: What is the henchbeing getting out of the relationship? Money? Duty? Loyalty? Hero worship? Fear? And you have to decide when the line gets crossed and how they respond.

## Mary Sues and Marty Stus

A Mary Sue/Marty Stu is a character that outshines everyone else in the story. They co-opt the story. They're extremely capable and make the players feel like their place in the story is being supplanted.

Players value their spotlight time and their wheelhouse — the part of the story they've claimed as "my thing." That's why players naturally don't overlap on classes, skills, and party roles.

### The Balm Against Mary Sues

1. **Specialize the NPC.** Give them a descriptor ("the combat medic," "the con artist," "the wilderness guide") that doesn't overlap with someone else's spotlight. Spotlight infringement CAN be broken with good reason, but be deliberate about it.

2. **Give the NPC a weakness or flaw** that periodically handicaps them. The more powerful the NPC or the more spotlights they intrude on, the more weaknesses you need.

## Handling Tagalongs at the Table

### The Tagalong Sheet

The moment an NPC joins the group, take out a piece of paper and write their name and any details. Every time you "discover" a new thing about that NPC, write it down. This helps you maintain consistency.

### Playing The NPC Card

Write the Tagalong's name and a quick descriptor on an index card and leave it on the table where everyone can see it. When the NPC speaks, hold up or point to the card. This reminds everyone the NPC exists and indicates when you're speaking AS the NPC.

### Who Controls the Tagalong

**Every Tagalong is an NPC. You are always the NPC's brain.** Never relinquish that — even for class features like familiars or animal companions.

For dice rolling and combat control, it doesn't really matter. You can hand stats to a player or keep them yourself. General guidelines:
- **Henchbeings** that follow orders: hand to a player
- **Henchbeings** that are plot MacGuffins or might not be loyal: keep yourself
- **Independent allies**: often keep yourself, but not always

Even if you hand over control, always stand ready to veto any action the NPC wouldn't take. That NPC is still YOUR character — part of the world, which is YOUR character.

It is a myth that letting the players control the Tagalong makes them feel more engaged or less resentful. Engagement comes from the NPC being a relatable human. The rest comes from not creating a Mary Sue.
