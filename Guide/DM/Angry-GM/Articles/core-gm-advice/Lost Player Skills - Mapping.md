# Lost Player Skills: Mapping

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Lost Player Skills: Mapping](https://theangrygm.com/lost-player-skills-mapping/)
**Date:** November 29, 2019
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Related:** [[Maps - Whats On Your Table]], [[Lost Player Skills - Calling]]

---

## Summary

Player mapping — where a designated player keeps a map of the dungeon or wilderness as the party explores — used to be an assumed part of D&D's core gameplay loop. Its disappearance has contributed to a loss of exploration feel, increased GM burden, and communication problems at the table. Angry argues it should come back, but modernized.

---

## The Problem

Something feels "off" in modern games — a laboring quality to the flow of conversation between GM and players. Issues with presenting information, evaluating it, and communicating decisions. Older GMs feel it; newer GMs assume it's just how things are.

These problems connect to the loss of two player skills that used to be in the rules: **mapping** and **calling**.

---

## Historical Context

The Mentzer Red Box (1983), AD&D 1E, and AD&D 2E all included sections on mapping. One player was appointed as the mapper. The mapper's job:
- Keep a map of the party's explorations
- Serve as the party's navigator
- Track where the party hadn't been yet
- Know how to get back to the entrance

This was both a player role AND a character role — the rules assumed a smart group of adventurers would keep an in-game map.

---

## What Player Mapping Adds

### Navigational Challenges
Player-drawn maps enable challenges meaningless with GM-provided maps:
- Teleporters whisking players to distant rooms (must start a new map and watch for landmarks)
- Shifting halls or rotating rooms that confound the mapper
- Magical confusion effects making the party think they're going the wrong direction
- Mapper errors adding organic challenge

### Enhanced Exploration Feel
- **GM-revealed maps** make players function like robot vacuum cleaners — ping-ponging around until they've cleaned the fog of war. It's a checklist.
- **Player-drawn maps** make exploration an act of intellectual conquest — charting and understanding the space. The map becomes a journal of the adventure.
- When players consult their own map to find a way out, they *earned* those directions.

### Hex Crawl Support
Player mapping was essential for hex crawl games. The party explored hex by hex, mapping terrain and points of interest. Failed navigation rolls meant mapping wrong until they discovered they were lost.

---

## Modernizing Mapping: Flowcharts, Not Blueprints

**Throw out precision.** All the party needs is a **flowchart**:
- A room = a box with a name
- A connection = a hash mark or line
- A hallway = a line

The map only needs to answer:
1. How did we get here?
2. How do we get back?
3. Where do we go next?
4. Where was that room with the thing we need to revisit?

Anyone can map this way with a little practice.

---

## Implementation: Appointing a Mapper

1. **Ask the party to appoint a mapper.** Write the name down. This conveys mapping is expected and absolves the GM of providing maps.
2. **Explain the flowchart method.** Boxes and lines are all that's needed.
3. **If they refuse, don't force it** — but don't change the game either. If they get lost, it's on them.

### Narration Benefits
With a designated mapper, direct spatial descriptions specifically to them:

> "You enter a large chamber with a black obsidian altar. **Alice** [mapper], apart from the double doors you entered by, there's another set in the opposite wall. In each side wall, open archways lead out. **Bob** [caller], you notice statues in alcoves in the four corners. **Dave** [cleric], your eyes are drawn to a runic inscription on the altar."

This works because:
- The mapper tracks spatial details; others focus on descriptive/interactive details
- People are more attentive when addressed specifically
- Players ask each other for details instead of the GM
- The party doesn't pull the GM into deliberations as much

## Designing Maps for Player Mapping

### Simplify Your Own Maps
- Use straight corridors and rectangular rooms where possible
- Avoid convoluted, graph-paper-filling layouts with pointless winding passages
- If a corridor has no encounters or decisions in it, simplify it

### Every Room Needs a Distinguishing Feature
Empty rooms are fine (they help with pacing), but **bare** rooms are not. Every room needs one feature for labeling:
- Patches of purple mold
- Skulls and bones
- A candelabra in the corners
- An inch of water on the floor
- Graffiti, a red sarcophagus, etc.

This helps the mapper label rooms and recognize when they've entered the same room from a different direction.

### Handle Secret Rooms Explicitly
Without precise graph paper, players can't notice "extra space between walls." Instead, tell them explicitly after they've explored the surrounding area: "Something seems odd — the rooms in this wing don't seem to fill all the space." They still search and investigate, but the clue becomes a reward earned through exploration.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Appoint a mapper. Make it an expected part of the game.
2. Players map with flowcharts — boxes, lines, and labels. Not blueprints.
3. Player-drawn maps transform exploration from a checklist into intellectual conquest.
4. Direct spatial narration to the mapper; direct other details to other players.
5. Design maps that are flowchart-friendly: simple layouts, distinctive rooms, no pointless corridors.
6. Every room needs at least one distinguishing feature for labeling.
7. The GM's exploration map stays behind the screen. The players earn their own.
