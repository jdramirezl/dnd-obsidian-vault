# Better Narration Through Visualization: A Lifestyle Guide

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/narration-through-visualization/)
**Date:** January 31, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

TTRPGs are games of imagination! Or so every RPG book claims before burying you under math, probability, grids, and paperwork. Your job as the Game Master is to get a bunch of inattentive, hyperactive, selfish sociopaths to imagine the same thing you are. True Game Masters bring all the imaginations at the table in sync. Your only imagination synchronization tool is **Narration**.

## The Art of Narration

Your job is to act as the players' eyes, ears, and brains. Your campaign's world exists only in your head. You've got to tell the players everything their characters perceive and know. Consistency and clarity are key. Drop the flowery prose, be clear, and get to the point.

Narration comes in four flavors:
- **Scene-Setting Narration** — describing locations, situations, and events so players can decide what actions to take
- **Exposition Narration** — delivering factual information directly to players about what their characters know
- **Resolution Narration** — revealing the results of characters' actions
- **Transition Narration** — moving the action across time and space

## Setting a Scene in Two Easy Steps

Most GMs just open their mouths and vomit forth descriptive prose. But good Scene-Setting has two steps: **Scene Construction** then **Scene Description**. Most folks skip Construction and just start blurting out Descriptions.

Scene Construction means deliberately, purposefully imagining a scene before saying a single word about it. People skip it because it's hard, and because their imaginations are atrophied.

### Why Scene Construction Matters

Imaginations aren't holodecks. You can't close your eyes and find yourself immersed in a four-dimensional dreamscape. Instead, it's a hodgepodge of sensory impressions and mental associations — senses of color, pattern, texture, echoes of sounds, faint whiffs of odors. That's all you need. You don't need a conscious illusion; you just need an imaginary construct nudging the talking parts of your brain.

Once a scene has started, the more quickly and clearly you can answer questions and resolve actions, the better the game flows. A clear image in your head means clear, concise, consistent answers. Moreover, the more clearly you describe the world, the better players can predict likely outcomes of their choices — the key to making good, rational decisions.

### The Simple Solution: Breathe, Then Speak

Practice this habit: before you Set a Scene, **stop**. Don't open your mouth. Close your eyes. Take one long, deep breath — inhale through your nose, exhale slowly through your mouth. Open your eyes. Now start talking.

Do it every time. Make it consistent. The point is to give your creative brain one quiet second to think about the scene. When you start talking before your imagination wakes up, your creative brain is stuck listening to the words and imagining what you're describing. That's backwards. You want to describe what you imagine.

The habitual cue of closing your eyes and breathing deeply trains your brain to kickstart creativity. As a bonus, conscious slow breathing lowers pulse rate and stress levels — great for stage fright.

> **Text Boxes Kill Brain Cells:** You don't internalize ideas when you read text aloud. Your brain translates symbols into sounds without the other parts getting involved. If you must use Boxed Text, read it to yourself first, then summarize from memory.

## Better Scene-Setting Through Visualization

Visualization is just imagining really well — building an imaginary something in your brain. Despite the name, it's not purely visual. It's a feast for all the senses. Well, more like a menu with some pictures and the faint aroma of food wafting from the kitchen.

### The Visualization/Description Feedback Loop

Visualization and Description don't come sequentially — they feed each other in a loop. When you Visualize, your imagination piles up sensory impressions. When you Describe, you help that coalescing process. Your imagination seizes on the words your brain generates and uses them to clarify the Visualization. Which makes it easier to Describe. Round and round.

The more you Describe a scene and refer back to that Description, the clearer the Visualization becomes. Keep describing and redescribing scene elements as it plays out. Keep calling attention to the most important or forgettable elements.

> **Repetition Is Good:** If you want players to notice a detail or remember a scene element, repeat it at least three times. Your imagination is just as flighty as any player — it needs reminding too. If you don't mention the rain constantly, everyone forgets it's raining.

## Your Poor, Atrophied Imagination

Most people suck at Visualization because their imaginations have atrophied. You live in a world of constant sensory stimulation. You can't tolerate boredom for one second. Your creative imagination works best when the rest of your brain is quiet — daydreaming fills awkward brain silences. Constant noise means your creative imagination hasn't gotten out of bed in years.

### Resurrecting a Mostly Dead Imagination

You've got to make lifestyle changes:

1. **Limit sensory noise.** Stop the constant background podcasts and videos. Practice focusing on one thing. Replace audiobooks with lyric-free music if silence kills you.

2. **Set aside 10-15 minutes daily for boredom.** Sit quietly, gaze out the window, let your mind wander. Don't lie down, keep eyes open, don't stare. Boredom will hurt at first — your brain is an addict. Power through it.

3. **Read every day.** Carve out time exclusively for reading. It floods your brain with linguistic representations of sensory impressions — strength training for your imagination. Reading text is better than audiobooks, but audio works if it's all you can do (and you're only listening, not multitasking).

Do this for two weeks and your imagination will start getting out of bed.

## Your Imaginary Fitness Program

Three exercises — pick one, do it every other day (or daily) for two weeks:

### Exercise 1: Say What You Saw

Get a book of fantasy art (or print pictures — get away from the screen). Find a quiet place. Study a picture until you really know it. Close the book. Describe the picture out loud with as much detail as possible. When you run out or forget details, go back to the picture, study again, then describe more. 10-15 minutes per session.

### Exercise 2: Free Description Journaling

Get a notebook and writing prompts (random location tables from RPG books work great). Set a 15-minute timer. Generate a prompt, start the timer, and **Free Write** a description. Don't think too hard, don't edit, don't stop writing. Just put down every word that drifts through your brain. Focus on pure description. Eventually you'll spin out description from any prompt for 15+ minutes.

### Exercise 3: Build a Sensory Sanctuary

Construct a relaxing scene in your brain and learn to conjure it at will. Recall a moment when you felt content, relaxed, safe. Close your eyes. Anchor yourself: "vacationing with my family in Busch Gardens, Virginia on a warm summer evening."

Recall visual details. Then auditory. Then smell and touch. Then emotions. Sit with the scene, eyes closed. Open your eyes, look around, close them again — see if the scene is still there.

Write down everything about the scene afterward. Each time you return, set a 10-minute timer and reconstruct the scene. Over time, it'll just be waiting for you whenever you want to visit.

Every GM needs a happy place.
