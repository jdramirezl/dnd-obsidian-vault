# Dear GMs: Metagaming is YOUR Fault

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dear-gms-metagaming-is-your-fault/)
**Date:** July 22, 2016

---

## The Definition of Metagaming

A metagame is a set of rules and structures that lie outside of the rules of the game but still affect the game. In RPGs, the social contract is part of the metagame.

But in D&D, the word has been co-opted to mean: **metagaming occurs when a player makes a decision for their character based on information that the character doesn't have access to.**

Two basic forms:

### Player-on-Player Metagaming
The Asshole Paladin and the Asshole Thief. The thief is secretly evil and steals from the party. The players know but the characters don't. The paladin uses "detect evil" on the thief — something he'd never have done without player knowledge.

### Asshole GM Metagaming
The party encounters a troll. A player recognizes it and uses fire. The GM tells the player they aren't allowed to use fire because their characters have never seen a troll before and they are "ruining the challenge."

In either case: **a character is making a choice based on information that SOMEONE ELSE thinks the character shouldn't have.**

---

## Metagaming is Bullshit

It is IMPOSSIBLE not to metagame. Not hard — literally impossible.

The "strike it from the record" problem: if a judge tells a jury to ignore inadmissible DNA evidence, they can't. Even consciously trying to ignore it, they'll view everything else in the worst light. That's how human brains work. We can't pretend we don't know things.

Any attempt to control metagaming just creates a new game — the player now plays a game of trying to figure out when they're "justified" in saying their character has "discovered" the thing. **That's ALSO metagaming.** Trading one form for another.

---

## Metagaming is a Fever

Metagaming is like a fever — a sign of illness, actually a sign that your body is trying to fight illness.

**If you start having problems with metagaming, it's usually the result of some other problem in your game.** Most metagaming is the players trying to fix a problem.

---

## Secrets and Lies

The problem in player-on-player metagaming is **secrets**. Game-breaking secrets are a ticking time bomb. They are ALWAYS going to break the game.

The obvious question: "Why did the GM allow the paladin and the thief into the same game?"

The paladin thinks the party works together as a team. The thief thinks everyone is in it for themselves. Those expectations are completely incompatible.

**Player-on-player metagaming is only a problem where dangerous secrets are floating around. And dangerous secrets are a ticking time bomb.**

---

## Challenge Yourself

The GM creates a challenge where the party fights a regenerating creature. If the players open with fire, the GM thinks the encounter is "too easy" and the party hasn't "EARNED" a victory.

But there is **no way to figure out most challenges prone to metagaming.** How is a clueless player SUPPOSED TO figure out the troll's vulnerability? They just act at random until they stumble on it.

**A challenge that can be "broken" by a specific piece of information is a poorly designed challenge.**

The question: **"Does this challenge become MORE interesting if the players know the information or LESS interesting?"**

A single troll is boring if the players know its vulnerability. Unless fire is a limited resource. A cave full of trolls requiring resource management? Interesting. A troll shaman shielding allies from fire? MORE interesting. A mine with gas pockets that explode from fire? MORE interesting.

If you catch your players metagaming, it's a sign you messed up. Either make your game impossible to metagame OR make it so metagaming doesn't break it.

---

## The Realism Argument

Numbers in the game MEAN something. They exist as analogs for things that have a reality in the game world. A rock climber knows how to assess whether a climb is within their skills. The character knows things about their world that the player can only understand through numbers.

There's no good reason to assume characters don't know anything. Pop culture has existed since the dawn of time. Every Greek citizen who had seen a certain play knew the answer to the Sphinx's riddle.

**The characters in D&D don't grow up in a vacuum.** They have stories, myths, books, plays, songs, legends, and trivia. The bard class is PROOF that pop culture exists in D&D.

It is just as easy to argue that a character knows a random thing as it is to argue that they don't.

---

## Yelling About Metagaming Makes You a Dick

Most metagaming isn't problematic. It's only problematic because you have some messed up idea about how the game is supposed to work. The problematic metagaming that really DOES break something is a sign of another problem. Fix THAT problem. THAT problem is usually you.

**No matter how you slice it, metagaming is your fault.**
