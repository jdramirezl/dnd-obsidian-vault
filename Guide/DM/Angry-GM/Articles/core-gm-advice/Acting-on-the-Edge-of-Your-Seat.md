# Acting on the Edge of Your Seat

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/acting-on-the-edge-of-your-seat/) | **Category**: Core GM Advice

---

## Key Advice

- **Every action has a micro pacing curve:** decision → deliberation → moment of highest tension → resolution. The die roll should BE the climax, and the result should be instantly knowable the moment dice hit the table.
- **Post-roll math kills dramatic tension.** Adding modifiers, comparing to secret DCs, rolling damage after a hit — all of these extend the "held breath" moment past its natural breaking point and deflate excitement.
- **Do the math BEFORE the roll.** Calculate the target number the player needs, announce it, then let them roll. Instant success or failure recognition.
- **DCs and ACs should NOT be secret.** Players need target numbers to make informed decisions, and revealing them lets the die roll be the moment of truth rather than a prelude to more arithmetic.
- **Damage rolls are the real problem, not to-hit rolls.** The to-hit roll is in line with every other action resolution mechanic; the damage roll is the odd one out that adds a second, deflating resolution step after the climax.

## Summary

This exploratory article examines action resolution mechanics through the lens of dramatic pacing. Angry draws an analogy to the Borderlands video game, where early playtests felt sluggish not because the guns were weak, but because there was too much delay between pulling the trigger and seeing the result. The same principle applies to RPGs: every action has a natural pacing curve (decide, deliberate, hold breath, resolve), and the die roll should be the climactic moment where tension breaks. Anything that delays knowing the outcome after the dice land — adding modifiers, checking secret DCs, rolling damage — deflates that tension.

The article surveys various dice systems (d20, dice pools, roll-under, Yahtzee-style) and evaluates them against this pacing principle. Roll-under systems like GURPS and Call of Cthulhu score well because you know your target before rolling. The old THAC0 system, despite its reputation, also had this advantage. Dice pool systems like FFG's Star Wars are the worst offenders because they require extensive post-roll interpretation. The article argues that the ideal system front-loads all math and deliberation before the roll, so the moment the die hits the table, everyone at the table knows the result.

This has practical implications even if you're not designing a system. In D&D, you can improve pacing by revealing ACs and DCs, pre-calculating the number a player needs to roll, and considering whether damage rolls are worth the dramatic cost they impose. The broader lesson is that action resolution mechanics should follow the natural pacing curve of human attention and emotion.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Reveal ACs and DCs to your players.** Stop keeping target numbers secret. Characters in the fiction would have a sense of how hard something is; the numbers represent that knowledge.
2. **Pre-calculate target numbers.** When a player declares an attack, subtract their modifier from the AC and say "You need a 14." Let the die roll be the instant answer.
3. **Consider static damage.** If you want to experiment, try using average weapon damage instead of rolling. This eliminates the anti-climactic second roll after a hit.
4. **Minimize post-roll mechanics.** Avoid systems or house rules that require decisions after the die is rolled (spending resources to modify results, confirming crits, etc.). Front-load those choices.
5. **Watch your players' breathing.** Literally. Notice when they inhale before a roll and when they exhale after. If there's a long gap between the roll and the exhale, your resolution system has a pacing problem.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> The moment the dice hit the table, every person at the table should instantly know the outcome — front-load the math, reveal the targets, and let the roll be the climax.
