# Professor Angry's Office Hours: Dealing with Problematic Actions

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/angry-office-hours-1/)
**Date:** March 28, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

## Overview

A follow-up Q&A session clarifying the previous True Game Mastery lesson on "Dealing with Problematic Actions." Angry responds to comments, feedback, and Discord discussions about knowledge checks, rolling behind the screen, the Investigation skill, and how GMs should handle information delivery.

---

## Core Philosophy

True Game Mastery goes *beyond* running games by the book. RPG systems empower average people to run perfectly okay games — they're not designed to produce consistently great ones. Great games require judgment, intuition, and practice no rulebook can provide.

> "I can't give you a rule or definition, you need to use your own best judgment. And even if your judgment sucks right now, it gets better every time you use it."

---

## Knowledge Skills — The GM's Job

- The GM's **job** is telling players what their characters see, hear, perceive, and know — proactively, as part of narration.
- Players should never waste in-game actions "thinking really hard" to recall information.
- **How** you determine what characters know is up to you: roll behind the screen, use passive scores, ask for modifiers on the fly, or use judgment. The method doesn't matter.

**Example:**
> "You open the door and… hey, Adam, Ardrick is proficient in History, right? What's his modifier? Okay… so… as your torchlight spills into the gallery, it falls on a marble statue of a soldier in ancient Zethinian regalia. Ardrick recognizes the symbol as that of General Pevan Ardorus III…"

---

## The Investigation Skill in D&D 5E

- The PHB says Investigation lets characters make deductions — which is what Angry advises against.
- Angry used Investigation as-written in *The Fall of Silverpine Watch* because that module teaches running by the book.
- As a True Game Master: either excise the skill, or accept it as one of the system's warts. Every system has problems. Pick your battles.

---

## Describe Facts, Not Deductions — Leave Gaps

**The principle:** Describe what characters see, hear, perceive, and know — and not one iota more. Leave gaps for players to fill in through action or deduction.

**Bad:** "You deduce these people were killed by radioactive-bullet-shooting weapons."
**Good:** "The bodies are riddled with bullet holes. Red, swollen skin with blistering surrounds the wounds. Burns around the wounds, but no charring of hair or clothing — that suggests radiation burns."

> "Knowledge-type skills are basically just roll-for-an-answer mechanics. They're boring. They leave nothing for the players to do."

### Insight/Intuition — Same Principle

- **Bad:** "The NPC is lying."
- **Better:** "The NPC is nervous, cagey, dodgy."
- **Best:** "A single bead of sweat rolls down his forehead as he answers your question."

Categorical answers end investigation. Behavioral descriptions invite follow-up questions, probing, and deduction.

---

## Rolling Behind the Screen

- It's not hard. Just roll a die and add a modifier.
- If a player doesn't trust you to roll checks behind the screen, that player doesn't trust you enough to sit at your table.

---

## Handling Search Actions

How much detail must a player provide? **Enough that you can describe the action back and they agree that's what they meant.**

> "You're searching the room? You intend to ransack every corner and every piece of furniture, looking for anything interesting, valuable, hidden, or dangerous? That'll take about half an hour?"

Once the player agrees, they're stuck with whatever happens. No tap-backs.

---

## On Dungeon World's Spout Lore

Spout Lore is a clunky action at odds with Dungeon World's fiction-first philosophy — basically "stand around thinking really hard." "Hey, GM, I have a question about the world" is a perfectly valid interaction that doesn't need a mechanical wrapper.

---

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Proactively deliver information** as part of narration. Don't wait for players to ask.
2. **Describe facts, not conclusions.** Leave gaps for players to fill in.
3. **Roll knowledge/perception checks behind the screen** when it serves pacing or secrecy.
4. **Every system has warts.** Pick your battles and run games.
5. **Repeat back declared actions** and get player confirmation before adjudicating.
6. **Do your homework.** Know what things look like in your game world.
