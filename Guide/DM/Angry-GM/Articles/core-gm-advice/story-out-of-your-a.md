# I'm Making This Up As I Go: Pulling Story Out of Your A$%

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/story-out-of-your-a/)
**Date:** April 28, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Series:** Improve Your Improv (Part 3 of 3)

---

## Summary

The third part of the improvisation series, covering narrative improvisation — adding story elements (characters, scenes, lore, consequences) to the game on the fly. Angry argues that narrative improv is simultaneously "the worst thing a GM can do" and absolutely necessary. The article provides practical rules for doing it well: understand that good ideas are overrated, don't improvise when you don't have to, keep it small, know the purpose of everything you add, go with the flow when it's working, don't wreck your existing plans, write everything down, and have tools to cover your weaknesses.

---

## Key Points

### Narrative Improvisation Is Terrible (But Necessary)

- A good game is a well-structured machine. Narrative improv is like adding a toaster to a cuckoo clock on the fly.
- Creating under a deadline of "immediately" is extremely difficult. You have to be willing to censor nothing yet police everything for consistency, tone, and structure.
- But it's unavoidable: players ask unexpected questions, go unexpected places, and create consequences you didn't plan for. And sometimes you just have a cool idea.

### Why You End Up Improvising

1. **Players go off-script** — they ask questions, go places, or do things you weren't ready for.
2. **You notice problems** — pacing is off, players are bored, plot holes emerge, motivations are unclear.
3. **Consequences ripple** — player actions create outcomes that require new game elements later.
4. **You have a cool idea** — stupid but fun. 90% of Angry's narrative improv falls here.

### Rules for Good Narrative Improvisation

#### 1. Understand the Value of Good Ideas
- Good ideas are seriously unimportant. Crappy ideas are fine. No ideas are fine.
- For reactive improv, just do SOMETHING that isn't terrible. Start describing and let your brain catch up.
- "A good plan violently executed now is better than a perfect plan executed next week." — General Patton

#### 2. Don't Improvise When You Don't Have To
- If players aren't forcing your hand and you don't have an amazing idea, stick with your plan.
- Sometimes you have to ride out a fender bender rather than overcorrect into a guardrail.
- **GMs who brag about improvising everything are trying to ruin your game.** A well-planned game will ALWAYS be better than pulling everything from nowhere. Improv doesn't save work — it just adds time pressure.

#### 3. Improvise as Small as Possible
- Add the smallest story element that works. Need a combat? Goblins or rats, not a new faction.
- Need lore? Answer the question and stop. Don't invent entire organizations when all you need is an NPC.
- Don't try to build connections in the moment. That comes later.

#### 4. Know the F$&%ing Point
- Everything has a purpose. Adventures have motives. Encounters have dramatic questions. Scenes have a reason to exist.
- Before adding ANYTHING, know why it's there. If you don't know what it's doing in your game, it doesn't belong.
- This rule and "improvise small" push and pull at each other to keep improv focused.

#### 5. Go with the Flow
- Sometimes your brain starts producing genuinely good material — an NPC develops a backstory, a tribe gets a complicated history. Let it happen.
- But don't let creative spew extend past the element's purpose. If the shopkeeper is just a shopkeeper, cut it off no matter how interesting the backstory.

#### 6. Don't Wreck Your Plans or Your Game
- Don't change established reality based on player theories. "Listen to player theories and make one correct" is terrible advice.
- Everything you've built is driven by what you know is true. Going back on it weakens the threads of your reality.
- Wrong player theories are GOOD — they prove players have agency (they can be wrong) and add challenge to mysteries and investigations.

#### 7. Write Down Everything
- Keep a blank piece of paper at every game. Every new NPC, town name, lore detail — write it down immediately.
- After the session, review the list. Anything that could reach beyond its moment gets developed: answer unanswered questions, build connections, integrate into the world.
- **This is where small improv elements ripple through the game** — but it happens between sessions, not at the table.

#### 8. Know Your Weaknesses and Have Tools
- Improv amplifies weaknesses. Bad at names? Keep a list. Bad at combat encounters? Have the Monster Manual accessible. Can't draw maps on the fly? Have some ready.
- Pay attention to which improv moments scare you most — those are the ones you need tools for.

### Improvisation Is Okay

- It's necessary, it's fun, and it's what makes RPGs responsive to player choices.
- But it's a balance — not superior or inferior to planning. They're different tools for different situations.
- Over-reliance makes games harder to run and puts unnecessary pressure on you. Under-reliance makes games static and unresponsive.
- **No one can tell you the right balance. It's personal.**

---

## Core Principle

> View story improvisation like a rat views a trap: something to escape from as quickly and desperately as possible. Do the bare minimum needed, write everything down, and develop it properly between sessions.
