# GMs Have Secrets Too

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/gms-have-secrets-too/)
**Date:** February 4, 2019

---

## The Rule of Thumb for Secrets

> If the secret's revelation adds to the fun of the game for everyone, it's a good secret. If the revelation detracts from the fun or leads to PC-on-PC violence, it's a bad secret.

### Player Secrets

- Characters can have secrets from other characters
- But if the secret *must* stay hidden from other *players* or everything is ruined, it's a bad secret
- **Good:** A tomboy princess disguised as a male adventurer — other players could play along if they found out
- **Bad:** A secretly evil rogue in a party of good paladins — revelation leads to anger and PvP

### GM Secrets

- **Fudging die rolls:** If players would be pissed to learn you were doing it, you're bad and wrong
- **Monster knowledge / "metagaming":** If your encounter becomes boring once a player knows a troll's weaknesses, you designed a crappy challenge
- **Legitimate secrets:** Dungeon maps, murder mystery solutions, hidden Perception/Stealth rolls — these add tension

## Discovery ≠ Exploration

Discovery is the joy from **adding to your knowledge of the game** — and the game is bigger than just the game world. It extends to mechanics, character options, and system surprises.

### Three Illustrative Anecdotes

#### 1. Tharizdun in D&D 4E

The 4E designers hid Tharizdun — God of Madness and Destruction — from every player-facing book. Not in the PHB god list, not in Divine Power, not named in the Monster Manual. Only in the DMG and a later campaign supplement.

This gave every GM one secret evil god to spring on players — already part of the official cosmology, reserved for GM use.

#### 2. Hollow Knight's Power Progression

The expected "double jump" was withheld while unexpected powers (air dash, wall cling) were given instead. The game surprised the player by subverting genre expectations. The double jump came very late, after the player had forgotten about it.

The joy came as much from being surprised by unexpected abilities as from using the powers themselves.

#### 3. Secret Feats for a Pathfinder Fighter

A player's fighter was earning a mentor's trust. A standard Core Rulebook feat as "reward" was disappointing — the player could have just chosen it. But an obscure feat from a supplement the player hadn't read, presented as the mentor's secret technique? The player was thrilled.

Same trick worked for a wizard discovering "ancient dragon empire" spells from supplements — spells no NPC ever used except those connected to the ancient empire.

### The Key Insight

> Anything related to the game can be a discovery. Gameplay options — feats, spells, powers — can be discoveries just like anything else.

This is why magic items are in the DMG, not the PHB. They're more fun when players don't know the complete list.

## Secret Options: A Design Philosophy

If designing an RPG, Angry would put a portion of options — spells, feats, magic items, crafting recipes, summoned creatures, divine entities — in the GM Guide with the note: **"These can only be acquired during play."**

### What Goes in the Secret Stash

- Mostly little stuff — a few options no player would miss, slightly esoteric or specialized things
- A few big game-changers not every GM wants — like **resurrection magic**, giving the GM permission to decide how death works ("opt in instead of opt out")
- A grab bag reserved for the GM to use as rewards, discoveries, or in-game achievements

### The Circle of Trust

> "You're still part of a fraternity of secret keepers and game creators. We've agreed that these secret tidbits are entirely for you to use as you see fit. We won't spoil the surprise if you won't. Welcome to the Circle of Trust."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Test every secret** with the revelation rule: would knowing it add to or detract from the game?
2. **Discovery extends beyond the game world** — mechanical options and system surprises scratch the discovery itch
3. **Reserve some options for in-game discovery** — feats, spells, items are more exciting when players didn't know they existed
4. **Secret options must be balanced** — they need to fit alongside existing options at the same power level
5. **The GM deserves a secret stash** — curated options that can only be earned through play
6. **Opt-in beats opt-out** — better to give GMs permission to offer something than force them to ban it
