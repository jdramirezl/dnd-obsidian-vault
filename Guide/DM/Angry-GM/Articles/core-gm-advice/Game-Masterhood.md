# Game Masterhood

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/game-masterhood/)
**Date:** May 9, 2018

---

## Core Thesis

> Once you become a GM, you are, forever after, a GM. GMing is something you are, not just something you do.

Being a GM changes your perspective on the game in a fundamental way. Once shifted, you can't go back.

---

## Once a GM, Always a GM

GMing isn't just an activity — it's an identity. Like earning a degree or becoming a parent, it permanently changes how you see the game. You can go back and play, but you'll never NOT be a GM.

People who haven't experienced the shift say "I've run a few games, but I mostly play." But one day, something clicks. You start calling yourself a GM — and meaning it.

---

## When Do You Become a GM?

The moment you fully accept **responsibility and ownership** for the game — even though you don't have full control and may not be good at it.

### The Parenting Parallel

Becoming a GM involves two things:

1. **Responsibility** — You own the game. If you drop the ball, no one recovers your fumble. The game reflects on you.
2. **Power/Authority** — You have authority over other people and the game world.

It's the worst kind of responsibility: **you are fully responsible for something you have only partial control over.** You can do everything right and the game can still go bad.

> That's what it really means to take responsibility. You're going to do everything in your power to make something work, you may not have all the power you need, but you're going to own the outcome regardless.

---

## How GMing Changes You

Based on a Twitter poll of GMs:

### Positive
- **Respect and selflessness** — More respectful of other GMs, less focused on their own character, more focused on bringing other players in, less argumentative

### Neutral
- Better ability to assess a game's style and decide if it's a good fit
- More likely to walk away from games that don't suit them

### Negative
- Decreased tolerance for "bad GMing"
- Inability to distinguish "bad" GMs from "learning" GMs

> Perspective and competence are not the same things. Having keen eyesight helps you find your target, but you can still be a bad shot.

---

## GMs as Players: The Good and Bad

### The Good
- Respect and selflessness at the table
- Understanding of the GM's burden

### The Bad
- **The "Helper" GM** — Tries to figure out the GM's plan and play along, bending their character to what they think the story needs. This screws up responsive GMs who alter plans based on player choices.
- **The Backseat GM** — Reminds players of rules, makes calls, answers questions before the GM can, gets passive-aggressive when overruled.

> "I DON'T NEED YOUR HELP AND THIS GAME DOESN'T NEED TWO GMS! JUST PLAY THE DAMNED GAME!"

### The Pattern
Good GMs make good players. Poor GMs make poor players. The correlation is strong.

---

## The Other Side: Power and Authority

Power can corrupt. Once you exercise authority over your own game, you assume you know what's best. When things go wrong, authority becomes poisonous — you get overprotective and overcontrolling.

### The GM Who Doesn't Count

It's possible to become a GM for the wrong reasons:
- Living out your own dreams vicariously without caring what players want
- Running games because you can't get out of it, resenting every minute
- Enjoying the power while ignoring the responsibility

**This is NOT the same as being committed but bad at it.** The GM who genuinely thinks players will love their dream game can grow — if players make them see it isn't working, they'll care and try to do better.

> It's accepting responsibility for the game that makes you capable of growth.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **GMing is an identity, not just an activity** — it permanently changes how you see games
2. **The transformation is about accepting responsibility** — full ownership of something you only partially control
3. **Responsibility + Authority = the GM equation** — how you handle both determines what kind of GM you become
4. **Good GMs make good players** — the correlation is strong
5. **Growth requires accepting responsibility** — without it, you can't learn from mistakes
6. **Power without responsibility is poison** — it leads to selfish, controlling, or resentful GMing
