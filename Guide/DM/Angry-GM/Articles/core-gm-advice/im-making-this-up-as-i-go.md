# I'm Making This Up As I Go

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/im-making-this-up-as-i-go/)
**Date:** April 8, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Series:** Improve Your Improv (Part 1 of 3)

---

## Summary

The foundational article in Angry's improvisation series. Defines what improvisation actually is (designing in the moment, NOT just "making stuff up"), explains why it's simultaneously the most important GM skill and the most overvalued one, and introduces the critical concept that all improvisation sets precedent — meaning careless improv can destroy player agency by breaking the consistency of the game world.

---

## Key Points

### What Improvisation Actually Is

- **Improvisation = composing and performing without prior preparation.** It comes from the same root as "provision" — it means "not provided."
- It is NOT vomiting forth random ideas. It is designing encounters, scenes, and rules on the fly — with all the same structural requirements (dramatic questions, conflict, decision points) as planned content.
- **Improvisation is a Zen state:** action and thought are yoked together. Planning IS executing.

### Why Improvisation Is the Most Important Thing Ever

- RPGs are unique because a human brain stands in for the computer/book, providing infinite degrees of freedom for player choice.
- Without improvisation, an RPG is just a Choose Your Own Adventure with limited options.
- The GM must be ready to handle anything players imagine — actions not in the rules, directions not in the adventure, consequences not anticipated.
- **Angry's Law:** "If there is something you aren't ready to handle, players will do it."

### Why Improvisation Is Overvalued

- It seems like a panacea that eliminates prep work. GMs tell newbies to "just wing it" with a blank page and some names.
- But good improvisation doesn't save you anything — it requires all the same narrative and mechanical knowledge, just under extreme time pressure.
- **It's an ADVANCED skill that's required from day one.** New GMs should start with heavy planning and improvise in small doses.
- Most "improv advice" is useless aphorisms ("yes, and…", "no, but…", "rule of cool") that encourage mindlessness rather than actual design thinking.

### Improvisation as Precedent — How It Can Ruin Your Game

This is the article's most critical concept:

- **All choices depend on the ability to predict outcomes.** Players make decisions by imagining consequences based on their understanding of how the world works.
- **If the rules of the world change without reason, decision-making becomes impossible.** Players are forced to act at random, which destroys role-playing.
- The donut analogy: If donuts suddenly contain 600,000 calories and your friend Steve becomes a homicidal maniac overnight, you can no longer make rational choices about donuts or Steve.
- **Fiction is a fantasy of cause-and-effect.** Our brains crave consistency. Characters that behave randomly ruin stories. Rules that change randomly ruin games.
- **Every improvised element becomes part of the world permanently.** Players will use it to shape future decisions. It must fit everything that came before and will influence everything that comes after.

> Through improvisation, you are NOT inventing the world — you're revealing the world.

### The Two Kinds of Improvisation

1. **Mechanical Improvisation** — Making rules calls when the rules fail (weird actions, unexpected interactions, forgotten rules, stupid rules). Covered in Part 2.
2. **Narrative Improvisation** — Adding new story elements on the fly (scenes, characters, lore, consequences). Covered in Part 3.

Both rely on consistency and foreknowledge. The more you know about your game and world, the stronger your improvisation.

---

## Core Principle

> Improvisation is MORE than making stuff up. It's designing stuff the moment you need it. It's still design. And everything you improvise must maintain the consistency that makes player choice meaningful.
