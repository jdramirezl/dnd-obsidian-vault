# When to Stop Narrating

This article addresses a common GM struggle: how to handle protracted game actions — travel, tracking, exploring, crafting, rumormongering — that eat up lots of in-game time but don't involve meaningful decisions. The core concept is **Tab Stop Narration**.

---

## The Problem: Protracted Tasks

Many game actions share three traits:
1. They consume lots of **game time** (hours/days in-world) but not much **play time**
2. They don't involve **meaningful, impactful decisions** once started
3. Their outcomes can be described in **summary form** or with a single highlighted event

Examples: long-distance travel, tracking, town exploration, rumormongering, crafting, foraging.

Most GMs handle these poorly because modern gaming culture encourages acting out every single moment. Narration isn't a step-by-step process — it's responding to player input with blobs of text that contain the necessary elements (scene-setting, outcomes, transitions, invitations to act).

---

## Blobby Narration

Narration elements — Set the Scene, Invite a Player to Act, Describe Outcomes, Transition — aren't separate steps. They should come out as a natural **blob of narration** with all components woven in. Running an RPG is like running a text adventure: the player provides input, you spit out all the text needed until the next input is required.

> Delivering good Game Mastering narration is like puking a soup made of yarn onto your game table.

---

## Tab Stop Narration

Like pressing Tab in a form jumps to the next input field — no matter how far away — narration should jump to the next point where the players need to make a meaningful decision.

### The Process

1. **Players declare a protracted action** (e.g., "We travel to Castle Frazzledazzle")
2. **Gather all needed information** — ask about watch rotations, supplies, etc. Ask once; it won't change unless something forces it
3. **Resolve everything before narrating** — roll random encounters, navigation checks, tracking checks, etc. Pre-roll until you hit a failure or the destination
4. **Identify your next tab stop** — the next point requiring meaningful player input (a random encounter on day 4, a navigation failure on day 3, or the destination itself)
5. **Narrate the blob** — summarize everything from now to the tab stop in one flowing passage

### Example

> "You leave Bungusville and follow the dusty Castle Road east. Within a few hours, you've left the village and farms behind and find yourselves surrounded by gently rolling grass and scrubland. You travel for days, the scene much the same — mark off three days of rations, please — until midafternoon on the fourth day. That's when you find yourselves…"

---

## Narrative Pauses (Not Tab Stops)

For long, uneventful stretches, insert **narrative pauses** — brief moments that convey a sense of time and distance without requiring real decisions. These are "Press X to Continue" moments for pacing, not gameplay.

### What Pauses Do

- **Convey time/distance** — prevent the "fast travel" feeling
- **Give players space** to interact with each other or stay in character
- **Break up bookkeeping** — let players spot supply crunches before they become crises

### What Pauses Look Like

Describe camping the first night. Confirm watch rotation. Mark off rations. Apply long rest. "If there's nothing else before you break camp…? Okay, you continue on your way." Then narrate to the next tab stop or insert another pause a few days later.

### Pauses and Resource Management

True Game Masters have a rough idea of how long the party's supplies will last. Insert pauses for bookkeeping to give players a chance to see emergencies coming. If you cover two weeks of travel when the party only had eight days of food, you didn't give them the chance to spot the problem.

This applies to all protracted task resources: crafting materials, medical supplies, money for carousing/networking, etc.

---

## Pause for Players, Not for Dice

Pauses are for **pacing and player brains**, not for die rolling.

- Pre-roll random encounter checks at the start — they help set tab stops
- Pre-roll ongoing checks (tracking, navigation, crafting) until you hit a failure or the next tab stop
- **Resolve the entire action before narrating anything** — use the resolution to decide how to narrate

If the party gets lost on day 3, slow down narration at that point and go day-by-day so they can watch supplies dwindle. If a crafter ruins materials on day 3, that's a tab stop — they might want to cut losses or buy more supplies.

---

## The Full Process Summary

When players undertake a protracted task:

1. **Stop and think** — how much gameplay does this cover? When's the next meaningful decision?
2. **Gather information** — ask players what you need to know
3. **Roll all dice** — resolve everything needed to reach the next tab stop
4. **Consider the players' side** — anything they need to see coming? Build pauses before potential disasters
5. **Decide your narration approach** — tab stops, pauses, or segment-by-segment if it's a push-your-luck situation
6. **Only then narrate** — deliver your narrative soup, full of scene-setting and transitional yarn

The goal: skip moment-by-moment gameplay when the action doesn't demand it, without hurting the gameplay experience.
