# Dealing with Problematic Actions

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/problematic-actions/)
**Date:** March 21, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery

---

Today's lesson deals with truly problematic actions — actions that, for one reason or another, are just really hard to adjudicate: recognizing, recalling, examining, intuiting, spotting, searching, skulking, sneaking, speaking, schmoozing, sensing, and sympathizing.

Three core principles handle these — *The Three Laws of Game Mastering NonRobotics*.

Five broad action classifications illustrate them:

- Thinky, Facty Actions… or Non-Actions
- Spotty, Searchy Actions… and Non-Actions
- Sneaky, Skulky Actions
- Speaky, Schmoozy Actions… or Interactions
- Feely, Sensy Actions… and Non-Actions

> **Actions Ain't Skills** — Modern RPGs have screwed action adjudication by conflating actions with skills. Skills are game-mechanical tools used to resolve actions. "Ardrick hides behind the curtain" is an action. Skills are just modifiers you add to die rolls.

---

## The Three Laws of Game Mastering NonRobotics

### 1. Automatic Responses Aren't Actions

Actions are deliberate choices players make on behalf of their characters. If something ain't a deliberate choice, it ain't an action.

- **Actions are interactive.** If a character's not acting on something in the world, they're not taking an action.
- **Actions are external.** Anything happening only inside a character's head ain't an action.
- **Actions are visible.** If a character's not doing something clear and obvious on a movie screen, they ain't taking an action.

Anything automatic happens automatically.

### 2. Players Choose; Characters Act

Players choose intentions and approaches. Characters execute those actions. When the outcome's uncertain, it's the characters' capabilities at issue, not the players'.

Players are the character's reason and will and nothing else. Each player is trapped in a little room inside their character's brain.

### 3. True Game Masters Speak Only Facts

Tell players what their characters see, hear, perceive, and know. Nothing else. Don't tell players what their characters deduce, believe, feel, or conjecture.

A lot of things that look like facts are, in reality, deductions — it's vital you know the difference.

> **Don't Play the Game for the Players** — RPGs can only challenge players in terms of strategy, problem-solving, and deduction. If you don't let the players handle that, your RPG isn't a game at all. It's a luck-based activity. That's why you can't allow players to play characters smarter than themselves. Smarts is the only thing these games can test.

---

## Thinky, Facty Actions… or Non-Actions

Attempts to recall relevant information aren't actions. When characters encounter something they might know about — monsters, symbols, languages — True Game Masters determine what the characters know and tell the players. Immediately. When they Set the Scene.

When a player asks whether their character "recognizes a thing," respond: "I already told you everything your character knows; I always do."

Build trust by telling them *how* their characters know what they know:

> You see a strange, cat-like predator with six legs and tentacles. Because she studied the Foundational Arcane Texts, Beryllia recognizes it as a coeurl, a cunning predator that feeds on potassium and warps perceptions by emitting sonic vibrations.

---

## Spotty, Searchy Actions… and Non-Actions

Most sensory actions aren't actions at all — they're as automatic as recognition and recall. True Game Masters determine whether characters become aware of things and describe them accordingly.

To uncover things they can't passively perceive, characters must interact with the environment — move around, poke, prod, look under things, move things aside. They can't just "look around really hard."

**The big lesson: describe the signs of a thing instead of the thing itself.** Players never spot traps or secret doors or hidden goblins. They spot raised tiles, nearly invisible seams in brickwork, or the toes of hobnailed boots peeking out beneath tapestries.

All Spotty, Searchy Actions are ruined if the players see their die rolls. Players never roll their own Perception checks. Ever.

---

## Sneaky, Skulky Actions

Actions characters take to conceal the signs of their presence. They go hand-in-glove with Spotty, Searchy Actions.

Example cat-and-mouse encounter:

> Cabe ransacks the goblins' storeroom quietly. A goblin guard hears a noise and moves up the hall. Cabe hears scuffing of booted feet. He hides behind a tapestry and closes his lantern. The goblin enters, spots the open chest, and starts casting about the room. Cabe palms his dagger. The goblin moves toward the tapestry. Cabe leaps out and tries to stab the goblin before he can scream…

That entire stealth encounter arises from properly adjudicating one action at a time.

---

## Speaky, Schmoozy Actions

Three key points:

1. **Speaky Actions can't change a player-character's mind.** That'd require telling a player what their character believes or feels — against the Law.
2. **It doesn't matter whether players speak as their characters or describe their social actions.** Players just put ideas in their characters' heads.
3. **The outcome's down to the character's social aptitude and skills.** However inspiring the player's speech, that's just what the character thinks they sound like in their own head.

**Yes:** take into account the relevance of arguments, evidence, leverage, and emotions appealed to.
**No:** do not take into account eloquence, passion, or poetic turn of phrase.

---

## Feely, Sensy Actions… and Non-Actions

People-reading actions. Intuitive people spot emotional leakage — when someone's emotional state slips out in tone, expression, and body language incongruously with their actions or words.

An intuitive character might spot concealed anger behind a friendly handshake, hidden fear behind a sales pitch, or a totally bland emotional state behind a friend's invitation into a dark forest. It's down to the **player** — not the character — to deduce the ulterior motive, the blackmail, or the demonic spider in disguise.

---

## Eyes on the Prize

- **Automatic responses aren't actions.** Handle them automatically.
- **Players choose while characters act.** The character's skills and abilities matter, not the player's performance.
- **True Game Masters speak only facts.** Distinguish between the signs of a thing and the thing itself. Tell players the signs and let them deduce the thing.
