# How I Roll: The Angry Manual of Style

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/angry-manual-of-style/)
**Date:** July 14, 2021
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

This is a sort of statement of my core beliefs and a style guide. Basically, if I was the sort of GM who'd make my players read a bunch of bulls$&% about how I run my game and why I run it that way and what I expect from them — this is what I'd give them.

## The Tao of Angry

### The Big Triad: Agency, Consistency, and Engagement

I devoted an entire chapter of my book, *Game Angry: How to RPG the Angry Way* to these three things. I called them *the Hearts of the Game.* The players must have agency because role-playing is all about making choices. The world must be consistent, understandable, and persistent so that the players can make good, rational choices and so that the consequences of those choices follow logically. And because the *g* stands for *game,* an RPG must supply an engaging gameplay experience.

### Think First, Then Rules

TTRPGs aren't possible without a brilliant and infinitely imaginative human brain running the show. Whenever I have to do anything at the game table, I turn to my brain first and trust it to figure out how to run things. Whenever I turn to the rules, that's a deliberate choice to use those specific tools — the game's mechanics are tools — to resolve a particular situation. The rules help my brain run the game. But my brain runs the game.

### The World Is What It Is And I Decide What It Is

The game takes place in a consistent, persistent, understandable fictional world. That world cannot be ignored. And it can't be broken. It's my job to keep it persistent, consistent, and unbroken. If anything threatens the world — be it a stupid rule, a ridiculous abstraction, or a player's idiot wants — the world wins.

### It's All About the Action On the Screen

Like movies, TV shows, and video games, RPGs communicate through the medium of action. Everything's about what the characters do. Choice is just a precursor to action. An action is a choice expressed. The s$&% going on in the character's heads? It doesn't matter except insofar as it informs the actions the characters take.

### I'm the Character's Eyes, Ears, and Brains

The world of the game only exists in **my** head. To let the players project their characters into it, I have to give them all the information they need to build a copy of the important bits of my head world in their own brains. I have to be clear, concise, accurate, and honest.

### Players Shouldn't Have to Ask Questions

The players have to know what's in their character's brains at all times. They shouldn't have to ask me for information. If they do have to ask, I screwed up as *eyes, ears, and brains*.

### The Players Must Bring Their Brains

An RPG is a game. The players — not the characters — ultimately determine the outcome. You can't leave the player out of the player-character. The player's own mental skills — deduction, logic, strategy, planning, attentiveness — they're all part of the game. Characters don't win the game, players do.

### Characters Gather Information and Execute Actions; Players Draw Conclusions and Formulate Strategies

A firm dividing line between player skill and character skill: The world feeds information to the character's brain through senses and perceptions and experiences. That information gets passed to the player's brain. The player assembles it into a plan of action. The player feeds that plan back to the character. The character executes it.

That's why it's the player that decides *what to say* to the NPC but the character that determines how well it's said and whether it works. That's why players can't have their characters solve mysteries just by rolling dice. Players can play characters more charismatic than they are but not smarter than they are.

## Regulae Meae

### Rules of Dice

**When I Need a Die Roll; I'll Ask For It.** Your job is to decide what your character is trying to accomplish and what specific action they're undertaking. My job is to determine the outcome. If I need a die roll, I'll ask for it. I'll decide what to roll, when, what it means, who rolls, and who sees the results.

**Background Rolls and Passive Checks.** Whenever your character runs into a situation where their eyes, ears, magical senses, previous experiences, memories, or education might provide information, I will provide it using passive scores, Intelligence checks, etc. If you think there's something to perceive or know and I didn't tell you, your character probably didn't perceive it. You'll need to take a real, visible action to find out more. Things that aren't actions: looking around really hard, being really attentive, thinking really hard, trying to remember anything.

**Some Proficiency Required.** There's some things you can't attempt — or know — without proficiency. Lots of actions and almost all specialized knowledge are gated behind proficiency requirements. Not even with a natural 20.

**You Can't Help with Everything.** Some actions benefit from an extra pair of hands. Some don't. When people team up, sometimes they aren't helping each other so much as each trying to succeed on their own.

**Your Character Can't Invent a Solution.** You will never be allowed to roll a die to determine whether your character can create a solution to a problem on their own. Your character can supply information and execute plans. But you've got to solve the mysteries and puzzles.

### Rules of Action

**You Don't Have to Put on a Show.** As long as you're clear and concise and give me all the information I need, I don't care how you deliver it. You don't need to speak in character or put on an accent. It's okay to say, "I threaten to blackmail him" or "I spin a sob story to lower the price."

**Declare the Smallest Possible Action.** Just tell me your character's current intention and action. If the words 'and then…' or 'and if that works, I'll…' come out of your mouth, **stop**.

**Don't Pontificate; Don't Broadcast Your Thoughts.** If you play your character consistently and make good decisions, others can gradually come to know what's happening inside your character's head. That's how real people learn about other people.

**If You Want to Communicate, Communicate!** There's a simple action players never seem to remember: **talking**. Your characters can pass information to other characters provided they're within earshot and share a common language.

**Respect the Clock.** The game's action is playing out in my head, synchronized and on the clock. If you search a room for fifteen minutes, Bob has time to take three five-minute actions. In combat, a round is six seconds. If you take too long to think or try to give a speech, I'm going to cut you off.

**Your Character, Your Action.** Your character is yours alone. You cannot role-play by committee. If you solicit other players for help deciding your action, I will stop you.

### Rules of Information

**Pay Attention.** I take my role as "eyes, ears, and brain" seriously. I'm detailed, repetitive, careful with narration, and I telegraph everything. I assume characters share useful information as soon as they can. I hate recapping — so pay attention.

**Metagame.** The human brain cannot pretend it doesn't know something. Bring in whatever outside knowledge you've got — I'm demanding you do it. But information from outside the game world is risky; it could be right or wrong. I'm never going to turn outside information against you, and I won't let you hold ridiculously wrong false beliefs.

**I've Got Your Back.** I'm on your side. I will never act with malice, never try to screw you, never act arbitrarily. I will warn you when you do something dangerous or stupid. On the day I kill your character, you will be awake, you will be facing me, and you will be armed. Otherwise, there's just no honor in the kill.
