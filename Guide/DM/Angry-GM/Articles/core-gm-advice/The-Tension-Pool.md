# The Tension Pool

## Key Advice

The Tension Pool is a simple, system-agnostic mechanic that creates rising tension during slow-paced gameplay (exploration, investigation, downtime) by making **time-wasting** and **reckless actions** carry tangible risk. It uses 6d6 and a visible container to impose a psychological sense of urgency that players can't help but respond to — even when they try to "game" it.

## Summary

The Tension Pool works on two classifications of player actions:

- **Time-Consuming actions** → Add a die to the pool (represents time passing)
- **Reckless actions** → Roll the pool (checks for a Complication)
- **Both** → Add a die, then roll
- **6th die added** → Roll and clear the pool (the "hour" resets)

If any die shows a **1** on a roll, a **Complication** occurs — an unexpected setback, encounter, hazard, or narrative development that costs the party resources or time. Complications should be annoyances, not disasters, and players should see the causal link between their actions and the consequence.

The **Time Pool** variant ties the pool to specific time scales (minutes/hours, hours/days, days/weeks) for more precise time-tracking. Each die represents a fraction of the pool's total time unit.

The mechanic works because it mirrors real human psychology around time awareness, risk assessment, and stress — tricking players into behaving more like their characters would under actual pressure. Players who "game" the system are actually role-playing more authentically.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Get 6d6 and a transparent container** — place it visibly in the center of the table so all players can see and hear dice being added
2. **Classify actions during exploration/investigation** — ask yourself: is this Time-Consuming? Reckless? Both? Neither? Most actions are neither
3. **Add a die loudly** for Time-Consuming actions — one die per action, allow simultaneous actions from other players during the same time window
4. **Roll the pool** for Reckless actions — if any die shows 1, introduce a Complication; if the pool is empty, roll one die but don't add it
5. **Clear on the 6th die** — always roll before clearing; never remove dice without rolling
6. **Pause the pool during encounters** — combat, chases, and negotiations have their own pacing; the pool is for the slow parts
7. **Prepare a Complication table** — organize by rarity (Common/Uncommon/Rare/Very Rare) and roll 1d8+1d12 to determine severity
8. **Don't modify the core mechanic** — get creative with Complications instead; build unique tables per adventure site or scenario
9. **For time-tracking (Time Pool)** — set a scale (Minutes/Hours for dungeons, Hours/Days for town, Days/Weeks for downtime) and add dice as time passes

> [!tip] The Tension Pool's power is psychological, not mechanical
> Don't hide the pool or roll behind the screen. The visible, audible act of dropping dice into a glass bowl is what creates tension. Players must see the connection between their choices and the rising risk. The pool trains players to value efficiency and caution without you ever having to lecture them about it.
