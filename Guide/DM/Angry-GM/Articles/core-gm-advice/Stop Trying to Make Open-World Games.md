# Stop Trying to Make Open-World Games

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/stop-trying-to-make-open-world-games/)
**Date:** July 31, 2024
**Category:** Random Bullshit (Part 1 of 2)

---

## Overview

Every time a new open-world video game drops, GMs want to recreate that experience at the tabletop. This article argues why that's a bad idea, why it's impossible, and why you don't need to. Part 1 covers the problems; Part 2 covers the good news.

---

## The Five-Part Answer

1. What do you even mean by "real open-world campaign"?
2. How do you think you can pull it off?
3. You really shouldn't do that
4. You can't
5. Fortunately, you don't have to

---

## 1. What Do You Actually Want?

Strip away the analysis and what GMs really want is:

> "I loved my time in *that game's* world and I want to give my players that same joy as they explore my world."

The Open-World Experience: the game drops you in a world and invites you to wander at your own initiative, undirected. Go where you want, do what you want, in any order. Accept or reject quests as you desire.

---

## 2. The Naive Plan

Most GMs would build a big world, scatter interesting POIs, let players discover them, and play out explorations. The obvious holes: massive up-front prep, and the "discovery problem" — how do players actually *find* things to do at the table?

---

## 3. Why You Shouldn't

### Pacing Problems
Long stretches of wandering looking for something to do. No urgent goals pulling players forward. If you're not intrinsically motivated by exploration, you notice the bad ratio of wandering to doing.

### Balance Problems
Player-directed games let you go anywhere while gaining power. Hit challenges too early → frustration. Too late → boredom.

### Complacency
The longer you play, the more you skip or trivialize incidental content.

### The Hidden Design Layer
Open-world video games use hidden tricks to make the experience work:
- *Breath of the Wild* funnels players along certain paths with ~90% effectiveness using terrain and line-of-sight
- *Elden Ring* uses funneling, chokepoints, and gating — barely open at all
- Minimaps and Ubisoft towers were early solutions to the "sandbox problem"
- Modern games ensure something to see within minutes of choosing to wander

> There's a lot more to *that game* than plonking fun stuff on a big map. Most people would get bored in a *real* open world.

### Less Fun with Friends
Most open-world games are solo. Team-based games need strong goals to unify the team. Group decision-making about "where next" bogs down into committee debates.

---

## 4. Why You Can't

### You Can't Design It
You can't build the whole world up front. You'll end up faking it with prep tricks, limiting options to 2-3, or manipulating players into the one adventure you want. At some point you're not building an open world — you're faking one.

### You Can't Waste Time Wanderin'
Players value RPG time differently. Video games feel like there's always another hour later. At the table: "If we don't get anywhere tonight, we wait a whole week." A half-hour of climbing a mountain is fine in a video game; it's a rebellion at the table.

### You Can Only See the World in Your Mind's Eye
TTRPGs are narrative, not visual. Video game exploration tests your perceptiveness — you notice details with your own eyeballs. At the table, the GM describes what catches your character's attention. Having details pointed out feels completely different from noticing them yourself.

> You cannot capture the experience of wandering a world and noticing interesting things at the RPG table. The medium can't do it. Without it, you can't make a campaign feel like *that game*.

---

## 5. You Don't Have To (Part 2)

> You can capture a similar spirit of open-world adventure without doing anything special. You're probably already doing it.

---

## Key Takeaways

- The desire is about capturing a *feeling*, not recreating mechanics
- Open-world video games have serious problems that designers spend years mitigating with hidden tricks
- Those problems are **worse** in TTRPGs
- The core open-world experience — visual exploration and self-directed discovery — is something the TTRPG medium fundamentally cannot do
- Good open-world games give you the *perception* of freedom that a good GM already provides — an open-world campaign is "a copy of a copy"
- The answer is to use the tools of your own medium
