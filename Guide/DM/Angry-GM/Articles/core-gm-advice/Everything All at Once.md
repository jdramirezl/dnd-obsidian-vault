# Everyone Doing Everything All At Once

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/everything-all-at-once/)
**Date:** April 28, 2023
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## The Problem: Temporal Anarchy

Outside of combat's imposed turn order, characters in RPGs are doing whatever whenever. Actions happen simultaneously, overlap, interrupt each other. NPCs and game events jump in with no regard for sequence.

A True Game Master must maintain the illusion that players aren't playing a game but exploring an actual imaginary otherworld. Unless you have no other choice, don't impose artificial constraints. Characters must be free to act whenever they wish — jump into conversations, rifle through chests while the rogue picks a lock, go shopping while the paladin meets the king.

But this chaotic temporal anarchy is hard to run. You must take the chaos and weave it into a playable game and satisfying story. That comes down to three things:

1. **Putting events in sequence** — our brains need cause-and-effect order
2. **Keeping the world's clock running** — time has mechanical effects and the world must change
3. **Knowing where everyone is and what they're doing** — you must always have all information to resolve anything

## Three Superpowers

### 1. The Power to Not Resolve Things as They Happen

You can break up the Declare-Determine-Describe Cycle. Put it on hold between the *Declare* and *Determine* steps.

> **GM:** Cabe steps into the room, heading toward the chest. Adam, as Cabe enters the room, what does Ardrick do?

Instead of immediately resolving Chris's action, freeze time and invite the next player to act.

### 2. The Power to Describe Inceptions and Intentions

Describe characters *initiating* their actions — freeze time a split-second after they start.

> **GM:** The orc tightens his grip on his falchion and snarls. He shifts into a combat stance. He's going to attack. Adam, what does Ardrick do?

This creates space for players to react.

### 3. The Power to Leave Action Bits on the Cutting Room Floor

Nothing happens until you describe it. Players declare long, complex actions, but situations change. By only describing the START of actions, you give players the chance to pivot when things change.

> **Chris:** I cross the room to the chest, search it for traps, and pick the lock.
> **GM:** Cabe steps into the room, heading toward the chest. Adam, what does Ardrick do?

Everything after "steps toward the chest" is just a plan in the character's head — not locked in.

## The Action Queue

The key technique for managing simultaneous actions outside of combat.

### How It Works

1. **Introduce the Encounter** — Set the scene, invite the principal character to act
2. **Declare** — The principal character's player declares an action
3. **STOP** — Don't resolve it yet. Freeze the world at the first step of the action
4. **Invite the next player** — Describe the frozen moment, ask what their character does
5. **Repeat** — Keep going until every character has an action queued (even if it's "wait")
6. **Resolve** — Unfreeze and resolve actions in logical order, re-inviting players to act as their actions complete

### Critical Rules

- **Never resolve any action until you know where every character is and what they're doing**
- **Never let a player queue up more than one action** — just drop the extra declarations
- **Never let a character float without an action** — even "watch and wait" counts
- **Nothing happens until you describe it**
- After resolving an action, immediately invite that player to queue a new one

### Taking Notes

Keep a scratch pad. Scrawl shorthand: "A lookout" and "C chest." This is why requiring every character's name to start with a different letter is useful.

## For Advanced GMs: The Action Clock

Instead of a simple queue, experienced GMs use an intuitive "Action Clock" — a mental clockface with fuzzy increments appropriate for the current action (10-minute increments for dungeon delving, 1-4 hour increments for town activities).

Five considerations for ordering resolutions:

1. **In-world time** — don't resolve a 30-minute action before a 5-minute one
2. **Likelihood of follow-up actions** — chest search leads to lockpicking, handle early
3. **How protracted the resolution will be** — bookshelf examination involves questions and description
4. **Likelihood of pulling everyone's attention** — treasure discovery draws the whole party
5. **Likelihood of wrecking the encounter** — a mimic attack ends exploration, starts combat

**Goals:**
- Keep things dynamic — bounce from character to character
- Actions with follow-ups come early, bounce away, then return
- Extended resolutions go in the middle
- Ongoing actions ("Ardrick remains vigilant") are convenient bounce points
- Avoid characters getting committed to things they'll be distracted from

## Key Takeaway

Good pacing trumps a fast pace every time. The Action Queue leads to more attentive, more involved players — even when some characters don't have much to do. Players are more likely to jump in because the queue gives space for everyone.

Start running every encounter with an Action Queue. Get it down cold. Then experiment with the art of Time Management.
