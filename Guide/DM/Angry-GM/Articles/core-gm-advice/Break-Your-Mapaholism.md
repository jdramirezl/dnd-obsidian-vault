## Key Advice

- **Maps have a cost.** Prep time, table time, and opportunity cost. Every map you make is time not spent improving encounters, sleeping, or living your life.
- **Not every combat needs a tactical map.** A fight against a single ogre in open terrain gains almost nothing from a grid. Save maps for fights that earn them.
- **Classify encounters as Minor, Major, or Climactic.** Minor encounters never need the grid. Major encounters usually do. Climactic encounters always do. This makes pulling out the battlemat feel significant.
- **Overusing maps zooms players out of their characters.** Combat becomes a strategic chess game viewed from above. Players stop imagining the world through their characters' eyes.
- **Running some fights without maps keeps players grounded** in their characters' perspectives and makes the mapped fights feel more important.
- **Exploration maps are actively harmful.** They make dungeons feel small and simple, rob players of the wonder of discovery, and train them to stop listening to narration.
- **Let players draw their own maps.** It gives them agency, intellectual conquest, and lingering mystery about whether they got it right.
- **Your narration skills atrophy when you rely on maps.** Combat is the most narratively demanding thing you run. Without regular practice narrating fights, you get worse at it.
- **The more visual aids you provide, the less players listen.** This is a documented neurological pattern—over-reliance on visuals degrades auditory retention.

## Summary

Maps are a tool with real costs: prep time, table time, and a hidden cost to immersion and narration skill. Tactical maps enhance complex fights but zoom players out of their characters' heads, turning combat into a board game. Not every fight benefits from a grid—many combats are simple enough that a map adds nothing but overhead. Angry recommends classifying encounters as Minor (no map), Major (usually mapped), or Climactic (always mapped), so the battlemat itself becomes a dramatic signal. Exploration maps are worse: they make dungeons feel small, kill the wonder of discovery, and train players to stop listening. Instead, let players draw their own maps for agency and mystery. Running fights without maps regularly keeps your narration skills sharp and your players engaged.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Before every combat, ask: "Does this fight need a map?"** If the answer is "not really," run it theater-of-the-mind.
2. **Classify your encounters during prep.** Label each as Minor, Major, or Climactic. Only Major and Climactic get maps.
3. **Aim for roughly half your session's fights to be mapless.** This keeps players in-character and makes mapped fights feel special.
4. **Stop providing exploration maps to players.** Describe the dungeon narratively and let players draw their own maps if they want one.
5. **Practice narrating combat without visual aids.** Start with simple fights and build up. Describe positions, terrain, and threats from the characters' perspective.
6. **Use setting maps sparingly.** A single reference map on the table is low-cost, high-value. A full VTT dungeon with fog of war is high-cost, negative-value for immersion.
7. **Watch for signs of mapaholism:** players not listening to descriptions, asking you to repeat things constantly, or disengaging when there's no visual on screen. These are symptoms.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Maps are a tool, not a default. Use them deliberately for fights that benefit from tactical precision, and run everything else narratively. Your narration skills will sharpen, your players will stay in their characters' heads, and pulling out the battlemat will become a dramatic moment instead of routine.
