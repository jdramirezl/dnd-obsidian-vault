# Lost Player Skills: Calling

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Lost Player Skills: Calling](https://theangrygm.com/lost-player-skills-calling/)
**Date:** December 2, 2019
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Related:** [[Lost Player Skills - Mapping]]

---

## Summary

The "caller" was a player role described in early D&D rules. The caller was the player who communicated the party's collective decisions to the GM. Angry argues this forgotten role solves multiple modern table problems: the "we do that" problem, diffusion of responsibility, poor teamwork, and chaotic group decision-making.

---

## The "We Do That" Problem

After extended deliberation, players often turn to the GM and say "okay, we do that" — expecting the GM to have tracked every detail of their discussion, separated action declarations from discarded ideas, and filled in blanks. This is unreasonable and leads to miscommunication.

What follows is the GM having to review all actions, repeat them back, and get confirmation — often revealing that even the *players* aren't clear on the plan.

---

## A Team Is Not a Group of Individuals

**The test:** Ask each player privately if they can state:
- Race, class, and background of each other character
- Class build choices of each other character
- Two unique combat abilities of each other character
- Two skill proficiencies of each other character
- Top two best and worst ability scores of each other character
- One-sentence personality, one-sentence goal, and one backstory detail for each other character

The point isn't a perfect score — it's gauging how much interest each player has in the other players at the table. There's a visible trend of players acting as a group of individuals rather than an actual team.

---

## What Is a Caller?

The caller was the player allowed to talk to the GM during collective action. Outside of combat and direct social interaction, the party would discuss amongst themselves, then the caller would present the final plan:

> "Alice is going to keep lookout, Bob is going to search that treasure chest for traps, and Carol and I are going to look through the bookshelf."

The caller is NOT a leader. The caller is a **foreman** — they package the group's decision for GM consumption. The word "caller" is deliberately chosen over "leader" because the role carries no authority, only responsibility for communication.

---

## Benefits of Using a Caller

### 1. Separates GM from Deliberation
Players don't assume the GM is listening during planning. This reinforces the conversational tennis of RPGs: GM sets scene → players decide → caller communicates → GM resolves. It preserves player sovereignty and agency.

### 2. Makes Simultaneous Actions the Norm
Without a caller, GMs resolve one action at a time as players blurt things out. With a caller, everyone's time must be accounted for before the GM resolves anything. This prevents scrums, makes timekeeping easier, ensures no player is forgotten, and teaches players they can split up and act simultaneously.

### 3. Prevents Dominant Players from Monopolizing
Without a caller, the fastest talker acts first. With a caller, no one can trump everyone by ringing in first. The party must reach consensus.

### 4. Fosters Teamwork
Nobody acts until everyone acts. This reinforces that each character is part of a team, not an individual taking individual actions. Players start thinking of themselves as a unit even during combat and social interaction.

### 5. Gets Players Talking to Each Other
Without a caller, the game becomes five separate conversations between individual players and the GM. With a caller, the normal mode is players talking to *each other*. The GM is the narrator and referee, not a collaborator in the heroes' decisions.

## Diffusion of Responsibility

Group decision-making is hard because of **diffusion of responsibility** — when humans are part of a group, nobody feels individually responsible. Nobody wants to be the one who says "okay, this is the decision" because that person becomes accountable if it goes wrong.

The caller short-circuits this. Even though the caller's explicit duty is just to pass along decisions, the fact that they're singled out means they can't hide in the amorphous blob of the group. They naturally start:
- Imposing structure on discussions
- Summarizing choices and calling for votes
- Proposing compromises
- Keeping the party on task

This is the same principle as pointing at someone specific in an emergency: "You, in the blue jacket, call 911!"

---

## Accountability and Team Resilience

The caller might fear accountability for bad decisions. But:
- Well-adjusted people already feel responsible when things go wrong, regardless of role
- The teamwork dynamic the caller fosters ("we succeed or fail as a team") actually *protects* against individual feelings of blame
- The stronger the team identity, the better equipped the group is to handle and recover from failure

---

## Implementation Notes

- The caller is a **player** role, not a character role. The caller doesn't need to be the in-game leader or face.
- Works outside of combat and direct social interaction — situations where the party takes collective action.
- During combat or when the party is spread out, the GM interacts directly with individual players as normal.
- The GM should not accept "okay, we do that." The response is: "Sorry, what do you do?" directed at the caller.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Appoint a caller — not a leader. The caller communicates the party's collective decisions to the GM.
2. The caller solves the "we do that" problem by ensuring decisions are packaged clearly before the GM acts.
3. The caller fosters teamwork by making "nobody acts until everyone acts" the default.
4. The caller short-circuits diffusion of responsibility by singling someone out.
5. The caller gets players talking to each other instead of each having separate conversations with the GM.
6. The caller prevents dominant players from monopolizing action.
7. The GM stays out of deliberation. The GM is the narrator and referee, not a collaborator in the party's decisions.
