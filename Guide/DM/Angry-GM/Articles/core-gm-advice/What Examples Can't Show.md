## Examples of Play and Gamecasts

### What They Do Well

- Demonstrate the conversational nature of RPGs
- Show the bouncing between narration and resolution
- Illustrate the tightrope between controlling characters and being characters
- Provide helpful introductions to the hobby

### What They Can't Do

- **They can't let you see inside the GM's head.** The Alchemy of Game Mastery is about making good choices in ambiguous situations. Examples of Play show the *results* of those choices, not the *process*.

### Boardgaming Does It Better

Boardgaming Gamecasts come in multiple flavors:
- Entertainment Gamecasts
- Instructional Gamecasts (explain the game as it's played)
- Rules presentation Gamecasts (substitute for reading the rulebook)

TTRPG Gamecasts are almost exclusively entertainment. The industry lucked into them as viral marketing and doesn't know how to use them deliberately as teaching tools.

## The Alchemy of Game Mastery

### Game Running vs. Game Mastering

**Game Running** is easy — executing rules and describing results. Anyone can do it.

**Game Mastering** is what you do when the rules aren't enough. It comes down to answering one or more of three questions:

1. **"How can I adjudicate this action?"**
2. **"How can I present this result?"**
3. **"How does this result change the game's world?"**

A great GM relies on wisdom, intuition, and experience to answer these questions quickly — coming up with a great, good, or merely satisfactory answer and acting on it in the blink of an eye.

### GMing Is an Alchemy

- Part science, part art, part mysticism
- Deeply subjective and emotional
- Relies heavily on intuition
- Can only truly be learned through experience and experimentation
- The best way to get good is to run a lot of bad games

But a good teacher can give students a head start — help them skip early experimental phases. Each generation starts a little further along the curve.

## The Vision Problem

### Why Vision Matters

Most RPG GMing advice is **behavioral** — it focuses on what the GM *does*. It's not **visionary** — it doesn't aim the GM toward anything.

> What does it mean to be a Game Master? What does a good GM look like? What separates a Game Master from a Game Runner?

This question must be answered with a clear, concise statement of vision — a motivation, not a list of behaviors. It has to drive choices.

### Why Designers Fail at This

Most designers don't try to figure out what a GM *is* on a philosophical level. They know what a GM *does* and assume that's enough. But:

- Vision is vital for intuitive, experiential alchemies
- You need a vision to know how to answer the questions you face
- Neophytes can't invent their own vision — it must come from a teacher or system
- As neophytes approach mastery, they'll replace the received vision with a personal one

### The Homework

The article ends by asking readers to reflect:

> What does it mean to be a Game Master? What does a good GM look like? What separates a Game Master from a Game Runner?

The answer should be a clear, concise statement of vision — a motivation that drives choices, not a list of behaviors.

## Notable Comment (Angry-Endorsed)

**BurgerBeast's answer:**

> "The GM should try to be as unnoticeable as possible during play."
>
> Every decision the GM makes should seem so natural to the context that the characters would have probably predicted it. This encompasses things only the human brain can handle — it takes into account the players, the story/genre/tone, and makes the rules into tools applied exactly as expected. The goal is to minimize the presence of "players" at the table by creating an experience that feels like there are only characters and a world.

Angry's response: "I REALLY like this."

## Summary

> Examples of Play and Gamecasts are great introductions but can't teach the Alchemy of Game Mastery — the art of making good choices in ambiguous situations. What's missing from GM education is a clear Vision of what it means to be a Game Master: not a list of behaviors, but a motivation that drives choices. Without that vision, you're just groping in the dark.
