# How Can This Be True?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-can-this-be-true/)
**Date:** February 19, 2016

---

"Where do you get your ideas?" — the world's second oldest question.

All GMs who run regular games are content creators. Unpaid, unprofessional content creators. And there are two types: those who update randomly and infrequently (and eventually hemorrhage fans or burn out), and those who crank out regular content reliably. The difference comes down to one magic word: **inspiration** — and whether you believe in it.

## The Myth of Inspiration

We believe in this magical idea called Inspiration — that ideas are floating around in the universe and occasionally get sucked into your brain. It's bullshit. Some people's brains are wired a little better for absorbing ideas and churning them around, but that's just natural human perception, pattern recognition, and associative thinking at work. Everyone has it.

The only difference between people who wait for inspiration and people who crank out content on a deadline is that the content creators **don't believe in inspiration**. They don't wait for idea fairies to fly up their nose.

## How Do You Force Yourself to Create Ideas

You can't. You can't just squeeze the right muscle and suddenly have an idea. Creating isn't like pooping.

But you CAN get pretty deep into a creative process without any actual creativity or original thought at all.

## Creating Without Ideas

- **Build a combat encounter.** Open your Monster Manual, find a monster by CR, build an encounter, write stat blocks, roll for treasure, map an arena. Takes literally NO thought.
- **Start drawing something.** A side-view of a dungeon site. Just doodle. A scribble while watching TV can become a megadungeon.
- **Free Writing.** Open a blank document and start typing whatever crosses your mind. Don't stop. It's garbage and you'll throw it away, but it forces your mind to think about the project. Free write until useful things start coming out.

These techniques prime your mental pump. If you let your mind wander, it'll go to interesting places.

## How Can This Be True

Creating without creativity is great when you have time. But some people have exactly three hours to prep, no more, no less. That's when it's time for a game called **"How can this be true?"**

Marvel Comics used to offer a **No Prize** — an empty envelope. To earn one, you had to notice a continuity error and then **figure out an explanation** for it. You had to accept the "mistake" as true and explain how it could be true.

A lot of nerds have crippled their creativity by training their brain to scream "that's impossible." If you train your brain that way, it'll stop being able to embrace the fantastic.

Instead, a creative plays "how can this be true?" — you accept everything as true. Whatever you are seeing, it really did happen. Screaming "that's impossible" won't help because YOU SAW IT HAPPEN. Instead, the puzzle is to figure out what you didn't see that made it all work.

### Why This Works

Your brain is not great at inventing things out of nothing — that's not a super useful survival skill. What your brain IS really good at is **making connections, associations, and explanations**. A lot of choices we make are instinctive and automatic; it's only after the fact that the brain analyzes the snap judgment and tries to figure out why.

Your brain is REALLY good at explaining stuff and not so good at inventing stuff. That's why "how can this be true" is super useful.

### Example

You sit in front of the TV and draw a dungeon made of crystals and fire. Now figure out: why is there a dungeon made of crystals and fire? Who would live there? Why would the PCs go in there?

And you have that encounter with an iron golem and a githyanki wizard. Why are they in the dungeon of crystals and fire? Obviously, the wizard has set up his artifact forge there. He's forging a fire-crystal golem. His iron golem is his bodyguard. The dungeon is on the border of the elemental planes of Earth and Fire. He's been sending raids of enslaved fire archons into the material world to steal supplies.

Created from typing random words.

**The point:** You CAN'T create ideas on command. What you can do is take random elements, smash them together, and then explain them. As long as you don't immediately default to "well, that's impossible."

## Massaging Ideas

Come up with your random elements BEFORE you need to be creative. Keep them in your head. Then, whenever you don't need your brain, think about them.

Lay in bed for fifteen minutes before getting up and think about WHY a dungeon of crystals and fire is a thing. While showering, ask yourself what a githyanki wizard wants with crystals and fire.

**The problem:** People nowadays spend a lot of time distracting their brain — podcasts in the shower, music in the car. They never let their brain relax and daydream. Sometimes, you need quiet for your brain to just play with the toys you're giving it.

## Generating Random Ideas

Once you get good at "how can this be true," the only problem is where to find random things to smash together. Everything is an idea:
- Random doodles
- Art and monsters in RPG books (or ANY book)
- Video game elements
- Random words on a page
- Flip open books to random pages
- Story Cubes or picture dice
- Random Google image searches

Whatever you get, remember that they are true. So "how can they be true" is the question.

## "How Can This Be True" and the Forgotten Improv Step

After you introduce some random element into your game because it seemed like a good idea, you've got to spend time away from the table playing "how can this be true." You HAVE TO figure out how the new element connects to everything that came before.

Some improv GMs just keep using random elements and wait for them to end up connected. That's no different than waiting for Idea Fairies. A good GM spends the car ride home thinking about who the NPC is, what they want, why they joined the villainous organization, and what they were really up to. That way, they can build stories off of the NPC later and the NPC seems like a coherent part of the world.

**"How can this be true" is the often forgotten but utterly vital step in improvisation.**
