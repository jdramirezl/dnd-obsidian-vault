# How to Run Encounters… NOT!

> [Source: The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-run-encounters-not/)

---

## Key Advice

- **An encounter is just the moment PCs become aware of something they can interact with** — nothing more; stop overdefining it
- **Don't categorize encounters by type** (combat, social, exploration) — doing so unconsciously limits player choices and your own GMing
- **Never call for Initiative until the players give you permission** — i.e., until they declare they're fighting
- **Start encounters as early as possible** — the earliest, smallest detail a character *might* perceive (sounds down a tunnel, distant figures on a ridge, a rustling in the brush)
- **Describe only what characters perceive** — not one detail more; leave conclusions for players to draw
- **Telegraphing**: Describe the smallest thing a player might want to react to — "the lead orc tightens his grip and shifts his weight" instead of "roll Initiative"
- **Run a Background Sim** — track what's happening off-camera (unseen assassins, consequences of past actions) but don't obsess over it
- **Game Masters don't roleplay** — they manage narrative and gameplay; NPCs are game elements, not the GM's characters
- **Reactive Game Mastery**: Describe, invite action, determine outcome — that's the whole job; don't try to do more
- Mechanical tools (combat rules, social mechanics) are pulled out **only when** players choose a specific approach — not before

## Summary

Stop thinking of encounters as typed chunks of gameplay. An encounter is simply the moment characters become aware of something interactive. The GM's job is purely reactive: describe what characters perceive, invite action, determine outcomes. Start encounters at the earliest possible moment of awareness — hearing voices down a tunnel 100 feet away, spotting figures two miles across open terrain. Describe only raw perceptions, not conclusions. When preparing any encounter, ask three questions: When might characters become aware? What exactly might they perceive? What happens if they don't notice? This approach gives players real choices instead of funneling them into pre-typed mechanical resolutions. Don't roll Initiative until players choose to fight. Don't open the social mechanics until players choose to negotiate. The tools serve the players' choices, not the other way around.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop labeling encounters**: Drop "combat encounter," "social encounter," etc. from your vocabulary — just run the game
2. **For every encounter, answer three prep questions**:
   - When is the earliest moment characters *might* become aware?
   - What is the smallest detail they *might* perceive?
   - What happens if they don't notice?
3. **Telegraph, don't trigger**: Instead of "the orcs attack, roll Initiative," say "the lead orc tightens his grip on his sword and shifts his weight — what do you do?"
4. **Practice the Background Sim**: Track off-camera events simply — where is the assassin? What are the goblins doing when the party can't see them?
5. **Delay mechanical tools**: Don't open the combat chapter until players declare combat; don't invoke social mechanics until players choose to negotiate
6. **Grab a published module and practice**: Pick encounters, determine the earliest awareness moment and smallest perceivable detail for each — do this between sessions as exercise
7. **Show, don't tell**: Let players observe NPCs doing their thing before interaction begins — approach the shop, see the NPC through the window finishing business with someone else

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> An encounter is just the moment characters notice something interactive. Start early, describe only perceptions, and let players choose their approach before you reach for any mechanical tools. Your job is to describe, invite action, and determine outcomes — nothing more.
