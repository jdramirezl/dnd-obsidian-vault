# Don't Roll the Dice When You Can Hide Them Instead

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dont-roll-the-dice-when-you-can-hide-them-instead/)
**Date:** April 12, 2017
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## The Core Argument

GMs overvalue randomness and undervalue hidden information. This leads to unnecessary die rolling that slows down the game and steals player agency rather than enhancing it.

Certain skills are essentially passive, automatic things — functions of a human brain and awareness. When you walk into a room, you notice things (Perception). You recognize things and recall facts (Knowledge skills). Your brain does this automatically. There is no action, no choice, and nothing you can do to affect the outcome. It's totally passive.

## Passive vs. Active Skills

**Passive skills** serve two purposes:
1. **Exposition** — giving players information to deal with situations
2. **Clues and warnings** — alerting players that there's more to investigate

In either case, passive skills SET UP action. They provide information for better decisions or incentive to dig for more.

**Active skills** involve a player making a choice, describing an action, and swaying the outcome. Searching for traps (actively interacting with the world), asking leading questions to make an NPC nervous, conducting a religious ritual — these are active uses.

**Passive rolls should be streamlined.** They aren't worthy of die rolls. Instead:
- Use a binary check for Proficiency ("if you're proficient in Nature, you recognize this")
- Use the skill modifier to gate information ("if you have +5 in Nature, you know this much; +10, you know more")

## Why Randomness Feels Wrong Here

Rolling dice for passive checks creates problems:
1. **It bogs down the game** — every room means five die rolls before the GM can give information
2. **Low rolls create mistrust** — players who see bad rolls immediately want to "search again"
3. **Secret GM rolls are slow** — recording scores, rolling, doing math, then delivering info

These rolls are **speedbumps in the narration** during the most boring part of the scene — the part where players aren't doing anything.

## The GM Perspective Problem

To a GM, there is NO hidden information. If a trap requires Passive Perception 15 to spot, the GM can just check character sheets and know the outcome. No surprise. It FEELS like a screwjob.

But **the player doesn't see your notes**. To them, that page could have anything on it — or nothing. There could be a thousand bits of knowledge or three or none gated behind various numbers. The space for possibilities is infinite. When given information, they assume lower scores would mean less, higher scores would mean more, and different skills would yield different information.

**GMs lose this perspective the minute they sit behind the screen.** Their sense of hidden information's value gets distorted.

## Randomness vs. Hidden Information

Both serve the same purpose — adding tension and surprise. But they feel different:

| Randomness | Hidden Information |
|---|---|
| Feels cold, impartial, uncontrollable | Feels discoverable and empowering |
| Emphasizes chance over choice | Emphasizes the choice made at character creation |
| Only fun when driven by player choice | Works even without immediate player choice |

Random Knowledge checks are **randomness without any sense of immediate control**. The choice that matters (picking the skill) happened days or weeks ago at character generation. The die roll just adds noise.

With passive/hidden information, you emphasize the CHOICE: "Because you were smart enough to select this skill, you get this information everyone else would have missed."

## The Monster Stats Paradox

GMs jealously guard monster statistics while undervaluing hidden information elsewhere. But characters SHOULD be able to assess challenges — martial artists read their opponents, fencers are trained to evaluate the person across from them. Letting players know DCs and AC as part of their decision-making process is actually good for the game.

## The Takeaway

Die rolling is fun because it introduces randomness. But that randomness is only fun FOR PLAYERS if they feel like they have some control over it. The randomness needs to be driven by a choice OR the players need a way to react to it. **Passive Knowledge and Perception rolls meet neither criteria.**

Random die rolls should be added SPARINGLY — only when in direct response to a player's choice or when it can be affected by a choice the player has to make.

As a GM, it's hard to see the value of hidden information because of your position at the table. But hidden information feels discoverable and empowering to players. Randomness, without player control, just feels like a screwjob.
