## Key Advice

- **You are the sole source of truth.** Regardless of what players read in rulebooks or setting guides, your actions during play teach them everything they actually know about the world and the game.
- **Information turns random actions into meaningful choices.** Without information, player decisions are coin flips. With it, choices carry weight, consequence, and character.
- **Information empowers players at multiple levels.** It makes choices meaningful → enables better choices → creates new options → can resolve conflicts entirely → brings the world to life.
- **Informational Proximity is the first rule.** Share information as close to the moment it becomes relevant as possible. Front-loading lore clogs player brains and wastes table time.
- **Classify information into four categories:** Irrelevant (keep to yourself), Interesting (context/theme/tone), Useful (impacts decisions), and Necessary (required for success).
- **Interesting information brings the world to life.** It provides context, explanation, and emotional weight—even if it doesn't directly affect player choices.
- **Useful information is like treasure.** Treat it the way you treat magic items—dole it out deliberately. At its best, it lets players completely subvert challenges, and that's a good thing.
- **Necessary information is the least important to understand** because GMs rarely mess it up—except when the information IS the adventure (e.g., murder mysteries).
- **It's okay for players to fail.** If necessary information is gated behind challenges, the party might not get it. That's a valid failure state, just like dying in combat.

## Summary

The GM's most important job is communication. Information management is the deliberate delivery of facts about the game world to empower players. Information works at escalating levels: it makes choices meaningful, enables better decisions, creates new options, can bypass obstacles entirely, and—most importantly—brings the world to life by making it feel like a place that operates on reason. Angry classifies information into four categories: Irrelevant (don't share it), Interesting (context, theme, tone that enriches the world), Useful (impacts player decisions from slight edges to complete encounter subversion), and Necessary (required to complete the adventure). The key principle is Informational Proximity—share information as close to the moment of relevance as possible. Campaign lore dumps at session zero are mostly wasted cognitive load.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Audit your prep for irrelevant information.** Before each session, ask: "Does this fact matter to what the players are doing right now or very soon?" If not, cut it.
2. **Categorize every piece of information** you plan to share as Interesting, Useful, or Necessary before the session.
3. **Deliver information at the point of relevance.** Don't front-load backstory. Reveal the history of the haunted tomb when the party reaches the tomb, not during session zero.
4. **Treat Useful information like treasure.** Plan where and how players acquire it. A monster's weakness, a secret passage, a noble's leverage—these are rewards.
5. **Identify Necessary information early** and ensure there are clear paths for players to obtain it. If it IS the adventure (like a mystery), accept that failure to find it means the adventure fails.
6. **Use Interesting information for emotional weight.** A sad anecdote about a missing NPC costs nothing but makes the players care more about the quest.
7. **Respect cognitive load.** Players can only hold so much. Every irrelevant fact you share pushes out something important.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> You are the players' only window into the game world. Every piece of information you share should be deliberate, timely, and purposeful. Classify it (Interesting, Useful, Necessary), deliver it close to the moment it matters, and stop dumping lore nobody needs yet.
