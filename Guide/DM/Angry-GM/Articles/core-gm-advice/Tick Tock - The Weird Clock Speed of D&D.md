# Tick Tock: The Weird Clock Speed of D&D

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/tick-tock-the-weird-clock-speed-of-dd/)
**Date:** June 23, 2017
**Category:** Random Bulls$&%

---

## Summary

D&D has two fundamentally different game flows: combat (a slow-ticking continuous clock with turns) and everything else (a series of narratives halted by decision points, like a game book). Most GMs don't understand this distinction, which causes the game to grind to a halt during transitions, travel, and any moment where players need to wait. Understanding decision points — what they are, when to insert them, and how to present them — is the key to keeping the game moving.

## The Two Clocks

### Combat: Continuous Clock with Slow Ticks

In combat, continuous action is divided into discrete turns. The game proceeds player to player in initiative order. The game world updates at fixed intervals. Players sacrifice the ability to react freely in exchange for structured participation.

### Everything Else: Game Book Flow

Outside of combat, D&D is **not continuous at all.** There is no metronome, no turn order, no structured update cycle. The flow works like a game book:

1. **Narrative description** of a scene
2. **Decision point** — player makes a choice
3. **Outcome** of that choice
4. **Transition** narration
5. **Next scene** description → repeat

The narration only pauses when there is a decision to be made. Once a decision is made, narration continues until the next decision. If there is NO decision, keep narrating.

## The Core Problem: GMs Ask for Non-Decisions

GMs love asking "what do you do now?" This creates halts because:

1. **Continuing a declared action is NOT a decision.** If the party decided to travel for three days, they don't need to re-decide each morning.
2. **"What do you do?" implies an important decision exists.** It signals previous choices have been invalidated.
3. **Open-ended group questions trigger group decision-making weirdness.** Nobody wants to deny anyone else the chance to act.
4. **Players don't think of "waiting" or "not acting" as a decision.**
5. **Most players are uncomfortable with open, unconstrained decisions.**

### Example: The Three-Day Journey

The party decides marching order, navigator, forager, lookout, watch order, and pace. When is the next decision point? **When they reach their destination.** Random encounters may interrupt, but after each one the party should simply keep going.

## How to Present Decision Points

### Offer Firm Options Plus a Reminder of Freedom

> "The alien drops out of the ceiling. There's a desk nearby to hide under and a doorway to the left. You could also try to run or fight. What do you do?"

Creative players seize on the open-ended reminder. Everyone else won't get paralyzed.

### Insert Decision Points Aggressively

Slam the brakes on narration every moment something changes — **as long as they are real decisions:**

- The alien appears → decision
- You hide successfully but it's prowling → decision
- It starts moving toward you → decision
- It starts moving away → decision

> "You should be driving your game like a student driver with a foot on the gas and the other on the brake."

### Make Transitional Decisions FOR the Party

- After the briefing: "You head to the dungeon entrance..."
- During travel: "You continue your journey on the second day..."
- After combat: "Do you want to take a short rest or keep going?"

### Use Opt-Ins and Opt-Outs

**Opt-out:** Tell the party what will happen unless someone objects.
> "If none of you have other business, you'll wait out the next few hours."

**Opt-in:** Tell the party what won't happen unless someone speaks up.
> "Unless anyone asks the sage questions, the sage will be on his way."

Give a moment for someone to speak up, then move along.

## Key Takeaways

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| Don't ask for non-decisions | If the party already decided to travel, narrate the travel |
| Present firm options | "Short rest or keep going?" |
| Remind of freedom | "...but you can try anything" |
| Insert decision points aggressively | Every moment something changes, pause for a choice |
| Make transitions for the party | "You head to the dungeon entrance" |
| Use opt-ins and opt-outs | "Unless anyone objects, you wait until nightfall" |
| Recognize the two clocks | Combat = turns; everything else = decision points |
