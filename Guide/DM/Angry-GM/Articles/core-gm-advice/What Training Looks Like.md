# What Training Looks Like and Why It Matters

> **Source:** [The Angry GM — What Training Looks Like and Why It Matters](https://theangrygm.com/what-training-looks-like/)
> **Date:** September 14, 2022
> **Series:** Town Mode (Supplemental)

---

## Context

A supplemental Town Mode article addressing how to handle character Training (leveling up) in a way that's neither handwaved away nor reduced to "find a class trainer in every town." Training is reframed as Ongoing Professional Development, and five Training Modes are defined as a townbuilding tool.

---

## Why Training Matters

Training — requiring characters to spend downtime and money to level up — is good for fantasy RPGs because:

- It forces regular returns to civilization
- It creates meaningful time and money costs
- It drives Town Mode interaction
- It anchors individual character roles in the town

### Training as the Center of Town Mode

Training is the de facto center of Town Mode. It's something players want, can only get in Town, and forces regular interaction with Town. It helps build towns because it provides players with destinations and goals.

### Why Handwaving Sucks

If Training is abstract ("I go Train"), it gives the GM nothing to build around and gives players nothing to look for. No world interaction, no exploration of Town.

### Why "Class Trainer in Every Town" Sucks

The WoW approach — one high-level trainer per class in every town — strains credulity and makes for a gamey world.

---

## Training as Ongoing Professional Development

PCs aren't apprentices. They're professionals — journeymen at least. Training between adventures isn't about learning skills from scratch. It's about:

- **Synthesis** — Absorbing and integrating experience
- **Experimentation** — Trying things in low-pressure situations
- **Reflection** — Deepening connection to skills and ideals
- **Interaction** — Building contacts and seeing different perspectives
- **Recharging** — Professional development before the next high-pressure situation

A fighter doesn't need someone to teach whirlwind strike — he needs to practice the thing he accidentally did against the ogre. A wizard doesn't need fireball lessons — given time and research materials, he can work out the formula. A cleric needs time to reflect, meditate, and reconnect.

---

## The Five Training Modes

### 1. Academic Training

Research, study, experimentation, instruction, consultation with experts. Done in libraries, labs, archives, or with sages and scholars.

**Money buys:** Office supplies, reagents, consumables, books, equipment, fees/bribes for access, expert consultation time.

**Solo option:** Own lab equipment and book collections in a safe place.

### 2. Martial Training

Exercise, physical conditioning, sparring, target practice, drills, forms, martial instruction, brawling, pit fighting.

**Money buys:** Access to training facilities (militia grounds, barracks, gymnasiums, monasteries, back-alley brawling circles), training partners, practice equipment (breaks frequently), fight entry fees.

**Solo option:** Open space. Better with equipment (dummies, archery butts, mechanical obstacles).

### 3. Networking Training

Interacting with others — professionals trading gossip and secrets in guild halls, criminals rubbing shoulders with merchants and law enforcement, minstrels chatting with travelers, priests ministering to their flock.

**Money buys:** Food, drinks, gifts, bribes, finders fees, dues.

**Solo option:** Not really possible without a community. But a PC with a nice house can host events.

**Subtypes:** Networking with a professional group (guild, troupe) vs. networking with folks about town.

### 4. Practical Training

Practicing a skillset in low-stakes situations — wilderness survival, artisanship, burglary, con artistry. The difference from adventure is **safety**. Minor crimes around town are safer than dungeon delving.

**Money and facilities:** Varies by skillset.

### 5. Ritual Training

Ceremonies, vigils, rites, meditation, reflection, arcana, spirituality. Connecting with a deity, supernatural force, ideal, ki, chi. Priests pray and study scripture. Knights sit vigils and reaffirm oaths. Monks fast and meditate. Warlocks do warlock things.

**Money buys:** Ritual materials, access to ritual spaces (fees or donations).

**Solo option:** Needs long hours of safety, quiet, privacy. Benefits from constructed sacred spaces.

---

## How to Use the Five Modes

### Primary and Secondary

Each PC gets a **primary** Training Mode (most beneficial) and a **secondary** (can settle for in a pinch), based on class, background, and skills.

### Differentiation Creates Choices

Once defined, Training Modes can be differentiated:

- **Time:** Some require more or less time
- **Cost:** Some are cheaper or more expensive, varying by location
- **Availability:** Not all modes available everywhere (why everyone needs two)
- **Extra benefits:** Academic training might provide information, adventure hooks, or access to non-core spells. Practical training might let you earn money on the side.
- **Risks:** Some modes carry risks (arrest, injury) but with better payoffs
- **Solo vs. Group:** Training with others is better than training alone. Mentored training provides a 10% XP bonus toward the next level.

### Townbuilding Checklist

The five modes serve as a checklist for stocking Town Mode towns with Essential Services. Assign each PC their modes, then build people and places where they can engage in them.

---

## Time Always Counts

Per Angry's comment:

1. Town time costs money (Lifestyle costs)
2. Training costs money (more time = more cost)
3. Town time raises the possibility of Complications
4. Town time allows Threats to grow (adventures may get worse)

> "Time should ALWAYS count. Whether it's in the dungeon or outside of it."

---

## Summary

> Training reframed as Ongoing Professional Development — not apprenticeship — solves the problem of needing class trainers everywhere while giving GMs concrete things to build towns around. Five Training Modes (Academic, Martial, Networking, Practical, Ritual) provide a townbuilding checklist that creates destinations, interactions, choices, and consequences. Each PC gets a primary and secondary mode, and the differences between modes create meaningful strategic decisions during downtime.
