# Why You Can't Interrupt Your Players

This article addresses a cluster of common GM struggles that aren't really about Game Mastering — they're about **assertiveness** and **authority**. If you struggle to start sessions, stop player chatter, maintain narration without being interrupted, pull the game back on track, or say "No," this is for you.

---

## The Core Issue: Assertiveness

**Assertiveness** is the ability to clearly communicate your wants, needs, desires, and boundaries. It comes from believing your own needs are **equally as important** — not more, not less — as anyone else's.

> Assertiveness is you saying to the world, "I deserve to be treated with respect."

### Why Assertiveness Is Hard

Most people struggle with it, for two main reasons:

1. **Agreeableness** — a personality trait that values harmony and dislikes conflict. Highly agreeable people put their own needs aside to avoid strife. If you see yourself as a "nice person," you're probably agreeable — and asserting yourself means swimming upstream against your own personality.

2. **Self-Respect** — assertiveness requires believing your needs are worthy of respect. If you struggle with self-confidence, it's hard to assert boundaries you don't fully believe you deserve.

Neither of these makes you a bad person. They just mean assertiveness takes more work for you.

### Assertive ≠ Asshole

Assertiveness is the line between:
- **Passivity** — putting your needs last
- **Aggression** — demanding everyone put your needs first

Assertiveness puts everyone — you included — on a level field. It's often a starting point for negotiation and compromise.

## Basic Assertiveness Training for GMs

Quick habits to build assertiveness at the table:

### Practice Saying "No"
Once a week, tell someone "No." The more you do it — and find people handle polite refusal without hating you — the easier it gets.
- "No, you cannot take that broken feat."
- "No, your halfling can't ride the barbarian into battle."
- "No, I can't change the game night this week."

### Say "I" Instead of "You"
Get comfortable speaking about yourself:
- Instead of "You need to stop coming late" → "I would like you to be on time so I can start promptly"
- Instead of "You keep interrupting me" → "I need to be allowed to finish my narration before questions"

### Focus on Behaviors, Not People
- Instead of "You're becoming unreliable" → "You were late to the last three sessions"
- Instead of "You're distracting the game" → "When you joked around at the start of each combat, I found it distracting"

### Establish Firm Boundaries
Decide in advance and write them down:
- "I will start the game session at 6:15 PM"
- "When I am narrating, players are not to interrupt me"

### Script What You'll Say
Write it out and rehearse:
- "It's 6:15 everyone. Time to start the game. Let me recap the last session…"
- "I would appreciate being allowed to finish my narration before questions are asked"

## The Authority Problem

Game Mastering requires more than assertiveness — it requires **authority**. Authority is assertiveness amped up: you must assert the needs of the *project/task* over the needs of individual team members.

### Why Authority Matters

Everyone at the table wants the game to happen, but people suck at making things happen on their own:
- Adam can't level up his character because he'd rather play video games
- Beth can't stop blurting out jokes
- Chris can't remember the rules
- Danielle can't get everyone to settle on a date

The GM isn't just a game mechanic — they're the **president of the gaming club**, the manager of people. Someone has to take responsibility for making the game happen.

### Why Authority Is Uncomfortable

Most people don't like assuming authority over friends, especially over a "dumb game." It's uncomfortable and burdensome. And because authority rides on assertiveness, it's extra hard for people who already struggle with assertiveness.

### The Reframe

You may not be the most qualified person to run the group — but you're **willing** to do it. Every time the players show up, they're voting you into power. They're admitting they can't or won't do the job and they're looking to you to do what they can't. To some extent, they **expect** you to lean on them and make demands.

> If you're uncomfortable being a Game Master, you're more qualified to run games than most.

People who are reluctant to take power recognize it as a burden and a responsibility — not a prize. That's exactly the right attitude.

## What You Can Do

**If you just need a boost:** Use the assertiveness habits above. Practice them consistently.

**If you want more structured help:**
- Take an Assertiveness Training course (Skillshare, Udemy, books, or through a mental health professional)
- Look into basic **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — a toolset for understanding the relationship between thoughts and actions, highly esteemed and well-researched

**If this article left you deeply uncomfortable:** Speak with a mental health professional. Distance and online therapy options make this easier than ever.

The uncomfortable truth: this isn't really a Game Mastering problem. Assertiveness is a social skill that GMs need, but it's a personal development issue, not a game design one.
