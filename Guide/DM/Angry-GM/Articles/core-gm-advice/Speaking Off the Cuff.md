# Stupid Narration Tricks: Speaking Off the Cuff

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/speaking-off-the-cuff/)
**Date:** April 14, 2026
**Series:** Stupid Narration Tricks

---

## Overview

This article teaches **extemporaneous speaking** — talking without a script while sounding like you're reading from one. It's the foundational skill for all GM narration. The article provides six practice drills (plus a bonus game) taking about 10 minutes total per day.

The key insight: most people start sentences with no idea where they're going. They talk faster than they think. **Artful narration requires being in control of what comes out of your mouth.** Every word and sentence must serve a purpose clear in your head before you speak.

---

## Why This Matters for GMs

Advanced narration tricks — mysteries, clues, thematic consistency — require a GM who picks words with care. You can't use those techniques if you just let random words pour out.

**Example:** An animated corpse had a key on his belt. The GM had to mention this detail multiple times during combat, naturally woven into narration, so players could notice it without being told directly.

---

## Tips Before Starting

1. **Pick different topics every day** — don't reuse; it defeats the purpose
2. **Don't restrict to gaming topics** — pick easy things; the skills transfer
3. **Do NOT record or evaluate yourself** — conscious evaluation kills skill formation

---

## The Six Drills

### Drill 1: Lexical Pausing (1 Minute)
At the end of every thought or sentence, deliberately pause for a half-second to a second. The real benefit isn't eliminating filler words — it's creating mental space where creativity and improvisation can happen.

### Drill 2: No-Stall Speaking (2 Minutes)
Speak on a topic for two minutes. If you stall, repeat the last thing you said until you get unstuck. If stalling isn't your problem, skip this or combine with Lexical Pausing.

### Drill 3: Progressive Compression (2 Minutes)
Find a book passage. Summarize it in one minute, then thirty seconds, then fifteen seconds. Builds awareness of how much time talking takes, and the ability to identify and prioritize ideas. This is the **Recap Skill**.

### Drill 4: Progressive Expansion (2 Minutes)
Pick a topic with a start and end (a process works best). Talk on it for fifteen seconds, then thirty, then one minute. Builds the ability to add details when you have space.

### Drill 5: Ass-Pulling Description (1 Minute)
Describe something that might appear in your game — a character, location, feature, object — as you would to players. Use Lexical Pausing, don't stall. A downloadable worksheet includes 100 random things to describe.

### Drill 6: Key-Phrase Insertion (1 Minute)
Pick a random thing to describe AND a random key-phrase (color, texture, mood, sensory detail). Ass-pull a description for one minute, working the key-phrase in at least once. Bonus: add an oblique reference back to it. This is the foundation for weaving clues, themes, and narrative puzzles.

---

## Bonus: Key-Phrase Insertion in the Trenches

Before your next session, identify a key detail to feature across several encounters or a whole scene. Reference it explicitly and implicitly throughout play. At the start of the *following* session, ask players if they remember it.

**Example:** Burial mound walls of loose, muddy earth with tree roots. Referenced in scene-setting, transitions (dirt knocked loose, roots clinging to clothes), and encounters (missed bullet thudding into earthen wall, spraying dirt).

## Key Takeaways

- Artful narration = deliberate communication, not wild improvisation
- Most people talk faster than they think — fix it with deliberate pauses
- **ABCs:** **A**lways **B**e doing daily drills unless you want your GMing to go to **C**rap
- These drills build toward advanced techniques: multi-session mysteries, surprises, reversals, narrative puzzles
- Skills are like saws — they always need sharpening
