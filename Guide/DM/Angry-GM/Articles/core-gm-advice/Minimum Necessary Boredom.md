# Minimum Necessary Boredom

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/minimum-necessary-boredom/)
**Date:** September 13, 2018
**Category:** Core GM Advice — Exposition & Information Delivery

---

## Core Concept

Exposition — delivering information to players — is necessary but inherently boring. Every word of exposition that isn't adjudicating an action tightens a "noose of boredom" around your game. The goal is to use the **minimum necessary boredom**: share only what the players need, in as few words as possible, at the right time, and in the right form.

---

## Why Exposition Matters (and Hurts)

All the information you create about the world, NPCs, gods, kingdoms, and lore is a pile of **potential exposition**. Players need information to make meaningful decisions. But when exposition is happening, **no one is playing the game** — players are just listening, not making choices.

The hard truth: players care primarily about two things:
1. **Their own creations** — their characters, their choices, their stories
2. **Actually playing the game** — making decisions and seeing results

Your lovingly crafted world details? You're the only one who truly cares about them. That doesn't mean they're worthless — background information helps you make consistent decisions — but players don't want to passively absorb your creativity.

---

## Angry's Three Rules for Avoiding Expository Masturbation

### Rule 1: Use the Minimum Necessary Boredom

Share information only when it passes this test:

> **If the players would likely make a different decision, or if their chances of success would likely be different with the information, then it needs to be shared.**

Then use the **minimum number of words** to get the idea across. Boil everything down to the biggest, broadest, essential ideas.

**Example — Wizard persecution world detail:**
Instead of pages of War of the Magi history, give players one paragraph:
> *"Two centuries ago, a cabal of tyrannical wizards tried to conquer the world and committed many atrocities. As a result, all non-divine magic users are feared and persecuted. Any use of arcane magic in front of witnesses will probably get you executed by soldiers or burned alive by a mob."*

That's everything they need. Three ideas: wizards tried to take over, the world hates wizards, public magic gets you killed.

### Rule 2: You Can Always Add More

You can't **un-share** information, but you can always share more later. Start with the biggest, broadest main ideas and sprinkle details over time as they become interesting or relevant.

- If you bury an important fact under pages of boring history, players won't read it and you can't make it interesting again
- If you start with a brief explanation, you can expand when it becomes relevant in-game or when a player asks
- Don't worry about it seeming "obvious" when you reveal a legend right before it becomes relevant — obvious is **not boring**, and not boring wins

### Rule 3: Know When to Punch and When to Goose

Exposition takes different forms along a spectrum:

**Punching** (direct, unmistakable exposition):
- Campaign documents, direct narration, GM telling players what their characters know
- Painful but **impossible to miss or miscommunicate**
- Use for: Big, important information that significantly affects player choices (e.g., wizard persecution before character creation)

**Goosing** (subtle, embedded exposition):
- Grinding information into tiny details sprinkled throughout gameplay
- Environmental storytelling: a bonfire with human bones and a broken wizard staff on the roadside
- NPC interactions that hint at world details
- Dropping evocative names ("the War of the Magi") without full explanation — context fills the gaps
- Less painful but **can be missed or misunderstood**
- Use for: Optional information, extra details, rewarding attentive players, fleshing out things you've already "punched" them with

**The bigger the choice or consequences, the less optional the information should be** — and the more you should punch rather than goose.

---

## Practical Guidelines

- **You will always create more information than players need** — that's fine, it helps you make consistent decisions
- **Don't make players watch your creative process** — create whatever you want privately, but be selective about what you share
- **Campaign documents should contain only the biggest, most important ideas** — everything else gets goosed into the game during play
- **Cut your campaign document down to punches only**, then keep the cut material aside to goose players with during gameplay
- **Players who wanted passive exposition would read books** — they're at your table to make choices

---

## Key Takeaway

> Exposition is like a noose around your game's neck. Every unnecessary word tightens it. You can't un-strangle your game, but you can always share more information later. Start minimal, add as needed, and match the delivery method to the importance of the information.
