# Breaking Your Map Addiction

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/breaking-your-map-addiction/)
**Date:** March 30, 2026

---

## The Scourge of Mapaholism

I am The Angry GM, and I am addicted to maps.

Modern gaming has made it easier than ever to bring gorgeous maps and visual aids to your game table. Poster maps, books of quick maps, wall projectors, Air Casting to a flat-screen TV, virtual tabletops with line of sight and lighting effects and fog of war. I love that shit.

But every advancement is a tradeoff. You get benefits, you get costs. And sometimes, you don't realize what your gains are costing you.

Use visuals. Love them. But don't let them take too much from you.

RPGs are narrative games played by storytelling. The thing that makes them unique is the sense of unconstrained freedom. You can do anything you can imagine. The only way to pull that off is language and imagination — the most unconstrained media we have. As a Game Master, you must be able to use language and inspire imagination. **Anything that lets you not do that is bad for you.**

Visual aids can **help** your narration, or they can **replace** your narration. One is very good. The other is very bad. It's easy to slip from one to the other without noticing.

### The Problem with Exploration

One of the things that makes exploring indoor and underground spaces exciting, challenging, and tense is that your awareness of the space is so constrained. There's wonder in entering a cave and having no idea how far it goes. That's how a dungeon should feel — disorienting and lost. Because your life might depend on finding the way out, you desperately need to keep a mental map. That's the core of a navigational challenge.

The less players rely on their internal mental maps or their ability to keep notes, the less they're playing a navigational challenge. Laying out the entire dungeon map — even concealed under fog — saps the wonder and mystery. Even map edges and token positions relative to edges provide orientation clues.

Ideally, show players nothing their characters can't currently perceive. When they move out of a room, the room should vanish completely. Otherwise, there's no challenge to navigation, and explorable spaces feel smaller and less mysterious.

### The Problem with Combat Movement

When actions aren't passed through the Game Master, they undermine the core gameplay loop. Players move their token on the grid and say "I move like so and then attack." This leads to **square-counting** — trial-and-error pathfinding that's basically Minesweeper. It's not a good gameplay challenge.

The question isn't "which route will let the player safely cross the battlefield within their movement speed" but rather "does there exist any route that would allow the character to get from here to there in roughly six seconds?" You don't need to know the route, just that one exists. Unless the answer is unambiguously "no," assume the answer is yes.

A combat turn should sound the same whether you have a map or not:

> "I move off to my right to get a clear shot at the orc warfighter and then shoot an arrow from my longbow at him."
> "Cool, roll your attack."

When you force players to use only words to convey actions and identify creatures unambiguously, their situational awareness increases drastically. They're forced to keep track of descriptive details and interactional relationships. You lose all of this when you let the map be your intermediary.

The map is too precise. Too deterministic. No one stands totally still for 24 out of every 30 seconds. No one fills a five-foot square. No one moves the same exact speed in perfect straight lines. If you give the map authority, your combats are constrained board games.

**The map exists not to determine positions or adjudicate actions. The map exists so you can clearly show the players what the situation looks like.** If the map isn't showing what you think it should, change the map. Nudge the miniatures.

## Three Rules for a Better Relationship with Maps

### 1. Only Put Tokens on the Map When Description Isn't Sufficient

Never put character tokens anywhere on the map unless you actually need to show precise position and there's no way to describe a reasonable approximation.

Use maps to show rooms — draw out the bookshelves, chest, reading stand, display case. But there's no reason to put player tokens on the map. "Ardrick is by the display case, Cabe is kneeling by the chest, and Beryllia and Danae are walking along the bookshelves" is sufficient. If you're forgetful, drop tokens on the items where players are interacting — that's relational, not positional.

Unless and until a combat or tactical situation starts, you don't need tokens on the room map. Don't put them down in anticipation of a tactical situation starting several rounds later.

### 2. Don't Let the Players Touch the Minis or the Map

Players must describe their actions to you unambiguously. Then, as part of describing the outcome, you update the map accordingly.

> **Adam:** Ardrick charges at the orc.
> **GM:** Which orc?
> **Adam:** The one I'm pointing at.
> **GM:** Ardrick is pointing at an orc? Which one?
> **Adam:** I charge the big orc who's been doing all the talking in the middle of the line and stab him with my sword.
> **GM:** Got it, roll the attack.

This only works if you then fiddle with the map to make the situation clear whenever it's ambiguous. Periodically describe the situation and futz with miniatures. Even purposely put miniatures slightly off the grid to drive home that the map is an abstraction.

Players who are used to tactical boardgame D&D have trouble adjusting, but they come to appreciate it. Because you're permissive, tend to assume more is possible than the grid says, and never let them blindly screw themselves. Never say "Ha, you stepped in the wrong space! Six opportunity attacks!" Instead: "Are you aware that there is no way for you to dodge through the enemy line in that direction?"

### 3. No More Dungeon Maps

Players cannot see the map of any space the characters can't currently see. Everything outside the current room is hidden behind the black.

At a physical table, use a gridded mat or flip map — only put the current room on the table. Don't use poster maps of the whole dungeon.

On virtual tabletops, change the fog-of-war setting so players can't see previously revealed areas. Foundry and Roll20 have Exploration Mode settings for this.

The better method is splitting the dungeon into one-room maps, each as its own scene with the right mood lighting. Is that a giant pain? Yes. Should you do it? Absolutely. Basic, bland rooms and hallways? Handle those narratively. Only provide maps of rooms that need maps.

Because I want my players disoriented. If you feel oriented, are you even really dungeon crawling?
