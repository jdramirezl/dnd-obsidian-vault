## Key Advice

- **Information takes three shapes: Facts, Implications, and Clues.** Facts are stated outright and instantly usable. Implications require a logical leap. Clues are fragments that must be assembled into an implication, then translated into a fact.
- **Gamespeak and Worldspeak are equally valid.** "The dragon is resistant to fire" (gamespeak) and "the firebolt barely singes its scales" (worldspeak) say the same thing. Use both. Always clarify worldspeak with gamespeak so there's zero ambiguity.
- **Drop your epistemophobia.** GMs are irrationally terrified of giving players information. When players USE knowledge to make optimal decisions, they don't feel bored—they feel smart. That's the goal.
- **Always clarify mechanical effects in gamespeak.** Once a player has seen a resistance, immunity, or magic item bonus in action, state the exact mechanic. Ambiguity isn't mystery—it's unfair.
- **Balance Impact against Ease of acquisition.** Easy-to-get or easy-to-use information should have low impact. Hard-to-get or hard-to-use information should have high impact.
- **Any fact can be shaved into an implication; any implication can be broken into clues.** This is the core art of designing mysteries and puzzles.
- **Player skill is inherent and unavoidable.** Players—not characters—must translate implications and assemble clues. This is no different from tactical decisions in combat.
- **Use Intelligence checks as a failsafe, not a replacement** for player reasoning. Reduce the DC as more clues are gathered. Never use the "get a clue" nudge-roll—it's boring and frustrating.
- **Impact spectrum:** Low = interesting/thematic info. Middle = tactical edges, bonus options. High = encounter-bypassing solutions, necessary information.

## Summary

Information in an RPG is facts the GM deliberately shares during gameplay to empower players. Those facts exist in both gamespeak (mechanical) and worldspeak (narrative), and both are equally important—always clarify worldspeak with gamespeak after a mechanical effect is revealed. Information takes three shapes: Facts (instantly usable), Implications (require a logical leap), and Clues (fragments that assemble into implications). The art of mystery and puzzle design is breaking facts down into clues and scattering them. GMs must balance the Impact of information against the Ease of acquiring or using it: easy information should be low-impact (thematic flavor), while hard-won information should be high-impact (encounter-bypassing solutions). Angry urges GMs to overcome their "epistemophobia"—when players use knowledge to make smart decisions, they feel empowered, not bored.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Clarify every mechanical effect in gamespeak** the moment players observe it. "The dragon takes half damage from fire—it's resistant to fire damage." No ambiguity.
2. **Design mysteries by working backward.** Start with the fact (the killer's identity), shave it into implications, then break those into 4–6 clues scattered across the adventure.
3. **Set a minimum clue threshold** for mysteries. Require at least 3 of 5 clues before allowing an Intelligence check failsafe, reducing the DC by 5 for each additional clue.
4. **Match information shape to impact.** Thematic/interesting info → state as facts freely. Tactical edges → deliver as implications. Adventure-critical solutions → require assembling clues.
5. **Stop hiding monster stats out of fear.** After the first round of combat, give players AC ranges, resistance info, and rough HP status. They'll feel smart using it.
6. **Never use the "get a clue" nudge roll.** If players are stuck, either give them the answer or provide a new scene where another clue can be found—don't make them roll Intelligence repeatedly.
7. **Treat Useful information like magic items.** Plan where it appears, how it's earned, and what advantage it grants. Don't scatter it randomly.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Every fact you share can be delivered as a plain fact, an implication, or a set of clues. Match the delivery method to the information's impact: low-impact stuff gets stated outright, high-impact stuff gets earned through clue assembly. And for the love of the game, stop being afraid to let your players know things—knowledge makes them feel like winners.
