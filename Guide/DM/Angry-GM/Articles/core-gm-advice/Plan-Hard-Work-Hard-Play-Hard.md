# Plan Hard, Work Hard, Play Hard

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/plan-hard-work-hard-play-hard/)
**Date:** May 9, 2019
**Series:** Practical Adventure Building

---

## Working Hard AND Working Smart

Writing your own adventures is hard work — some of it boring and tedious. That's unavoidable. But you don't have to embrace it either. The goal is to **work smart** (minimize unnecessary work) while also **working hard** (accepting that good adventures require effort).

- **Working Hard** = writing things down: text descriptions, stat blocks, maps, mechanics — physically recording adventure elements.
- **Working Smart** = keeping things in your head. It's still planned, you just don't write it down because you can remember it.

Everyone's balance is different. Over time, the amount of prep work tends to decrease as you learn what you need to spell out and what you can keep in your head or invent on the fly.

## Planning Hard, Planning Soft, and Not Planning at All

### Hard Planning

Figuring out absolutely everything you can before you sit down to run the adventure. Maps, stat blocks, NPC motivations, encounter mechanics — all determined in advance.

**Strengths:**
- Almost always leads to the strongest game experience
- More detail, more consistency, better handling of unexpected player actions
- You know the goblins' motives, so you can handle conversation, stealth, or powder-keg explosions

**Weaknesses:**
- Heavy workload or cognitive load
- Inflexible when things deviate *too far* from the plan
- Requires contingency planning to remain useful
- Wasted effort if players never encounter the content

**Best for:** Static situations where player options are well-defined — dungeon crawls, mystery adventures.

**Weakest when:** Things are dynamic and open-ended, where small player actions can cause dramatic change.

### No Planning

Deliberately leaving blank spaces in the adventure to fill in at the table.

**Strengths:**
- Minimal prep workload
- Incredibly flexible and responsive
- High player agency — the world bends to their whims

**Weaknesses:**
- High pressure on the GM at the table
- Quality is highly variable (depends on mood, energy, skill)
- Consistency suffers — details get jury-rigged together
- Hard to run mechanically complex scenes (combat, stat blocks)
- GM can become too reactive, leaving players directionless

**Best for:** Player-driven situations — organizing town defenses, cat-and-mouse chases, open-ended exploration.

### Soft Planning

The middle ground — starting the work of planning something but leaving details to fill in at the table.

**Examples:**
- Random encounter tables (stat blocks without encounter context)
- A chase scene you know will happen but don't pre-build mechanics for
- An NPC ready to deploy but not placed in a specific location
- A list of clues doled out in order as players explore, regardless of which locations they visit
- Lists of NPC names, inn names, random dungeon dressings

**Key insight:** The flaws of Hard and No Planning stack up faster than the benefits when you move toward the middle. Soft Planning works best when Hard Planning is too much work and No Planning is too much pressure.

## Mixing and Matching

Every homebrew adventure includes a mixture of all three. For each element, make a **deliberate choice**:

| Element | Planning Type |
|---|---|
| Castle map and most encounters | Hard Plan |
| Journey to the castle (random encounters) | Soft Plan |
| Pre-built encounters placed flexibly in the woods | Soft Plan |
| Blank rooms in the castle to fill as needed | No Plan |
| Wandering ghost villain encounters | Soft Plan |
| Clues about the curse | Soft Plan |
| Ghostly NPCs invented on the fly | No Plan |
| Everything else in the world | No Plan (default) |

Adjust based on your strengths. Leave lots of blanks if you like improvising. Plan everything mechanically if you feel better with a Hard Plan. The key is making the choice deliberately rather than by default.
