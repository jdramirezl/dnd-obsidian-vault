# Welcome to Corneria: Establishing Without Expositing

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/welcome-to-corneria-establishing-without-expositing/)
**Date:** October 30, 2018

---

## Summary

Angry teaches how to establish a settlement's tone without exposition — using "show, don't tell" and "do, don't show" techniques. This is foundational advice for setting design, specifically for how settlements feel at the table.

## Core Concept: Tone

Every settlement should have a **single, simple tone** paired with a **story detail** that explains it. Tone is the overall mood/feel. The story detail gives it a reason and something to build on.

### Why Tone Matters for Settlements
- Settlements are where adventures begin — great for setting adventure tone
- Different tones differentiate settlements (visual differences alone aren't enough)
- Evolving tone shows players they live in a dynamic, changing world
- Once established (~1 month of sessions), you can expand with additional details

### Rules for Settlement Design
- **Less is more** — don't overcomplicate with complex histories, factions, neighborhoods
- Start with ONE tone + ONE story detail
- Level of detail should grow proportional to screen time
- Repetition is key — tone gets established over time with repeated applications
- Settlements get most screen time when players first arrive; taper off after

## Three Example Settlements

### Underthumb (Oppressed)
**Tone:** Oppressed — crushing weight, broken spirits, hopelessness
**Story Detail:** Under military occupation by a rival lord's army

### Powderkeg (Tense)
**Tone:** Tense — powder keg, ticking time bomb, storm on the horizon
**Story Detail:** Also under military occupation, but the people haven't given in. Two big angry dogs on opposite sides of a flimsy fence.

### Bucolia (Content)
**Tone:** Content — things are going right, no complaints
**Story Detail:** Blessed by a god of home/hearth/community, founded by a priest, nice temple in the middle

## Technique 1: Sneaky Narration (Show, Don't Tell)

Every scene-setting and every transition is an opportunity to convey tone. Build a **list of narrative details** — evidence someone would notice walking around — and sprinkle ONE into every scene or transition.

### Underthumb Details
- Soldiers standing tall, chatting, making fun of passers-by
- People giving soldiers wide berth, heads down, no eye contact
- Quiet streets, people shuffling, going about business without joy
- Shopkeepers don't hawk wares, just wait to be noticed
- Inn: sullen silence except raucous soldiers getting handsy with barmaid
- People hurrying away from any commotion (not running — running gets you chased)

### Powderkeg Details
- Everyone alert, on guard, hands on weapons
- Soldiers in tight groups, civilians in tight groups, space between them
- Everyone quiet — listening for signs of trouble
- Almost no business being conducted
- Knots of citizens muttering darkly, eyeing soldiers hatefully
- Inn divided between whispering citizens and tense soldiers, no one relaxing
- Everyone probably armed

### Bucolia Details
- People relaxed, friendly, chatty, know each other
- Smiles, hellos, lingering conversations
- Nice to strangers, charitable, benefit of the doubt
- Lots of kids everywhere, strong family values
- God's symbols/colors prominent, traditional knickknacks (like horseshoes over doors)
- Market: no transaction without an hour of small talk
- Inn: constant hellos, table-hopping, gossip
- People gawk at disasters instead of running — underestimate danger

**Rule:** One detail per scene/transition. Don't overdo it. Repetition breeds memory.

## Technique 2: Encounter Scenes (Do, Don't Show)

Better than showing: let players **interact directly** with the tone. Create scenes whose sole purpose is making players respond to the settlement's character.

### Underthumb Scene
Soldiers loudly insulting/harassing the barmaid. No one steps up. Players might intervene — but the landlord and barmaid beg them not to, even apologize to the soldiers. The scene shouldn't let PCs change the tone (that's what adventures are for).

### Powderkeg Scenes
- Three civilians follow a soldier into an alley with malice. PCs might help, diffuse, or arrive to find themselves standing over a corpse.
- PCs challenged on the street to declare allegiance. All groups — soldier and civilian — stop to watch and size up the newcomers.

### Bucolia Scenes
- Obnoxious fearless children taunting the PCs
- Asked to make a donation
- Caught by a gossip who wants to bend their ear for an hour
- Impromptu dance at the inn, PCs swept up in it
- A young villager develops a crush on a PC, making their current partner jealous

### Usage Rules
- Use frequently when players first arrive in a new town
- Taper to once every two sessions or so
- These scenes should NOT advance plot or build into adventures
- They exist solely to establish settlement character interactively
- Keep them under control — this is a game of adventure, not The Sims
