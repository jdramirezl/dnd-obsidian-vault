# 5 Simple Rules for Dating My Teenaged Skill System

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/five-simple-rules-for-dating-my-teenaged-skill-system/) | **Category**: Core GM Advice

---

## Key Advice

- **Players declare actions or ask questions — never skill names.** The player says what their character does in the fiction; the DM decides which skill (if any) applies.
- **Only roll when success is possible, failure is possible, AND failure carries a cost or risk.** If any of those three conditions is missing, skip the roll — auto-succeed or auto-fail.
- **One roll is usually enough.** Don't break an action into multiple rolls unless the situation changes between attempts. Re-evaluate Rule 2 before every subsequent roll.
- **Proactively give players knowledge they would have.** Don't make PCs "examine" things to trigger knowledge checks — if a character would recognize something on sight, tell them immediately.
- **Design encounters around approaches with pros and cons, not around skill names.** Each viable approach to a problem should have at least one reason to choose it and one reason to avoid it.

## Summary

This foundational article lays out five rules that govern when and how to use skill checks in any RPG system. The core philosophy is that dice should only hit the table when the outcome is genuinely uncertain and the stakes are real. If the party can just keep retrying with no consequence, there's no drama — just give them the success and move on. This eliminates the tedious "roll until you succeed" loop and the arbitrary "three strikes and you're out" retry penalties that some systems impose.

The article also attacks the common player habit of declaring skill names instead of fictional actions ("I roll Perception" vs. "I search the bookshelf for hidden compartments"). By forcing players to describe what their characters actually do, the DM retains the flexibility to assign the appropriate skill — or no skill at all — and players engage with the game world as a living place rather than a menu of buttons. This naturally leads to more creative problem-solving and richer roleplay.

Finally, the article argues that encounter design should focus on differentiating approaches rather than skills. When the party faces a guarded gate, the interesting question isn't "which skill modifier is highest?" but "do we bribe, bluff, intimidate, sneak, or persuade — and what are the trade-offs of each?" Each approach should carry distinct consequences regardless of success or failure, making the player's choice of method as important as whether they succeed.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Enforce the "no skill names" rule.** When a player says "I roll Perception," redirect them: "What is your character actually doing?" Use gentle correction (or sarcasm, if that's your table's style) until it becomes habit.
2. **Before calling for any roll, run the three-part test:** Can it succeed? Can it fail? Is there a cost or risk for failure? If any answer is "no," don't roll.
3. **Eliminate retry penalties.** Drop any house rule or system rule that imposes cumulative penalties for retrying. Instead, use the three-part test — if there's no cost to failure, auto-succeed; if there is, one roll handles it.
4. **Use passive knowledge scores.** Keep a list of each PC's knowledge skill modifiers. If the passive score (10 + modifier) meets the DC, the character simply knows the information — no roll needed.
5. **When designing skill encounters, list approaches (not skills) with at least one pro and one con each.** This becomes your prep notes for the encounter. Assign skills and DCs at the table based on what the players actually describe.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Only roll dice when it matters — when success, failure, and consequences are all on the table — and let the players describe actions, not invoke skill names.
