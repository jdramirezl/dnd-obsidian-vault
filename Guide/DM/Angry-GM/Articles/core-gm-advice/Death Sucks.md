# Death Sucks

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/death-sucks/)
**Date:** March 18, 2016

---

## Death Sucks

When a PC dies, the entire game grinds to a halt. The PCs face an ugly decision: continue with reduced numbers, or retreat and regroup? The player has to make a new character. A character represents investment — time, creative effort, emotional attachment. The GM loses tailored storylines and NPC connections, and often feels guilty.

**PC death absolutely sucks. It disrupts the entire game, it's emotional, and it creates extra work for everyone. Which is exactly how it SHOULD be.**

---

## A Case for Death Sucking

All those terrible things are what give death its impact. Death sucks — it's disruptive, emotional, unexpected, disastrous, frustrating, scary. If we're talking about a game truly about role-playing, all that crap helps the player and character find emotional synchrony.

Death is not the end of a story. Death is the beginning of a new story about a world that lost someone important. **The reason Superman isn't as impressive as Batman is because Superman is immortal. Batman is mortal. He COULD die. Death provides a context for heroism.**

The reason most people are NOT heroes is not training, talent, or resources. It's because they are not willing to risk their lives.

---

## Life Without Death

If you're trying to take away the sting of death, why leave it in the game at all? Just assume anyone that goes down during battle is stable. As long as the party pulls the fight out, they're alive.

That's okay. As long as you accept the characters will behave like immortals. Once PCs are immortal, they will never fear a threat again.

---

## Do Players Choose Their Risks?

A simple test:

> Cowardly goblins are bullied by a hobgoblin. The rogue wants to sneak up and knock out the hobgoblin. Can this work?

If your first thought is "they're cheating out of a fight," your players don't get to pick their fights. If your first thought is "cool, stealth check, Con save, Intimidate check," good for you.

If players don't choose whether they take life-or-death chances, death loses some of its meaning.

---

## Coping with Death

There's value in NOT having a standard operating procedure. When death happens, I want it to mess everything up.

### Dead Player Playing
Get the player generating a new character right away. Don't delay with busy work.

### Moving On?
Give PCs the choice of continuing or retreating. Make retreat an adventure in itself. Don't let session time make the decision for the characters.

### A New Life
Priority ONE: get the new PC into the game ASAP. The new PC is constrained by the current campaign's realities — that's the one penalty for death.

**The ONE penalty.** New PC should match the dead PC's level and XP. Don't PUNISH players for dead PCs. They've been punished enough.

---

## A Case for Punishing Death

Death CAN carry a cost in games where the challenge element is primary — megadungeons where "earning a high level character" is part of the game. Otherwise, no reason to attach painful costs.

---

## Three Alternatives to Normal Death

### Schrodinger's PC
Death saves are rolled in complete secrecy — even the GM doesn't know. When someone examines the dying character, the GM asks the player privately: "alive or dead?" The player can give any answer regardless of dice. Each player decides for themselves whether to deal with death.

### Scars of Peter Molyneux
PCs don't die. Failed death saves mean critical injury — coma, 24 hours to recover. The character gets a lasting scar or penalty: reduced speed, -1 to a save or skill, etc. A player can choose to retire rather than play a scarred mess.

### The New-U Station
PCs are bound to a magical artifact and cannot die. Death means resurrection — naked and screaming — at the artifact in 1d3 days. The campaign involves unraveling WHY they keep coming back and protecting the secret.

One player exclaimed: "I wish we could just DIE when we die. That would be easier." **Death sucks, but if you run your game right, immortality is worse.**
