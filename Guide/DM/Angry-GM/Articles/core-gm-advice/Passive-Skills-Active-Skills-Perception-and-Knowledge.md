## Key Advice

- **Active skills are triggered by deliberate player actions.** Searching a room, probing a conversation—the character chooses to do a thing. Roll when they act.
- **Passive skills are target numbers, not player rolls.** Passive Perception is the DC that a hiding creature's Stealth check must beat. The goblin fails to hide, not the PC "succeeds at noticing."
- **Think of traps and secret doors as having "pre-rolled" Stealth checks.** The creator concealed the trap at some point in the past. That DC is the trap's Stealth result, checked against Passive Perception.
- **Perception and Insight are parallel.** Perception detects hidden physical things; Insight detects hidden social things (lies, bluffs, mind control). Both can be active or passive.
- **Active Insight means probing a conversation.** Asking leading questions, watching for contradictions, making accusations to gauge reactions. GMs rarely use this but should.
- **Knowledge checks are inherently passive.** You either remember something or you don't—there's no "active" brain-flexing. The GM should call for Knowledge checks automatically when a character sees something they might know about.
- **Players should never have to ask for Knowledge checks.** The moment a character encounters something they might recognize, the GM calls for the roll. Waiting for "do I know what that is?" is bad GMing.
- **Knowledge can be active too—as research.** Arcana isn't just recall; it's knowing how to use libraries, find experts, and conduct experiments. Religion means knowing which scriptures to consult. GMs rarely use this dimension.
- **The system is inconsistent.** Sometimes the actor rolls, sometimes the target rolls, sometimes nobody rolls. D&D was built around action resolution but never figured out the difference between actions and things that just happen.

## Summary

Active skills trigger when a character deliberately does something (searching, probing, investigating). Passive skills are target numbers that something else rolls against—Passive Perception is the DC a hiding goblin's Stealth must beat, not a roll the PC makes. Traps and secret doors can be thought of as having "pre-rolled" Stealth checks from their creators. The system breaks down because D&D inconsistently assigns who rolls: sometimes the actor, sometimes the target, sometimes nobody. Knowledge checks are inherently passive (you remember or you don't) and should be called by the GM automatically—never waiting for players to ask. Knowledge can also be active in the form of research: knowing how to find answers through libraries, experts, and experimentation, though GMs rarely use this. Insight works like social Perception and can be both passive (noticing lies automatically) and active (deliberately probing a conversation).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Treat Passive Perception as a DC for others to beat**, not as a roll the PC makes. When a goblin hides, it rolls Stealth against the party's Passive Perception scores.
2. **Think of trap/secret door DCs as pre-rolled Stealth.** Compare them against Passive Perception automatically as characters move through the area.
3. **Call for Knowledge checks proactively.** When a character encounters something they might know about, YOU initiate the roll. Don't wait for "do I know what that is?"
4. **Use active Insight.** When a player says they want to probe a conversation—asking leading questions, watching for tells—that's an active Insight check, distinct from passive lie detection.
5. **Expand Knowledge skills beyond recall.** Let characters use Arcana to research in libraries, Religion to consult scriptures, Nature to identify tracks through experimentation. Knowledge is a field of study, not just a memory bank.
6. **Don't let active rolls go below passive scores in practical effect.** If Passive Perception already detected the goblin, the active search is moot—the goblin was already spotted.
7. **Accept the system's inconsistency** and make rulings. Sometimes the actor rolls, sometimes the target rolls. Pick whichever makes more sense for the situation and be consistent within your game.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Passive skills are target numbers for other things to roll against, not rolls the PC makes. Active skills trigger from deliberate player actions. Call for Knowledge checks yourself—don't wait for players to press the "do I know?" button. And remember: the D&D skill system is conceptually messy, so make clear rulings and stick with them.
