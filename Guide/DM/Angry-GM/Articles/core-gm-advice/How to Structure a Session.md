# How to Structure a Session

Sessions are the real "episodes" of your game — not adventures. No one writes adventures as if they'll be chopped across multiple sessions, but the players' brains treat each session as the unit of experience. Good session structure is the difference between an okay game and a great one.

---

## The Session Is a Metagame Concept

The game's narrative breaks down into campaigns, arcs, and adventures — story-based divisions. But sessions are time-based: whatever fits into 3–5 hours. Sessions don't care about story boundaries. The players will view sessions — not adventures — as the episodes of the game.

The game has a nested, parallel narrative structure at every level: campaigns, arcs, adventures, encounters, and individual actions all follow the same beginning → middle → resolution shape. Sessions should too.

---

## Running a Well-Structured Session

### Know What Your Players Can Get Done

Every GM should know how much stuff their group gets through in a typical session. How many scenes? How long does combat take? How long do they argue over obstacles? This lets you plan content that fits the session and create natural chokepoints.

### Know When Your Session Ends — Always End Early

Set a definite end time and never hit it. You'll lose 10–20 minutes because you need to find the right ending point. That's by design.

### Vary the Gameplay

If your entire session consists of just one thing — all combat, all "role-playing," all interaction — you're running a bad session. Pace and structure require variety: slow climbs, fast falls, rising and falling tension and stakes from scene to scene.

### Administration and Bookkeeping First

When players arrive, let them be social briefly, then handle all non-game business:
- Clarify rules
- Buy equipment
- Schedule next session
- Hand out XP from last session

Goals:
1. Nothing non-game happens after the game starts
2. Nothing remains to do after the game ends

This also transitions players from "social mode" to "game mode."

### The Recap

- Done at the **start** of the session (not via email or newsletter)
- **Brief** — hit the high points relevant to the current session: the goal, why it matters, relevant villains, NPCs, locations
- Done by **the GM, not the players** — you control emphasis, foreshadowing, and tone

### Start with Incitement

Every session begins with the players reacting to an incitement to act by making a **new decision**. Not a "press A to continue" non-decision.

Key principles:
- **Skip ahead** to the first interesting thing if needed — don't start with the party resting or traveling uneventfully
- Handle transitions from last session during bookkeeping: "You were going to rest, so heal up and prepare spells now"
- Don't start with random encounters or unimportant events
- If players want to change plans during the skip, they'll interrupt — that's fine, it still pulls them into the game

### End on a Story Beat

Once the end of the session approaches, look for a good stopping point. End there, even if it's early. Leaving players wanting more is far better than leaving them exhausted.

**Best ending points (in order):**
1. **A major victory** — the party accomplishes something significant (best ending)
2. **A defeat or reversal** — something big and bad happens; end on the reveal
3. **An incitement to new action** — end right before the next big thing; "you arrive at the dungeon… and we'll continue next session"

Cliffhangers are actually incitements or reversals — they're not the best ending, just a subset.

**Special case — adventure/arc/campaign ending:** Never separate the denouement from the ending. If the party beats the dragon, don't make them wait until next session to return to town, celebrate, count treasure, and tally XP. This is the only time bookkeeping should happen at the end.

### The Non-Denouement

For sessions that don't end the story, the best denouement is a **non-denouement**: leave the story hanging open and let the players transition themselves out of the game.

As players pack up, they naturally reflect on and discuss what happened. This fixes the game in their minds. Let it happen:
- **Don't engage** in post-session discussion — stay quiet, listen, answer simple questions
- **Don't put anything** between the final story beat and the players' departure — no bookkeeping, no admin

---

## Summary

Good session structure comes down to three things:

1. **Start right** — bookkeeping first, recap, then incitement to action
2. **End right** — on a story beat (victory > reversal > incitement)
3. **Mix up the middle** — vary gameplay types, pace, tension, and stakes
