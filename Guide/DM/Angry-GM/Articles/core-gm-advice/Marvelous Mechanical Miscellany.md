# The Angry GM's Marvelous Mechanical Miscellany

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/marvelous-mechanical-miscellany/)
**Date:** November 14, 2016
**Series:** Improve Your Improv

---

## Overview

A toolkit for improvising mechanical outcomes in D&D 3.5, Pathfinder, and D&D 5E. Covers how to set DCs, modifiers, damage, save DCs, attack rolls, and complex encounter tracking on the fly — plus how to rip off existing game elements for ad hoc effects.

---

## Core Principles

### Close Enough Is Good Enough
Three GMs setting a Climb DC at 10, 12, and 15 are all fine. One at 5, one at 30, and one saying "impossible" — someone is very wrong. Consistency matters; precision doesn't.

**Practice:** Never open a rulebook at the table for DCs. Make it up. Get close. Be happy.

### Logic Before Rules
Use the action adjudication flow: What is the player trying to accomplish? How? Can it succeed? Can it fail? Is there a cost preventing retries? Only THEN use rules. Your logic always wins.

### Trust Your Gut
If something smells funny — breaks verisimilitude, feels unfair, violates the spirit of the rules — it's probably bad. Don't feel you have to explain yourself. Just go with your gut.

## Ability Checks and Skills

**Everything is an ability check.** Attack rolls and skill checks are just ability checks with extra modifiers. The process:

1. **Pick ONE ability score** based on what the character is doing:
   - **Strength:** Raw muscle — climbing, jumping, shoving, breaking
   - **Dexterity:** Agility, coordination, fine motor — balance, lockpicking, acrobatics
   - **Constitution:** Health, endurance, resilience — holding breath, resisting transformation, marching
   - **Intelligence:** Figuring out and remembering — logic, reason, recollection of facts
   - **Wisdom:** Awareness, perception, willpower — senses, intuition, resisting fear
   - **Charisma:** Social interaction — any character influencing another character

2. **Decide if ONE area of training applies:**
   - 3.5/PF: Skill ranks, Base Attack Bonus, or Combat Maneuver Bonus
   - 5E: Proficiency bonus (skills, tools, or weapons used as intended)

This lets you separate raw ability from training. Example: intimidating a peasant with a sword demonstration = Charisma check + BAB/Proficiency (combat training applies to the social interaction).

## Modifiers

### 3.5 and Pathfinder
For each circumstance: **+2 or -2** (the Golden Rule). Minor circumstances: +1/-1. Major circumstances: **+5 or -5**. Stack multiple modifiers.

### 5E
One positive or negative circumstance: **+2 or -2**. Multiple or major circumstances: **Advantage or Disadvantage**. One modifier only — Advantage/Disadvantage is mathematically ~+5 average.

## Difficulty Class

### 5E (Simple)
- DC 10 = Easy for a hero
- DC 15 = Medium
- DC 20 = Hard

That's basically all you need. DC 5 is too easy to roll on; beyond 20 is rare.

### 3.5 and Pathfinder
1. **Base difficulty:** Easy = 5, Moderate = 10, Difficult = 15
2. **Benefits from training?** Add +5
3. **Is it "leveled"?** (Would the task reasonably be harder because of when it occurs in the game — e.g., a tyrant's locks vs. a peasant's door?) If yes, add the PC's average level

**Examples:**
- Breaking a door: Moderate (10), no training benefit, leveled in enemy stronghold → DC 10 + party level
- Complex puzzle-lock: Difficult (15), benefits from training (+5), leveled → DC 20 + party level

## Damage

### The System
1. **Pick baseline:** d6 (low), d8 (medium), d10 (high)
2. **Continuous/repeating?** Reduce die size by one step
3. **Leveled?** Roll half as many dice as PC levels (minimum 1)
4. **Not leveled?** Roll 2 dice
5. **Hits multiple characters?** Halve the number of dice

**Examples:**
- Campfire (low, continuous, not leveled): 2d4/round
- Hellfire explosion trap at level 8 (high, one-shot, leveled, multi-target): 4d10 → halved to 2d10
- Toxic frostfire cloud at level 5 (medium, continuous, leveled, multi-target): 2d6 → halved to 1d6/round

## Save DCs and Attack Rolls

### Save DCs
- **3.5/PF:** Moderate save = 12 + half PC level. Adjust ±5 for easy/hard
- **5E:** 10 + current proficiency bonus. Use modifiers for difficulty

### Attack Rolls
- **3.5/PF:** Use party level as attack bonus. Add +4 for accurate attacks
- **5E:** Not explicitly covered; use proficiency + relevant modifier

## The Ten-Point Scale

For improvised complex encounters (negotiations, trials, rituals, encounter traps):

1. Assume a **ten-point scale**, start at **5**
2. Successful actions add **1-2 points**
3. Failures lose progress
4. Reach **10** = goal achieved
5. Drop to **0** = scene lost, beyond salvaging

**Rules:**
- Some failures shouldn't cost progress — use logic
- An opposing force can shift the score the other way
- There MUST always be a way for the score to drop to zero, or success is guaranteed and you're wasting time
- Adjust difficulty with smaller/larger scales or different starting points
- In practice, scales of ~6 work well (3 successes, 3 failures)

## Ripping Off Magical Items, Feats, and Spells

For ad hoc effects and consequences, steal from existing game elements:

- Restore a war shrine → blessing that works like a +1 weapon
- Dig up blackmail → model as *charm person* effect
- Earn merchant guild respect → benefits of the Negotiator feat
- Gain a boon from librarians → *legend lore* spell effect

### Balance Rules
1. Don't give anything wildly inappropriate for character level
2. Anything you wouldn't put in a treasure horde needs limits (specific circumstances, specific NPCs, etc.)
3. Using existing game elements won't break the game more than the designers already did

> Players eat this up. Give them "The Respect of the Guild of Merchants: +2 to Diplomacy and Sense Motive with guild members" and they'll think you're a genius who plans for everything.

## Key Takeaways

- Everything is an ability check — separate ability from training
- DCs: 10/15/20 in 5E; 5/10/15 + training (+5) + level (if leveled) in 3.5/PF
- Damage: pick a die, adjust for frequency/leveling/multi-target
- The Ten-Point Scale handles any improvised complex encounter
- Steal from existing game elements for ad hoc effects — players love named rewards
- Close enough is good enough; logic before rules; trust your gut
