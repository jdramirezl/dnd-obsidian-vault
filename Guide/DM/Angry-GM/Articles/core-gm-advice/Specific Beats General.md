# Specific Beats General: Streamlining Action Adjudication

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/specific-beats-general/)
**Date:** December 22, 2021
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## Overview

This article introduces **general approaches** — pre-adjudicated default methods a GM uses to handle common, repetitive actions — and argues that players should always be encouraged to break out of them with specific, clever plans. General approaches streamline play but must never replace creative player agency.

---

## What Is a General Approach?

A general approach is a macro: a whole sequence of gameplay actions and mechanical resolutions triggered by a single player declaration. It handles things that come up often and work the same way every time.

**Example — Selling Treasure:**
> When the party is in a settlement, one or more party members can spend one day of downtime selling non-cash treasures. They visit markets, shops, collectors, and fences. No checks needed, no interactions played out. At the end of the day, the GM narrates the results. Unusual or overly expensive items might not find buyers. Magical items cannot be sold this way unless mistaken for mundane.

**Example — Searching a Room:**
> Compare passive Perception scores to hidden things' DCs. Describe everything perceived in scene-setting. If a PC searches, they roll a single Wisdom (Perception) check compared to each undiscovered thing's DC. A single good roll reveals everything findable. The search is a time-consuming action.

General approaches also arise from player behavior. The third time players search a door for traps, listen through it, peer through the keyhole, and test the lock, that becomes their automated "general door-searching approach."

---

## Why General Approaches Are Dangerous

They share the same problem as skill lists: they train the brain to stop imagining options not on the list.

> The first thing you tell someone about RPGs is "you can do anything you can imagine." The last thing you want is to sabotage that with pre-programmed possibilities.

### Rules for Safe Use

- **Tell players a general approach exists**, but don't spell out full mechanics
- **Don't hand players a list** of approaches as menu options
- **Make it clear there are better options** — general approaches yield average outcomes, not optimal
- **Keep the mindset:** "If I'm using general approaches, my players are playing wrong" (never say this aloud)

---

## Specific Beats General

When players change the parameters — when they get specific — the GM must actually think instead of running on autopilot.

### Ways to Break Out of General Approaches

1. **Take a risk** — Haggle for a better price, but risk a worse outcome on failure
2. **Accept a tradeoff** — Sell quickly for a lower price
3. **Get creative** — Run a scam, leverage world knowledge, find a specific buyer
4. **Use world knowledge correctly** — Drop a guildmaster's name for advantage, donate to a temple for services instead of gold

### Applied to Searching

- **General:** Roll one Perception check, find everything above the DC
- **Specific search:** "I search the bookshelf for a book that might be a secret switch" → advantage on the check (but won't find the trapped flagstone)
- **Very specific:** "I try to move each torch bracket to see if either is a switch" → auto-success, no roll needed

---

## Key Takeaways

- General approaches are **default, mindless actions** — what players do when they can't think of anything better
- They should yield **average, fair outcomes** — never optimal
- "You can get something quick, cheap, or good — but not all three"
- Specific player actions should always have the **possibility of a better, different, or more interesting outcome**
- Action adjudication still applies — clever plans still require die rolls when success and failure are both possible
- When players use world knowledge correctly, reward them — but faulty assumptions cause plans to fail
