# Gaming on the Clock — Running a Game in a Time Slot

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/gaming-on-the-clock/)
**Date:** October 31, 2022
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

Running RPG sessions in very specific, very tight time slots is tricky. Whether you run convention games, game store events, a game club, or a West Marches style drop-in game, you've got to know how to cram a complete scenario into however many hours you've got and not one minute more.

## Making an Ass of Your Players: Expectations and Assumptions

Managing Player Expectations isn't your job in a time-slot game; don't try. If the players have the wrong expectations, that's on them. Running the game you promised for all the players is way more important than making any one player happy.

When you're running a Time-Slot Game, you're on the Clock. The Clock is king. Not your players. Not even you. The Clock's needs trump everyone's because the Clock ensures the players with the right expectations get the game they signed up for.

### What Players Should Expect

1. **Low-complexity game with limited options.**
2. **A focused, undistracted game.** No flipping through rulebooks for ten minutes. The GM handles all rules calls. Keeping the game flowing is more important than following the rules — close enough is good enough.
3. **A somewhat guided — maybe even linear — gameplay experience.** Players won't be allowed to go too far off the rails or waste an hour shopping.
4. **A team sport.** Sabotaging the team means getting told off.
5. **Play whatever character they're handed** without complaint or substitution requests.

### What You Should Expect

1. **Assume one or more players won't know how to play.** Expect to teach the game.
2. **You are a Mentor and Ambassador.** Your first job is to turn less experienced players into enthusiastic RPG fans.
3. **Do not expect players to bring anything.** Bring cheap pads, pencils, dice, pregens, and all game materials yourself.
4. **Expect any player who brings their own character to cause a problem.** Bring enough pregens for everyone and make every player use one.

## How To Make a Time-Slot Adventure

### Five Scenes or Five Encounters

The magic number is **five**.

**Site-based adventure (dungeon crawl):** A beginning scene, an ending scene, and a dungeon with five significant encounters (including an introductory encounter and a climax). The dungeon can have 5–10 rooms total, but only five contain encounters. Two or three should contain interesting lore/minor treasures. The rest can be empty.

**Event-based adventure:** Five scenes total. Scene 1 is the opening (setup). Scene 5 is the closing (resolution). The penultimate scene contains the climax. Scene 2 contains the incitement. Each middle scene can have one or two encounters or events.

### Everything's Optional: Modular Encounters

There are only three non-optional adventure bits:
1. **The climax** — a conflict that resolves the adventure
2. **The beginning** — a goal the players know about
3. **A resolution** — players know how the climax fulfilled or cost them their goal

Everything else is optional. Don't build an adventure that requires anything more than a beginning, an ending, and a climax. Assume anything else might get skipped.

### Simple Branching Paths

Two good ways to build branching paths:
1. **Pick Your Goal** — An early choice defines which challenges the party faces or which goal they pursue.
2. **Looped Branch** — Two paths that lead to the same place (e.g., stealth challenge vs. combat challenge).

Combining both gives you four adventures from one-and-a-half adventures of writing.

## Running a Time-Slot Game

### Setup and Breakdown

Whatever time slot you've got is literally all the time you get. Skip elaborate gaming props. Pack so you can drop stuff on the table and start.

### Be a Clockwatcher

Have a clock handy. Keep a timeline in your head:
- Saying goodbye and breaking down: 10–15 minutes
- Resolution breathing room: 10–15 minutes
- **Climax must end 20–30 minutes before session end**
- **Climax must start ~1 hour before session end**

If things run long, wrap them up. Fudge, narrate the ending, cut encounters — whatever you've got to do.

### Distributing Pregens

**Do not** let players see a pregen before they've decided to play it. **Do not** let the table discuss character choices as a group.

1. Prepare one-line descriptions. Read them aloud, placing a mini/token/card for each.
2. Pause one beat. If someone blurts a preference, hand it to them.
3. Point at remaining players one at a time and ask them to choose.
4. If they won't choose, choose for them. "Fighter it is. Next!"

### Cutscening

Cutscening is telling the players what happens to their characters without their input. It's ugly but necessary for:
- Opening and closing scenes (scene-setting, mission-setup, resolution)
- Ending encounters early or skipping entire scenes when the clock demands it

> "Good job, your plan succeeded; let me tell you what it looked like."

No dice rolls needed. No choices. No input. Just narration to keep the game on schedule.

## Conventions Always Leave You Feeling Dirty

The constraints necessary to make convention/club events happen are all terrible for RPGs. Angry's Time-Slot Gaming Bestest Practices involve things you know are just plain wrong — not managing expectations, rushing character choices, taking away agency. But if you handle it the way it has to be handled, everyone's still going to have a great time.
