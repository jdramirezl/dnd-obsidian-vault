# How to Code a Scene

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-code-a-scene/)
**Date:** February 26, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## The Concept

Every adventure is a series of interconnected scenes (rooms). Each scene is a container for the stuff in it. The adventure is a list of interconnected rooms. If you understand the format, you can create scenes and fill them with stuff.

This isn't prep instruction — it's the anatomy of what a scene needs to function. Whether you write it down or improvise it is up to you. Improvisation is just designing on the fly; you still have to design.

## Scenes vs. Locations vs. Events

Before planning scenes, decide: are your scenes **locations** (physical rooms with physical exits) or **events** (things that happen, where the same location can host multiple scenes)?

Location-based adventures tend to have many minor scenes (e.g., "the room with two exits" — just a choice point). Event-based adventures rarely have empty scenes like that.

## The Scene Checklist

### 1. Name

Give your scene a name. Locations: "Sentry Cave," "Moonlit Grotto," "Baltac's Trading Post." Events: "Battle at the Gate," "Meeting the Innkeeper," "Chasing the Assassin." It helps more than you think.

### 2. Purpose

Why does this scene exist? What will the PCs get out of it?

- Learn about the dungeon?
- Meet an important NPC?
- Make a decision?
- Get exposition?
- Choose between making a friend or foe?
- Rest, shop, gain resources?
- Overcome an obstacle? Slay an enemy? Gain information?

**Be specific.** Not "gain information" but "learn the weaknesses of the pigs of Durotar." Not "meet NPC" but "introduce the Legitimate Businessman who will later turn out to be the villain."

**Do not continue if you can't explain why your scene exists.** There are almost no bad reasons for a scene to exist. But no reason is a bad reason.

### 3. Setting

Where does the scene take place? How far afield can it wander? You need to be able to visualize it well enough to describe it to players. Write down as much as you need — a prose description, a few adjectives, or just a name like "Moonlit Grotto" or "Windswept Bridge."

### 4. Interactive Bits

The things in the scene players can interact with: NPCs, runes, magical gates, treasure chests, levers, bridges, statues. Interactive bits serve the scene's purpose.

**Key principles:**
- If the purpose is "learn about swine," you need a source of that information (a book, a swineherd)
- If an NPC will never be in combat, you don't need combat stats — just the information they'll impart
- If you want to inject life, give the NPC some personality
- Think about what purpose each interactive bit serves in the scene and the game
- **Don't try to plan everything** — tables, chairs, and common objects exist implicitly
- Beware of overthinking AND underthinking

### 5. Establishing Shot

The first bit of description players get about a scene. It must establish:
- The **setting** (where they are)
- The **goal or purpose** (what's happening)
- The **explicit interactive bits** (what they can interact with)

If the scene is an encounter, also call attention to the **source of conflict** and explicitly state the goal.

Example:
> "You're in the one room hovel of the swineherd. Canvas tent with giant monster ribs supporting it. The smell of bacon rises from the cookpot. The dirty orc swineherd offers you cups of bacon tea. 'So, you want to know about Durotar swine? Sit down and I'll rustle us up some grub.' You notice a sequined costume and feathered boa draped over one chair, with a sewing kit beside it."

### 6. Hidden Interactive Bits

Things players must discover: hidden chests, traps, assassins, specific books among hundreds. For each hidden bit, figure out:
- What it is and what it does
- **How the PCs can discover it** (Passive Perception threshold, active search DC, triggering it by walking over it, pulling aside the carpet, etc.)
- **How it's hidden** — understanding the mechanism lets you handle any player action

### 7. Goals, Prizes, and Takeaways

What will the PCs leave the scene with? What might they leave with? How do they acquire it?
- Write out the information they learn
- Figure out treasure amounts
- Detail magical items, what they do, what they're worth

### 8. Outcomes, Exits, and Transitions

Where does the game go once the scene is done?

- **Outcome-dependent:** "If the PCs win, they continue along the trail. If they lose, survivors are dragged to the Orc Camp."
- **Player-choice:** After the scene, players choose where to go next (three hallways, visit the suspect or the crime lab)
- **Open structure:** Players can go anywhere from anywhere; only specify when something changes the structure
- **Hidden transitions:** Secret doors, clues that open new paths, NPCs who grant access only if conditions are met

Players need to know their options. State possibilities at the end of the scene: "He gave you a suspect's address, but his evidence needs analysis at the crime lab. And you still haven't visited the police chief."

## The Basic Code (Summary)

Every scene needs:

1. **Name**
2. **Purpose**
3. **Setting**
4. **Interactive Bits**
5. **Establishing Shot**
6. **Hidden Interactive Bits**
7. **Takeaways**
8. **Exits/Transitions**

That's the framework. The devil is in the details of designing the interactive bits, takeaways, exits, and settings — that's where things really shine.
