# But I Digress… Structure

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/but-i-digress-structure/)
**Date:** March 6, 2024

---

This is a digression — expanding on something mentioned in a True Campaign Managery lesson about Structure. It's useful if you're planning to homebrew a campaign, especially as you set your Campaign Vision.

## Structure: What Is?

Structure describes how the bits and pieces of your campaign or game fit together. If you step back way far from your game and squint, Structure is the shape your campaign would sorta take.

But Structure is a **descriptive** thing. You can't define it in concrete terms. You can only describe it. It's very complex and nuanced, so you can only compare Structures by broad similarities. Like looking at faces in clouds — you can't talk in terms of water molecules and air currents. Two clouds might both have old man faces, but no two are quite alike.

## Structure: Why Matters?

**Pacing** is pretty much the thing that separates good games from crap games. Pacing isn't about speed — it's about the ebb and flow of emotional content. How Tension rises and falls, how you foreshadow events, how you resolve mysteries. Pacing is about how your players' emotions pull them through the game.

Structure describes how different elements join up — how your game moves from one thing to the next. **Structure and Pacing are totally intertwined.** Structure defines the large-scale Pace of your campaign. While moment-by-moment gameplay determines how players feel about a particular encounter, Structure drives how they feel about entire adventures and campaigns.

## Structure: Don't Ignore

If you choose an open, sandboxy, player-driven Structure, you're abdicating control over long-term pace to your players and their whims. That's a crapshoot — leaving it up to random, dumbass players to stumble into a game that feels good to play.

Since Campaign Management is about setting your game up for long-term success, keeping at least a finger on the Structure wheel will only do you good.

## Structure: Don't Overthink

You can overdo Structure. In a drive to force your game to conform to a predefined Structure, you can crush the freedom and openness from your game. Structure is descriptive, not prescriptive. You're better off being *aware* of Structure as you develop your Campaign Vision than trying to force one.

The more you get used to looking at Structure, the more your games will just *come out* well-structured. Some people are naturally good storytellers because they naturally fit their stories to the right shape without thinking about it.

## A Tale of Three Structures

Games have three different Structures to be aware of. All are important.

### The Shape of the Gameplay

**Gameplay Structure** is about what the players are doing — how gameplay switches between different ways of playing. Modes of Play and Gameplay Loops.

Example: a standard fantasy adventure campaign. Players explore a town and gather information until they find a quest. They prepare, trek across the wilderness, delve a dungeon, then hike back to town. Exploring a Town, Preparing for Adventure, Trekking the Wilderness, and Delving the Dungeon are different Modes of Play. How they're arranged and how players move between them is Structure.

Structure is **fractal** — it looks the same at every scale. Exploring a Town mirrors the campaign loop: get a lay of the land, find points of interest, pursue something, interact to complete a goal, then look around again.

The Structure described above is a **Gameplay Loop** — it ends where it begins. That's not the only kind. 4X strategy games have Explore, Expand, Exploit, Exterminate. Even open-world games like *Breath of the Wild* have different Modes if you look closely.

Gameplay Structure is about recognizing that it's not so much the *game* that switches Modes as the players' brains.

### The Shape of the Story

Every game is also a story — a narrative. Not because games are special, but because everything we do as humans is a story. Every experience leaves a narrative behind. The stories that endure are the ones our brains can fit into the right shape. That shape is **Narrative Structure**.

Terms like Three-Act Story, Kishōtenketsu, and the Hero's Journey describe different Narrative Structures.

While Gameplay Structure tends toward nested loops and cycles, **Narrative Structures tend toward rising and falling action.** Narrative Structure is also fractal — stories have rising action, climaxes, and resolutions, and so do individual scenes and character actions.

### The Shape of the Scenario

**Scenario Structure** is about how the individual elements of the game's challenge are splayed out and how the players navigate them. The map of a dungeon is a diagram of its Scenario Structure. The specific path players follow — or the sum of all possible paths — and the things they deal with on that path.

Terms like Event-Based, Site-Based, Branching Paths, Gauntlets, and Open Worlds describe Scenario Structures. They're all about how players pick their paths through the game.

While Gameplay Structures settle into nested loops and Narrative Structures are wobbly rising trendlines, **Scenario Structures are often depicted as flow charts.** Every adventure is a dungeon.

## The Terminology Trap

Don't fall into the Terminology Trap. Terms like Open-World, Adventure of the Week, Gameplay Loop, 4X, and Three-Act Story are useful for broadly lumping together similar Structures, but they're far too broad for actual analysis or planning. They shouldn't be viewed as templates.

**Never use jargon to describe your game's Structure.** You need at least a paragraph — several paragraphs — to describe a structure usefully. Those jargony terms just say "those paragraphs you wrote have some broad similarities to mine."

## The Three That Are One

A campaign is nothing more than a string of events — "Here's what happens; what do you do?" Thus, a campaign has but one Structure. It's all just about how one event follows another. But those three Structures are all present, hidden in the same tangled mass of actions, resolutions, choices, and transitions.

Designing an adventure or campaign is about interweaving this — building one Structure that works as a Gameplay Structure, a Narrative Structure, and a Scenario Structure.

**Why do Boss Fights work?**
- **Gameplay Structure:** Part of a cycle of gathering information/resources, building power, and overcoming challenges
- **Narrative Structure:** The climax that resolves the final conflict — the boss is the source of conflict or the last obstacle
- **Scenario Structure:** The culmination of a path the players chose to prepare themselves for the challenge

**Why does Adventure of the Week work?**
- It's the Gameplay Loop described above
- Each adventure acts as a self-contained Narrative and Scenario
- Adventures naturally have incitements, rising actions, climaxes, and resolutions

The takeaway: be aware of these three Structures. Think about your home games and favorite video games. Try to describe their Structures in prose, not terminology. Get good at recognizing them. And absolutely do not ask for a list of adventure and campaign structures so you can Mad-Lib yourself to a game.
