# Maps: What's On Your Table?

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Maps: What's On Your Table?](https://theangrygm.com/maps-whats-on-your-table/)
**Date:** November 25, 2019
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Related:** [[Lost Player Skills - Mapping]], [[Lost Player Skills - Calling]]

---

## Summary

Most GMs are either over-reliant on maps (75%) or have thrown them out entirely (24%). The right approach is to understand the three types of maps, use each for its intended purpose, and resist the urge to map everything.

---

## The Confusion

New and transitioning GMs are confused about maps because:
- Published adventures are full of gorgeous maps that seem like they *must* be shown to players
- Virtual tabletops make it effortless to share massive maps with fog-of-war
- Social media showcases expensive gaming setups (projectors, 3D terrain, custom tables)

**Key insight:** Most maps in a module are for the GM, not the players. They're flowcharts of the adventure dressed up to be pretty.

---

## Three Types of Maps

### 1. Tactical Map
**Purpose:** Resolve combat. A to-scale map with tokens showing positions during a fight.

- Needs to be clear and clean — show only what affects combat
- Abstract symbols and line-drawings are fine
- Extraneous artsy details are unnecessary and can be confusing
- Players need to know: where they can stand, what blocks movement/line of sight, what hinders or hurts them, and where interactable things are
- **That's it.** The tactical map is D&D combat chess.

Since D&D 2000+, the rules assume precise position tracking on a square grid. The *form* doesn't matter (minis, gummy bears, graph paper) — arguing about form is useless.

### 2. Exploration Map
**Purpose:** Help players understand the layout of a space they're exploring.

- Very optional — Angry recommends **ditching it entirely**
- Hard to use at a real table (requires gradual reveal, eats space and time)
- VTTs make it pull double duty as the tactical map, creating confusion
- If you must have one, use a **minimap** — a hasty schematic in a corner of the battlemat
- **Better solution:** Have the players draw their own map (see [[Lost Player Skills - Mapping]])

### 3. Setting Map
**Purpose:** Ground players in the world. Show the region, kingdom, city, or town.

- Does NOT serve a mechanical purpose — don't track precise positions on city streets
- Combines establishing shot (TV/movies) with menu screen (video games)
- Drop it on the table and leave it while players are in that locale
- Evokes tone, reminds players where they are, shows what's available
- Usually just 1-2 maps needed: region + home base city/town

## From Module to Map: Practical Advice

### Identify What You Need
- **Tactical maps:** Only for locations where fights will happen. To-scale, with tokens.
- **Setting maps:** Region map and any major town/city. Print on a single sheet, laminate for long-term use.
- **Everything else:** Skip it. Maps of individual houses, neighborhoods, shops — it's eye candy.

### Choose a Tactical Map Solution
The important question: **Prepared in advance vs. generated at the table?**

- Prepared saves time during play but requires materials for all possible encounters
- On-the-fly saves prep time but requires speed and skill
- **You must be able to generate tactical maps on the fly** regardless — unexpected fights always happen

> **The Fundamental Paradox of Game Mastering:** Things always go better the more prepared you are, but preparation is inversely proportional to versatility.

### Ditch the Exploration Map
- Don't try to gradually reveal a full dungeon map at a real table
- If you want one, use a minimap (hasty schematic in a corner)
- Better yet, have the players keep their own map

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Three types of maps: tactical (combat), exploration (dungeon layout), and setting (world/region).
2. Tactical maps are for combat only. Keep them clear and functional, not artistic.
3. Setting maps are for grounding — drop one on the table and leave it. Don't use them mechanically.
4. Exploration maps should be ditched or replaced with player-drawn maps.
5. Most maps in a module are for the GM, not the players.
6. Be able to draw tactical maps on the fly. Versatility is essential.
7. Don't over-map. Use maps sparingly and only when they add to the experience.
