# How to Manage Combat Like a True Game Master

> [Source: The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/manage-combat-like-a-master/)

---

## Key Advice

- Combat management is about **Speed, Ownership, and Clarity** — the same skills as running the rest of the game, just with zero margin for error
- Use the **Declare-Determine-Describe (DDD) Cycle** rigorously — never skip a step, especially not the Describe step
- **Players declare intentions, not actions** — slap the dice out of their hands until they've told you *who* they're attacking and *how*
- **Minis and maps are visual aids, not narration** — you must be able to run any combat with nothing but your voice, dice, and paper
- **Monster turns aren't turns** — they're narration between player turns; resolve them as fast as possible
- **Telegraphing** is the single most important combat narration skill: use every bit of narration to reveal enemy Stats, Status, and Strategy
- Use narration to imply mechanics, never blurt out numbers — "your arrows bounce off its hide" beats "it's immune to piercing"
- **Transition smoothly** between turns — the end of one player's outcome description should flow directly into the next player's action invitation
- Don't let players describe their own kills ("How do you want to do this?" robs you of narrative control and useful information delivery)
- **Know your monsters** before the fight starts — review stat blocks, take notes, highlight key abilities

## Summary

Combat is the most mechanically rigorous part of any RPG, yet it must feel like the fastest and most exciting. The solution is mastering the DDD Cycle: invite the principal character to act (reminding them of conditions and surroundings), get a proper action declaration, resolve mechanics quickly in the background, then narrate the outcome. Monster turns should feel like seamless narration, not separate gameplay chunks. The secret weapon is **Telegraphing** — using every piece of narration to communicate enemy statistics (thick hide = high AC), status changes (bloodied, morale broken), and upcoming strategies (the warlock is preparing something big). Telegraph status changes immediately when they happen, warn players early about big incoming attacks, and sprinkle statistical information opportunistically throughout descriptions.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Enforce proper declarations**: Stop players from rolling dice before declaring intent — "Who are you attacking and with what?"
2. **Never skip outcome narration**: Even on a miss, describe what happened narratively — use it to telegraph enemy stats
3. **Practice gridless combat**: Run one fight per session without visual aids to sharpen your narration skills
4. **Prep your monsters actively**: Before each session, read stat blocks with a pencil and highlighter — note key abilities, tactics, and telegraphing opportunities
5. **Master turn transitions**: Practice flowing from one player's outcome directly into the next player's invitation — "...the goblin collapses. Nearby, Danae squares off with two goblins — what does she do?"
6. **Telegraph in three layers**:
   - *Status*: Announce changes immediately ("the goblins' morale breaks")
   - *Strategy*: Warn before big actions ("the warlock is raising his staff, preparing something")
   - *Statistics*: Weave into outcome descriptions ("your sword glances off its thick, armored hide")
7. **Handle analysis paralysis**: Count to three silently, then prod — if still nothing, skip the turn narratively

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Combat isn't a board game — it's narrated action. Master the Declare-Determine-Describe cycle, telegraph everything about your enemies through narration instead of numbers, and keep turns flowing seamlessly. Speed comes from ownership and preparation, not from skipping narration.
