## Summary

A two-part memo: one for players on how to take actions properly, and one for GMs on how to train and support that behavior. The core formula is **"I want to X by Y using Z"** — state your goal, your method, and your tools.

---

## Memo to the Players: Taking Action

### Listen First, Think Second

1. **Listen** to the GM's full description. Don't stop listening the moment you hear "treasure chest" or "ogre."
2. **Think about your goal.** Don't look at the character sheet. Ask: "What am I actually trying to accomplish here and now?"
3. **Think about how** to make that happen. What action will get you there?
4. **Think about tools and resources.** Equipment, training, talent — but also the world around you. Is the ogre on the edge of a chasm? Is the guardsman young and nervous?

### Talk Third, Fourth, and Fifth

Tell the GM what you want to accomplish, how, and with what:

- "I want to **kill the ogre** by **stabbing him** with **my longsword**."
- "I want to **open the chest** by **breaking the lock** with **that iron prybar**."
- "I want to **scare the guard away** by **threatening him** — **we outnumber him four to one**."

**The formula: "I want to X by Y using Z."** It'll never fail you.

### Bonus Tips

- **Don't make your GM guess.** The more the GM knows about what you're trying to do and why, the better off you are. If you think there's a trap, say so.
- **One thing at a time.** Don't spout a long string of actions. Stick to one action with one outcome. RPGs are a conversation, not speeches.
- **Don't sweat the character sheet.** It's a bunch of bonuses, not a list of possible actions. Be willing to risk failure. The right action for the situation usually beats the thing you're best at.
- **Don't sweat the rules.** TTRPGs are open-ended. Tell the GM what you want to do. The rules are his problem.
- **Don't ham it up.** You don't have to be super descriptive or talk in character. Be clear. Speak naturally.
- **GMs love when you pay attention.** Using information the GM provided — in the scene or earlier — usually earns you a bonus.
- **When all else fails, ask.** Ask the GM. Don't ask about rules — tell the GM what you're trying to accomplish and they'll help you make it happen.

## Memo to the GM: Taking Action

### Don't Fill in the Blanks

Force players to say "I want to X by Y using Z" every time. If they leave something out, make them fill it in:
- "I attack the ogre." → "With what?"
- "I pick the lock." → "With what? Your bare hands?"
- "I persuade the guard." → "How? What's your argument?"
- "We go through the door." → "Who's first? Weapons readied? What weapons?"

They won't learn if you don't teach them.

### Don't Make the Players Do More

Players have one job: clearly and concisely state what they're trying to accomplish, how, and with what. That's it. Don't make them spin elaborate prose. Don't make them speak in character. Don't give prosaic thespians extra bonuses for performance.

### Stop the Players from Doing More

Some players put on a big performance for every action — flowery descriptions, bad accents, inner monologue. Performance role-play is great for YouTube but sucks for team-based games about adventurers overcoming challenges. Interrupt performers and ask what they're doing, how, and with what.

### Reward Interaction

Give boosts when players:
- Use the environment to solve problems
- Use information they've discovered
- Look for ways to work together

Make sure they see it. Tell them when you reduce a DC. Give advantage for clever tool use.

### Don't Answer Questions About the Rules

If a player asks about rules, they have an ulterior motive. Never make a ruling until you know what you're ruling on. Ask: "Why do you want to know? What are you trying to accomplish?" Keep asking until you pry an action declaration out of them. Make players think about the world, not the rules.

### Earn Your Players' Trust

Players must trust you to work with them — to try to make their goals happen. This means:

- **Give the benefit of the doubt.** If something went unsaid or forgotten, assume best intentions. Don't be a gotcha GM.
- **Tell stakes, risks, costs, and consequences** unless you have a really good reason not to. Give players a chance to back out.
- **Practice this line:** "If you do X, you're risking Y; are you okay with that?"
- **Tell them when something is impossible.** Say "that won't work; but what's your goal? Maybe there's a way." Don't just say "you fail" or worse, "roll a die; you fail!"
- If PCs are in hostile territory, assume they're ready for a fight even if they forget to say it.
- Warn players about opportunity attacks they don't see coming.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Players:** "I want to X by Y using Z." Listen, think about your goal, then communicate clearly.
2. **GMs:** Force the formula. Don't fill in blanks. Don't reward performance over clarity.
3. Reward players for using the environment, information, and teamwork.
4. Don't answer rules questions — dig for the action declaration behind them.
5. Earn trust by giving benefit of the doubt, stating stakes, and never being a gotcha GM.
6. "If you do X, you're risking Y; are you okay with that?" — make this your catchphrase.
