# How NOT to Teach Newbies D&D

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-not-to-teach-newbies-dd/)
**Date:** December 6, 2017
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

*This article is written in reverse — sarcastic "bad advice" with the real advice embedded in the "Some GMs would…" paragraphs at the end of each section. The key takeaways are extracted below.*

## The Real Advice (Inverted)

### Step 1: Sell, Don't Teach

Players don't need to understand most of the rules to play. They just need to listen to descriptions, declare actions, and roll dice on command. The human brain adjudicating the game makes everything else possible.

**Do this:** Focus on helping players understand the most basic concepts. Start with "describe your action and we'll see what happens." Bring in rules as needed and explain only exactly as much as is needed at one time. Present a small number of clear, concise options instead of overloading them with every possibility.

### Step 2: Skip the Paperwork — Get to the Fun

Character generation is one part coming up with a cool idea to four parts filling out a form, looking stuff up in books, and adding up numbers. For newbies, this is a massive roadblock.

**Do this:** Use simplified pregenerated characters. Let players pick from familiar archetypes like "knight," "barbarian," "dwarf soldier," "elven hunter," "wizard," and "halfling burglar." Leave names, physical descriptions, genders, and personality traits blank for players to fill in. Say: "We'll use these for a few sessions. Then, if we decide to continue, we can work on creating characters of your very own."

### Step 3: Be Familiar, Be Approachable

New players are drawn to D&D because of their familiarity with fantasy tropes. Nothing makes it easier to lose yourself in a world than a sense that it's familiar, recognizable, and comfortable.

**Do this:** Draw players in with familiar worlds, characters, story elements, and tropes. Make the game feel like a comfortable sweater players can easily slip on. Gradually add unique elements to something familiar and comfortable. Don't bury them in exposition, unfamiliar creatures, or complex political factions on day one.

### Step 4: Don't Demand Commitment

**Do this:** Forestall discussions of future games. Reassure players that the possibility exists for another game but caution them to wait and see how the first session goes. Allow players to relax and judge the single session on its own merits. If someone is on the fence, offer another one-shot in a couple of weeks instead of demanding they commit to a campaign or leave forever.

### Step 5: Don't Bench the Newbies

**Do this:** Run a separate session just for new players — one that doesn't involve experienced players so the focus can be entirely on drawing them in. Or invite one or two experienced players to join but make sure they take a passive role and frequently engage the new players. Adapt the experience to the players instead of forcing them to jump in and keep up.

## Summary of Principles

1. **Be a salesman, not a teacher** — make them love the game, don't bore them with rules
2. **Use pregens** — skip character creation for the first session
3. **Use familiar tropes** — orcs, goblins, elves, dwarves; save the exotic stuff for later
4. **Run a one-shot first** — no commitment pressure
5. **Dedicate the session to newbies** — don't throw them into your existing group's ongoing campaign
