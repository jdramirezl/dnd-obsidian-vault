# How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Hate the Dice

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-i-learned-to-hate-the-dice/)
**Date:** March 31, 2021
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

The ideal number of times to roll dice is zero. And this isn't some bullshit about running a session that's "all role-playing." Even all-roleplay sessions should still touch dice. Because dice are fun and not rolling dice sucks.

## The Evolving Rules for When to Roll Dice

Over the years, Angry has given multiple rules about when to roll dice:

1. **The Classic Rule:** Roll the dice whenever a creature attempts an action that has a reasonable chance of success, a reasonable chance of failure, and failure carries some kind of risk or cost.

2. **The Gamble Rule:** Die rolls are gambles. Players propose bets — if they warrant a die roll, you roll.

3. **The Conflict Rule:** Roll the dice whenever a conflict needs resolving.

4. **The Aspirational Rule:** If you can find any excuse — however small — not to roll the dice, take it. Try your hardest to never, ever need dice. Ever.

All four rules are used simultaneously. The question is: how do they fit together?

## The Burden of Wisdom

Wisdom comes from experience. It's not knowledge (you can get that from books). It's not common sense or intuition. Wisdom is a gradual understanding of how things actually work — a model of the world you build one day at a time by actually moving through it.

After years of running lots of games, a wise GM develops a good sense of what works and what doesn't. Intuitively. Instinctively. Without really thinking about it. The challenge is figuring out what you're actually doing and why — and whether you can explain it to others.

## Subjectivity Makes the Best Games

The GM's subjective, arbitrary, flawed human brain is the most important part of the RPG experience. This is ironic given that RPG players tend to hate subjectivity and see it as a flaw.

**But subjectivity is literally the best reason to play RPGs.** Computers can only follow preprogrammed rules and generate random numbers. If all you do as a GM is execute the rules and roll dice, you're no better than a computer — actually worse, because you're slower and have no graphics card.

What you're better at — what computers can never do — is everything else. You can improvise, imagine, run hypotheticals and counterfactuals, pull new stories out of thin air.

**GMing's an art, not a science.** You do it by feel. There are basic rules and principles, just like color theory and composition. But none of that makes a painting a painting.

### Balance is Active

Balance is an active, ongoing thing — inherently unstable. When you stand on one foot, your muscles constantly tweak and adjust. The conditions of a group gameplay experience are constantly changing.

Balance isn't always one-to-one either. If one side's heavier, the balance point shifts. Every force in your game isn't created equal. And the weights change every day.

The art of GMing comes down to recognizing, from one moment to the next, where the game is and where it should be, and knowing exactly how hard to pull in which direction. Every decision gives your game a shove. You're trying to keep the game a tolerable distance from the hypothetical point of perfect game for you and your friends.

### Aspirational Ideals

People hate aspirational ideals — rules that are impossible to follow perfectly or that represent an unattainable theoretical state. But if you can't see the value in striving toward the good and finding victory in getting even a tiny bit closer every day, maybe it ain't gaming advice you need.

## Hating Dice and Rolling Them Anyway

**Dice are fatty, sugary, high-calorie RPG foods.** Dice are fun. Dice are fair. There's nothing more even-handed than knowing the odds and rolling. That's why most GMs overindulge on dice given half a chance. And that's why the aspirational ideal is to hate dice — because your instinct will always be to grab for them.

Your judgment makes your game the best it can possibly be. The dice can never give an answer that's better than the one your brain can come up with. In the long run, educated guesses from even the most average person will win more often than random guesses.

Dice bring tension, excitement, and perceived fairness — all good things. But they're not the only way to add those things. And a small amount of die-rolling goes a long way.

### The Approach

The rules, the dice, and the game's mechanics are things any stupid computer can handle. They should be your **last resort**. Using them diminishes your game — moves it away from RPG toward boring video game. Rules add cognitive load. They're rigid and inflexible. Dice are random number generators that answer questions without thought.

**Always look for an excuse NOT to roll the dice:**

- When the players are using their brains and engaging with the world beyond the rules and mechanics on their character sheets, they deserve you using YOUR brain instead of scurrying to the random number generator.
- If you can argue the odds of success are high enough that failure's implausibly unlikely — don't roll.
- If you can argue the odds are so vanishingly small that failure's practically guaranteed — don't roll.
- If one outcome would feel like a gimmee, a gift, a deus ex machina, a screwjob, or a robbery — don't roll.

**Roll dice when you can't find any good, solid reason not to.** When the players don't give you an excuse. When the rules don't give you an excuse. When the situation doesn't give you an excuse.

Any GM can find uncertainty in any situation. Any GM can find certainty. The problem isn't that GMs can find both — it's that they're inclined to look for the uncertainty because they want to roll the dice. Wise GMs learn to look for the reasons NOT to roll.

### Players Can't Seduce Dragons

Don't let players roll dice against impossible odds on the off chance they'll roll a reality-altering natural twenty and force you to invent a ridiculous contrivance. Players can't seduce dragons with dice. Not ever.

## Postscript: Some People Hate Dice

Some players and GMs hate the dice — feel like dice hold them back, prevent them from doing what they want, wreck clever plans with arbitrary randomness.

For players, that's fine. As a GM, you can't give a shit. Use the dice when they're the right tool for the job.

But if you're a GM who hates the dice, your attitude ain't healthy either. You need to find your own aspirational rule — one that leads you to look for the excuse TO roll the dice in every situation. You still won't use the dice enough, but you'll get a lot closer to the healthy middle ground.
