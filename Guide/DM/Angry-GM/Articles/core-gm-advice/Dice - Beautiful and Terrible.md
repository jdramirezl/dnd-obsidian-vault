# Dice: Beautiful and Terrible as the Dawn (Whatever THAT Means)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dice-beautiful-and-terrible/)
**Date:** June 26, 2019
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## What the Dice Represent

The dice represent nothing. And everything. They are a complete abstraction. They never represent the same thing twice. Trying to explain them is like trying to explain the science behind the magic in Harry Potter — pointless, impossible, and it ruins everything fun.

The idea that "when a player rolls a Strength check, the result on the die represents the actual physical force they bring to bear on the task" is utterly stupid. It misunderstands randomness, game design, how D&D works, and how the actual world works.

What the dice really represent is the limits of human knowledge, human perception, and determinism. When you try to break down a door, in theory, with enough scientific equipment, you COULD determine exactly how much force you need. But the air temperature affects the elasticity of the door, the humidity plays a part, a thousand other factors matter. This is the essence of chaos theory. And humans just aren't that precise — did you get enough sleep? Are your muscles fatigued? Did you notice the slick patch on the floor?

**The dice exist because the universe is unpredictable.** Attributes represent potential. DCs represent approximations for how difficult a task is for a reasonable person under reasonable conditions. If you're not coming into action adjudication with that mindset, you can't run a good game.

## Why Dice at All

Human beings need to be surprised. We have a hardwired tendency to find the unpredictable exciting. Games need an element of surprise to be fun.

But games don't need dice or randomness to surprise us. Games can surprise us simply by **hiding information**. In chess, we can't read our opponent's mind. In card games, we don't know what's in our opponent's hand. Hidden information creates surprise.

If you're a player in an RPG, pretty much the entire game is about being caught off guard by hidden information. You have no idea what's around the next corner, whether the chest is trapped, what the monsters will do, or how many encounters you have to survive today.

**For GMs, it's different.** There's only one bit of hidden information: what the players will do. And that's usually pretty predictable. That's why GMs have urges to add random elements — random encounter tables, random dungeons. The GM needs dice more than the players because they give the GM opportunities to be surprised.

So why do RPGs need dice if they already have tons of hidden information?

Because we need to believe things are ultimately **fair**. If we make the right choices, we should get the right outcomes. But imagine if every outcome was controlled by another human being — the GM just announcing "a goblin killed you dead." That feels unfair.

**RPGs use dice because dice are impartial.** You can't say they hold a grudge or play favorites. Everyone can see them. The outcomes are random but predictably random — random in a way everyone at the table can see and understand.

## Nobody Wants Fairness

Here's the problem: dice are utterly fair. They make no judgments, have no ill will, play no favorites. And that's why people hate them. **People actually hate fairness.**

Our brains cope with two opposing ideas:
1. The universe is random and beyond our control
2. If we apply reason and make the right choices, everything will work out

We don't want fairness — we want **justice**. We want the outcomes we think we deserve. The fighter with Strength 18 who rolls a 1 fails to break down a door, but the wizard with Strength 8 who rolls a 20 succeeds. That feels unjust. The fighter DESERVES to break down the door.

## Mitigating the Dice

We need unpredictability for tension and excitement. We need it to be fair and impartial. So we use dice. But we hate them because they can't be controlled.

**That's called strategy** — and that's how players earn their victories. Flank an opponent for a bonus. Use a crowbar. Work as a team. Use magical items. Move to get a clear shot. Fight defensively. The GM who complains about players seeking bonuses and avoiding penalties is complaining that players don't want to live or die on coin tosses.

Players love and hate the dice. They trust them implicitly — more than they trust the GM — until the dice turn against them.

## Practical Advice

### Just Because You Have to Roll Doesn't Mean You Have to Roll

Roll only when there is (a) a chance the action could succeed, (b) a chance it could fail, and (c) a risk or cost associated with the attempt. **Except when the best outcome of the die would be unjust** — then just narrate the result.

Example: After the fighter fails to break down the door and the wizard steps up, just tell the wizard he fails too. The door is too sturdy. Find another way through.

### The Do-Over: When One Roll Isn't Good Enough

When the stakes are really high — a character lives or dies on the roll — one die roll may resolve the action but feel unjust. It's okay to offer a "mulligan." Not a reroll — that feels like pity. Instead, offer a **narrative second chance**.

They miss the critical jump across the ravine? Offer a saving throw to catch themselves: "You come up just short. You start to fall and flail wildly at the ledge. Make a saving throw to catch yourself."

Keep it to one do-over. Don't mitigate everything. The players should still be in a terrible place. But one extra roll when stakes are super high tends to placate most players.

**Remember:** The dice are exciting, surprising, and impartial. They make the game fun. They are also the embodiment of an existential terror lurking in the darkest recesses of every human mind. So maybe don't overuse them.
