# Adjudicate Actions Like a Motherf$&%ing Boss!

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/adjudicate-actions-like-a-boss/) | **Category**: Core GM Advice

---

## Key Advice

- **Every player action has two components: Intention (what they want to accomplish) and Approach (how they're trying to do it).** Never adjudicate until you can clearly identify both.
- **Every action resolution produces two outputs: Outcome (did it succeed or fail?) and Consequence (how did the chosen approach change the world?).** Outcomes come from Intentions; Consequences come from Approaches.
- **Consequences exist regardless of success or failure.** Whether you successfully intimidate the guard or fail, the fact that you chose intimidation over diplomacy changes the world — the guard now dislikes you either way.
- **The Approach is where all the roleplay lives.** It's what differentiates one character from another and what makes choices meaningful beyond "which modifier is highest."
- **Never contradict a Consequence once it exists.** If the party left obstacles intact behind them, anyone following has a harder time. Honor the world-state your players' choices created.

## Summary

This article provides a complete step-by-step framework for how a DM should process any player action, from declaration to narration of results. It breaks the core RPG loop (GM describes → player acts → GM resolves) into granular sub-steps that most rulebooks gloss over. The key insight is that action resolution isn't just about success or failure — it's about extracting two inputs (Intention and Approach) and producing two outputs (Outcome and Consequence).

The Intention is what the player wants to achieve; the Approach is how they're trying to achieve it. The DM must identify both before doing anything else. The Intention determines what success and failure look like. The Approach determines which mechanics to use AND — critically — what consequences ripple out into the world regardless of the outcome. This is the mechanism that makes player choices matter beyond the immediate moment. Lying to your boss to cover a mistake has different consequences than admitting the mistake, even if both approaches succeed at avoiding punishment.

The article also provides a practical template for narrating results: restate the player's approach, attach the outcome with "and" or "but," and mention immediate consequences when natural. This simple formula ensures players feel heard, understand the result, and see the connection between their choices and the game world's response. The broader lesson is that if you skip consequences — if success and failure are the only outputs of action resolution — you're running a game, but you're not running a roleplaying game.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Always identify Intention and Approach before adjudicating.** Ask "What are you trying to accomplish?" and "How exactly are you doing it?" until both are crystal clear.
2. **Check if the Approach can actually achieve the Intention.** If not, the action fails — no roll needed. Don't let any action lead to any outcome; maintain logical connections.
3. **Apply the three-part test from Five Simple Rules** (can succeed, can fail, cost of failure) to determine if a roll is needed.
4. **After determining success/failure, always ask: "What Consequence follows from this Approach?"** Think about how the NPCs, objects, and locations involved have been permanently changed by the player's chosen method.
5. **Note Consequences and honor them later.** Keep a running list. When a Consequence can naturally come up in a future scene, make sure it does. Never contradict an established Consequence.
6. **Narrate results using the formula:** "[Restate Approach], and/but [Outcome]. [Consequence if immediately relevant]." Example: "You threaten the guard, but he won't budge. He narrows his eyes — he'll remember this."
7. **Don't require multiple rolls for a single action** unless there's a visible benchmark for progress and a meaningful reason to drag it out.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Outcomes tell you if the action worked; Consequences tell you how the player's choice of method changed the world — and Consequences are what make an RPG a *role-playing* game.
