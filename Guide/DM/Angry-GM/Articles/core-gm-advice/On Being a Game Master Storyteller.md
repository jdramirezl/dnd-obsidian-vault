# On Being a (Game) Master Storyteller

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/on-being-a-game-master-storyteller/)
**Date:** March 17, 2021
**Series:** Narrative BS for GMs
**Category:** Core GM Advice — Narrative Theory for RPGs

---

## Core Concept

RPGs are a unique blend of **game** and **narrative** — not two separate things sitting side by side, but mixed together like a milkshake. The GM must walk the line between game design and storytelling, mastering (or at least achieving basic proficiency in) both disciplines. Narrative theory isn't academic fluff — it's a practical toolkit for building and running compelling games.

---

## The Dual Nature of RPGs: Yin and Yang

RPGs have always had two recognized parts — fluff/crunch, story/game, role-playing/rules. But focusing on the **distinction** between them is a waste of time. What matters is what happens when they're **blended together**.

### What Each Element Provides

| **Games** | **Narratives** |
|---|---|
| Test players against obstacles and challenges | Create emotional connections and empathy |
| Fill the need for competition and achievement | Resonate emotionally, create bonds |
| Players operate within constraints using tools and skills | Connect author to reader, reader to character |
| Dopamine — accomplishment, focus, strategy | Oxytocin — belonging, social bonds |

### The RPG Milkshake

In RPGs, story and game **complement each other**:
- The story provides **stakes and motives** that pure games (like chess) never have — emotional connections beyond a desire to win
- The game makes stories **personal** — players control outcomes and experience characters' victories as their own
- The story emerges from gameplay; gameplay evolves in response to the story

No other medium achieves this as purely as tabletop RPGs. Video games, board games, and gamebooks all try to merge narrative and gameplay, but none match the open-ended, real-time creative response of a human GM.

> This is why removing the creative human brain (the "unholy grail of GM-less play") always diminishes the RPG experience.

---

## The GM as Master of Two Realms

Every choice you make as a GM is a **game design choice**, a **storytelling choice**, or both:

- Introducing an NPC = storytelling (communicating desires, motivations, personality through interaction)
- Adjudicating a die roll = game design (deciding how the game should play out)
- Following rules for random encounters = game design (even "just following the rules" is a design decision — if it puts players in an unrecoverable death spiral, that's on you)

The books saying you can override rules "whenever you want" aren't just giving permission — they're giving **responsibility**. The game's designers are saying: "This is an open-ended, evolving game and you need to be ready to redesign it at a moment's notice."

## Respect the Disciplines

Storytelling goes back ~30,000 years. Structured game design goes back ~5,000 years. Collectively, humanity has gotten very good at both. There are **rules** to storytelling and game design — tested across every culture on Earth, enduring for millennia.

### What This Means for GMs

- **Use what works.** Tens of thousands of years of experience have produced reliable frameworks. Don't throw them away to invent everything from scratch.
- **Understand tropes before rejecting them.** Starting a fantasy campaign in a tavern isn't lazy — it's a near-perfect opening for fantasy adventure narratives, and Angry does it consciously every time.
- **Respect the artists who came before you.** Every generation can reach higher because they stand on the shoulders of the previous one.
- **Learn the rules so you can break them deliberately** — for impact and style, not out of ignorance.

This applies equally to:
- Deconstructionists trying to "subvert expectations"
- GMs who reject tropes/clichés without understanding them
- Improvisationalists who reject structure and planning as unnecessary or harmful

## Why "Just Improvise" and "Never Plan" Are Wrong

### The "Wrong-Headers" Argument
*"The GM should just create situations and react. Planning steals player agency."*

### The "Impossiblers" Argument
*"Players can do anything, so you can't impose narrative structure. Why bother?"*

### Why Both Are Wrong

**First:** The GM doesn't just react — the GM **reacts with purpose**. You can't control player choices, but there's always an infinity of possible outcomes that follow logically from those choices. You choose which outcomes actually happen. You have agency too. Sometimes steering is easy (pulling a train switch); sometimes it's hard (sailboat full of kittens in a typhoon). But you can always steer.

**Second:** The narrative is **emergent**. Your game will have a plot when it's done whether you planned one or not. Players will interpret it through narrative structures because that's how human brains work. You can either:
- Use your power to steer toward the best narrative possible, or
- Hope the emergent narrative is accidentally good

### What "Plotting a Game" Actually Means

It doesn't mean planning every moment in detail. It means using narrative theory as a **framework** — deciding which improvised elements to throw at players, which to save, and which to discard.

> "You can sketch a painting in advance or throw paint on a canvas. Either way, you've got to understand color and composition and perspective and how to use all the different brushes."

Angry invents 75–99% of his game content on the fly. But he uses narrative theory to decide what to invent and when.

## What This Series Will Cover

A practical translation of narrative theory into RPG-specific tools:
- Plot and plot structure as planning tools for campaigns and adventures
- The ins and outs of conflict
- What makes good settings and good characters
- Tweaked and scaled-back theory focused on practical application, not academic analysis

The goal: **help GMs build and run games around compelling narratives** — not teach you to analyze movies to death, but to tell a good story that players can play.

---

## Key Takeaway

> RPGs are uniquely powerful because they blend game and narrative into something no other medium can match. As a GM, you need both storytelling skill and game design skill — not as separate disciplines, but as complementary forces. Learn the rules of both crafts, respect the millennia of practice behind them, and use that knowledge whether you're planning in advance or improvising at the table.
