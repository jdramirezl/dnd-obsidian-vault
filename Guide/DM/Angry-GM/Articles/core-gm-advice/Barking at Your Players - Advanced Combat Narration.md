# Barking at Your Players: Advanced Combat Narration

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/barking-at-your-players-advanced-combat-narration/)
**Date:** March 15, 2019

---

Imagine we're going to add a new mechanic to *D&D 5E*. A new combat action only monsters can take. A free action called Charge Up: once per turn, a creature can use it, and at the end of their turn, they become Fully Charged until the end of their next turn. A creature can't Charge Up if already Fully Charged. The GM must declare the monster is taking the action. Then take monsters with big signature abilities — dragons with breath weapons — and add a conditional: they can only use that ability when Fully Charged.

How does that change the game? Nothing mechanically changes except the monster has to decide one turn in advance, and the GM has to announce that plan to the players.

This isn't a hacking article. This is about narrating combat — especially when your players won't — and the advanced things you can do with combat narration to make the game better AS A GAME.

## Why Won't My Players Describe Things?

One of the top complaints from GMs is that their players just won't describe stuff in combat. The most they get is "I attack the goblin with my sword, 17 to hit." Or worse, just "17."

The minimum action declaration for an attack includes the target and the weapon. "I attack the goblin with my longsword" is the minimum acceptable declaration.

Many GMs want flowery purple prose. They want "I lunge forward, dodging the goblin's attack, and I swing my longsword in a wide arc, slashing at the goblin." Which they call "role-playing in combat."

Some players will do that — and those players are usually annoying as hell because they have no sense of when to STOP. Every attack becomes a Shakespearian soliloquy.

The useful advice: "if that's what you want the game to sound like, well, isn't that YOUR job? YOU'RE the narrator!"

This is generally how I do it:

> Bob: "I attack, 17."
> Angry: "I'm sorry, what?"
> Bob: "Oh, crap. I attack the goblin with my longsword. 17 to hit."
> Angry: "No. You had to redeclare the attack, you reroll the die."
> Bob: "I attack the goblin with my longsword." *sigh* "13 to hit."
> Angry: "You hit. But barely. Damage?"
> Bob: "5 slashing damage."
> Angry: "Bob charges the goblin and catches it with a swing of his sword. The goblin tries to dodge backward, and it gets grazed. Just a flesh wound. It readies its dagger to counterattack, but before it can, Carol…"

You view the mechanical part and the narration part as two different levels of play. Narrate the situation, resolve the action, narrate the result. It's YOUR job as the GM to take the resolved action and turn it into part of the narrative and transition to the next player.

If you're frustrated with the level of description your players provide, you're really just complaining that the players aren't doing your job for you.

## Fast and Blunt

With five combats a day, five rounds each, against three monsters, everyone attacking every round — that's 175 attacks per session. At ~75 words per minute of creative narration, a 37-word description takes 30 seconds. That's 90 minutes of narration per session.

That's why I don't do full descriptions for every attack. Some get the full treatment, many get "Bob slashes the goblin; it reels backward but keeps fighting. Carol, what do you do?" Some get "Bob grazes the goblin. It barely notices. Carol?"

Every minute you narrate is a minute the players aren't playing. Players are there to make choices and play out results. Your job is to bring the world to life in the briefest way possible.

Combat should be fast and tense. Your narration needs to be tense and exciting and fast too. Short, punchy description — but not all the time. The first round, be very descriptive. It drops off quickly. Ramp up for decisive events. Once a round, get in one medium-length description.

## Emotion is Better than Description

Engagement isn't about narration, it's about emotion. Making the players feel like they are IN the action.

> Carol: "I bash the goblin with my mace. I hit AC 17 for 7 points of damage."
> Angry: "Solid hit! He really felt that! He's bloodied! Dave, you're up. Want to finish him off?"

Nothing remotely descriptive there. Almost pure emotion. Exciting, rewarding, and it passes momentum to the next player. It puts the players IN the battle.

> Dave: "I attack one of the zombies with my sword. 13 AC for 3 damage."
> Angry: "A graze. Doesn't even slow it down. Alice, the wall of zombies is still advancing. What do you do?"

The trick to narrating combat:
- **Strong narration** sparingly — establish mood at the start, punctuate decisive events
- **Medium narration** occasionally — keep the mood up
- **Short, emotional, fragmented narration** most of the time — maintain pace without losing feeling
- Switch consciously between narration and resolution like a dolphin

Don't worry about being repetitive. Players don't notice repetition. But they notice what you're repeating. If the goblin is always dodging and nimble, they'll notice the goblin feels like a goblin.

## Information is Better than Emotion

Most GMs want to help players "see" the combat. But moving too far toward "making it not feel like a game" obfuscates the issue. Players also need to figure out how best to face challenges.

Use narration to convey useful information:
- Don't let narration confuse what's happening mechanically
- Give sense of how badly the monster got hurt relative to its total HP
- Describe misses based on the monster's defenses: dodges for nimble creatures, bouncing off armor for heavy armor, blocks for shields
- If players can't guess monster stats from your descriptions, fix your description

Have you noticed that the only tactical decisions players make is which attack to use against which target? Once they settle in, they stay in that groove. Very rarely do players take defense actions or reposition.

What if the dragon couldn't breathe fire without warning the players one round in advance? Players would spread out, cast resistance spells, use consumable items preemptively. They'd shift from "what attack should I do next" to "I'm about to get blasted with fire, do I have a response?"

"But that will make things too EASY!" Those people are idiots. Players don't get bored with easy fights — they get bored when their choices don't matter. If the fight is easy because you made all the right choices, that's a good fight. You earned that win.

In video games, enemies wind up their attacks with visible animations. You decide whether to land a blow or dodge. That's what makes *Dark Souls* difficult but fair. Choices driven by information.

This is **telegraphing**. Telegraph the big moves a round in advance. Yes, monsters sometimes get locked into bad moves because players counter them — but that makes players feel smart and tactical.

## Barking

A particularly advanced form of telegraphing for groups of intelligent opponents. In video games, NPCs speak out loud — "try to flank," "grenade out," "taking fire." These **barks** tell the player what NPCs are thinking, allowing players to react. It's telegraphing through dialogue.

When you have intelligent monsters on the battlefield, do they communicate? Does the leader say "crap, they have a healer? Take that guy down!" Does anyone say "I'm hurt, someone help me"?

Most GMs don't have their monsters communicate. Then they wonder why players don't communicate in combat either. If enemies don't talk, players can't overhear plans. Telegraphing through barking makes enemies feel more alive, more real, and gives players information to make interesting tactical choices.
