# What Do Players Know Anyway?

> **Source:** [The Angry GM — What Do Players Know Anyway?](https://theangrygm.com/what-do-players-know-anyway/)
> **Date:** December 24, 2024
> **Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

## Context

Part of the True Scenario Designery series, this installment covers the relationship between Goals and Outcomes in RPG scenario design, and why the players' perspective on Goals is the only one that matters.

---

## Key Concepts

### Goals Are Always the Players' Goals

Every game needs at least one Goal — the thing the players are trying to accomplish within the game by interacting with gameplay elements. Goals like *rescue the princess* or *protect civilization from the orcs*, not meta-goals like *have fun* or *tell a story*.

True Scenario Designers establish Goals and build Scenarios around them. But the Goal is only meaningful from the **players' perspective**. There is no such thing as a "Real, Actual Scenario Goal" that differs from what the players think they're chasing. If the players think the Goal is X, then the Goal is X.

### What Goals Do

Goals provide three things:

1. **Context** — The reason the players engage with gameplay. Without a Goal, encounters are disconnected, abstract gameplay. Goals also inform decisions: can the heroes accept a bribe from goblins? Depends on the Goal.

2. **Direction** — When players face decision points, Goals help them choose. Need a divine spell? Head to the temple.

3. **A Yardstick for Winning and Losing** — Goals let players measure progress and evaluate outcomes. If the Goal was to stop the pillaging goblins and they're still pillaging, you Lost.

### Explicit vs. Implicit Goals

- **Explicit Goals:** Directly stated (e.g., an NPC hires the party for a specific task).
- **Implicit Goals:** Inferred from in-game context. Most RPG scenarios use implicit Goals. The Scenario Designer sets up a situation and counts on players to infer the Goal.

Implicit Goals are great for RPGs because they feel organic. Players think they're masters of their own destiny. The Scenario Designer just sets up choices and predicts outcomes.

### "Because That's How the Game Works"

True Scenario Designers know genre conventions. If an evil Empire attacks a peaceful town in a fantasy adventure game, the vast majority of players will fight the Empire and save the town. You don't need to explain why — that's just how the game works.

### Goals ≠ Outcomes

This is the critical insight:

- **Goals** = what the players are trying to accomplish
- **Outcomes** = the possible endings the players can actually achieve (or fail to achieve)

The Goal doesn't have to be one of the Outcomes. Usually it is, but sometimes it's not:

- **Unwinnable Scenario:** None of the Outcomes are close enough to the Goal for players to feel like they won.
- **Unlosable Scenario:** All Outcomes are close enough to the Goal that players feel like champions regardless. These are common and valid in TTRPGs.

### Outcomes Must Follow from Goal Pursuit

While Outcomes can be distinct from Goals, they must evolve naturally from the players' pursuit of the Goal. The Outcomes must be somewhere on the path the players follow while chasing the Goal.

### Goal Closure

Players won't stop chasing the Goal just because they hit an Outcome. Whatever the Scenario's Outcome, the Goal must be a done deal when the players reach it. From the moment the Scenario starts, the Goal must be sliding inexorably toward closure.

### Vague Goal Alignment Is Fine

Players rarely articulate their Goals precisely. If you asked five players separately, you'd get three different answers and two shrugs. That's fine. As long as everyone's Goals align enough to get them working together and following designed paths, it works. A bit of wobbly Goal mismatch is actually good for TTRPGs.

## The Pop Quiz Example

> The players are in town when the Empire attacks. All they can manage is barricading a dozen civilians in a bunker. When the smoke clears, they emerge into a smoldering ruin.

- **What do the players think the Goal is?** Save the town / fight the Empire.
- **What is the actual designed Goal?** The same thing — Goals are always the players' Goals.
- **What are the Outcomes?** How many lives they saved. The Goal (save the town) and the Outcome (save some civilians) are related but distinct. The Goal is unwinnable, but the Outcome follows naturally from pursuing it.

---

## Summary

> Goals define what the players are trying to do. Goals help players make decisions and measure progress. Goals are only meaningful from the players' perspective. True Scenario Designers never think in terms of "Player Goals" vs. "Real Scenario Goals."
>
> Outcomes are the possible endings. Goals and Outcomes are distinct. The Goal can be one of the Outcomes, but doesn't have to be. Outcomes must evolve naturally from Goal pursuit. And the Goal must reach closure when the Outcome hits.
