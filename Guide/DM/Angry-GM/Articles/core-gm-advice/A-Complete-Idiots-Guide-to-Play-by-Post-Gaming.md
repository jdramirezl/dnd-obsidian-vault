# A Complete Idiot's Guide to Play by Post Gaming

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/a-complete-idiots-guide-to-play-by-post-gaming/) | **Category**: Core GM Advice

---

## Key Advice

- **The core problem with PbP isn't slowness — it's the loss of dynamic, back-and-forth exchanges.** Asynchronous play demands fewer, higher-content messages rather than rapid-fire negotiation.
- **Choose systems that minimize negotiational back-and-forth in action resolution.** Powered by the Apocalypse games, Genesys, and Everway are strong candidates because they resolve actions in fewer exchanges with richer outcomes.
- **Resolve at the level of gambits and sorties, not individual actions.** Poll all players for their disposition in a scene, then resolve the entire round at once — especially in combat and social encounters.
- **Splitting the party is a feature, not a bug, in PbP.** Simultaneous independent actions are natural in asynchronous play; lean into them.
- **Set clear guidelines and expectations upfront** — shot-clock response times, post length limits, separate chatter channels, and rules for pre-choosing conditional outcomes.

## Summary

This article is an outsider's analytical take on Play by Post gaming — Angry openly admits he dislikes the format and has minimal experience with it. But his analysis of *why* PbP struggles and what would make it tolerable is rooted in solid game design thinking. The central insight is that the real enemy of PbP isn't the slow pace (which is actually a feature of low-commitment play), but the way traditional RPG systems rely on rapid back-and-forth exchanges that become agonizing when each message takes hours to arrive.

D&D is particularly ill-suited to PbP because its action resolution involves constant micro-negotiations: declare action, DM picks skill, player invokes abilities, roll, DM checks DC, player spends resources to modify, etc. Systems like Dungeon World (PbtA) work better because the GM simply listens for a "move trigger" in the player's natural description and resolves it with one roll that produces a high-content result. Genesys works because its narrative dice pool produces rich, multi-dimensional outcomes in a single throw. Everway works because it resolves entire gambits rather than individual swings.

The practical takeaway for anyone running PbP: restructure your game around polled simultaneous actions, minimize scenes that require naturalistic conversation or tactical combat, establish a side channel for player discussion, and accept that the format demands a different style of play — more zoomed-out, more narrative, less granular.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Pick a system designed for low-negotiation resolution.** PbtA games (Dungeon World, etc.), Genesys, or Everway are strong choices. Avoid systems with lots of interrupt-style abilities or multi-step resolution.
2. **Establish a "Guidelines and Expectations" document** covering response time expectations (e.g., check in every 24 hours), maximum post length, and how to handle absent players.
3. **Create two channels:** one for official narration/action declarations, one for player chatter and planning.
4. **Poll all players before resolving anything.** Wait for every player to declare their action for the current "pass" before you narrate any results.
5. **Have players pre-choose conditional outcomes** when the system offers choices on partial success (e.g., "If I can't get a clean shot, I'll move into the open for a better angle").
6. **Resolve at the sortie/gambit level** rather than individual attacks — describe the overall flow of a combat round or social exchange in one narrative post.
7. **Lean into splitting the party** in safe areas. Let players pursue independent goals simultaneously; it's one of PbP's natural strengths.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Play by Post demands systems and styles that trade rapid back-and-forth for fewer, richer exchanges — pick your system and structure your game accordingly.
