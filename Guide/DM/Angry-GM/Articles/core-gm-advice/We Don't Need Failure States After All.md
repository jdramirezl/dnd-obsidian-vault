# We Don't Need Failure States After All

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/dont-need-failure-states/)
**Date:** November 23, 2022

---

## Summary

Angry argues that the entire decades-long conversation about "failure states" in TTRPGs is largely unnecessary. Inspired by an Adam Millard video about failure in video games, he realizes that the standard RPG failure narrative doesn't actually apply to TTRPGs — because TTRPGs have something better.

## The Standard RPG Failure Narrative (Distilled)

> Roleplaying games need failure and loss. Players can't win every challenge. But losing isn't fun. So failure has to exist but be fun — maybe more fun than success. Failure can't bottleneck the game. And D&D's only real failure state is death, which is the worst failure state. So D&D needs more/better failure states built into the system, even though nobody can explain what that would look like.

Every conversation about failure in TTRPGs, ever.

## Why Video Games Can't Teach Us About Failure in TTRPGs

In video games, you never really lose — you respawn, reload, or start over. You only truly lose when you quit. TTRPGs don't work that way. There's no reload, no respawn, no "try again."

But there IS a video game model that works: **open-world survival/simulator games** (Dwarf Fortress, Rimworld, Space Engineers). In these:
- If you screw up, you live with it
- You can't reload — you salvage and adapt
- There's usually no explicit win state

These games share two vital characteristics with TTRPGs:

### 1. Persistent Continuity
Every session follows from the last. Events form a consistent narrative built one session at a time. It's not isolated attempts to win like board games.

### 2. Memory
When something happens, the game world remembers. The princess who died last week is gone. The cult that succeeded has consequences. The world reacts and evolves.

## The Broken Narrative: RPGs Don't Need Failure

The failure narrative starts with: "Actions must have consequences; therefore, failure must be an option."

**The first part is true** — actions must have consequences. It's a TTRPG axiom.

**But the second part doesn't follow.** Persistent Continuity and Memory already ensure actions have consequences — without requiring explicit "failure states."

### Angry's Example

His last adventure: heroes went into a haunted forest, killed a savage despoiling a spirit's demesne, met a blind herbalist, and encountered an accused arsonist. The heroes won — they pretty much couldn't lose the main quest. But:

- The blind herbalist had to be put in elder care
- The arsonist case went uninvestigated
- The world changed as a result of what happened AND what didn't happen

These weren't designed as "failure states." They were just characters and stories that existed alongside the main quest. When the adventure ended, the world was different. An arsonist in jail, a blind herbalist down the street — things that weren't true last week.

> "I didn't build that shit into my adventure as failure states. They were just characters and stories for the players to see — and interact with — on the way to their pretty much predetermined success."

## The Key Insight

Instead of asking "how can this adventure fail?", ask:
- **"What's in the world on the way?"**
- **"What other stories exist alongside this one?"**
- **"How are things different in the world now?"**

You don't need to sweat failure states. The idea of systematic, mechanical failure states in D&D is probably impossible AND totally unnecessary — **provided the GM has a good memory or takes good notes.**

## Core Principle

> Actions have consequences because the world has Persistent Continuity and Memory. If you do all this right — keep the clock running, remember the world's history — then maybe it doesn't matter if the players can't lose an adventure. Because the world evolves, everything the players do matters. And if everything matters, you don't need to go out of your way to make certain things matter. They already do.
