# The Big Picture: Everything You Ever Need to Know About Game Mastering

## Key Advice

This is Angry's master glossary and conceptual framework for understanding RPGs, the GM's role, and how games are structured. Every other piece of GM advice he gives builds on these definitions. The GM is not a player — the GM is **part of the system**, a human game mechanic whose role is to facilitate player choice and consequence. The two minimum GM skills are **Narration** and **Adjudication**.

## Summary

### What Is an RPG?

An RPG is a game of **choice and consequence**. The GM presents a situation → players decide on actions → GM determines outcomes and describes results → new situation. That's the core loop of every RPG ever made.

### The GM's Role

- The GM is a **game mechanic**, not a player — the human component that allows the game to respond to any player choice
- GMing is a **performance**, not a game — the GM has no win condition, but should find the experience satisfying
- The GM's core duty: facilitate **decisions and consequences** by being fair, consistent, showing cause-and-effect, providing goals, and maintaining a satisfying narrative

### The Three GM Hats

1. **Running the Game** — presenting situations, adjudicating actions, describing consequences (the primary activity)
2. **Administrating the Game** — handling scheduling, interpersonal issues, social dynamics (the GM has both authority and duty here)
3. **Creating the Game** — designing content, from improvised details at the table to full adventures and campaigns

### Minimum GM Skills

1. **Narration** — presenting clear, concise, useful information so players can make good decisions
2. **Adjudication** — determining outcomes by understanding player intent, approach, and applying rules/judgment

### Game Structure (Nested)

- **Actions** → the atom of RPGs (intent + approach → outcome + consequences)
- **Scenes/Encounters** → series of actions around a dramatic question; encounters have conflict
- **Adventures** → complete stories with motivation, goal, climax, resolution
- **Adventure Paths** → connected adventures with an underlying narrative arc
- **Campaigns** → the sum total of all sessions with continuity (not a formal structural element)

### Narrative Structure Elements

- **Goal** — achievable, measurable, with clear success/failure benchmarks
- **Motivation** — the personal drive behind pursuing a goal
- **Incitement to Action** — the event that kicks off the adventure
- **Tension** — awareness of uncertainty; rises and falls but trends upward
- **Conflict** — when goals/desires cannot all be achieved (external, intraparty, internal)
- **Climax** — highest tension point, immediately before resolution
- **Resolution** — outcome revealed, tension released
- **Pacing** — the ebb and flow of tension and speed toward resolution

### Narrative Elements

- **Story** — emergent, holistic; only exists after play
- **Characters** — entities with motivations that can take actions (if it has goals and acts, it's a character, not setting)
- **Setting** — everything that isn't a character
- **Themes** — subtextual concepts exploring human experience
- **Tone** — the moods/emotions evoked; varies scene to scene along a spectrum

## Actionable Steps

1. **Internalize the core loop** — Situation → Decision → Outcome → New Situation. This is your job at the table, every moment
2. **Accept all three hats** — you are the game runner, the administrator, AND the creator; don't shirk the social/organizational duties
3. **Master narration first** — practice describing situations clearly and concisely before worrying about anything else
4. **Master adjudication second** — learn to identify player intent and approach, then determine outcomes using judgment and rules
5. **Think in nested structure** — every action is inside a scene, every scene inside an adventure; higher levels provide context and goals for lower levels
6. **Give every adventure a goal, climax, and resolution** — these are non-negotiable for a satisfying experience
7. **Distinguish characters from setting** — if it has motivations and takes actions that affect the story, it's a character, even if it looks like a setting element
8. **Manage pacing consciously** — vary tension between high and low, but trend it upward; don't let the game flatline or spike without relief

> [!tip] The GM is a game mechanic, not a player
> You exist to respond to any choice the players can imagine. You don't have a win condition — you have a duty. Embrace that. The satisfaction comes from the performance, the creative expression, and the surprise of an unpredictable narrative. If you're trying to "win" against your players, you've misunderstood the role.
