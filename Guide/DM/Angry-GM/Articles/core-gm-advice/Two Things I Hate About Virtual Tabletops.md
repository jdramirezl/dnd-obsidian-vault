# Two Things I Hate About Virtual Tabletops (And One Thing I Love)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/two-things-i-hate-about-virtual-tabletops-and-one-thing-i-love/)
**Date:** November 25, 2025

---

## Summary

Angry examines the costs and benefits of virtual tabletops (VTTs) for online RPG play. He identifies **one thing he loves** and **two things he hates**, while emphasizing that online gaming itself is great.

### The One Thing He Loves: Individualized Line-of-Sight and Fog of War

VTTs can give each player a unique view based on their character's senses — darkvision, torch radius, etc. This creates powerful moments impossible at a physical table, like standing in a pool of light surrounded by oppressive darkness with no sense of the cavern's size. Different players seeing different things forces in-character communication and amplifies tension.

### Hate #1: Automapping Destroys Exploration Tension

VTTs remember everywhere players have been, showing grayed-out previously-visited areas. This destroys the tension of exploration — players should have to build mental maps, take notes, or draw physical maps. The limit of memory should be a source of danger. His solution: chop dungeons into single-room maps, close each when players leave, and mix in narrative-only rooms.

**Key principle:** If you're running an exploration-based game and you provide an automap feature, you're destroying a lot of the tension and engagement that are the hallmarks of exploring dangerous, hostile, and alien spaces.

### Hate #2: VTTs Kill GM Versatility and DIY

Game mastering has strong DIY roots — quick hacks, improvised trackers, repurposed dice as counters. VTTs can't do anything they weren't programmed to do. You can't:
- Use a die as a counter or tracker on the fly
- Write reminders in character sheet margins
- Quickly add undefined items to inventory
- Improvise a battle map with the same speed as a dry-erase flip mat
- Implement a simple mechanical tweak without writing code

This matters because **open-endedness, flexibility, and adaptation are central promises of RPGs**. VTTs are built as clients to execute rulesets, not as true virtual tabletops. The DIY barrier also blocks the gateway drug that leads GMs from small tweaks to homebrewing to building their own systems.

### The Challenge

Scale your online game down to the absolute minimum tools needed, then add back one at a time. See which ones actually make a difference and which you can live without. The automation is costing you — know precisely what you're paying.

**Core framework:** Online gaming ≠ VTT gaming. Minimum viable online game = communication + shared dice rolls. Everything else is gravy with costs attached.
