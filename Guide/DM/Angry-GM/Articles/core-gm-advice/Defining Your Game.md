# Defining Your Game

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/defining-your-game/)
**Date:** August 14, 2011

---

## Defining Role-Playing

Role-playing is about assuming a hypothetical situation and trying to decide how you (or another character) would behave in that situation.

**The act of visualizing the situation, understanding the character, and deciding on an appropriate course of action is 90% of role-playing.** The remaining 10% is about presenting that decision to the other participants — and that last 10% is more about helping others role-play than about your own RP.

It doesn't matter how you present your decisions. First person, third person, specific voice, narration — none of that matters in terms of whether you are role-playing or not. All presentation techniques are classified as **"acting."**

**RP = imagine a hypothetical situation, project yourself into the mind of your character, and decide on a course of action.**

---

## When You Touch the Dice, You Stop Role-Playing (FALSE)

Consider: two characters at a roulette table with their last $20. Risky McGambler (reckless, always waiting for lucky breaks) and Cautious Von Meticulous (careful planner, secretly jealous of Risky's exciting life). Both decide to play — for very different reasons based on deep character understanding.

Now the GM picks up dice to resolve it. Does that mean it wasn't role-playing? Of course not. **Role-playing is what happens before and after the resolution of actions.** It is in the decision about what action to take and in the next decision about how to respond to the outcome.

---

## How Strong is Your RP

Not all RP is equal. There's a spectrum:

### Weak RP
Occurs when a player doesn't have to project themselves into the character's head. If the same player would make the same decision regardless of character, the RP is weak. Usually when a decision is based on reason/logic with an optimal answer. Solving a complex riddle is weak RP — it comes down to reasoning ability, not personality and goals.

### Strong RP
Driven by **internal conflict**. When a character is confronted by two things they want (or two things they don't want), their goals are in conflict and the player has to resolve that conflict. This requires the player to really get inside the character's head, go beyond the personality, and add something new. In real life, we show our personalities most strongly when conflicted.

---

## Those Are Fighting Words (Combat and RP)

Can you have strong RP in combat? It's possible, but exceedingly rare.

Combat is a tactical game — figuring out optimal strategy. Give the same fighter to several players and most will make very similar decisions. Differences come from tactical skill, not character personality. **Paradoxically, weak RP in combat is very accurate RP** — trained fighters put personalities aside and respond by rote.

Strong RP in combat requires putting characters' goals in conflict. Example: the only way to win a fight is to let two characters die. Resolve that, players.

Strong RP generally requires characters to give up one thing for another or choose between bad outcomes. 4E combat specifically strives to avoid that — clerics don't give up attacks to save injured characters, etc.

---

## Mixing it Up

A good session should include scenes of weak and strong RP. Strong RP is weighty, hard, and slows things down. Five hours of tough decisions is hard on anyone. A good game wanders back and forth along the RP spectrum.

Sometimes we want to get inside a character's head. Sometimes we want to solve tricky puzzles. Sometimes we want to kick the crap out of some greenskins and take their stuff.

---

## What's a System to Do?

A system can encourage or discourage RP:

### Visualizing
Anything helping players imagine the situation: strong consistent settings, logical rules, visual aids like maps and miniatures. These help players assess situations and likely outcomes.

### Projecting
Anything helping players understand their characters: strong archetypes, canonical details, personality traits on character sheets, bonuses/penalties derived from personality traits. These create incentives to use character personality as a guide.

### Deciding
Incentives for making good RP decisions. Mechanical incentives for following character personality. Ensuring decisions have consequences — good and bad — to set up conflicts. Freedom of decision is very important.

---

## How Does 4E Shape Up?

4E strives to remain staunchly neutral on RP — like every other edition of D&D. It provides a well-defined world and rules for creating situations, a strong method of action resolution, and then waits for players to make decisions. It bookends the RP.

D&D doesn't care if you role-play. You can play the entire game referring only to mechanics, or you can build strong characters and use characterization to drive every choice. It functions either way.

Compare to FATE (Aspect/Personality driven resolution), Warhammer Fantasy RP (party tension mechanics), or Mutants and Masterminds (complications = hero points). Those systems mechanically incentivize RP. D&D doesn't.

---

## So What's the Problem?

Every participant comes in with different expectations. No one who has read the Dresden Files rules will sit in without interest in strong RP. But with D&D, there is no default level of RP implied. Players can come in wanting no RP, lots of RP, or something in between.

If the group doesn't discuss expectations first, mismatches occur. The RP neutrality of D&D is a trade-off: you open the game to lots of different experiences, but you risk mismatched expectations.
