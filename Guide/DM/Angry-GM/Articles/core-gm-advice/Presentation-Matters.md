# Presentation Matters

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/presentation-matters/)
**Date:** October 26, 2022
**Category:** Random Bulls$&%

---

## The Power of "You" — Second-Person Perspective

D&D 5E is written in **second-person perspective** — addressing the reader directly with "you." This single word choice makes the books dramatically more readable than 3.5E's detailed-but-sterile third-person declarations.

Compare:
> "A creature can make an attack against any other creature within its melee reach by making an attack roll to determine whether the attack is a hit."

vs.

> "You can attack any creature in your melee reach. Make an attack roll to determine whether your attack hits."

Your brain literally requires more effort to parse indirect, declarative, third-person sentences than direct, imperative, second-person ones.

## Dungeon World and the PbtA Core Mechanic

**Dungeon World** (2012) — a Powered by the Apocalypse (PbtA) game — codified the conversational flow of RPGs into **Moves**:

> **Hack and Slash:** When you **attack an enemy in melee,** roll+STR. On a 10+, you deal your damage and avoid their attack. On a 7-9, you deal damage but the enemy attacks you.

This is brilliant design because it encapsulates the GM's most meta job: **listening for action declarations**. The GM waits for players to say something that triggers a Move — something that qualifies as an actual action requiring mechanics.

### The Alexa Analogy
As a GM, you're like an always-listening device. Players say lots of inane stuff. Occasionally, someone states their character's intention to do something that might affect the game world. You wake up, resolve the action, describe the result, and shift back to listening mode.

## The Simplest RPG Is the Best

Imagine an RPG where characters have just six ability scores with modifiers and a list of single-word skills. Nothing else.

This would be the **easiest to play, easiest to run, deepest, and most fun RPG possible.** Players know their character's strengths and skills. The GM listens for actions, picks an ability, sets a DC, and asks for a roll. If a player says "I'm trained for this," they get a bonus.

Everything layered on top of this simple foundation is more likely to **impede** the experience than enhance it. Especially if the players can see it.

## Where PbtA Failed: Players Seeing the Rules

Despite the brilliant design philosophy, **Dungeon World never works as intended at the table.** Players don't describe fictional actions and wait for the GM to trigger Moves. Instead they say:
- "I *Hack and Slash* the ogre"
- "Can I *Spout Lore* about this city?"
- "A trap? I *Defy Danger* it"

The rules explicitly forbid players from invoking Moves, but they do it anyway because **they can see the Moves.** Reference sheets, character sheets — once players see the mechanics, they interact with them directly instead of engaging with the fiction.

Hiding the Moves doesn't work either — some Moves (like *Spout Lore*) never get triggered if players don't know they exist. Others (like *Defy Danger*) get misused as saving throws when players invoke them directly.

---

## The Lesson

**Every rule the players can see and touch damages the open-ended, freeform nature of RPGs.**

- How rules are presented and who gets to see them matters more than the design intentions behind them
- Players should be able to play the vast majority of the game without naming a rule or asking for a die roll
- If players are invoking rules by name and calling for rolls, you're in board game or video game territory, not RPG territory
- The GM's job is to listen for fictional actions and resolve them — not to let players push mechanical buttons

This applies to D&D as well: the more player-facing rules and direct mechanical invocations a system includes, the further it drifts from open-ended roleplaying.
