# Tone Policing Sir Bearington

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/tone-policing-sir-bearington/)
**Date:** April 13, 2016
**Category:** Random Bulls$&%

---

## Summary

Every game has a tone — a general mood and emotional feel. The GM's job is to protect that tone. The Sir Bearington story and "Natural 20/Natural 1" stories are funny, but they represent terrible GMing because they ignore two critical things: (1) the GM must decide what is possible BEFORE dice are rolled, and (2) what is "possible" is governed not just by world logic but also by the game's tone.

## The Step the GM Skipped

The basic principle of action adjudication:

1. Player declares action
2. GM determines what the player wants to accomplish and how
3. **GM determines whether the player CAN get what they want by doing what they're doing**
4. GM uses rules to determine whether the action succeeds
5. GM describes the outcome and applies consequences

Most GMs skip step 3 and go straight from "player declares action" to "roll a check." This is how people end up seducing bugbears and oozes.

> **DECIDE WHAT IS POSSIBLE BEFORE YOU ROLL DICE.**

The rules don't tell you what is possible. They tell you how to determine the outcome of actions. The GM decides whether an action should even get into the rules.

## Two Criteria for "What's Possible"

### 1. Simulation (World Logic)

The D&D world starts with the same foundation as our universe — cause and effect, gravity, mortality, time. Players need consistency to make decisions and assess consequences. This is the GAME side of what's possible.

But people will argue endlessly about whether female dragonborn have boobs or whether fireball works underwater. The realists ignore fantasy; the fantasy wonks ignore consistency. Neither is fully right.

### 2. Tone (Narrative Feel)

Every story has a certain feel — a general mood and emotional weight. This is **tone.**

- You don't watch a horror movie for laughs
- You don't watch a comedy for catharsis
- You don't watch a sober drama to be frightened

The Sir Bearington story is funny because it's absurd — it subverts expectations. But most humor comes from subverting expectations, and RPGs are about making choices based on understanding cause and effect. There's inherent friction.

### Tone Breaks in Practice

**The Hobbit:** Characters ride a collapsing bridge down a bajillion-mile chasm and are fine. Later, you're supposed to fear for their lives on a cliff. But why? They survived cartoon physics earlier.

**Harry Potter:** The tone matured well as the series progressed, but Arthur Weasley's comical misunderstanding of muggle technology became increasingly weird as we learned the Ministry had an entire infrastructure maintaining secrecy.

**Star Wars Episode I:** Four simultaneous climax stories with four different tones. Your emotions can't keep up.

Tone can vary — it has to, or emotions get fatigued. But wild swings must be handled carefully. Pixar handles tonal shifts wonderfully. George Lucas and Peter Jackson often don't.

## The GM's Job: Tone Policing

Messed-up tone is the fastest way to get people to emotionally disengage. If emotions can't make sense of how things should feel, players check out. Emotional engagement is critical in RPGs because you have to be invested in your character to make decisions for them.

### What This Means in Practice

- If Sir Bearington is in your party, you can't pull off the Razing of the Shire
- If you're running Lord of the Rings, you can't have Sir Bearington
- If you're running Crouching Tiger, a player jumping to a rooftop is fine. If Aragorn does it, you'd call foul.

### Trust Your Gut

Most GMs already have a vague sense of tone. Have you ever had a player bring a character that felt too silly? A wacky gnomish inventor? A bard who only communicates through mime? A character named "Murry McMurrystine"? Have you felt in your gut you should say no?

**That's tone kicking you in the teeth.** And it's your JOB to say no. As the GM, it is your job to safeguard the tone for you and your players.

## Natural 20s and Natural 1s

The "funny crit/fumble stories" follow the same pattern:

1. Player tries to do something ridiculous
2. Player rolls a 1 or 20
3. GM decides the ridiculous thing succeeds/fails in an over-the-top way
4. "Hilarity ensues"

This is terrible gaming. A natural 20 is a character using their full potential — the best they can do. Sometimes your best still isn't good enough. A nat 20 on seduction doesn't work if the target isn't interested. A nat 20 on Athletics doesn't let you jump to the moon.

The GM must decide what's possible BEFORE dice hit the table.

## The Balance

How do you balance world logic and tone? It's subjective. You feel your way through. Consistency of both tone and reality matters, but sometimes preserving one breaks the other. Whatever compromise you go with is probably okay.

> "Sir Bearington is okay if your game is Animaniacs. But not if it's Lord of the Rings. The only thing that's NOT okay is not thinking about it."

If you're aware of these ideas, you'll tend to keep things mostly consistent. If you find yourself really struggling on a decision — going back and forth — it's probably because the decision can go either way and be fine. Flip a coin.

**The one rule: never let someone roll a die until you already know what the possible outcomes are.**
