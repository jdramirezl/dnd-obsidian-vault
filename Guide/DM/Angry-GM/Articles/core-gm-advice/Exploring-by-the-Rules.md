# Exploring by the Rules

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/exploring-by-the-rules/)
**Date:** June 23, 2021
**Series:** GMing Basically

---

## Core Lesson

> Whatever game system you're running: know the rules, use the rules, and don't f$&% with the rules.

Knowing the rules and properly playing by them is important if you want to run a good, fun, interesting, meaningful game.

---

## Meaningful Choices Emerge from Mechanics

Angry doesn't *create* meaningful choices. They emerge from the complex interplay of external constraints (game mechanics) and internal motivations (the players' and characters' needs and wants).

Most people think meaningful choices must involve moral dilemmas or deep character moments. But most meaningful choices aren't that heavy. Even minor choices about combat actions or whether to press on or return to town can reveal things about the characters, the players, their experiences, and the world.

---

## The Road Trip Example

A hypothetical party — Team Streptococcus — must travel from Perrin's Mill to Highgrove. Two routes:

- **The Road:** 50 miles, safe, predictable. ~3 days travel.
- **The Wilderness:** 20 miles as the dragon flies, through rugged hills and dense forest. ~2 days, but with navigation risk, encounter risk, and supply concerns.

### The Factors That Make the Choice Meaningful

Each is a "grain on the scale" — no single mechanic drives the decision alone:

1. **Travel Speed:** 2 mph on roads, 1 mph in rough terrain. 16 miles/day vs 8 miles/day.
2. **Food & Supplies:** ~5 days of food per member without encumbrance penalties.
3. **Navigation:** Roads are trivial. Wilderness navigation is imprecise — dead reckoning, landmarks, weather interference. Getting lost can add days.
4. **Random Encounters:** Roads = fewer hazards, more travelers, less discovery. Wilderness = more danger, more unique discoveries.
5. **Party Motivation:** Adventurers seeking discovery? Questers with a deadline? Escorting a fragile NPC? Racing against time?
6. **Party Composition:** Does the ranger have favored terrain: forests?

The "correct" answer is always "it depends." The meaningfulness pops out once you add sufficient detail — mechanical constraints plus player motivations.

### How the Trip Plays Out

The party follows the lakeshore, then breaks north. Two dangerous encounters on day one burn resources. They hunker down. Rain the next day penalizes navigation. A swollen stream forces a detour. They emerge off-course near the Howlwind Hills. From a treetop, they spot a mysterious ruin — but only have two days of food left.

Now they face layered decisions: check out the ruin and risk running out of food? Ration and push through? Forage? Mark the trail and come back later? Every choice grows from previous choices.

## Breaking the Watch One Gear at a Time

> GMs open up their watches and start pulling out gears and springs and screws that don't seem to be doing anything and then they can't understand why they don't know what time it is anymore.

Most GMs evaluate every rule in a vacuum. They can't find a situation where encumbrance alone drives an interesting decision, so they drop it. Then random encounters. Weather. Travel tasks. Meanwhile, they flip through the rules looking for "the exploration system" and can't find it — because that stuff is **emergent**.

### What GMs Get Wrong

- They don't know what rules are in the PHB and DMG (pages 181-183 have travel rules many have never read)
- They evaluate rules in isolation instead of as part of a system
- They gut rules without understanding how they interact
- They then complain the game "doesn't have exploration mechanics"

---

## The Actual Advice

Run your games as close to "by the book" as you can manage. The 5E rules aren't perfect, but they're better than most GMs think.

> Open the book and learn how to run D&D. Run it the way the book says for at least six months before you touch a single thing.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Know the rules** — actually read the PHB and DMG cover to cover
2. **Use the rules** — don't gut mechanics you haven't tried in practice
3. **Don't f$&% with the rules** — run RAW for at least 6 months before houseruling
4. **Meaningful choices are emergent** — they come from the interplay of many small mechanics and player motivations
5. **Every mechanic is a grain on the scale** — encumbrance, weather, navigation, random encounters, travel pace — none matters alone, but together they create depth
6. **Most GM hacking is streamlining, not removing** — Angry rarely advocates removing rules; he streamlines them to work better
