# Schrödinger, Chekhov, Samus

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/schrodinger-chekhov-samus/)
**Date:** August 15, 2010
**Category:** Hacking Rules

---

## Overview

Introduces "Project Slaughterhouse" — a dynamic site-based adventure planning and management tool for large, open environments (mega-dungeons, ruined cities, wilderness regions). Combines Metroidvania-style zone design with faction-based population management and Schrödinger's Gun philosophy to create living, responsive adventure sites.

---

## Core Concepts

### Schrödinger's Gun

**Nothing in the game is true until the party observes it.**

Combines Schrödinger's Cat (things exist in superposition until observed) with Chekhov's Gun (every detail should matter). Since the DM can't predict what players will do, the DM can change unobserved details to serve the story.

**Examples:**
- An important NPC the party keeps missing? Move them to the next unexplored room.
- An artifact the party uses in an unexpected way? Let it work if they haven't seen otherwise.
- The party doesn't know how many orcs are in the fortress — only what they've directly encountered.

### Zones (Inspired by Metroidvania Games)

Break the adventure map into interconnected thematic regions — like Brinstar, Norfair, and Maridia in Super Metroid. Each zone:
- Is self-contained with multiple entrances/exits to other zones
- Has consistent terrain and flavor
- Has space for 4–5 encounters
- Can be covered in roughly one adventuring day
- You need about **twice as many zones as experience levels** the adventure spans

Zones can be dungeon maps, city districts, wilderness regions, or abstract areas navigated through narrative/skill challenges.

### Factions

Organized populations of creatures that respond to player actions as a group. A faction is any group whose fate is linked — a tribe, a cult and its summoned creatures, aberrants spawning from a rift.

- Aim for **half as many factions as zones** — leave unclaimed and contested territory.
- Each faction has a **roster**: a list of creature types (not a census) used to build varied encounters, including pets, servants, slaves, allies, and unique individuals.

## The Zone Stat Block

The centerpiece of Slaughterhouse. Each zone has stat blocks defining its role, population, and responses to player actions.

### Zone Roles

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Lair** | Faction HQ. If a faction loses all lairs, they leave the adventure site permanently. Source of new monsters. |
| **Outpost** | Loosely controlled territory. Faction will abandon it if holding becomes too costly. |
| **Contested** | Two+ factions fighting for control. If one is eliminated, the other claims it as an outpost. |
| **Unclaimed** | No faction interested. Populated by unaligned creatures; factions may wander through. |

### Population Thresholds

Population is measured in XP worth of creatures. Thresholds prevent the party from cheesing encounters one at a time:

- **Full Population:** Resets if the party retreats and rests before making a dent.
- **Depleted:** Party has killed enough (roughly half) that the population stops resetting. Progress is saved.
- **Abandoned:** Population gives up and leaves (roughly one encounter's worth remaining).

### Triggered Actions

Special conditions that change the zone's state:
- Assassinating faction leaders causes internal chaos → abandonment
- Closing a planar rift removes the aberrant faction → other factions move in
- A faction claiming a key location (temple) changes their behavior elsewhere

### Zone Transitions

When a zone changes hands, a new stat block takes effect. The Fortress might go from "Lair of Orcs" → "Outpost of Lord Vizier" → "Unclaimed Territory," each with its own stat block.

## Example: The City of Ur'Gunna Dy

A ruined Bael Turath city, levels 5–9.

**8 Zones:** Noble Quarter, Artisan Quarter, Market Quarter, Slums, Fortress, Palace, Temple, Sewers

**4 Factions:**
- **Blood Pudding Orcs** — violent tribe spreading through the city
- **Pointy Stick Gnolls** — violent tribe trying to kill everything
- **Lord Vizier von Steward** — tiefling cult with devils, wants to reclaim the Temple for Asmodeus (potential ally!)
- **The Slimy Bunch** — aberrants entering through a Far Plane rift in the sewers (close the rift to defeat them)

**Dynamic interactions:** Orcs and gnolls fight each other and Lord Vizier. The party could ally with Lord Vizier. Closing the sewer rift causes orcs and gnolls to bring their war underground.

## Using Slaughterhouse

### Prep
- Heavy upfront work: multiple stat blocks per zone, rosters per faction, zone maps
- But this is expected for open-ended sandbox games

### Between Sessions
- Repopulate explored areas quickly using stat blocks and rosters
- Remove defeated factions, abandon their outposts, update zone states
- Adjust based on population thresholds and triggers

### During Play
- If the party enters an unpopulated zone, the roster and stat block give you everything needed to populate it on the fly

### Multi-DM Potential
- Multiple DMs can share the same dungeon using rosters and stat blocks
- Different parties can explore the same site, each changing it for the other
- The whole system can be shared in ~50 pages plus maps

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Break large environments into themed zones** with multiple connections.
2. **Organize monsters into factions** that respond as groups to player actions.
3. **Use population thresholds** to prevent one-encounter-at-a-time cheese.
4. **Prepare zone stat blocks with transitions** — what happens when a zone changes hands.
5. **Embrace Schrödinger's Gun** — nothing is true until the party observes it.
6. **Leave empty space** for creatures to wander into and for the environment to feel alive.
7. **Faction personality through mechanics** — cowardly factions abandon outposts quickly; determined ones dig in.
