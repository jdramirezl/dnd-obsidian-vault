# How to Run an Online Game… If You Must

> [Source: The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-run-an-online-game/)

---

## Key Advice

- **All you truly need is a conference call** — everything else (VTTs, dice bots, shared docs) is optional
- **Audio quality is the #1 priority** — your brain works harder to understand speech without visual cues; poor audio destroys games
- **Do NOT play music in online games** — it competes with the already-strained audio processing your players' brains are doing
- **Virtual games run slower** — expect less ground covered per session; smaller groups work better online
- **Focus and immersion are inherently reduced** — this is neuroscience, not player laziness; electronic communication taxes the brain
- **Trust your players with dice** — let them roll physical dice at their desks; there's no cash prize for pretend elves
- **Beware Creeping Doom** — every VTT feature has a hidden cost (learning curve, prep time sink, gameplay lag); the sum is always greater than the parts
- VTT features are tools, not requirements — **use only what improves your game enough to justify the cost**
- If you're burnt out on online games, **scale back the electronic tools first** — they're likely the source of the weight
- **D&D is not ideal for online play** — rules-light, narrative-heavy systems play to the virtual medium's strengths

## Summary

Running games online is a compromise, not an upgrade. The single most important thing is clear, stable audio — invest there before anything else. Your brain literally works harder to process speech without visual cues, which is why online games feel more exhausting and run slower. VTTs like Fantasy Grounds are useful but every feature (dynamic lighting, macros, initiative trackers) carries hidden costs: learning curves, extra prep doing data entry instead of creative work, and gameplay lag from clicking through interfaces. These costs accumulate into "Creeping Doom" — a gradual burnout where your game becomes a slog under the weight of life-improving technology. The antidote is minimalism: start without a VTT, add features only when they clearly earn their cost, and scale back ruthlessly when things feel heavy. If starting fresh, consider a lighter system that plays to the online medium's strengths (narrative immersion, character focus) rather than its weaknesses (visual precision, tactical grids).

## Actionable Steps

1. **Prioritize audio**: Invest in a good conferencing solution (Discord for free, Zoom for reliability) before any VTT
2. **Ban background music**: It actively harms comprehension in online games — full stop
3. **Start without a VTT**: Try running with just voice, physical dice, and shared cloud folders first
4. **If using a VTT, audit your features**: List every feature you use; for each, honestly assess whether the gameplay improvement justifies the prep time and cognitive load
5. **Let players roll physical dice**: Trust them; the game is better when rolling is fast and tactile
6. **Keep groups small**: 3-4 players is the sweet spot for online; more players = exponentially more slowdown
7. **If burnt out, scale back tools first**: Strip your VTT down to just a battle map and dice roller; track everything else the old way
8. **Consider system fit**: For a new online campaign, consider rules-light systems that emphasize narrative over tactical precision

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Online games need clear audio and minimal tools, not maximum features. Every VTT bell and whistle has a hidden cost that accumulates into burnout. Start simple, add only what earns its keep, and scale back ruthlessly when the game starts feeling heavy.
