# I'm Making This Up As I Go: When Rules Fail

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/when-rules-fail/)
**Date:** April 21, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice
**Series:** Improve Your Improv (Part 2 of 3)

---

## Summary

The second part of the improvisation series, covering mechanical improvisation — what to do when the rules fail you. Rules fail because: there's no rule for the situation, you forgot the rule, or the rule exists but is stupid. Angry provides a framework for making rulings based on three criteria (verisimilitude, tone, and game balance), explains why every ruling sets a permanent precedent, and walks through three concrete examples.

---

## Key Points

### When Rules Fail

Rules fail for three reasons:
1. **No rule exists** — players can try literally anything; infinite degrees of freedom means infinite gaps.
2. **You forgot the rule** — hundreds of pages of nitpicky rules; you can't remember everything.
3. **The rule is stupid** — design choices involve tradeoffs (balance vs. verisimilitude); sometimes the designers got it wrong or created new problems while closing loopholes.

### The Rules Are NOT the Most Important Tool

- The GM's brain is the most important tool. Rules are like judges on a game show — the host (GM) makes most decisions and only consults the judges (rules) when unsure.
- **The Nine Dots Problem:** People imagine constraints that aren't there. Creating a rule for situations A, B, and C makes people assume D is impossible.
- The correct order: (1) Player says a thing → (2) GM decides if it's possible or impossible → (3) GM decides if rules are needed → (4) GM uses rules. Your brain is first AND last.

### Know Your Core Mechanic

Every RPG has a core mechanic that fits on one page. For D&D (all modern editions):

> Roll 1d20 + Ability Modifier + Other Modifiers vs. Static DC

Know the DC scale (5E: 5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30), how to modify rolls (advantage/disadvantage in 5E; ±2 to ±4 in 3E/4E/PF), and how saving throw DCs are calculated. This lets you resolve almost any action without looking anything up.

### What's Possible? — The Three Questions

Reject simplistic rules like "always say yes," "no, but," or "rule of cool." They encourage mindlessness. Instead, ask three questions:

1. **Verisimilitude** — "Does it make sense based on how this world works?"
2. **Tone** — "Does it fit the way this world feels?"
3. **Game Balance** — "Will this break the game mechanically?"

The first two are gut checks. Balance is harder — it requires you to foresee consequences.

### The Third Triforce: Game Balance

Balance is how all the system's numbers and probabilities work together so that:
- A CR-appropriate encounter is challenging but survivable
- Each PC contributes roughly equally regardless of class/race mix
- The GM can predict and plan outcomes

Breaking balance means you can no longer find the sweet spot of "challenging but not frustrating."

### All Improv Is a Precedent

**Every mechanical ruling becomes a permanent rule in your game.** Players will always assume things work consistently. The moment you allow "stab the cyclops in the eye for free blinding," that's the rule forever. You CAN go back on decisions, but each reversal pushes players toward "act at random because the rules keep changing."

### Three Warning Signs for Balance Problems

1. **The Three-Times Test** — Imagine the same situation arising three more times this session. Would you feel good giving the same answer each time?
2. **The Player Exploitation Test** — As a player, how often would you use this ruling? Would you ever have a reason NOT to?
3. **The Frequency Test** — How often will this situation come up? Multiple times per session = serious concern. Rarely = probably fine.

### Prioritizing the Three Criteria

There is no universal priority. It depends on your table:
- **Challenge-focused groups:** Balance and verisimilitude beat tone.
- **Collaborative storytelling groups:** Tone and verisimilitude matter most.
- **Wacky/loose groups:** Tone can take a backseat.
- **Abstract dungeon crawlers:** Tone and balance matter; verisimilitude less so.

Even within these preferences, extreme violations of any criterion will break the game regardless of priority.

### Compromise: Finding the Middle Ground

When a ruling doesn't cleanly pass all three criteria:
- **Offer alternatives** — Drill down to the player's desired outcome and find a way to deliver it without the problematic element.
- **Add costs, consequences, or penalties** — The flying tackle works, but both PC and target take falling damage. The eye stab works, but with disadvantage.
- **Find similar rules** for guidance on how the system handles comparable situations.

**Goal:** Make a call you'll feel good about being a permanent part of your game.

### Three Worked Examples

#### 1. Monk/Rogue Sneak Attack with Unarmed Strike (5E)
- **Tone:** Fine — monks already punch through armor with ki energy.
- **Verisimilitude:** A little odd, but monks are odd. Fits the game's level of realism.
- **Balance:** Most finesse weapons already do equal or more damage than martial arts dice. One sneak attack per turn is reasonable. The monk could use a monk weapon and unambiguously qualify anyway.
- **Ruling:** Yes, allow it.

#### 2. Fireball Underwater (5E)
- **Balance:** No problem — a fireball is a fireball, meant to blast enemies.
- **Verisimilitude:** Big problem — fire penetrating and exploding underwater breaks suspension of disbelief.
- **Tone:** In a world where elements interact (water beats fire), it feels wrong.
- **Ruling:** No underwater fireballs.

#### 3. Stabbing a Cyclops in the Eye to Blind It
- **Tone:** Fits standard fantasy adventure perfectly.
- **Verisimilitude:** Borderline — called shots in active combat are harder than games suggest, but D&D combat is closer to mythology than real warfare.
- **Balance:** Serious problem — if it works easily, players will gouge eyes in every fight, trivializing encounters and making piercing weapons dominant.
- **Ruling:** Compromise. (1) Player must explain how they reach the eye (probably a skill check). (2) Attack has Disadvantage. (3) If it hits, the creature is blinded. (4) This only works because a cyclops eye is a large target — against normal-sized creatures, it's beyond possible.

---

## Core Principle

> When rules fail, think through verisimilitude, tone, and balance. There is no universal shortcut. Every ruling is a precedent. Make a call you'll feel good about being permanent, and compromise when the answer isn't clean.
