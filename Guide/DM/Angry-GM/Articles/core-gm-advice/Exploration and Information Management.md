# Exploration and Information Management

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/exploration-and-information-management/)
**Date:** March 9, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## The Core Principle: Everyone Starts Stupid

Every player starts every session essentially stupid. You never know how much your players know about the game, your world, or the adventure. Anything you think the players and characters need to know must be revealed in the adventure. This brings us to two types of scenes for information management: **exposition scenes** and **exploration scenes**.

## Exposition vs. Exploration

**Exposition scenes** — you simply hand the players information. An NPC tells them what they need to know, a document provides crucial info, a vision accosts the characters, or you just tell them what their characters already know. Players are fairly passive — they just receive information.

**Exploration scenes** — information is available but PCs have to make choices to discover it. Instead of a briefing, PCs ask questions. They play out a dream sequence. They dig through a library asking specific questions. The key is interactivity.

The two often meld together. A briefing (exposition) can morph into an exploration scene as PCs ask further questions. That's fine.

**The major difference:** Exploration scenes can result in missed information. Exposition scenes guarantee delivery.

## Exposition Encounters

When you add a source of conflict and a dramatic question to an exposition/exploration scene, it becomes an **encounter**. The moment you add a die roll, you've added a question: will the PCs acquire the information?

Examples:
- The briefing officer is withholding classified information — can the PCs convince him?
- The bard is insane and his information is jumbled — can the PCs break through?
- Information is scattered across a library — can the PCs piece it together?

You can also create encounters without dice — a riddle or cryptic clue provides conflict and uncertainty without rolling.

## Types of Information

### Mandatory Information
Absolutely necessary. The adventure literally cannot function without it — the goal, the motivation, transitions between scenes. **Must always be delivered.** This is why most adventures start with exposition.

### Objective Information
Represents the objective or means to accomplish it. The identity of the killer, the location of the hidden shrine. Without it, PCs can participate but can't succeed. **Can be broken up** — a murder mystery scatters clues across scenes.

### Helpful Information
Makes the adventure easier. Enemy weaknesses, treasure locations, secret passages. **Rewards players** but isn't necessary. Best hidden on side paths or behind encounters.

### Background Information
Context about the world, characters, or events. The killer's tragic childhood, the civilization that created the weapon. Doesn't help mechanically but makes the world make sense.

### Foreshadowing Information
Not immediately useful but sets up future events. Future adventure hooks, introducing factions that become important later. Creates the sense of a larger, interconnected story.

## How Optional Is Objective Information?

This is the hardest decision every GM faces. It comes down to your approach to failure and the adventure's purpose.

**If gathering clues IS the adventure** (like a murder mystery), then objective information should be missable. The more obscure and difficult the clues, the more challenging the adventure. If the PCs mess up badly enough, the mystery goes unsolved.

**The boss fight analogy:** If you wouldn't let PCs automatically win a boss fight regardless of dice and decisions, don't do that in a mystery either. But if you're prepared to handle failure, failure is not a problem in an RPG.

## Creating Exposition Scenes

You already know the purpose (deliver information). The rest is deciding:
- Where does the scene take place?
- What form does the information take? (NPCs, dreams, maps, documents)
- Create the appropriate interactive bits

A single scene can contain multiple types of information hidden inside different layers. An NPC might contain mandatory info on the surface, helpful info if pressed, and foreshadowing if the PCs dig deep enough.

## Information and Improvisation

Everything above is about planning. But improvisation is constant in D&D. Key principles:

**Think about the INFORMATION first, the FORM second.** If PCs ask unexpected questions, give the same information in different ways.

**Disguise exposition as exploration.** Let players ask questions and make sure you give all planned information in response. If anything gets missed, use the "oh, one more thing" approach — the NPC adds a detail just as the scene ends.

**Players will invent exposition scenes.** One day they'll decide to research at a library you didn't plan. Use your information list — plant it in the new scene. You can even turn it into an encounter on the fly.

**For information-based adventures, HAVE the information beforehand.** Do NOT improvise an entire murder mystery from nothing. Those mysteries suck. You need the information in your head first.

### The Exception: "I Wonder What That Was About"

Sometimes, when an adventure falls flat or pace slows, insert a character or event from the ether. See how it plays out. Write it down. After the adventure, figure out what it was and what it meant. You're inventing foreshadowing without knowing what it's foreshadowing. The important step: after the adventure, write it down and figure out what that was all about.

Information management relies on YOU having the information you're managing. That takes work. Lazy GMs need not apply.
