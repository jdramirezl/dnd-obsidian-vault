# How to Be Wrong as a GM (And as a Patron)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-be-wrong-as-a-gm/)
**Date:** February 28, 2018
**Series:** Fanservice BS

---

## Part 1: Yes, There ARE Wrong Ways to Game

### "There Is No Wrong Way to Game" Is Harmful

There are plenty of wrong ways to game. The proof: the internet is choked with articles about "how to be a good player," "how to deal with problem players," and "what to do about bad GMs."

### "As Long as Everyone Is Having Fun" Is Useless Advice

1. **It provides no instruction.** Like a cooking instructor saying "the key is to make it taste good."
2. **Fun is momentary.** A good game is partly a good story — with highs and lows, setups and payoffs, tension and resolution.
3. **Fun is only one type of emotional engagement.** A brutal combat isn't "fun" in the moment, but when the final foe goes down and players are high-fiving, it's all paid off.
4. **Engagement requires pacing.** Like a runner who paces themselves rather than desperately staying ahead.

> It IS possible to game wrong. Neither FUN nor ENGAGEMENT are good metrics because they only work at a snapshot in time.

## Part 2: What to Do When You're Wrong

### Step 0: Get Your Mind Right

**Get blame and fault out of your head.** You're the captain. Anything that happens is your responsibility — even if it isn't your fault. Someone HAS TO be responsible, and you're in the big chair.

> Even when you're totally right, you can still be wrong.

**Accept that players' feelings are valid.** When someone is unhappy, assume they're unhappy for a reason. You can't dismiss their feelings, even if the problem is technically their own fault.

**Example:** A player's wizard keeps getting knocked unconscious because they play it as a front-line fighter. The player feels targeted. You can't say "he's playing wrong, so he needs to get over it." The feeling is real and must be addressed — without creating an adversarial relationship.

### The Mental Hurdles (In Order)

1. Be willing to admit to yourself you might be wrong
2. Be willing to take responsibility for being wrong
3. Be willing to take responsibility for anything under your jurisdiction, regardless of direct control
4. Then you can actually handle being wrong

### The Two-Step Fix

1. **Apologize**
2. **Fix it**

You must do both.

**If you catch it in the moment:** Stop the game. Apologize. Propose a solution. ("Let's assume you win here. Whatever you lost, you lost. But no one has died yet.")

**If it already happened:** Apologize first, then negotiate a solution with affected parties. ("I messed up, I'm sorry, let's work out a fix that works for everyone.")

### Important Caveats

1. **Only involve the affected parties.** Handle problems privately, not in front of the group.
2. **Keep solutions in-scope:**
   - **In-game problems → in-game solutions.** Character died unfairly? Restore them, give a new character, let them keep a treasured item. Don't buy them a pizza.
   - **Out-of-game problems → out-of-game solutions.** If you offended someone, mend the feelings. No amount of bonus XP fixes a personal hurt.

## Key Takeaways

1. There ARE wrong ways to game — "no wrong way" and "just have fun" are harmful non-advice
2. Fun is momentary; engagement and long-term satisfaction matter more
3. The GM is responsible for everything at the table, even things not strictly their fault
4. Players' feelings are always valid, even when the problem is technically their own doing
5. When wrong: **apologize first, then negotiate a fix** with affected parties
6. Keep in-game fixes for in-game problems, out-of-game fixes for out-of-game problems
7. Handle problems privately with affected parties, not publicly
