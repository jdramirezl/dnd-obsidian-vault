# What it Means to Know How to Play D&D

> **Source:** [The Angry GM — What it Means to Know How to Play D&D](https://theangrygm.com/knowing-how-to-play-dd/)
> **Date:** August 11, 2021

---

## Context

An article about the gap between knowing D&D's rules and actually knowing how to *play* D&D — how to function with basic proficiency in a medieval fantasy world. Proposes a "Dear Players" essay series to teach players (and GMs) the practical skills the rules assume but never teach.

---

## The Problem: Nobody Knows How to Play D&D

### It's Not About the Rules

Players know how to roll ability checks, take actions on their turns, and use their character abilities. That's not the problem. The problem is a **middle ground** between rules knowledge and strategic skill:

- How to look at a map and plan a route
- How to find a destination whose exact location you only roughly know
- How to run a social interaction encounter
- How to get information and do research in a medieval fantasy world
- How to communicate with the GM and other players
- How to agree on a course of action as a team
- How to strategize (not what strategies to use, but *how* to work together to come up with and execute a strategy)

### Why Nobody Knows

1. **The books don't teach it** — The rules give you pieces (travel pace, foraging, resting, wilderness encounters) but don't show how to put them together. It's like having all the card game rules but missing the turn order.

2. **Nobody reads the rules** — Everyone thinks they know the rules because they learned from someone else who also didn't read them. The *Monopoly* problem.

3. **Everyone thinks they're smarter than the designers** — GMs rewrite rules before even playing them as intended.

4. **"There's no wrong way to play"** — The insistence that D&D should be anything to anyone means vital information about how the game actually works gets dismissed as optional.

### The Hidden Resources Nobody Uses

Example: According to the D&D Player's Handbook, you can pay 10-50 gp and the GM will tell you whether a course of action is a good or bad idea. (Combining two rules from different sections of the PHB — hiring a spellcaster to cast Augury.) Every person in the medieval fantasy world would know that when you're stuck, you go to a priest and have them ask the gods. But players never think of it.

---

## The Three Levels of "How to Play"

1. **Rules** — How to roll checks, turn order, action economy. What's in the rulebook.
2. **World Proficiency** — How to function at a basic level in the D&D world. Not quite rules, not quite strategy. A sense of how to do things that always come up: wilderness travel, social interaction, dealing with traps and locks, getting information. **This is what's missing.**
3. **Strategy** — How to pick the right action, manage resources, make good choices. Learned over time.

### The Frame of Reference Problem

Players lack a frame of reference for the medieval fantasy world. They can't imagine possible solutions because they don't know what's possible. They don't know:

- That oracles and diviners exist and have reliable information
- How to find an expert on a topic
- How to extract information from an unwilling NPC beyond rolling Persuasion
- How to plan a wilderness expedition
- What to do when they first arrive in a new town
- How to gather information in a village vs. a town vs. a city

---

## The Proposed Solution: "Dear Players" Essays

Short letters — single pages — that GMs can print and hand to players:

> *Dear players… So, you want to get some information out of an NPC and you can't just beat it out of them. What do you do?*

> *Dear players… So, you found out you've got to evict a nalfeshnee from the local puppy orphanage, but you've got no idea what a nalfeshnee even is. How do you find out what you want to know about a monster?*

> *Dear players… So, you found an old journal describing a treasure-filled ruin hidden somewhere in the Doomdark Hills but it doesn't tell you exactly where. How do you find a thing in the wilderness?*

### The GM Problem

Most GMs also don't know how to put this stuff together. They know the rules and wait for players to invoke them without realizing there's a gulf between rules and actually making a plan. So every "Dear Players" essay needs an accompanying "Dear GMs" essay explaining the behind-the-scenes stuff.

---

## Key Quotes

> "I've got a whole world filled with quests and adventures and exciting locations to explore. All my players have to do is recognize them and then resolve — as a team — to do them or go to them or explore them and then figure out a strategy for doing, going, or exploring. And to gather whatever information they need to make that happen. All the resources are there. But they don't know how to put them together."

> "We all know this is a problem. All of us GMs. Because we're always looking for more codification. We need rules for social interactions. Social combat. We need better travel and exploration rules. Why do we feel like that's not there? Because it kinda is in the rules. But it's all broken apart."

> "The solution's not more rules. Because we don't want to turn our open-ended world of fantasy adventure into Wizard Monopoly. The solution's just to actually teach players how to play the game."

---

## Summary

> There's a critical gap between knowing D&D's rules and knowing how to *play* D&D — how to function in a medieval fantasy world, put scattered rules together into coherent actions, and navigate situations the rules assume you understand. Players lack the frame of reference to imagine possible solutions. The fix isn't more rules — it's teaching players (and GMs) the practical skills of adventuring through short, accessible essays.
