# The Declare-Determine-Describe Cycle

## Key Advice

The DDD Cycle is the fundamental rhythm of running an RPG at the table. Every moment of play follows this loop: **Narrate → Invite → Declare → Determine → Describe → Invite → ...** The GM acts as a **translator** between the narrative world (Worldspeak) and the game mechanics (Gamespeak), keeping the rules locked away so they don't wreck pacing and immersion. Players should never need to speak in game-mechanical terms.

## Summary

### Worldspeak vs. Gamespeak

- **Worldspeak** = narrative language describing the fictional world (what you'd see on screen)
- **Gamespeak** = mechanical language (dice, DCs, skill names, action types)
- **Naked Gamespeak** (Gamespeak without Worldspeak) is forbidden — always pair mechanics with narrative context
- Bad: "Make a DC 15 Athletics check" → Good: "The wall doesn't have many handholds; it's a difficult climb — DC 15"

### The Three Rules of Action Declaration

1. **Be Complete** — players must state what they're trying to accomplish AND how (intent + approach), including tools, resources, tone, etc.
2. **Be Concise** — no flowery prose, no describing outcomes before they're resolved; declaring an action is stating an *intent to attempt*
3. **Don't Cite Rules** — players describe what their character does in the world, not what game mechanic they want to invoke

### The GM's Duties During Declaration

- **Error Check** — enforce the three rules every time; train players through consistent correction
- **Post Warnings** — if an action is impossible, suicidal, or has hidden costs the character would know about (like provoking opportunity attacks), warn the player
- **Confirm the Action** — repeat back the action using Worldspeak and get the player's assent before resolving: "So, Ardrick intends to..."

### Determining Outcomes

Use your **brain first, rules second**. Decide: Can it succeed? Can it fail? What do success and failure look like? Is there a cost or consequence? Only then decide if you need dice.

### Describing Outcomes

Translate the mechanical result back into narrative. Repeat the action as an outcome instead of an attempt. Never skip this step — "That's a hit for 7 damage" is not description.

### The Full Rhythm

1. **Narrate** (breathe, then speak)
2. **Invite the Principal Player to Act** (speak to players about characters)
3. **Declare** (error check → post warnings → confirm action)
4. **Determine** (brain first, rules if needed)
5. **Describe** (translate outcome to narrative)
6. Loop back to Invite

### On First- vs. Third-Person Speech

It doesn't matter. Never reward or require in-character speech. A player can declare any action — even an inspiring speech — in a couple of third-person sentences. Requiring in-character performance is an obstacle to participation, especially in social scenes.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Make a sticky-note checklist** of the DDD cycle and keep it visible during sessions: Narrate → Invite → Error Check → Warn → Confirm → Determine → Describe
2. **Train players on the three declaration rules** — correct incomplete, verbose, or rules-citing declarations every time until it becomes habit
3. **Always confirm actions before resolving** — "So, [Character] intends to [action] by [method]?" Get a yes before touching dice
4. **Always warn about foreseeable consequences** — opportunity attacks, impossible actions, hidden costs the character would perceive
5. **Always narrate outcomes** — never just announce numbers; translate every mechanical result into what happens in the fiction
6. **Eliminate Naked Gamespeak** — pair every DC, modifier, and roll with a narrative description of what it represents
7. **Use rules as written when you use them** — but remember you don't have to use them for every action; your brain is the primary adjudication tool
8. **Practice fundamentals before adding flair** — get the rhythm smooth and automatic first, then dress it up with descriptive prose

> [!tip] You are a translator, not a rules processor
> Your job is to take player actions expressed in narrative terms, feed them through the rules engine only when necessary, and translate the result back into narrative. The less time spent in Gamespeak, the better your game feels. Build the habit of always narrating outcomes — even a simple "Ardrick's sword bites into the orc's shoulder for seven damage" is infinitely better than "that hits, seven damage."
