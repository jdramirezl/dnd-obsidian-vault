# Keeping Pace

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Keeping Pace](https://theangrygm.com/keeping-pace/)
**Date:** March 23, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## Summary

Pacing is the rhythm of a story — the speed at which things happen in scenes. It is NOT the same as "how fast the game moves at the table" (which Angry calls **flow**). Pacing is about adventure *design*; flow is about game *execution*. Understanding the distinction is critical.

---

## Pacing: The Real Definition

Pacing is the speed at which things happen within a scene. Every scene has a pace determined by its purpose and content:

- **Fast scenes** — lots of things happen quickly; individual details matter less than the overall scene. They create excitement, tension, and usually convey one single strong emotion. Combat, chases, heated arguments, training montages, and passionate encounters are all fast scenes.
- **Slow scenes** — fewer things happen; the audience engages their thinking brain. Slow scenes are for imparting information (exposition or emotional/character content), for emphasis, for building anticipation, and for varying emotional tone. Slow scenes are where the *story truly happens*.

Most stories have far more slow scenes than fast scenes. Most action movies have only about three strong action sequences — the rest is informational and emotional content.

---

## Pacing, Energy, and Emphasis

- Fast scenes make hearts race, create excitement and anxiety. The audience leans forward and engages their gut.
- Slow scenes make the audience more attentive. They sit back, their brains engage more, and they absorb information and emotional content better.
- This is why Star Wars doesn't explain senate politics during a gunfight — the audience isn't in the right frame of mind to absorb exposition during a fast scene.

---

## Slow Scenes Are Boring, Fast Scenes Are Tiring

The pace **must vary**. Otherwise:

- All slow = dull. If you emphasize everything, you emphasize nothing.
- All fast = exhausting. Fast scenes are a workout for the brain and body.

**Key rule:** Vary the pace and end fast.

- Sandwich fast scenes between slow scenes.
- Don't have one fast scene lead directly to another (this fatigues players).
- Don't build entire sessions out of slow scenes either.

---

## The All-Combat Game and the Long, Long Short Rest

A specific symptom of bad pacing: in combat-heavy games, the post-combat "should we short rest?" conversation grows longer and longer with each fight. Players get sidetracked, discuss resources repeatedly, and bring in more tangential considerations.

This is the players' subconscious response to fatigue from too many fast scenes without enough slow scenes. They are slamming the handbrake because their brains need a break.

**Important distinction:** Transitions are NOT scenes. Descriptive narration between encounters where players aren't interacting is not a slow scene. Slow scenes must be *interactive* — the players must be talking and making decisions.

When players invent their own scenes (sitting around camp talking, wanting to roleplay in town), it's because their brains are tired from the pace. When players are itching for a fight, the pace has been too slow.

---

## Flow: How Pacing Gets Messed Up

**Flow** is the word Angry uses for the distinction between the narrative pace (what the scene *should* move at) and the speed the game *actually allows* the scene to play out.

- Every scene has a drag chute: the rules and mechanics that sit between the players and the story.
- There's an inverse relationship: fast scenes (combat, chases) rely most heavily on mechanics, so the rules slow them down the most. Slow scenes rely less on mechanics, so the game lets them move freely — which is precisely what they don't need.
- Broken flow **only slows scenes down**. It can never speed them up.
- Flow is ALWAYS broken in an RPG the moment you stop to roll a die.

**The GM's job in every scene:** minimize flow-breaking. Keep people engaged, keep the action moving, minimize distractions, speed resolutions, never crack a rulebook if you don't have to.

---

## Setting the Pace (Adventure Design)

Two rules:

1. **Vary the pace** — alternate fast and slow scenes.
2. **End fast** — stories should end with fast scenes.

Genre affects the balance:
- **Horror:** Start mostly slow, build to sharp shocking fast scenes.
- **Action:** Start fast, wobble between slow and fast, end with a fast scene.

Empty rooms in dungeons exist to create slow decision scenes that break up fast combat scenes. Block-pushing puzzles in action games serve the same purpose.

---

## Narration and Pacing

The main speed control over any scene is your narration:

- **Slow and wordy** = running a slow scene. Signals players to engage their brains and pay attention to details.
- **Quick and curt** = running a fast scene. Drives players to disengage their brains and get their guts involved.

**Critical takeaway:** Words and descriptions don't make action scenes better — they work *against* the emotional weight of the scene. Don't be wordy and descriptive in combat. Keep it fast and punchy.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Pacing ≠ flow. Pacing is about scene design; flow is about table execution.
2. Every scene has a speed determined by its purpose. Fast = excitement, one emotion. Slow = information, emphasis, emotional complexity.
3. Vary the pace. Too much fast fatigues; too much slow bores.
4. Slow scenes are where the real story happens. They are more useful than fast scenes.
5. Transitions are not scenes. Players need interactive slow scenes, not just narrated downtime.
6. Your narration style controls the pace. Match your delivery to the scene type.
7. If players keep inventing their own slow moments, your game needs more slow scenes. If they're itching for a fight, it needs more fast ones.
