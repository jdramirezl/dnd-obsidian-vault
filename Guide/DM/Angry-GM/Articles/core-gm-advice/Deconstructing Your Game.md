# Deconstructing Your Game

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/deconstructing-your-game/)
**Date:** December 30, 2024

---

> "Angry, I know you think tropes and cliches are good, but how might one design a good scenario that deconstructs and subverts tropes if they were so inclined?"

## Tropes are Good

Game Masters have an aversion to tropes. Some believe anything that isn't original isn't creative and therefore isn't good. Some are elitists above "pedestrian fantasy crap." Some think fantasy stories are founded on problematic ideas and must destroy any nod to gaming tradition.

Tropes are tropes precisely because they make satisfying stories and games. They're narrative ingredients — stuff you throw in the bowl to make a narrative cake. You wouldn't say "I can't make a cake with chocolate because that's unoriginal." People love chocolate.

---

## Deconstruction, Subversion, and Other Metanarrative Parlor Tricks

### Deconstruction
Literary deconstruction attempts to show how a certain kind of story totally falls apart if you apply basic logic and follow story threads to their natural conclusions. Consider *Watchmen* — it deconstructed superhero stories by carrying tropes to their logical conclusion to show such stories would lead to utter crapsack narratives.

Deconstructionism takes a genre people love, tears it apart, makes it miserable, and claims the intellectual high ground.

### Subverting Expectations
If you know how stories fit together and what audiences expect, you can build a story that leads the audience to expect one thing and deliver another. Often — for some batshit insane reason — creators think the best way to subvert is to deliver something completely disappointing.

**Good example:** The 2011 *Muppets* film. The Muppets fail to save their theater — the donation counter was malfunctioning the whole time. But it works because: it was funny, it's the Muppets (metanarrative is their stock-in-trade), and the epilogue shows the theater was just a building — what mattered was getting back together. The story doesn't leave you feeling like shit for getting invested.

**Bad example:** Rian Johnson's *The Last Jedi*. Set up that Rey's heritage was super important, then revealed she's "just absolutely nobody but still better than everything you ever loved."

---

## The Problem with Metanarrative Tricks

At their absolute best, metanarrative tricks comment on stories people love — poke a little fun. But the vast majority of the time:

- **Deconstructions rip shit apart.** When you're done, all you've got is a broken thing.
- **Subversion sets you up for disappointment.** If the replacement isn't substantially better, you're just disappointed and feel misled.
- **Toying with tropes pulls the audience out of the story** to comment on it. If the audience is emotionally engaged, commenting on it reminds them it's just a silly little story.

---

## Why Do You Need To Comment?

Why is metanarrative tomfoolery a thing you want to do?

- **Validation seekers** need to show how clever and creative they are. They can't stop at a subtle wink.
- **Message GMs** want their games to teach lessons. Once you're conveying a message, you're providing propaganda, not running a game.
- **Medium haters** dislike traditional fantasy and want to take it apart and replace it.

The only reason to play metanarrative games intentionally is to make a comment *about* the narrative. Is that comment worth interrupting your game?

---

## Go Genuine or Go Home

G.R.R. Martin effectively deconstructs certain aspects of romantic fantasy — but that's not what he set out to do. He loves fantasy literature. He rereads Tolkien every year or two. He started *A Game of Thrones* because he was inspired to write a scene about a kid finding dire wolf puppies. The story took on a life of its own. Ned Stark didn't die because Martin wanted to tell people how terrible honor is — Ned Stark died because that's what happened in the story.

Martin plays cliché straight as much as he subverts it. Because he's telling a story. He's not trying to take things apart or provide a message. That shit just happens.

> Use the tropes that feel right. Don't purposely use them. Don't subvert or destroy them. If you just do what feels right and good, you'll probably end up being tropey because tropes feel right and good.

**"How can you effectively deconstruct and subvert tropes in your tabletop RPG adventures?" You can't. Not because it can't be done, but because the minute you *try* to do it, you've already failed.**

And also because you're just running a game of pretend elves around a dinner table for your friends. Get over yourself.
