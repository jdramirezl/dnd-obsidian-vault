# Don't Bring Me Solutions, Bring Me Problems

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/bring-me-problems/)
**Date:** April 11, 2018
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## Puzzles vs. Problems

A **puzzle** is a problem with a CORRECT solution. A **problem** is a puzzle in which ANY SOLUTION will do.

A puzzle generally has one or a small number of specific solutions. The person presenting the puzzle has a solution in mind and the goal is to deduce or discover that solution. Puzzles are basically like math problems — they have one answer, and your answer is either right or wrong.

A problem is just a goal with something in the way. And that's also basically the definition of any encounter ever.

## Games vs. Real Life

In most real-life situations when we have a goal and something keeping us from it, it doesn't matter HOW we achieve our goal. The goal is the important thing. Puzzles are voluntary diversions — they work because we agree to play by their rules.

RPGs are open-ended games designed to mirror real-life open-endedness. Personal agency and free will are at the core. To build a puzzle into an RPG, you must either:
1. Get the players to agree to suspend open-ended free-will for the puzzle
2. Create a situation where the characters agree to conform to the puzzle's rules
3. Create a puzzle whose rules can't be broken because of the nature of the puzzle or world

## The Gordian Knot Problem

When players try to cut the Gordian Knot — bypassing your puzzle with a creative but unintended solution — they're doing so for one of two reasons: they think they're being smart, OR they're refusing to engage with the puzzle on its terms.

**If the players come up with a good Gordian Knot solution, assess it fairly and let it work.** Give them the prize. RPGs are about solving problems, not puzzles. When it comes to solving problems, anything goes.

## How to Design Problems

### The Process

1. **Start with the goal** — something the players want (treasure, information, passage)
2. **Place the obstacle** — what's in their way
3. **Figure out the most obvious strategy** for bypassing the obstacle
4. **Ask why the heroes can't just do that** — the answer is your first layer of obfuscation

### Layers of Obfuscation

You can keep complicating a problem by adding more layers:
- Why can't they just kill the monster? It's protected by a magical charm — invincible.
- Why not sneak past? It has an excellent sense of smell and there are no hiding places.
- Why not distract it? It's chained up close to the treasure.

**Limit yourself to 2-3 layers** for most parties. Too many layers make the problem impossible.

### Clarity (For You, the GM)

Once you've figured out the goal, obstacle, and obfuscations, build a very clear picture of how everything works. You need to answer any question, no matter how strange:
- How long is the chain?
- How is it invincible exactly?
- Can the magic be dispelled?
- Can the animal be charmed? Befriended?
- How big is it? How heavy?

**You can't decide what details matter** — the players' solution will be built from the details they ask for.

### Tools: Blocks to Push, Levers to Pull

Give players tools in the scene — game elements they can use to build a solution.

**Obviously useful tools** interact with the obstacle or obfuscation. A tennis ball covered in puppy saliva near the guardian puppy. Puddles of stinky mud that might mask scent. These suggest solutions and reduce difficulty.

**Apparently useless tools** have no obvious purpose. An obnoxious bird on a birdbath that occasionally honks. These add complexity but can also add fun. **Warning:** Players assume everything has a specific purpose and will waste time trying to figure out "useless" tools. They are a layer of obfuscation in themselves.

For all tools, **know how they work in the world**. Why does the bird honk? How does the puppy feel about the honking? Can the bird be killed?

## Don't Get Attached to Your Problems

The entire point of an open-ended problem is to allow players the freedom to creatively use their tools to take it apart.

**If the players solve it with one spell or die roll, they win.** That's working as intended. The work and challenge is in FIGURING OUT the right tool to use. What appears to be a complicated stealth challenge might be completely solved with a single *sleep* spell and a sneak attack.

Build the problem properly, adjudicate it fairly, and if the players manage to solve it easily, that's a victory they earned with their brains. Complaining about that completely misunderstands the point.
