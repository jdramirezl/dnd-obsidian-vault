# How You Handle Your Dice Bag

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-you-handle-your-dice-bag/)
**Date:** March 30, 2022
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## Three Truths About Dice

### 1. The Dice Are Your Tool

Dice are the GM's tool for finding out whether actions succeed or fail. Die rolling is a tool **you** use to help **you** determine the outcomes of actions **you** can't adjudicate using your brain alone. There's no other valid reason to use dice.

Yes, dice provide fun, an illusion of control, a sense of fairness. But that's gravy. You don't drink gravy by itself. Gravy goes on top of delicious meat — meat is rolling dice for good reasons.

- If you let players roll dice even though you already know the outcome: wrong.
- If you roll dice for an action you can easily adjudicate in your brain: wrong.
- You roll dice to resolve actions you can't resolve any other way, only if those actions have meaningful impact.

### 2. Dice Are Loading Screens

Every die roll is a speed bump to gameplay. Rolling dice is fun, but everything around it — checking sheets, adding modifiers, comparing to DCs, arguing about rerolls — is a drag chute on an exciting, well-paced game. Never roll dice you don't need to roll.

### 3. Dice Are the Opposite of Challenge

A challenge tests your abilities. Rolling dice tests nothing — it's just statistically likely or unlikely. RPGs are a challenge because the players' decisions determine what dice they roll, or if they roll at all. Players who come up with clever plans get bonuses. Players who come up with really clever plans don't have to roll at all.

> Either you come up with a brilliant way to handle this situation or else I'm going to flip this coin to see if you live or die. *That's* a roleplaying game!

GMs who make players roll checks because they think letting players win without a die roll "isn't a challenge" are wrong. GMs who don't use Passive Scores because "there's no challenge if the outcome's certain" are wrong.

## Rolling Dice: The Basics

Before anyone touches a die, you must know everything about the roll:

1. **Can the action succeed?** If not, it doesn't. Don't roll. Impossible things are impossible.
2. **Can the action fail?** If not, it doesn't. Don't roll. Certain things are certain.
3. **Does failure change anything?** If failure doesn't mean anything and the character would just try again, the action can't fail. Don't roll.
4. **What does success look like? What does failure look like?** Know both before rolling.
5. **Which single ability score is most relevant?** You decide, not the book.
6. **Does any specific proficiency help?**
7. **What's the DC?** Easy (5), Medium (10), Hard (15), Very Hard (20), Nearly Impossible (25).
8. **Any modifiers?** Advantage, disadvantage, bonuses, penalties from the situation.

All of this must be determined **before** anyone touches a die.

### What You Cannot Do

- Call for a roll hoping a natural 20 will let you pull success out of nowhere
- Call for a roll hoping a natural 1 will create fumbling hilarity
- Ask a player to roll then invent an outcome based on the number
- Let the player dictate the ability score or proficiency used

Natural ones and twenties are NOT automatic failures or successes on anything except attack rolls in combat (5E). And that rule is stupid anyway.

## Rolling Dice: The Advanced Topics

### Who Rolls Where

It doesn't actually matter who rolls the dice or who sees them. But gravy is nice, so let players roll when it doesn't hurt the game. When does it hurt?

1. **Hidden information:** When the die roll gives players information that might change their decisions. Example: A player rolls a 2 on Perception and finds nothing — they'll assume they failed and want to try again. Roll a 19 and find nothing — they'll assume there's nothing to find. The number changed the player's decision. **Roll behind the screen.**

2. **Tension:** When you want to preserve tension for an extended scene. A rogue sneaking down a hall who rolled a 6 on Stealth knows she's going to fail — she's just waiting for the moment. **Roll behind the screen.**

3. **Pacing:** Some scenes can't handle gamey speedbumps. Social interactions need to flow. Roll quickly and quietly behind the screen.

### The Master List

You **must** be able to roll any check for any creature at any time. Keep a list with every character's ability scores, proficiencies, proficiency bonus, AC, and whatever else you need. One sheet, always visible, all stats at a glance. Having character PDFs in different tabs is **not** good enough.

### Passive Scores

Things that aren't actions don't get dice rolls. Being aware of your surroundings, noticing things, recalling information, intuiting, reaching conclusions — these are not actions.

Use Passive Scores (10 + total modifier ± 5 for advantage/disadvantage) for any situation where a character might notice, intuit, recall, reason, or conclude something without a deliberate action. If the DC to find a hidden thing is 15, any PC with Passive Perception 15+ just notices it. No roll. No search. This is the only right way.

This also applies to identifying monsters and knowing weaknesses — compare DCs to Passive Arcana, Passive Religion, etc.

### Opposed Rolls (Contests)

Contests are massively overused. Use them only when two creatures are actively working against each other and only one can succeed — two actions, one winner. Like grabbing for the same item or arm-wrestling.

When one creature is acting and the other is just resisting (sneaking past a guard), only one creature is doing something. Roll one check against a static number (Passive Perception, or just a DC). Two die rolls are worse than one.

Unless the resistance is a PC, just use a static DC and keep things moving.

### Group Rolls

Two types of group actions:

1. **Cooperative teamwork** (multiple people taking one single action): Use *Working Together* rules — the participant with the best modifier rolls a single check with advantage. Only if teamwork is useful and possible.

2. **Group actions** (everyone doing their own thing while helping each other): Use *Group Check* rules — everyone makes an individual check; if at least half succeed, everyone succeeds.

**For speed:** Roll them yourself. Grab a d20 for each PC, throw them all at once, read across your Master List. You can roll Stealth for six PCs in the time it takes one player to find their d20.
