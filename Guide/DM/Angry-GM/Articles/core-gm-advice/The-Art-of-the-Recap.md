# The Art of the Recap

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/the-art-of-the-recap/)
**Date:** June 16, 2016
**Series:** Up Your GMing Level

---

## The Recap Is Part of the Game

Starting a game without a recap is like blindfolding your players and yelling "go." They spend the first few minutes flailing around trying to remember what was happening, and they never transition from "sitting around being social" mode to "game mode."

The recap is the **starting line** for the game. It's always done at the session, right before play begins. Written recaps sent between sessions are supplements, not substitutes.

---

## Players Can't Be Trusted with Recaps

Three common arguments for player recaps, and why they're all wrong:

### "It helps me find out what players retained"
- Stuff only sticks through reinforcement and repetition
- If players remember the wrong things, making them recap **reinforces** the wrong things
- The recap is a tool for YOU to reinforce what's important, not a pop quiz

### "It increases player engagement"
- Starting with a test makes players nervous, not engaged
- Over time, players focus on note-taking and memorizing instead of playing
- Listening to a recap **warms up** the player brain — forcing them to perform one cold-starts them

### "Players can do fun in-character recaps"
- Replaces a precision information tool with an amateur thespian unreliable narrator
- Players have the entire game to be in character
- The recap is too important to the session's flow to hand over

## Your Memory Sucks

At the end of every session, **immediately** write a bulleted list of what happened:

```
- Got the quest to kill the goblins of Deathdanger Peak
- Researched goblin tribes
- Learned about Deathdanger Peak
- Vizier sent secret word to the goblins (players don't know)
- Traveled through the Forest of Doom
- Fought wolf spiders
- Climbed the cliff
- Slept in a bear cave, killed bear
- Arrived at goblin lair entrance in the morning
```

Do it during the denouement or right after you get home. **Don't leave it until tomorrow.** Keep a notebook of these lists.

---

## "Previously" Not "Last Time"

Modern TV shows say "previously on…" not "last time on…" because their recaps pull details from **any** prior episode that's relevant to the upcoming one.

Use this technique: if goblins from months ago are back in the story, remind players who they are and what happened. You can pause mid-session for a quick recap sentence when old elements resurface.

**Include character revelations too** — a PC revealing their warlock powers come from a Fey Queen or that they're an exiled noble. These are just as important as plot events.

## The Recap Formula

### Start: Goal + Motivation
State the current goal and why it matters.

> "You're on a quest to recover the Jade Idol of Macguffin. The king of Plotsylvania has agreed to make you stupid rich in return."

If it's part of a larger goal, state that too:

> "In your ongoing battle against the Kingdom of Tyrantia, you've learned they're trying to unlock the Seven Mystic Seals. One lies before you in the Miserable Mire of Misery."

### Middle: Important Details
Include only information that is **important to THIS session**:
- Ongoing threats or obstacles that will continue to be an issue
- Clues or information vital to solving the current problem
- Steps taken toward the goal
- Victories, defeats, and story beats from last session
- Important character revelations

**Exclude:** One-and-done encounters (fighting wolves, crossing rivers, climbing cliffs). Even if a player did something cool — they'll remember it on their own.

Use second person: "You defeated, you fought, you learned, you fled, you discovered, you decided…"

### End: Current Position → First Decision
Transition directly into gameplay:

> "Rested for the night, your camp broken down, you now stand on the edge of the Forest of Too Many Spiders…"

Then begin describing the scene and asking for the first decisions (marching order, lookouts, etc.).

---

## Better Recaps Through Lying

Use players' bad memories to your advantage. Slip in details that maybe didn't seem important at the time — or didn't quite happen that way — to set up upcoming revelations:

> "You've plunged deep into goblin territory to recover the Jade Idol. The king promised to reward you handsomely, though **his vizier was suspicious of you and advised the king not to hire you.** You fought numerous battles against the berserk goblins. Now you stand outside the goblin king's throne room."

That vizier detail is a quiet plant. When the party finds gold and a suspicious map later, their suspicions might go back to the vizier.

TV shows do this too — placing scenes from different episodes together in recaps to imply connections that weren't explicit before.

---

## Target Length

**5–10 sentences.** Stick to 5 if you can. More than that and your players are building dice towers.

Recaps are exposition. Exposition is boring. Keep it tight.
