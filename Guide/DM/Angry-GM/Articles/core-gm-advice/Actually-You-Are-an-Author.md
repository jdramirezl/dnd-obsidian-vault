# Actually… You Are an Author

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://theangrygm.com/actually-you-are-an-author/) | **Category**: Core GM Advice

---

## Key Advice

- **You are an author.** Your primary tool as a GM is words — narrative is your medium. Everything else (dice, maps, VTTs, minis) is supplemental to the verbal storytelling at the core of the game.
- **Good narration has two jobs: convey information clearly and invite emotional engagement.** Clarity (the game part) always takes priority over tone and pacing (the roleplay part), but both are essential.
- **Virtual tabletops have inverted priorities.** Many modern GMs treat narration as flair on top of the VTT experience, when it should be the other way around — the VTT is flair on top of the narration.
- **Stop using boxed text and pre-written narration.** It prevents you from adapting your delivery to the actual emotional state of the game and stunts your growth as an extemporaneous speaker.
- **Read new fiction for 15 minutes every day** — with your eyes, not your ears — to immerse your brain in vocabulary, structure, and narrative craft.

## Summary

This article introduces the "Stupid Narration Tricks" series by establishing a foundational argument: the Game Master is, functionally, an author. Not in the sense of writing a plot for players to follow, but in the sense that verbal narrative is the primary medium through which the entire game is delivered. The GM synthesizes everyone's contributions — player actions, dice outcomes, world state — into a single coherent narrative. That synthesis job belongs to one person, and it requires craft.

Angry argues that the rise of virtual tabletops has caused many GMs to treat narration as secondary to visual presentation. Maps, fog of war, animated tokens — these are great tools, but they're supplements to the narrative, not replacements for it. The game starts with what you say and how you say it. Great narration can create solvable mysteries, catchable lies, and intellectual challenges that don't require a single die roll. It can strengthen player characterization by how you describe the world through their characters' perspectives.

The article also confronts the biggest disadvantage GMs have compared to actual authors: extemporaneousness. Authors get infinite drafts; GMs get one take. The solution isn't to lean harder on pre-written text (which prevents adaptation and stunts growth), but to practice the skill of speaking off the cuff. The homework assignments — dropping boxed text cold turkey and reading new fiction daily — are the first steps in a deliberate practice regimen for narrative mastery.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop using boxed text immediately.** The only exception: you may pre-write the opening scene-setting for each session to help you get started. Everything else must be extemporaneous.
2. **Read new fiction for 15 minutes every day.** It must be something you haven't read before. Read with your eyes (not audiobooks) to let your brain absorb vocabulary and structure without emotional pollution from a narrator's delivery.
3. **Prioritize clarity over style in your narration.** Make sure players understand the situation completely before you worry about making it sound dramatic. If clarity and tone conflict, clarity wins.
4. **Audit your VTT reliance.** Try running some scenes or encounters as "narrative only" — no map, no tokens, just your words. Notice what changes about player engagement.
5. **Remember the synthesis job.** You're not just describing a world — you're taking player actions, dice results, and world state and weaving them into one coherent story in real time. Practice that integration consciously.

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Your words are your most powerful GM tool — invest in your narrative craft the way you'd invest in any other skill, because everything else is just supplemental.
