# How to Talk to Players: The Art of Narration

> [Source: The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-talk-to-players-the-art-of-narration/)

---

## Key Advice

- **The GM has three core jobs**: Adjudication (resolving actions), Narration (imparting information), and Role-Playing (making choices for NPCs/world) — everything else is gravy
- **You do NOT need to be a good actor** — you need to make good choices for NPCs and communicate clearly
- **Narration is about clear, concise information delivery** — not painting word pictures or engaging all five senses
- **"Spooky cemetery at twilight" is enough** — four words put the scene in players' heads; their imaginations do the heavy lifting
- **Less is more**: Use the fewest, simplest words possible and leave the rest to imagination
- There are **four types of narration**: Scene Setting, Transition, Exposition, and Adjudication (outcome description)
- **Scene setting is a call to action** — not an invitation to sit and listen; ~5 short sentences max
- **Always reset the scene after every transition** — remind players where they are and what's available after every fight, obstacle, or time skip
- **Transitions close scenes and open new ones** — acknowledge the end, describe changes in time/space, then set the next scene
- **Speak like yourself** — don't force Tolkien prose; natural language delivered clearly beats flowery stammering every time

## Summary

GMing requires three skills: adjudication, narration, and role-playing (decision-making for NPCs, not acting). Narration is the most misunderstood — it's not about purple prose or sensory immersion, it's about delivering information clearly and concisely. RPGs are an interactive, verbal, imagination-driven medium. Your players' brains will fill in the details from a few well-chosen words. Scene setting gets ~5 sentences: one to establish the location, one for mood/detail, and the rest for things players can interact with — always ending with the most urgent threat. Transitions close one scene and open the next, covering any changes in time and space. The most powerful technique is **resetting the scene** after every transition — after a fight ends, re-describe the environment and available options to pull players back into the action. Exposition delivers information and is best woven into narrative interactions, but don't be afraid to just tell players what their characters know.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Scene Setting Formula** (~5 sentences):
   - One sentence: overall location ("creepy, overgrown cemetery at dusk")
   - One sentence: mood detail ("fallen grave markers obscured by thick underbrush")
   - Remaining sentences: things to interact with, ordered least to most urgent — always end with the biggest threat
2. **Always reset the scene**: After every combat, obstacle, or time passage, re-describe the environment and available options — "With the zombies dead, you can freely explore the graveyard. You see mausoleums on the hills and a strange obelisk ahead. What do you do?"
3. **Transition properly**: Acknowledge the scene's end → describe time/space changes → set the next scene. Never skip from one scene to another without closure
4. **Keep it brief**: No more than ~3 details in a list; players can't rewind spoken words like text
5. **Let players fill in props**: If you say "plush bedroom," players will assume a bed, dresser, curtains — let them, and accept their additions
6. **Use your natural voice**: Speak casually and clearly; don't try to sound like a novel. Clarity beats poetry
7. **Deliver exposition directly when needed**: "Hey, here's what your characters know" is fine — just keep it brief and place it during natural pauses

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Narration is information delivery, not performance art. Set scenes in ~5 sentences ending with the most urgent threat, always reset the scene after transitions, and trust your players' imaginations to do the heavy lifting. Speak clearly, speak briefly, speak like yourself.
