# The GM's Burden and Other Stories

## Key Advice

Three game design principles every GM and designer should understand: (1) **The GM's Burden** — fundamental design problems should be solved by the system, not dumped on the GM; (2) **Metagaming as a Tool** — metagame mechanics can synchronize player behavior with character behavior; (3) **Dynamic Tension** — opposing mechanical forces create interesting decisions and prevent degenerate strategies.

## Summary

### The GM's Burden

The RPG ecosystem has three roles: **system designer**, **adventure writer**, and **GM**. Any game element that gets skipped at one level gets passed down to the next. System designers are trained professionals with the most resources; GMs are hobbyists doing this for fun. The more fundamental design work gets dumped on the GM, the fewer people can successfully run games.

The internet GM community has a bad habit of responding to systemic design problems with "just let the GM fix it." This is wrong because:

- The average GM is not a game design expert
- Fundamental problems (like the 15-Minute Workday) need **systemic solutions**, not per-adventure band-aids
- Raising the bar on GM skill requirements reduces the number of people who can run games
- Approachability matters — for the health of the hobby and the industry

### Metagaming as a Tool

Metagaming isn't always bad. Players can make rational decisions for their characters, but they often make decisions that are **too rational** — they can't feel what their characters feel (adrenaline, boredom, time pressure, urgency). Metagame mechanics can bridge this gap by turning irrational human impulses into rational mechanical considerations.

Example: Players don't feel the crushing boredom of sitting around for 12 hours doing nothing, but their characters would. A mechanical incentive to keep pushing forward creates the same behavioral pressure the character would feel naturally.

Hit points are already metagame — you can't feel how tired your character is, but the number on your sheet lets you make rational decisions as if you could.

### Dynamic Tension

The best game mechanics create **opposing forces** that pull players in different directions, with the balance shifting as play progresses. This prevents any single optimal strategy from dominating.

Example: Dwindling resources pull you toward resting, while accumulating XP bonuses push you to keep going. Early in the day, resources are flush so the pull to rest is weak. Late in the day, both forces are strong, creating a genuine dilemma.

**Perverse Incentives** occur when a mechanic drives the exact opposite behavior from what was intended (e.g., the 15-Minute Workday itself — rest mechanics intended to create resource management instead create a "blow everything and sleep" strategy).

Dynamic Tension protects against Perverse Incentives by ensuring no single force dominates. Two opposing "wrong" directions keep players bouncing around optimal play without ever landing on a degenerate strategy.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop saying "the GM should fix it"** — when you encounter a systemic problem in your game, recognize it as a design flaw, not a GM skill issue
2. **Identify Perverse Incentives in your game** — look for mechanics where the optimal strategy contradicts the intended gameplay (e.g., rest-spamming in D&D)
3. **Design opposing forces, not single incentives** — when creating house rules, build push-pull dynamics rather than one-directional bonuses
4. **Use metagame mechanics intentionally** — if players can't feel what their characters feel, a mechanical proxy (XP bonus, tension pool, etc.) can bridge the gap
5. **Evaluate the GM's Burden** — before adopting a "GM empowerment" solution, ask: could a less experienced GM implement this? If not, the solution needs to be more systematic
6. **Test for Unintended Consequences** — when hacking rules, imagine the mechanic in isolation and ask what extreme behavior it could drive; then check if opposing forces prevent that extreme
7. **Remember: RPG systems are engines, not games** — adventures are the games; the system should provide the fundamental tools so adventure writers and GMs can focus on content, not fixing design holes

> [!tip] Fundamental design problems need systemic solutions
> When you encounter a recurring gameplay problem, resist the urge to solve it with adventure-specific band-aids. If the problem stems from the system's core mechanics (like resource recovery creating the 15-Minute Workday), it needs a mechanical counter-force — not a time limit bolted onto every adventure. The GM already has enough to do without also being the system designer.
