# Why Run Games Anyway

A deeply personal and philosophical article where Angry explores why GMs run games, argues definitively that the GM is **not** a player, and examines what that means for understanding GM motivation.

---

## The GM Is Not a Player

> Is the GM a player? The answer is no. Don't be an idiot.

This matters because if the GM isn't a player, then formal game design frameworks (like MDA) can't be used to analyze the GM's engagement with the game. The psychological experience of running a game is inherently different from playing one.

### Why It Matters

- If the GM is a player, GMs can use game design tools to make themselves happy, and publishers can design systems that appeal to GMs
- If the GM is **not** a player, things get more complicated — and they are more complicated

### The Proof

To count as a game, an activity needs:
- **An explicit goal** — GMs don't have gameplay goals ("everyone having fun" is not a gameplay goal)
- **A competitive element** — nothing in the game works against the GM
- **Constrained rules** — the GM can change, break, or invent rules

GMs aren't doing any of the things people do when playing games. They're not pursuing goals, overcoming challenges, or constrained by rules and systems.

---

## Hobby vs. Game

If the GM isn't a player, what are they? A **hobbyist**.

### Games vs. Hobbies

- **Games** are interactive activities with goals, challenges, and constraints — they provide specific, well-understood psychological payoffs
- **Hobbies** are non-productive activities engaged in for pleasure — they're far less constrained and far more varied in the rewards they offer

Two hobby knitters can derive pleasure from completely different aspects of knitting. The same is true for GMs. Any two GMs might love GMing for entirely different reasons.

### Why This Distinction Matters

- Players have constrained experiences → easier to analyze and design for
- GMs have unconstrained hobby experiences → each GM must figure out their own rewards
- Game design frameworks work for players but **not** for GMs
- Publishers should treat GM tools like hobby tools (needles for knitters, guns for trapshooters, design frameworks for GMs)

> Just as knitters shouldn't stab themselves with their needles and trapshooters shouldn't discharge their guns into their own faces, GMs should not turn their gameplay analysis tools on themselves. It'll all end in tears.

---

## When to Analyze Your Motivation

Hobbyists don't usually need to analyze why they enjoy their hobby. The only times it matters:

1. When you're **not enjoying it anymore**
2. When you're trying to **pick up a new hobby**
3. When you're trying to **design tools for other hobbyists** or teach them

As a GM, you're better off understanding the Aesthetics of Play and how to design good gameplay experiences for players — since your hobby is providing those experiences — than trying to understand why you personally enjoy it.

## Angry's Personal Reflection

At heart, Angry is a **nuts-and-bolts homebrewer entertainer**:

- **Game maker / scenario designer** — started drawing dungeons on graph paper in math class at age 10 with Frank Mentzer's Basic D&D. Before D&D, spent hours with Excitebike's track editor and Wrecking Crew's level maker. Drew Super Mario levels on graph paper with friends
- **Entertainer** — always took it as his duty to make sure friends had a game to play. Invented hand-drawn point-and-click adventures on chalkboards, created pool-based boss fights, led neighborhood live-action adventures
- **Not a hacker** — understanding rules and tools is useful, but hacking systems wears him down. Homebrewing is about assembling a baseline experience from pieces, then bringing it to life through performance

> For me, homebrewing is about the simple joy of putting the pieces together into something uniquely my own and then inviting my friends to experience it. And relying on my ability to perform, weave a story, and adjudicate a game on the fly to provide a rich, deep, moving experience.

---

## The Invitation

Angry invites every GM to reflect on why they run games:
- Why did you start?
- Why do you keep at it?
- What kind of GM hobbyist are you?

This self-knowledge is especially valuable when you're feeling unsettled, uncertain, or anxious about your gaming life.
