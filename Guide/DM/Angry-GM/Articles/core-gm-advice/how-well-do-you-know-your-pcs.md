# How Well Do You Know Your PCs?

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-well-do-you-know-your-pcs/)
**Date:** December 12, 2025
**Category:** Core GM Advice

---

## Core Thesis

It is your job as a Game Master to know the player characters you're running a game around — mechanically. Not personalities and backstories, but their abilities, spells, feats, and how they all work. This isn't optional advice; it's instruction on how to do your basic-ass job.

## Why It Matters

Modern RPGs focus on specialized characters with gameplay depth built into character sheets rather than the core game. Even simple games like *Blades in the Dark* and PbtA games differentiate characters by moveset. Modern TTRPGs are built more like *Smash Brothers* than *Dark Souls* — every character has a different moveset.

As the GM, you've got to pace your game, adjudicate actions, and empower players to play and win. You can't do that if you don't know what buttons the players can press.

### The Shield Spell Problem

If you have a wizard with *shield* prepared, you have no excuse not to expect it to interrupt any attack. You should be saying: "The attack is a 12. Does that hit your AC, and do you want to throw up your shield?" Likewise: "You didn't use your Cunning Action. Did you mean to skip it?"

### "It's a Player's Job to Know Their Character"

Whether or not you think each player must know their own character, that doesn't mean it isn't *also* your job. Screaming about player responsibilities doesn't absolve you of your duties. When players inevitably fail your demands, someone has to know the rules. That someone is you.

## The Prep Process

> It is your job to review your players' characters before you start running a game for them and to re-review them every time those characters change.

### How to Review

1. **Collect character sheets** (or copies) before the first session
2. **Sit down with the sheet, the rulebooks, and a way to take notes**
3. **Review every ability, feat, and spell** — no matter how well you think you know the system
4. **Take actual notes** — remembering isn't taking notes; writing things down enhances memory
5. **Re-review after every level-up** or whenever new abilities are added

### Fixing Bad Character Sheets

It is your right and duty to tell players what to record on their sheets. "Chris, note that your Sneak Attack damage is +1d6." "Danielle, write down that *sacred flame* deals 1d8 radiant damage and allows a Dexterity saving throw." If someone says "I'll remember," smack them with your PHB and write it on the sheet for them.

## What You Need to Know

You don't need to memorize every rule, but you must be able to adjudicate any action the players are likely to take without cracking a rulebook and without relying on the player to know anything you don't.

### The Master Reference

What you want is:
- **A list of every ability each character has** with notes for abilities you can't rattle off from memory
- **A separate list/column of every conditional rule, Bonus Action, and Reaction** the players might trigger
- Updated every time characters level up
- Reviewed as part of your pre-session warm-up

### Key Categories to Track

- **Class and racial abilities** — how they work, including weird exceptions
- **Spellcasting** — how many spells prepared, which spells are Bonus Actions or Reactions
- **Conditional triggers** — "When you hit," "If a die shows a 1," halfling Lucky, Great Weapon Fighting rerolls
- **Bonus Actions and Reactions** — these affect pacing and how you run the game
- **Prompting players** — "You rolled a 2 on that die; as a great-weapon fighter, you get to reroll it"

## Summary

A disciplined review of every character sheet is part of your pre-campaign prep. A list of the abilities you need to adjudicate — including conditional rules, Bonus Actions, and Reactions — is part of your game notes. Reviewing that list is part of your pre-session warm-up. That's it.
