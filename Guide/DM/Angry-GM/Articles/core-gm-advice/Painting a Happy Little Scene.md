# Painting a Happy Little Scene

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/painting-a-happy-little-scene/)
**Date:** September 2, 2015
**Series:** Theoretical Adventure Building
**Category:** Core GM Advice — Scene Construction

---

## Core Concept

Scenes are the building blocks of adventures. Every scene has specific components that need to be in place for it to work — whether you plan it in advance or build it on the fly. Understanding these components lets you both **plan scenes** and **improvise scenes** effectively.

---

## Scenes vs. Encounters

Encounters **are** scenes — they just have extra elements. Both start and end the same way:

1. **Setting the scene** — Narration describing how the scene opens, presenting players with options
2. **Player actions** — Players take actions, GM adjudicates, results happen
3. **Transition** — Scene ends, leading to the next scene

**The difference:** Encounters add a **dramatic question** (will the PCs succeed?) and **sources of conflict** (opposition that might prevent success). In a plain scene, PCs make decisions without significant opposition.

**Transitions** can be:
- **Soft:** Little time passes, hard to distinguish from action results ("You head for the inn. Inside, the common room is dark and dingy...")
- **Hard:** Clear time skip or scene break ("You secure lodgings and get settled. In the middle of the night, you're disturbed by screaming in the streets...")

---

## Planning vs. Improv: Both Are Required

The truth: **both extremes are wrong.**

- You can't purely improvise from nothing — good improvisers build with a framework toward a goal, whether they realize it or not
- You can't preplan everything — players make choices that lead in unexpected directions

> Improvisation isn't operating without a plan. It's planning just a moment ahead of executing.

**Angry's advice:** Plan an adventure that works on paper before the PCs arrive. Then be flexible enough to go off script. **Overplan and be ready to discard** — it's easier to adjust from overplanning than underplanning.

Learning how to plan a scene enables you to both plan scenes AND build them on the fly.

---

## The W's and H's of Scene Construction

### WHY the Scene Exists

**Every scene must have a purpose.** Once that purpose is fulfilled, the scene is over.

Common purposes:
- Move heroes from motivation to resolution (finding clues, gaining leads)
- Reveal the world, establish mood, set tone, plant seeds
- Let players collate information and discuss (player-invented scenes)

**The GM must know the purpose** so they can:
- Recognize when the scene has accomplished its goal
- Transition out before the scene flops around doing nothing
- Shove players out the door if they're putzing around after the purpose is fulfilled

Even unplanned, player-invented scenes have a purpose — figure it out quickly.

### WHERE and WHEN the Scene Happens

Scenes are **only as local as they need to be:**

| **Scope** | **Example** |
|---|---|
| A single room | Examining a shrine with altar, font, pillars, statue, coffer |
| An entire town | Exploring different districts, inns, temples, markets |
| A whole island | Finding water, exploring ruins, searching for inhabitants |

A single room and an entire town work the same way in the GM's brain — they're just different scales of "here's a space with things to interact with."

**Time works the same way:**
- Some scenes are moment-by-moment (examining a crime scene)
- Some zoom in and out (exploring a town over several hours)
- Some cover an entire day (three teams splitting up on an island)

> "Continuous" doesn't mean moment-by-moment. It means actions follow from one another and are united in purpose.

**Determine setting and timeframe based on what you're trying to accomplish.** Start with the purpose.

### WHO and WHAT the Scene Contains

People and things are the **drivers of choices** — the levers players can pull:
- Read the runes, explore the cemetery, listen to the beggar, track the smoke monster
- The more levers you provide, the easier it is for players to start pulling them

**You don't have to create everything:**
- Players will invent things that suit their needs ("Is there a general store?")
- You can accept or discard their inventions
- Don't overcreate, but do create *something* that says "hey, come and play with me"

### HOW Players Can Accomplish the Purpose

Connect the **purpose** to the **things in the scene** to determine how PCs can get at the purpose:

- Purpose: Discover the victim was in the Hunter's Lodge → **How:** Signet ring on the corpse
- Purpose: Learn the murderer used poison → **How:** Discarded vial with noxious fluid
- Purpose: Learn the murderer is large → **How:** Big boot print in the mud (requires tracking check)

Not everything in the scene needs to connect to a purpose. But the key purposes need clear paths through the scene's contents.

> If there's conflict and uncertainty about whether PCs will succeed, the scene becomes an encounter. Otherwise, it's just a scene.

## Three Worked Examples

### Example 1: The Crime Scene

| **Element** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Why** | Discover clues: victim was in Hunter's Lodge (lead), murderer used rare poison (lead), murderer is big/beefy (optional clue), background details (optional) |
| **Where/When** | Ferry dock outside town, midday, time passes normally |
| **What/Who** | Corpse, crates/barrels (hiding places), sheriff |
| **How** | Sheriff provides background (possibly uncooperative = encounter). Corpse has signet ring (obvious). Searching hiding spots reveals discarded vial. Tracking check finds boot print. |

### Example 2: Exploring the Town

| **Element** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Why** | Convey: increased militarism, breakdown of race relations, outbreaks of violence. Optional: new temple and high priest. Each PC gets 1–2 clues about these themes. |
| **Where/When** | Whole town (map drawn), several hours from midday to dinner |
| **What/Who** | Three inns (human/dwarf/elf districts), new temple, central market, partially-built wall and gatehouse |
| **How** | Wall/gatehouse = militarism (impossible to miss). Market has only humans and dwarves; elf market is separate (racial tension). Dwarf side has gibbet with human corpses (violence + racial tension). Rumors of fights and arson overheard anywhere. Temple site shows preparations + armed untrained conscripts in new armor. |

### Example 3: Talking Things Out (Unplanned)

| **Element** | **Details** |
|---|---|
| **Why** | PCs need to collate data and discuss theories. They don't need to pick a direction — a big event (assassination attempt) is ready once they start going in circles. |
| **Where/When** | The inn, over an hour or two |
| **What/Who** | Just the PCs. Innkeeper on hand to interrupt or answer questions. |
| **How** | Let them talk. Watch for repetition, silence, or circular discussion. Then trigger the next event. |

---

## Key Takeaway

> Every scene needs a purpose (Why), a setting (Where/When), things to interact with (Who/What), and paths connecting those things to the purpose (How). Whether you plan scenes in advance or build them on the fly, these are the components you need. Start with the purpose — everything else follows from it.
