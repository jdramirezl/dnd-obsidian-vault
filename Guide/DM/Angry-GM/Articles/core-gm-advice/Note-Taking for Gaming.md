# Note Taking for Gaming Fun and Profit

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/note-taking-for-gaming/)
**Date:** November 30, 2023
**Category:** Core GM Advice — Note-Taking & Retention

---

## Core Concept

Note-taking isn't about building a reference book — it's about **improving retention**. If you take notes properly, you rarely need to refer back to them. The notes themselves serve as little more than quick reminders to jog your memory. Your actual goal is to **remember things without needing the notes**.

---

## The Real Purpose of Game Notes

Most GMs think note-taking is about recording everything so they can look it up later. This is wrong.

**The three actual purposes of game notes:**

1. **Build retention** — Help your brain remember things without needing the notes
2. **Provide reminders** — Quick triggers to aid recall of things you think you've forgotten
3. **Keep a to-do list** — Track future game events and consequences that need to be acted on later

> If you're trying to do more than that, you're doing it wrong. And you're probably frying yourself.

**Why this matters:** If your brain thinks the notes exist to keep you from having to remember, it won't try to remember anything. Memory is actually more reliable than notes — your brain stores associations and connections between ideas, not just isolated facts. Triggering any one idea also triggers everything connected to it.

---

## What to Actually Remember (and Note)

The list is shorter than you think:

1. **The game's plot** — Major events that affect the ongoing narrative (not every combat detail — just "the party fought through kobold guards to reach the dragon," not blow-by-blow)
2. **Players' current goals and plans** — What the party is trying to accomplish and their direction
3. **Names of recurring characters and locations** — Just the names, not pages of personality details
4. **Future consequences of PC actions** — If they offended someone, let an enemy escape, leaked information to a spy
5. **Your own future plans** — Hooks, location names, or plot threads you intend to follow up on

**That's it.** Anything not on this list is safe to forget.

### It's Okay to Forget

- You will forget things. No amount of note-taking prevents this.
- Most players don't remember most details either.
- If a player remembers something you forgot: "Oh, that's right, I forgot. His name is Dabner. Sorry." Problem solved.
- Don't fry yourself chasing perfect recall.

## The Two-Step Note-Taking Process

### Step 1: Ugly Session Scrawls (During the Game)

Have paper and pencil handy. Jot down anything you need to remember but might forget. A simple starting format:

| **Section** | **What to Record** | **Example** |
|---|---|---|
| **One edge of page:** Timeline | Major plot events, key moments | "met King Wilfenburger of Vulcroft," "killed red dragon" |
| **Opposite edge:** Names | Characters and locations to remember | "Ardry, Bartender, the Tarnished Ingot" |
| **Bottom of page:** Future actions | Consequences and plans to act on later | "offended Marshall of Amberall," "treasure in Ironroot Hills" |

- You can only write a few words per item — focus on key ideas
- It doesn't matter how ugly these notes are
- Over time you'll develop shorthand and learn what you do/don't need to record

### Step 2: Cleanup (As Soon as Possible After the Session)

Revise, rewrite, flesh out, and organize your ugly scrawl. Extract three things:

1. **Timeline of in-game events**
2. **List of names and places to remember**
3. **To-do list of future actions**

**How you organize is personal** — outline, three columns, flowchart, whatever works for you. The key is to **engage with, organize, and rewrite your notes.**

---

## The Critical Rule: Handwrite Everything

Research consistently shows:

> **Retention is vastly higher among people who handwrite their notes.** Typing kills retention.

- It's not about the tools — tablet + stylus works fine
- It's about the **action of handwriting** and how it engages your brain
- Handwriting is a more intricate, involved process than typing, which strengthens memory connections
- **Do not take notes by typing on a keyboard** during or after sessions

**The process:**
1. Handwrite ugly notes during the session
2. Handwrite the cleanup/rewrite after the session
3. *Then* you can transcribe to digital (OneNote, wiki, PDF) if you want — but only after the handwritten two-step process

---

## After the Two Steps

Once you've completed the handwritten two-step process, do whatever you want with the information:
- Write a prose recap for players
- Update a campaign bible
- Plan your next session
- Use it for session recaps

The "dark and dirty secret" is that **the notes themselves don't matter**. If you follow the two-step handwritten process diligently, you'll find everything else easier. You won't need to do much more than glance at your notes to write recaps or record details.

---

## Key Takeaway

> Note-taking is a memory tool, not a reference-building exercise. Handwrite your notes during the session, then handwrite a cleanup pass afterward. The act of writing — not the notes themselves — is what builds the retention that makes you a better GM. Trust your memory more than you think you can.
