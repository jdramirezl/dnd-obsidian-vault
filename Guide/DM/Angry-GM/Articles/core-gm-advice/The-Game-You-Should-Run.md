# The Game You SHOULD Run

## Key Advice

There is no One True Way to run an RPG — but there aren't infinitely many right ways either. The value of GM advice isn't in being told **what** to do, but in understanding **why** someone does what they do, so you can reason out your own approach. Good advice shows its mental work. GM decisions aren't simple tradeoffs — they're complex dilemmas involving multiple competing forces that must be kept in balance, and the right balance is different at every table and changes moment to moment.

## Summary

### Why Angry Writes the Way He Does

Angry doesn't want to tell you how to run your game — he wants to teach you how to **think about** running your game. His articles explain the facts, assumptions, and reasoning behind his approaches so that even if you disagree with his conclusions, you gain useful frameworks for reaching your own.

This is why he gives different answers to the same questions across articles — different situations, different tables, different priorities lead to different correct answers.

### Trusting Experts (To a Point)

Experts are reliable sources of **information** — facts gathered through rigorous experience and study. But the moment an expert starts telling you what you **should do** with that information, their opinion is just one of many valid interpretations. You know your goals, your table, and your players better than any expert.

Trust experts for: factual information, tested observations, design principles
Be skeptical when experts: prescribe specific solutions, claim one right way, don't show their reasoning

### Beyond Tradeoffs — Resolving Dilemmas

GM decisions aren't binary tradeoffs (pick A or B). They're complex dilemmas involving:

- **Agency vs. Consistency** — letting players do cool things vs. maintaining world coherence
- **Game needs vs. Story needs** — mechanical fairness vs. narrative satisfaction
- **Eight components of engagement** — challenge, discovery, expression, fantasy, fellowship, narrative, sensory pleasure, submission
- **Group needs vs. individual needs** — what's best for the table vs. what one player wants
- **Player needs vs. GM needs** — their fun vs. your sanity
- **Human relationships** — friendships, feelings, social dynamics

The right balance is different at every table and changes from moment to moment. Just because something is more important doesn't mean you can always choose it — sometimes the cost is too high in other areas.

### Accepting Limitations

Some things you can't do. Some things your system can't do. Some things TTRPGs can't do. Accepting limitations isn't defeatism — it's the foundation for making realistic plans and finding approaches that actually work for you.

### How This Knowledge Actually Works

You don't consciously weigh all these forces for every decision at the table. Instead, the more of this framework you internalize, the more your brain automatically produces good answers. It shapes your instincts. And when you do hit a genuinely hard problem, you have a framework for working through it deliberately.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Evaluate GM advice by its reasoning, not its conclusions** — if someone can't explain WHY they do something, their advice is only useful to people who think exactly like them
2. **Stop looking for the One Right Answer** — there are many right ways and many wrong ways; your job is to find YOUR right way for YOUR table
3. **Think in dilemmas, not tradeoffs** — don't just pick A over B; consider how to balance multiple competing forces simultaneously
4. **Know your own engagement preferences** — which of the eight components matter most to you? Which are you most sensitive to when they're missing?
5. **Know your players' engagement preferences** — same questions, different answers for each player; the group's balance is what matters
6. **Accept your limitations honestly** — if you can't improvise well, don't build a game that requires heavy improvisation; play to your strengths
7. **Accept your system's limitations** — D&D doesn't do social encounters well; don't fight the system, work within it or change systems
8. **Internalize frameworks, don't memorize rules** — the goal isn't to consciously apply these concepts at the table; it's to have them shape your instincts through study and reflection

> [!tip] The WHY matters more than the WHAT
> When you read GM advice — from Angry or anyone else — pay more attention to the reasoning than the recommendation. The facts, assumptions, and logic behind a conclusion are transferable to your table. The specific conclusion probably isn't. Build your own design philosophy from understood principles, not copied techniques. As Angry puts it: "I stand by everything I've ever said, even the contradictory stuff" — because different situations demand different answers built from the same foundations.
