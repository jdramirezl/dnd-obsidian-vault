# What Does Detect Magic Detect?

> **Source:** [The Angry GM — What Does Detect Magic Detect?](https://theangrygm.com/what-detect-magic-detects/)
> **Date:** April 4, 2023

---

## Context

Prompted by a Discord question about how *detect magic* and *identify* interact with magical ingredients (ectoplasm, magical flower sap, etc.), this article argues that intuitive rulings based on understanding your game world always beat rules-lawyer analysis.

---

## The Question

If substances like ectoplasm or magical flower sap have properties useful for crafting magical items and have magical effects when consumed, what would *detect magic* and *identify* reveal about them?

---

## Three Ways to Answer Any Rules Question

1. **Rules Lawyer Approach** — Pick apart the exact wording of every rule and interpret them. This is the worst approach when the answer isn't immediately obvious.

2. **Fictional World Approach** — Think about how the game world works and give the answer that best describes that world. Should be reasonably intuitive.

3. **Best Gameplay Approach** — Think about which answer leads to the best gameplay experience. Should be logical and consistent so players can predict outcomes and make good choices.

**Key insight:** If the rules answer isn't immediately obvious and intuitive, the rules aren't leading you to the right place. Don't trust them.

---

## The Official Answer Is Terrible

The Sage Advice Compendium (under "Is the breath weapon of a dragon magical?") creates two types of magic:

- Magic that exists as part of the fictional world
- Magic described in the game's rules

Something only counts as "rules magical" if it's a named magical item, a named spell, uses spell slots, or the description explicitly uses the word "magical." This means:

- Dragons aren't "magical" in a rules sense despite being impossible beings of raw elemental fury
- Displacer beast displacement and lycanthrope shapeshifting *can* be detected/dispelled (because they're described as magical)
- The GM can only determine what's detectable by referencing the rules — there's no way to intuit the answer

This gives the word "magical" two contradictory meanings and makes the system non-intuitive.

## Detect Magic in the Angryverse

When anyone uses *detect magic* in the Angryverse:

- Mundane perceptions become distant, gray, hazy, washed out
- Sounds become muted
- The caster becomes attuned to the ebb and flow of magic
- At first, only a vague sense that magic is present
- With concentration, magical auras come into focus — strongest first
- Auras have colors, textures, shapes — but they're approximations, not literal visuals. More like remembering a color or feeling something energetic
- All living things and magical things have auras, but it takes practice to perceive and comprehend them
- Different detect spells are attuned to different aspects: *detect magic* shows magical forces/energies, detect alignment shows spirits/souls, etc.

This is grounded in the real-world concept of astral projection and aura reading, influenced by Shadowrun 2E and early D&D editions. Magic flows through the ethereal plane; detect spells let wizards peek through the veil.

*Identify* reveals a magical aura so fully that a wizard can figure out exactly what the magic does and how to unleash it.

**Given this understanding:** Ectoplasm and magical flower sap *do* radiate detectable magical auras, and *identify* would reveal their properties. This call can be made instantly at the table with no rules analysis needed.

## Core Principles

### If a Player Tries Something, They Expect It to Work

If players cast *detect magic* on magical plant sap, they're expecting it to work. So it should work as expected. Or, if it doesn't, it should reveal something useful about the world.

### Spells Exist for Their Game Purpose

*Detect magic* and *identify* exist to allow players to identify and use magical stuff they find. That's their game purpose since their earliest incarnations. Exceptions built on technicalities are against the spirit of RPGs.

### Intuitive Rulings Beat Analysis

- At the table, you have seconds to decide. Your brain blurts out intuitive answers, which tend to be the best ones.
- Away from the table, you can reference rules and dig for answers. This *seems* like it'll lead to the most correct answer, but it usually leads to worse answers.
- The "rule at the table, review later" approach is backwards. The quick-and-dirty intuitive ruling *is* the best answer.

### Making Rulings Builds Understanding

The Angryverse's rich description of detect magic wasn't designed upfront — it emerged from years of making judgment calls. Making rulings is how you build intuitive understanding.

- **In a well-designed system:** Your rulings will allow the game's underlying design and world to emerge naturally.
- **In a poorly-designed system:** Your own rulings will provide a better experience than going "by the book."

---

## Summary

> Trust your intuitive judgment. Ignore the designers. Stop picking over the rulebooks like they're legal code. Whether you're running a good system or a bad one, intuitive rulings based on understanding your game world will always produce better narrative and gameplay experiences than rules-lawyer analysis.
