# Respect the Metagame

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/respect-the-metagame/)
**Date:** May 12, 2014
**Category:** How to F$&%ing GM

---

## Overview

An argument for understanding and respecting the metagame — the unwritten structural rules that allow an RPG to function. Uses the example of a selfish mercenary character who demands the party convince him to join every mission to illustrate how ignoring the metagame holds the game hostage.

---

## What Is the Metagame?

The metagame is the structure that allows the game to happen — **unwritten rules everyone follows without discussing them** to facilitate gameplay.

**Core assumption (most RPGs):** The game follows the group, and the group stays together. Everyone is part of the same team, the same story, the same game.

Most players understand this so instinctively that violating it is an alien concept.

---

## The Hostage Scenario

**Example:** A player creates a selfish mercenary who demands the party convince him to join every mission. "What's in it for me?"

**The problem:** This player holds the game hostage. Before anyone can play, they must play his mini-game: "Convince Me to Join the Party."

- He has **railroaded the other players** into playing his game his way.
- He has **robbed fellow characters of agency** by presenting a choice they literally cannot make.
- Most players will jump through the hoops — not from character motivation, but because they instinctively respect the metagame and know leaving someone out means they don't get to play.
- The selfish player **exploits** this instinct.

---

## The Metagame-Only Party

A party held together only by the metagame:
- Should have disbanded ages ago
- Can't make decisions because motivations are too different
- Gets bogged down in committee discussions over every choice
- **CAN'T break up yet CAN'T work together**

"Characters forced to work together" can be compelling — but only if separation genuinely isn't an option, and only if **all players and the DM agree beforehand.**

---

## The DM's Responsibility

1. Talk to players about what the game is about and how it works.
2. Figure out what **glue holds the party together** — with the players.
3. Once agreed, every player is bound by that premise.
4. Characters that don't fit the premise don't get approved.

> "As surely as you can't bring a space marine or a gnome bard to my D&D game, you can't bring a character that doesn't fit the premise."

It is NOT the player's prerogative to create whatever character they want without constraint. The DM ensures the metagame is respected so the game functions.

---

## The Player's Responsibility

- Make sure your character fits the group and the game.
- If you play a selfish loner, it's YOUR job to play them in a way that keeps them part of the group.
- Otherwise, the **player** (not the character) becomes the jerk-face loner.

But if a player doesn't do their job, it falls to the DM. Practically speaking, **everything is the DM's job.**

---

## Practical Takeaways

1. **The metagame is real and important.** It's the unwritten structure that makes the game work.
2. **Characters must fit the premise.** Full stop.
3. **Establish party glue during session zero** — with player buy-in.
4. **Don't let one player hold the game hostage** with character concepts requiring everyone else to accommodate them.
5. **Players who create obstacles to group function are being selfish** — no matter how "interesting" the concept.
6. **The DM is the final gatekeeper** on character approval.
