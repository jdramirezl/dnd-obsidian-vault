# Gaming in Fits and Starts

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/gaming-in-fits-and-starts/)
**Date:** December 11, 2024
**Series:** True Scenario Designery

---

*This Feature's part of my long-running series on advanced adventure and encounter design in tabletop roleplaying games, True Scenario Designery.*

*This specific True Scenario Designery lesson is part of a module about designing and mapping roleplaying game scenarios that the players can actually win. Or that they can lose. Or both.*

The next several lessons in this course — which I'm collectively calling a module; think of it like a chapter in a textbook — are about planning tabletop roleplaying game Scenarios the way other game designers plan other kinds of games. It's about using Goals, Outcomes, and Challenges to make Scenarios players can meaningfully Win and/or Lose.

Winning and Losing aren't Outcomes, but rather, they're qualitative labels players' brains assign — unconsciously — to their gameplay experiences. Players' brains do that whether the gameplay experience is over or whether it's still going on.

That means that one of your jobs — as a Scenario Designer — is to design Scenarios that send players' brains the right signals so they can determine whether they're Winning or Losing and when they've Won or Lost. You must also build space into your Scenarios for the players' brains to make those calls.

## Am I Winning?

Our brains are constantly assessing our standing in games. That's vital to the gameplay experience. Those unconscious, intuitive, experiential assessments help us make good decisions. When we think we're doing well, we keep doing what we're doing. When we think we're losing, we change it up. This ain't just about skill development, it's part of the basic decision-making process we follow every gameplay *turn*.

Those same *I'm winning* and *I'm losing* signals create feels. When we're doing good, we might feel excited, hopeful, optimistic, driven, energized, ambitious, proud, or whatever. When we're doing bad, we might feel tense, nervous, desperate, resolute, frightened, depressed, or whatever. This crap matters because every artist's ultimate goal — Scenario Designers are artists; games are art — is to make people feel things.

## Am I Getting Anywhere?

Separate from position-evaluation is a need to feel like we're making progress. Human brains don't really handle long-term, incremental tasks with delayed payoffs very well. Our dumb monkey brains are driven by results.

Thus, to keep us engaged, we need to break long-term tasks — like perpetual, never-ending roleplaying game campaigns — into chunks so we can periodically celebrate victories and reflect on defeats. That helps us feel like we're actually getting somewhere.

## But Don't Think Too Much About The Psychobabble

These concepts are vital if you want to create satisfying, long-term gameplay experiences, but they're also invisible, unconscious, internal things. No one's ever going to tell you they liked a game because "It clearly telegraphed my progress at regular intervals and gave me space to celebrate my incremental victories." You don't notice that shit, but players' brains do.

Most game designers won't even say crap like that. They might not even consciously know that's why what they're doing works. They just know what works through experience, education, practice, training, intuition, iterative design, and testing.

Everything above is the justification for the practical advice below. When I tell you how to structure games around Quests and Scenes and Acts, I'm telling you how to use the psychobabble to your advantage. That's what you should focus on. The rest is just fun facts.

## *Magic: The Gathering*: An Object Lesson in Incremental Winning and Losing

*Magic: The Gathering* is perfectly designed to do everything Scenario Designers must do with regard to players' brains assessing winning and losing.

The obvious: You can Win or Lose a match. Check relative Hit Point totals and field positions to assess how you're doing.

The slightly less obvious: The Combat Phase is basically a tentpole for each player's turn — effectively a turn climax. Every Combat Phase is a little game of its very own and the players can assess each Combat Phase as a Win or Loss. Thus, every turn centers on an incremental, temporal Win or Loss.

The non-obvious: Devoted players rarely play isolated matches. Winning a full *game* involves beating your opponent in two out of three matches. So Winning an individual duel is just another kind of incremental, temporal victory. Leagues and Tournaments add further layers of incremental Winning and Losing.

## Designing Scenarios Like a *Magic: The Gathering* Tournament

The Encounter-Scene-Adventure-Act-Campaign-Sidequest Hierarchy describes how most people design adventures and campaigns pretty well. But the point ain't the chunks or how you define them. Apart from Adventures and Encounters, none of those chunks matter. Adventures are just Scenarios and Encounters are just the individual, self-contained Challenges that comprise Scenarios.

### Angry's Scenario Design Hierarchy

- **Encounters** — The smallest unit of actual Scenario design. When PCs *encounter* something they have to overcome, defeat, circumvent, or avoid.
- **Scenes** — Chunks comprising several Encounters and non-Encounter gameplay stuff. Normally bounded by time, geography, and mode of gameplay.
- **Adventures** — The smallest Scenarios that are truly complete Scenarios. The defining unit of Scenario Design.
- **Acts** — Scenarios bigger than Adventures, comprising multiple Adventures that actually constitute a Scenario when strung together.
- **Campaign** — All the Adventures that together comprise a single, ongoing gameplay experience from beginning to planned conclusion.
- **Sidequests** — Chunks of gameplay that fit inside other chunks of pretty much any size and are typically non-continuous.

### Why Must It Be Like This?

True Scenario Designers build major Challenges into their Adventures separate from the Encounters that comprise them. They also need periodic Wins and Losses. That means you've got to be able to work Scenarios into your Scenarios to break up or extend the gameplay.

It's useful to have a nice, defined structure called a Scene because it helps you to design good Scenes, but it also doesn't matter how you break up your Adventure into chunks. It only matters that you do.

## The Quest Structure

Think about roleplaying game scenarios in terms of individual segments of gameplay with a **Goal**, a **Challenge**, **Outcomes**, and **Context**. That's the secret sauce that makes a chunk a chunk.

- An Adventure has a Goal, a Challenge, Outcomes, and Context. Missing any = not an Adventure.
- Every Encounter has a Goal, a Challenge, Outcomes, and Context.
- A Scene is a segment of an Adventure defined by a Goal, a Challenge, Outcomes, and Context.
- Acts don't become Acts until there's a Goal, Challenge, Outcomes, and Context tying the Adventures together.
- Campaigns are just campaigns if there's no overarching Goal, Challenge, Outcomes, and Context.
- Sidequests: gameplay spread across multiple Encounters/Scenes/Adventures connected to the same Goal, Challenge, Outcomes, and Context.

That's the Quest Structure. The presence of a Goal, a Challenge, Outcomes, and Context are what turns chunks of gameplay into Quests — real, legit Scenarios designed to impact the human brain the way human brains expect games to impact them.

But that doesn't mean any of this should be visible to the players.
