# Game Mastering Makes No Sense

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/game-mastering-makes-no-sense/)
**Date:** January 24, 2023
**Series:** True Game Mastery (Part 2)

---

## Context

This is the second part of the True Game Mastery series. After establishing Ownership and Investment in Part 1, Angry says to forget those terms — he'll rarely mention them again. Instead, every lesson going forward is written with the express purpose of helping you build player Investment or Own your game, even when it doesn't look like it.

---

## Game Mastering Is an Art, Not a Skill

There's a huge difference. Game Mastering is a **complex synthesis** of many skills — game design, storytelling, communication, logistics, creative writing, conflict resolution, leadership, social interaction, information management, puzzle design, organization, and more.

You could master every individual skill and still run sucky games. The synthesis itself is the art. Like painting: you can master brush technique, color theory, and composition individually and still not paint anything people want to look at.

### Arts Are Subjective

Game Masters produce entertainment experiences — part game, part narrative. Entertainment is entirely subjective, about evoking emotional responses. Emotions aren't rational.

But this doesn't mean it's impossible. If artists couldn't create broadly appealing emotional experiences, there'd be no popular culture. People's brains are more similar than different.

---

## The Best Teacher Is Experience (Experiential Learning)

Your brain is hardwired to learn complex, subjective arts — provided you get out of its way.

When you do something, all the brain parts involved fire at once. Your brain wires connections between them (neuroplasticity). The emotional, snap-judgment part evaluates whether things are going well or poorly. Good outcomes build strong connections; bad outcomes build weak, tentative ones.

That's Experiential Learning. Triggering it deliberately is called **Practicing**.

---

## Four Ways to Supercharge Learning

### 1. Above All, Run Games

Practice is the single most important path to mastery. If you've only got four hours a week, spend every minute running games. Quit watching videos and reading articles.

### 2. Feed Your Brain the Good Stuff

Consume media you enjoy — books, movies (good ones), board games, video games. Fill your brain with examples of good narratives and good gameplay. This trains your intuitive brain to tell good experiences from bad.

TTRPG-specific media is less valuable than training your brain separately on good narrative and good gameplay. Don't go overboard on actual-play streams — you'll compare yourself to better GMs, creating stress that impedes learning.

### 3. Work on Your Form

Use remaining time to work on specific skills giving you trouble. But **don't overdo it** — too much analysis detracts from learning. Work on one skill at a time.

### 4. Relax Your Brain

Your conscious, rational brain isn't useful with the subjective. It just endlessly asks "what are you doing? Why? Are you doing it right?" while the capable parts of your brain are mastering complex skills.

**Don't:**
- Record sessions and analyze every mistake (you'll convince yourself you suck)
- Try to memorize and implement every piece of advice perfectly
- Consume more analysis than actual games and stories
- Compare yourself to streamed GMs (stress kills learning)

> Trust your brain to do its thing without your attention. Don't overanalyze. Mostly, just relax and run games.

## Why Angry's Lessons Won't Always Make Sense

### Long-Term vs. Short-Term

True Game Masters worry more about how the game will feel tomorrow or next month than right now. Human brains are weird about present-moment pleasure vs. long-term engagement.

**Example:** Humans are happier with decisions they can't undo. Your brain does behind-the-scenes work to make you accept things you can't control. If you can undo a decision anytime, your brain constantly re-evaluates it (Law of Diminishing Returns). So: don't allow retraining or take-backsies, and make it hard to retire characters.

### Complexity Means Compound Effects

Small changes do many things at once. Limiting available races isn't just about creativity under constraints — it also improves long-term choice satisfaction, trains GM Ownership, makes it easier to build strong racial identities, and more. Never just one reason.

### Unintended Consequences

Fixing something can break something else. Strict ammo tracking + no encumbrance = players carry infinite ammo = tracking doesn't matter.

### Pain-in-the-Ass Mechanics Build Immersion

Fiddly bookkeeping like supply and ammo tracking is valuable *because* it's a pain. Ticking off an arrow makes you think of it as a real physical thing. The more little reminders that you're simulating a world, the stronger the Immersion. The more bookkeeping you drop for convenience, the shakier the illusion.

---

## Feedback Is Suspect

People know whether they like what they've got, but they're bad at explaining why. And they're awful at guessing what they'll like before they've experienced it.

> Never ask players to assess anything they haven't experienced.

---

## Judging Progress

Judge your progress by your **worst** sessions, not your best. Great sessions are rare and don't improve much. But if you keep practicing, your worst sessions get less and less bad. That IS progress.

> "My crappy games get slightly less crappy with every session" is about as humble a brag as you can Tweet. But it is progress.

---

## Give Changes Time

- **2-3 sessions** just to internalize any change
- **2-3 more sessions** before you see any impact
- **6-12 sessions total** before you can judge if a change is working
- Players resist change for the first 2-3 sessions, get used to it in 2-3 more, and can judge it in 2-3 more
- **Do not ask players if they'll accept a change.** Tell them you're changing things and you'll want their feedback in twelve weeks.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Game Mastering is a complex art** — a synthesis of many skills, not the sum of them
2. **Practice is king** — run as many games as you can; nothing else comes close
3. **Feed your brain good media** — stories and games, not just TTRPG analysis
4. **Relax and trust your brain** — overanalysis and comparison kill learning
5. **Long-term satisfaction often conflicts with short-term pleasure** — counterintuitive advice may be correct
6. **Fiddly mechanics build immersion** — don't drop bookkeeping just because it's annoying
7. **Judge progress by your worst sessions** — they should get less bad over time
8. **Give changes 6-12 sessions** before judging them
9. **Player feedback is suspect** — people know what they feel but not why, and can't predict what they'll like
