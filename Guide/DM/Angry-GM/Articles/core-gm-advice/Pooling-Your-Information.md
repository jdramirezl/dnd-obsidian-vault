# Pooling Your Information

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/pooling-your-information/)
**Date:** October 20, 2021
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## The Problem

D&D was designed around the idea that being forewarned is being forearmed — Vancian spell prep, crafting, monster vulnerabilities all reward forward planning. But players rarely have the information they need, and GMs don't know how to make it available. Modern D&D responded by removing the ways planning could meaningfully affect outcomes instead of teaching GMs information management.

---

## The Information Pool

A simple information management tool: **make a list of useful bits of information the players would benefit from having, then treat them like treasure — hide some in the adventure, hand out others as rewards for clever play.**

### How to Build It

1. After designing your adventure (or reading a published one), write ~10 bits of useful information as clear, concise facts
2. Mix the informational types (see below)
3. Plant about half directly into the adventure (attached to NPCs, rooms, encounters)
4. Keep the entire list handy during play
5. When players actively seek information, pick an appropriate fact and deliver it

---

## Five Kinds of Information

### 1. Strategic Information
Information that helps players **win** — overcome obstacles, accomplish goals, avoid defeat.
- Monster strengths, weaknesses, vulnerabilities
- NPC motivations, hopes, fears (for social encounters)
- Guard patrols, security details (for heists)
- Secret back doors, alternate escape routes

### 2. Puzzle Pieces
Information that makes something **possible** — without it, players can't access, accomplish, or solve something.
- The safe's combination (or the clues to deduce it)
- Core of every mystery adventure
- Can gate side content, optional rewards, or side quests

### 3. Spoiler Information
Information about **the plot** — who's a double agent, what happens at midnight, whether the baron will take your side.
- Advance warning of plot developments
- Eventually reveals itself naturally, but early knowledge is valuable

### 4. Backstory Information
Information about **why things are happening** — motivations, origins, history behind the current story.
- Provides context that enables emotional engagement
- Players who claim they don't care about story still benefit from it
- Without it, players mentally check out for reasons no one can identify

### 5. Worldlore
Information about **the setting** — history, people, places, how things work, gods, planes.
- Makes the setting feel like a real place worth caring about
- Another tool for emotional investment

## Ways to Deliver Information

Information can be **direct** (clear facts) or **indirect** (requires reasoning/deduction). It can also be **loaded** (carries implicit additional info) or **piecemeal** (broken into pieces that must be assembled).

### Delivery Methods

| Method | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Exposition** | GM tells players directly (common knowledge, Intelligence checks) | Fast, clear, reliable delivery of must-know facts |
| **Interaction** | Built into NPC conversations, mannerisms, behaviors | Players seeking info through social engagement |
| **Research** | Digging through documents, libraries, records | Players actively seeking knowledge |
| **Discovery** | Environmental details — inscriptions, corpses, scorch marks, artifacts | Planting nuggets throughout the adventure |
| **Reconnaissance** | Scouting, spying, staking out | Players investing time/risk to learn about threats |
| **Divination** | Magical/supernatural means — spells, oracles, visions, dreams | Cryptic, indirect delivery; pre-obfuscate for best effect |

## The Chair Analogy

Treat information like an interior decorator treats chairs:
- Put chairs where chairs should be (dining room, living room, porch)
- Have extra chairs on hand for when someone asks
- Don't obsess over making people sit in chairs or predicting how they'll use them
- Don't yell at people for using chairs in unexpected ways

Your job is to **make information available** — not to force players to find it or predict exactly how they'll use it.
