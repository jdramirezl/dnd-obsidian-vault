# Being In-Flex-Able

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/being-in-flex-able/)
**Date:** May 1, 2019

---

This article is about how GMs make the decision about what skills to use and what ability scores to roll — about flexible ability checks and why those are terrible. Let's call it "action flex-ability."

## There's No Such Thing as a Skill Check

There is no such thing as a skill check in D&D 5E. Nor in the d20 system. Simply put, skill checks don't exist. Even though the rules say they do.

All d20 games have this rule at their heart: whenever a character attempts to do something, the player rolls a d20 and adds an ability modifier to the result, then any other relevant modifiers, then compares the result to a DC. If the result equals or exceeds the DC, the action succeeds.

The books talk about skill checks, attack rolls, saving throws as if they are separate things. But a melee attack roll is just a Strength check with a combat training bonus. A saving throw is an ability check with a class training bonus. And a skill check is just an ability check with a bonus for relevant training.

The skill list is not versatile, not well-defined, and not all-inclusive. Some skills cast wide nets, others narrow, and they don't encompass everything characters can do. When a new GM tries to resolve "break down a door," they run down the skill list trying to fit the action into each skill, rescan to make sure they didn't miss something, and struggle to adopt the fallback of a plain ability check.

It's a lot easier to choose between six options — the six ability scores — than to choose amongst over a dozen skills and then decide none fit.

**Don't use skill checks.** Your job as a GM is to fit every action into one of six slots: Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, or Charisma. After that, if a player has a useful skill, let them apply it:

> **Player:** "I get a running start and jump over the pit."
> **GM:** "Okay, make a Strength check."
> **Player:** "I am trained in Athletics. Does that apply?"
> **GM:** "Sure, add your proficiency bonus to the roll."

This works for literally every action:

> **Player:** "I want to bend his wrist behind him and twist his arm until he gives us the information."
> **GM:** "Okay, make a Strength check."
> **Player:** "I am trained in Intimidation. Does that apply?"
> **GM:** "Absolutely it does. Add the bonus."

That keeps you from doing stupid things like using Charisma to resolve every circumstance of bullying.

Think about how much cleaner the character sheet would be without the entire skill list. Just a list of which skills the character is trained in. This removes the idea that if you're not trained in a skill, you can't do it. There's a huge psychological difference between "here's a list of all the things you can do with numbers measuring how good you are" and "you can do anything you want, and when you do these four particular things, you get a special bonus."

## Action Flex-Ability

Action Flex-Ability is a fallacy — a way of thinking that makes your game worse.

A character jumps over a pit. Under the "ability check" method, the argument becomes Strength vs. Dexterity. The correct answer is Strength.

There is a growing community of GMs who think both answers are equally valid and the player should use whichever score their character is best in. That's Action Flex-Ability: the idea that the ability score governing an action is not set in stone and that, with a good enough argument, a player should be able to switch it around.

Once the player declares an action, the GM should decide — based entirely on the action described, the intended outcome, and the GM's understanding of reality — which ONE ability score governs the action. Consistently. The answer for all characters across all similar situations should be the same at all times. If jumping over a pit is a Strength check, it is always a Strength check for everyone. Non-negotiable.

## The Best Answer is the Right Answer

One thing has to be the most "in play." There is one **whammo factor** — one key factor that above all else determines success or failure. Everything else just helps or hinders.

For a long jump, the key factor is distance. If you don't have the muscle power to make the distance, coordination can't save you. If you have the muscle power, coordination is very unlikely to turn success into failure.

As a GM, you're trying to distill the action down to the one trait that above all others determines success or failure. You're boiling a very complex action down to a single die roll. That is your job.

**Investigate vs. Perception example:** To find a matchbook partially hidden under a couch — that's Perception. Either you see it or you don't. For blood spatters on the wall whose pattern indicates the victim was attacked from the front — that's Investigation. They're plainly visible, but recognizing the significance takes deduction and logic.

It's not about simulating an action. It's about which ability score is going to be the **limiting factor**.

## A Hard GM is a Good GM

I am opposed to Action Flex-Ability because I like my RPGs to have actual creative role-playing. Flexible GMs remove the need for creative role-playing.

Suppose you're a weak but agile rogue facing a pit over a lava lake. You decide to jump. The GM asks for Strength. You ask for Dexterity with Acrobatics. A flexible GM agrees. You roll and succeed. You picked the most obvious way and generated a random number at it.

At my table: I remind you that acrobats develop tremendous strength, and your rogue skipped leg days. But acrobats also swing, balance on beams and tightropes — things that play into coordination more than raw strength. So you grab a torch sconce as a makeshift grappling hook, toss rope over a ceiling beam (Dexterity), and swing across with a flourish (Dexterity and Acrobatics).

**Players are driven to be creative when they can't simply throw their strengths at a problem.** If you allow players to negotiate the ability score, you remove the need for creative problem-solving. You're boiling the game down to throwing dice at a problem.

Too much flexibility turns the game into rules negotiation. Choosing which ability score to use is NOT an interesting choice — it's based on math. Coming up with an argument to use your best score is based on an ulterior motive, not on determining the best way to resolve an action.
