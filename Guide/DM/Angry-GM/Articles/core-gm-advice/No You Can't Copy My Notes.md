# No! You Can't Copy My Notes

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/no-you-cant-copy-my-notes/)
**Date:** January 27, 2016
**Category:** Core GM Advice — Planning vs. Prep

---

## Core Concept

**Game planning and game prep are two completely different activities.** Planning is making all the decisions about how the game should run — the world, NPCs, scenes, structure. Prep is writing that stuff down. Most of the planning happens in your head (in the shower, on a bike, lying in bed), and the notes you produce are just personal shorthand to support what you already know.

---

## Why You Can't (Usefully) Copy Someone's Notes

Angry's actual game notes are "indecipherable scribbled crap" — when they exist at all. A typical dungeon adventure spanning 2–3 sessions fits on a single page of cryptic abbreviations like "gob cv wtfl" or "spd lar crpse hlf eqp gp ptns(?)."

This isn't laziness — it's the result of 28+ years of GMing. The notes are just memory triggers for a carefully planned game. The planning happened elsewhere: while thinking, showering, biking, watching TV.

**The notes are useless to anyone else because:**
- They're personal shorthand tuned to one brain's memory
- They omit everything the GM can remember or improvise
- The actual value is in the planning, not the documentation

---

## Planning vs. Prep: The Key Distinction

| **Planning** | **Prep** |
|---|---|
| Making decisions about how the game runs | Writing those decisions down |
| Understanding world, NPCs, structure, narrative | Creating maps, stat blocks, flavor text, notes |
| Happens in your head, anytime, anywhere | Happens at a desk with paper/screen |
| Universal principles anyone can learn | Deeply personal to each GM |
| The actual skill of GMing | Just documentation |

A scribbled note like "waterfall cave, goblins" works because the GM already knows:
- How to improvise terrain on a battlemat
- Party level means 3–5 goblins in 5E
- Custom goblin types (archer, blocker, warlock) are ready
- Chokepoints, visibility, cover can be invented on the spot
- A halfling corpse means ~50gp, studded leather, short sword, sling, pack, 2 healing potions, potion of climbing

**The planning is all there. The prep is minimal.**

---

## Planning is Universal, Prep is Personal

Running a game is like an open-book test. Your notes are your cheat sheet — but they only work for you.

**The key to efficiency:** Know what you need to write down vs. what you can remember vs. what you can invent on the spot. This is highly specific to each individual:
- Some GMs remember stories and NPC motives easily but need encounter math written out
- Others can build combat encounters from nothing but forget every NPC name
- As you gain experience, you need fewer notes because you know the system, the world, and the needs of a good scene

**Start by overdoing it.** Write down everything. Over time, you'll learn what you can drop.

---

## Zero Prep ≠ Zero Planning

"Improvisation" that works is not pulling things from nowhere — it's **planning and executing in one step**. When Angry suddenly adds an NPC rival mid-session, it's because:
- The pace had slowed and needed a boost
- A faction needed a stronger introduction
- An interaction scene would balance the combat-heavy adventure
- The NPC was designed (on the fly) to be smart enough not to fight when outnumbered

Every "improvised" element serves a deliberate narrative or game purpose. The GM recognizes a need and fills it using their understanding of structure, pacing, and narrative.

---

## Why You Can't Teach Prep

Prep is unteachable because it's entirely personal. What works for one brain doesn't work for another:
- "NPC on an index card" methods work for GMs who can remember enough that they only need shorthand
- "One-page dungeon" formats work for GMs who can fill in the gaps from memory
- Neither works for someone who needs those gaps filled in

**What CAN be taught:** The principles of planning — how to structure adventures, build scenes, design encounters, understand narrative. Once you know what matters, you figure out which parts you personally need to write down.

---

## Practical Takeaways

1. **Don't confuse planning with prep** — you can plan extensively while writing almost nothing down
2. **Don't judge your GMing by your note volume** — sparse notes with deep planning beats detailed notes with shallow planning
3. **Start with too much prep and gradually reduce** — learn what you actually need written down
4. **Improvisation is instant planning, not randomness** — every on-the-fly decision should serve a purpose
5. **Focus on learning planning principles** — the prep format will sort itself out naturally
6. **Your notes are for you alone** — don't try to copy someone else's system; build your own

---

## Key Takeaway

> The stuff you write down is the least important part of running a game. The stuff you understand about how games work — structure, narrative, pacing, encounter design — that's what matters. Learn the principles, and the notes take care of themselves.
