# How to Motivate a Bunch of Lying Liars

> [Source: The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-motivate-a-bunch-of-lying-liars/)

---

## Key Advice

- **Goals and motivations are not the same thing** — a goal is the endpoint; a motivation is *why* the character pursues it
- **Players are liars** — they claim to be "true to their character" but will rationalize anything if the adventure appeals to *them* as players
- **Player motivation trumps character motivation** — always err on the side of motivating the player; they'll drag their characters along
- An adventure must motivate **both** the player and the character, but player motivation is more important
- **Don't bury the lede** — lead with the coolest, most exciting promise of your adventure
- Character motivations fall into three types: **Things** (treasure, glory, titles), **Ideas** (justice, freedom, goodness), and **Ego** (self-actualization, personal challenge, enlightenment)
- Player motivations map to engagements: **Challenge, Discovery, Fantasy, Narrative, and Submission**
- **Specific beats general** — tailor motivations to your actual players and characters when you can
- **Fewer motivations are better** — too many clash like too many spices; narrow down to the strongest ones
- Watch what characters *do*, not what backstories *say* — players are inconsistent about their own motivations

## Summary

Motivation is the third pillar of adventure building (alongside structure and resolution). Every adventure needs to answer two questions: why would the characters do this, and why would the players want to play this? Players will always rationalize character behavior to match what *they* want to do — so build adventures players want to experience and their characters will get dragged along. Character motivations come in three flavors (things, ideas, ego), while player motivations align with engagement types (challenge, discovery, fantasy, narrative, submission) and gameplay preferences (combat, interaction, problem-solving). The longer you run for the same group, the easier motivation becomes because players trust your style. For unknown groups, offer a broad mix. The motivation you write down is a tool for *you* — it helps you build the hook and first scene.

## Actionable Steps

1. **For every adventure, write down two lists**: why the characters would care (things/ideas/ego) and why the players would want to play (which engagements it promises)
2. **Lead with the coolest part**: When presenting the adventure hook, put the most exciting promise front and center
3. **Track character motivations actively**: Keep a running list of what each character *actually* pursues in play, not just what the backstory says
4. **Cover multiple engagements**: A good hook should promise at least 2-3 types of engagement (e.g., challenge + discovery + fantasy)
5. **Use campaign goals as motivation**: In long-running campaigns, progress toward existing player goals is a powerful motivator
6. **Narrow your motivation list**: If you've got too many motivations, cut to the 2-3 strongest — they need to fit into a single hook scene
7. **For new/unknown groups**: Offer broad motivations (the dragon scenario works because it hits treasure, greater good, challenge, and self-actualization all at once)

> [!tip] Bottom Line
> Build adventures that players want to play and they'll find a reason for their characters to go along. Motivate the player first, the character second. Keep your motivation list short, lead with the coolest promise, and watch what characters actually do — not what backstories claim.
