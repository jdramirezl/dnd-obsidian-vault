# Your Ability Scores Suck

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Your Ability Scores Suck](https://theangrygm.com/your-ability-scores-suck/)
**Date:** May 27, 2019
**Series:** Hacking Rules / Ability Scores

---

## The Core Argument

D&D's six ability scores (Str, Dex, Con, Int, Wis, Cha) were designed as **descriptive traits** in an era when they barely affected gameplay. Since 3E, they've become the **core action resolution system** — but they were never redesigned for that purpose. The result is a system with blurry lines, dump stats, and participation problems.

## How Ability Scores Evolved

### Pre-3E: Descriptive
- Ability scores described the character and gave minor bonuses to specific subsystems
- You never rolled a "Strength check" to open a door — you rolled a d6 with a Strength bonus
- Each situation had its own bespoke rule (THAC0, saving throws, d6 rolls, etc.)
- The GM mostly didn't consider ability scores when adjudicating actions

### 3E Revolution: Action Resolution
- Introduced the **ability check** as a universal mechanic — one rule to resolve any action
- All other rules were reframed to use this system
- Ability scores shifted from **describing the character** to **resolving character actions**
- This was the smartest thing to happen to RPGs

### 5E: Stuck in the Past
- 5E is a throwback — a de-evolution back toward 3E (maybe "2.75E")
- After 4E's perceived failure, WotC played it safe — no sacred cows were slaughtered
- The designers were likely constrained by corporate directives and the open playtest
- D&D is now stuck firmly in its own past, and that's not the designers' fault

## The Modern Definition

> **Ability scores are an action resolution system** in which every character possesses a specific list of capabilities used as a core component of determining the outcome of anything they do.

They are **not** primarily about describing the character. Anything that doesn't define how **every character** interacts with the world can be relegated to traits, feats, skills, or flavor text.

## Why the Classic Six Fail as Action Resolution

### 1. No Bright Lines (Wisdom vs. Intelligence)
- "Book smarts vs. street smarts" and "tomatoes vs. fruit salad" are not clear divisions
- Medicine/healing is Wisdom-based, but treating injuries is about **recognizing symptoms and remembering treatments** — that's Intelligence
- Perception/awareness got lumped into Wisdom for no clear reason
- The Wisdom/Charisma boundary is equally muddy (confidence, willpower)
- **Wisdom as a concept** = accumulated knowledge from experience. That should be tied to **level**, not a base stat

### 2. The Charisma Participation Problem
- Charisma is the **one and only** score governing all social interaction — a supposed "pillar of the game"
- Only characters with good Charisma participate socially
- Wizards can't have intellectual debates; Clerics can't use empathy — without Charisma to back it up
- **Compare to combat:** Combat uses Str, Dex, Int, Wis, Cha, and Con across attacks, AC, saves, initiative, and HP. Everyone participates through different scores
- Imagine if all combat ran off a single "Fighting" score — that's what Charisma does to social interaction
- **The fix is NOT** multiple Charisma scores. Instead: **no social ability score**. Use all ability scores in social situations via skills and GM adjudication

### 3. Passive/Vestigial Scores (Constitution)
- Constitution doesn't govern **actions** — it's a passive HP/save bonus
- HP is already affected by class, level, feats, and magic items
- Merge its defensive role into Strength (let Strength govern fortitude AND melee attacks)
- This would make Strength competitive with Dexterity, which is currently the most overloaded combat score

### 4. Dexterity Is Overloaded
- Governs: AC, evasion, missile attacks, initiative, finesse weapons, Stealth, Acrobatics, Sleight of Hand...
- Every character of every class benefits from high Dex
- It's the most overpowered ability score on the list

### 5. Intelligence Is Uselessly Passive (for Non-Casters)
- Knowledge checks aren't **actions** — they're speedbumps in decision-making
- If you're not a spellcaster, there's no reason to invest in Intelligence
- Let people choose areas of expertise without needing an ability score backing it

## The Damning Indictment

For any character of any class, the optimal ability score priority is:

1. **Class requirement** (Int for Wizard, Wis for Cleric, Str for Fighter)
2. **Dexterity** (overloaded with uses in combat)
3. **Charisma** (mandatory to participate in social interaction)

Of six ability scores, only **three** matter for any given character. One is mandatory for a pillar of the game. One dominates another pillar. The third is dictated by class. That's a broken system.

## Designing from Scratch

If building a new system, ability scores should be designed by:

1. **Figuring out how characters interact with the world** — not starting from a pre-existing list
2. **Breaking actions down along bright lines** — every action fits ONE and PRECISELY ONE score
3. **Ensuring broad applicability** — no score should gate an entire pillar of gameplay
4. **Eliminating passive scores** — if it doesn't resolve actions, it doesn't need to be an ability score
5. **Giving the GM simple questions** to determine the right score for any action

### What to Ditch
- **Constitution** — merge into Strength or handle via class/level
- **Charisma as a social gate** — spread social interaction across multiple scores
- **Knowledge/memorization as a core stat** — handle via skills, backgrounds, or expertise
- **The score/modifier duality** — the score itself is vestigial; only the modifier matters

## Genre-Specific Ability Scores (Examples)

### Pokémon RPG
Trainers interact with the world **through their Pokémon**. Physical stats are nearly irrelevant. What matters is the trainer-Pokémon relationship:
- **Empathy** — emotional connection (love-based training)
- **Spirit** — inspiration, respect, leading by example (mutual respect)
- **Command** — domination, willpower (authority-based training)
- Secondary: Physical, Mental (broad, rarely used)

### Harry Potter RPG
Four core traits map to the four houses and cover everything:
- **Courage** (Harry / Gryffindor)
- **Ambition** (Ron / Slytherin)
- **Intellect** (Hermione / Ravenclaw)
- **Loyalty** (Neville / Hufflepuff)
- Magic schools could associate with different stats

## The Ability/Skill Dichotomy

The ability + skill system is powerful **when designed correctly**:
- **Ability scores** = universal capabilities everyone has; the GM's tool for adjudicating any action
- **Skills** = specialized interactions; things some people do better or differently
- The default should be "**ability check first**, add a skill if appropriate" — not "find a skill, fall back to ability check if none fits"
- D&D presents this backwards, creating a "skill check first" mentality that limits creative play
