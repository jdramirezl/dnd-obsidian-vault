# Designing with a Strong D (And Also the Return of Crafting)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/designing-with-a-strong-d/)
**Date:** February 27, 2020
**Series:** Crafting a Crafting System

---

The only thing harder than starting a new project is restarting a project that you haven't touched in over a year. I stupidly made a public promise to bring some of my dead half-finished projects back to life. Especially my D&D 5E crafting system.

I'd had a bunch of discussions with people about the importance of starting with "the play experience" and focusing on "game feel." Which was something that a lot of people just don't do. Or have never heard of. Which is a shame because it actually makes it easier to design stuff. And because it makes it more likely your designs will actually do what you want them to do.

## How to Start

Every project starts with a single idea or need. No two are alike. But the end results are the same. After a lot of growth and nurturing, the idea emerges screaming and naked from the designer's brain as a fully formed game design thing.

Most designers just keep flinging ideas at the design until the thing seems mostly done and then they just fill in the blanks and tweak the thing. But that tends to lead to designs that have a disjointed feel to them. There's an easier way. There's a step you can insert between planting the initial seed and actually designing the game thing. It involves designing around something called game feel.

## All About the Feels

Game feel describes how the game feels. It refers to the fact that whatever it is you've designed, it couldn't possibly be mistaken for anything else. Even if you take off all the fluff and flavor and flair.

Game feel is hard to work with because it's subjective. There's no numbers to tweak or rules or systems to follow. Which is where most of the artistry and genius of game design really comes out. And where most game design screwups happen.

All of the people who complained that every class was a spellcaster in 4th Edition were actually complaining about the game feel. Obviously, every character wasn't a spellcaster. Fighters didn't cast spells. They used special moves they learned through intense training which they could use a limited number of times per day. And which weren't called spells at all. That's game feel right there. You can put any name or description on anything that you want. But, if that thing doesn't feel like what you say it is, it isn't that thing.

## Remember MDA?

The MDA model says that game designers and players approach games from two different directions. Players enjoy games because they satisfy particular entertainment needs (aesthetics). The designer's goal is to provide a satisfying play experience. The only tool the designer has is the game's mechanics. When all of the mechanics interact with each other and with the players, they produce a particular game dynamic, a play experience. And hopefully, the play experience causes the players to experience the right aesthetics.

## The Missing D

A lot of designers forget the 'D' between the 'M' and the 'A'. They realize they are trying to design mechanics that produce certain feelings in their players' brains, and then they just start making rules. They jump from aesthetics to mechanics.

Mechanics do not create aesthetics. Mechanics and aesthetics interact through the game's dynamics. They interact through the experience of play. And that's the missing step in the game design process.

## The Missing Step

You always want to plow into your design projects with a nice, big, strong D. As early as possible in the design process, you want to define the play experience you're going for and how that play experience is supposed to feel.

How do you do that? Grab a blank piece of paper and write out – in prose – how you see your game or system actually playing at the table. If you could watch people play with your design, what would it look like? And what would it feel like?

You want to describe as completely as possible exactly what people will be doing when they play your game. Not in terms of the game's fiction but in terms of what the players will actually be doing at the table. Will they be rolling dice? Will they be making choices? What choices? How will they feel about them?

You absolutely cannot do that in one pass. So, rewrite it a few times, expanding the details each time. Every time you finish a pass, walk away for a little while. Then come back and reread it. Look for gaps. Look for problems. Ask yourself what happens at the table when something goes wrong with your design.

## Example: The Crafting System Play Experience

### First Pass

> During the course of their adventures, the players find various materials. Between adventures, the players can use those materials to craft equipment including mundane equipment and magical items. Basically, they just trade in particular combinations of materials for the items they want.

### Late Pass

> There are several ways to find materials during adventures. The GM can place materials like treasure. Players can salvage materials from monsters they kill. Players can salvage materials from visible sources the GM places. The party can forage for materials during wilderness travels. By connecting resource acquisition to treasure, killing monsters, discovery of specific locations, and wilderness travel, it keys gathering resources to common adventuring tasks.
>
> Because gathering materials happens during adventures, it can involve dice rolls and action resolution. Ability checks using skill or tool proficiencies are required. This makes item crafting feel like a specialized skill that defines a character. It provides niche protection. It reinforces the idea that gathering materials is part of adventuring.
>
> Materials have three descriptors: a **rarity** that mirrors magic item rarities, a **form** that describes what the material is composed of, and some materials have **special attributes** describing particular types of magic or effects. It is not necessary to name or keep track of specific materials. Materials can be tracked as units with particular descriptors. For example, "three units of very rare, fiery mineral." However, the GM can provide specific names – very rare, fiery mineral might be ruby.
>
> Every mundane and magical item has a **formula** describing the materials required to craft it. The formula describes the number of units of materials with specific descriptors. For example, a flametongue sword might require at least 5 units of rare metal and at least 5 units of fiery material. Each formula also specifies materials that **cannot** be used. Such constraints add an extra dimension to the resource management and make it feel more like crafting is working out a puzzle.
>
> The actual crafting of items should be as simple as a player spending the required materials and adding the item to their character sheet. This is done to emphasize that crafting is something one character goes off to a laboratory to do alone. Off-camera. That allows players who enjoy the crafting puzzle to spend as much time planning as they want without holding up the game.
>
> Tool and skill proficiencies serve as a prerequisite for crafting specific types of items, but no roll is required.

### Issue: What If the Players Need a Specific Material?

> Players should be able to buy materials (at a cost that doesn't substitute for adventuring). Players should have an explicit option to communicate the need for a specific material to the GM – a downtime activity for researching materials. This prompts the GM to create an encounter, side quest, or adventure based around acquiring that resource. This provides increased player agency and trains players to communicate with their GM about what adventures they'd like to pursue.

### Issue: What Will the Players Do with Useless Materials?

> Materials must be salable. Through selling useless materials and buying desired materials, players can effectively convert materials from one type to another with the value serving as a conversion factor. For groups that don't opt-in to crafting, this system simply becomes an alternative treasure system. Monster corpses become sources of materials, meaning monsters that traditionally don't carry treasure can actually have treasure in the form of pelts, ivory, and valuable reagents.
>
> Selling materials should be efficient (full base value). Buying materials should not be efficient (to add a cost to conversion).

### Issue: How Do the Players Know What They Can Make?

> For every magical item, there is a formula or recipe. A character must know how to craft a specific magic item in order to craft it. Many magic item formulae are commonly known by any trained craftsman – these go on a player-facing list. The GM gets a master list.
>
> Players can learn unknown formulae through: GM modifying the player-facing list, finding formulae as in-game treasure, conducting in-game research, or experimentation (analyzing an existing magic item, or experimenting with materials to discover new formulae).

### Issue: What If the Players Don't Want to Craft?

> It should be possible to pay to have an item crafted from your materials. Players can commission specific magic items and pay a cost to have the item made. Because players can buy materials, this is also an alternative system for buying magic items. Every magic item's cost = cost of materials + cost of labor.

### Issue: Can Players Sell or Salvage Items?

> Both options should exist but neither should be optimal or particularly efficient. Salvaging materials from magical items should result in a loss of value. Salvaging materials from mundane equipment (melting down a hobgoblin's armor into common metal) works well and avoids the uncomfortable idea of flaying sentient humanoids.

### Issue: Are There Other Uses for Materials?

> Allow spellcasters to use materials as reagents that give an extra boost to their spells. For example, if you cast a fireball spell and use up a fiery material, perhaps you could reroll any 1s on the damage dice. Also possible: a system for temporarily imbuing items with qualities, or a class keyed specifically to the crafting subsystem (alchemist/artificer). These are beyond the scope of the basic system, except maybe the reagent system for spellcasters.
