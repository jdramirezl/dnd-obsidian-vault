# Crafting Herbcraft (Part 1): Conceptual Herbology

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/crafting-herbcraft-part-1/)
**Date:** September 25, 2017
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

Someone on Twitter said "one of my players has the herbalism kit proficiency in D&D 5E and they are really disappointed in how it works. He wants to gather herbs and make things during the game. And the system really doesn't let him do that." An hour later, I had developed the skeleton of a system for herbalism.

We're going to go step-by-step through the development of herbcraft. In two parts. In this part, we're going to talk about concepting. That is, the thing you do BEFORE you start building a mechanic to figure out what your mechanic should actually look like when you're done.

## Speaking of Turds: Crafting in 5E

In D&D 5E, crafting is a downtime activity. That means, it's done between adventures. A downtime activity is something you can spend time on between adventures if your GM offers you the option.

The herbalism kit is described on PHB 154. If you have a proficiency with the herbalism kit, you can use it to craft potions of healings and antitoxin. Both cost 50 gp per dose. So, under the crafting rules, a proficient character merely has to spend 10 days between adventures to create one potion of healing or one dose of antitoxin.

Technically, by a strict reading of the rules, you CAN'T craft potions of healing this way. The description of the potion of the healing in the PHB specifically refers to it as a magical item. And the crafting rules in the PHB specify that you can only craft non-magical items.

Even forgiving those issues, you can see how a player might be underwhelmed by the possibilities for crafting and herbalism in 5E. The system is bad and it should feel bad.

## Why This?

Concepting is the process of figuring out what your mechanic is going to do and how it should look when you're all done with it. It starts by putting yourself in the brain of the player who is excited about the mechanic.

Imagine there is this player, right? And he's looking through your rulebook and he suddenly sees "herbalism kit" on the list of tool proficiencies. Before he reads any of the rules or mechanics, he's already excited. Now, ask yourself WHY that player is excited? What does he expect to get out of it?

Obviously, the player wants to "do herbalism." He wants to gather ingredients and make potions. Specifically, medicine. The player wants to gather ingredients during the party's travels through the wilderness and make potions that either solve the party's problems or help the party prepare for encounters and adventures.

Now, look at the crafting and herbalism system in D&D 5E. Do they speak to that at all? Is there ever going to be a scene where a character is mortally wounded and the herbalist gets to run around gathering the ingredients to make life-saving medicine? No. Not in D&D 5E. Because the herbalism system is something you do after the adventure is over. In town. Assuming you have ten days to waste.

## Dear Player: It's Not Always About You

Just because something is a cool idea that will make a player happy, that doesn't mean it's a good idea. Concepting isn't just about figuring out what cool thing a mechanic should do. It's also about figuring out how that cool thing fits into the game. And fitting the cool thing into the game usually involves making some compromises.

## Who Are You Working For?

When you design a mechanic for your own personal home game, you know exactly who is using your system. But the moment you start designing a system for anyone else, things get a lot more complicated. The broader your target audience, the more approachable your system has to be.

You also have to consider whether the system is something that is primarily for players or primarily for the GM or for both. GMs are generally more tolerant of complexity and workload provided your system is adding something neat to the game. When you're designing a system primarily for players, you have to be cognizant of the GM. If you design a system that gives one player a cool option, but creates a lot of extra work for the GM, most GMs won't want to bother with it.

## Where Does Your System Fit?

You need to decide when your system is going to come into the game. Either your system is designed to work at the table or away from the table.

At-the-table mechanics need to be simple and snappy and easy to remember. They can't break the flow. Away-from-table mechanics can be more complex and more involved. But no one likes homework.

**AT-THE-TABLE MECHANICS ARE ONLY FUN FOR THE PLAYERS ACTUALLY USING THEM.** When you create a system that only one player is going to use, you have to consider the fact that every other player at the table is going to be bored while your system is in play.

Away-from-table mechanics can't rely on everyone in the party being involved. You also can't rely on GM adjudication. And you have to assume that random chance is basically off the table – you don't want die rolls involved unless the GM is involved.

## Concepting Herbcraft

We have an at-the-table system that is designed for players. Like most character abilities, the system doesn't really do much for the GM. Players do not like to duplicate skills. So exactly ONE player will be using that mechanic at any given table.

So, we have an at-the-table mechanic for one player that doesn't really do much for the GM. And that means the system has to be lightweight, approachable, snappy, and action-focused. One decision and one die roll. Done and done.

A player can easily use herbcraft in response to a specific problem. Someone is out of hit dice and needs some first aid. Or someone is poisoned or paralyzed or sick.

What if the player wants to prepare for an upcoming encounter or adventure? That rewards planning, research, scouting, and forethought. And those are things that can come from any player, so it also rewards teamwork.

The wilderness travel rules provide a great place for herbcraft. Those rules allow each player to assume a task while traveling. Foraging for food isn't really much different than gathering herbal ingredients. So, herbcraft can fit there nicely as an alternative wilderness travel task. Or it can fit into short rests between encounters.

As a single-player, at-the-table mechanic, the resolution has to be fairly simple. While it might have been fun to have lists of specific ingredients and complicated recipes and ways to experiment with them, all of those things would really bog things down. The GM would have to either place or randomly generate specific ingredients in every adventure. The player would have to keep track of their ingredient inventory. No. Thank. You.

Spellcasting provides a good analogue for herbcraft. Mechanically, the herbalist needs to pick an effect from a list and then make a single die roll to resolve the effect.

## Concept Concepted

And THAT is how you concept a mechanic. You don't just say "man, I want some herbalism in my game, let me start inventing rules." At this point, we haven't even really looked at the mechanics except in a broad way. And yet, we already have a pretty good idea of what the mechanic should look like and how it should be used. We know who will be using it. We know when it will be used. We've identified spaces in the game where it will fit nicely and even a few mechanics it will happily sit beside. We've even figured out what other game rules it will look kind of like.
