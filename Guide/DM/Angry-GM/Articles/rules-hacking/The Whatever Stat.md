# The Whatever Stat

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Whatever Stat](https://theangrygm.com/the-whatever-stat/)
**Date:** March 18, 2020
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## What Is the Whatever Stat?

A simple, versatile GM tool: **a name, a number, and one or more thresholds.** It tracks something in the game world that players can influence and that, in turn, affects the game.

- When players take actions that make the thing more "whatever," the stat increases
- When they make it less "whatever," it decreases
- When it hits specific threshold values, something happens

It's not a subsystem — it's a tracking tool that empowers GM adjudication and narration.

## Core Example: Crowd's Favor

The party enters a gladiatorial arena. The encounter isn't just about winning the fight — it's about winning the crowd.

- **Crowd's Favor** starts at 0
- At **3**: Party gets a morale effect (like *bless*) — the crowd is cheering them on
- At **6**: Party earns a 5% discount on city purchases for a week — guilds take notice
- At **10**: The emperor is impressed and invites the party to dinner — plot advances

The GM tracks this on a note atop the initiative tracker. It reminds the GM to:
1. **Describe** the crowd's mood in every narration
2. **Narrate changes** when the stat moves ("A roar of approval goes up…" or "A stunned silence falls…")
3. **Evaluate actions** against the stat — when a player tries to play the crowd, the stat provides a criterion for adjudication
4. **Notice indirect changes** — a critical hit might increase it; killing a downed gladiator might decrease it

## Five Rules for a Good Whatever Stat

1. **Descriptive, loaded name** — Short, punchy, specific. "Crowd's Favor" not "mood" or "excitement." The name is both a narrative prompt and an adjudication criterion.

2. **Players must be able to influence it** — Directly and indirectly. If it's just a timer ticking toward doom, it's not a Whatever Stat. Player influence can be complex (entire adventures to shift an Imperial Occupation stat) but must be imaginable.

3. **Must impact the game mechanically** — If it's just fluff, players will either ignore it or feel cheated. Like coins in Super Mario Bros: immediate feel-good (score), medium-term benefit (extra life at 100), and path guidance (plot advancement).

4. **Must be visible in the game world** — Characters need to perceive it through narration. Players can't influence what they can't see. Doesn't have to be direct — an NPC's attitude can be shown through behavior changes.

5. **Quality hangs on the thresholds** — This is where the real design work goes. The mechanical bit is intentionally simple to free you up for creative threshold design.

## Threshold Guidelines

Assuming the stat starts at 0, good threshold values are **1, 3, 6, and 10** (never higher than 10):

| Threshold | Difficulty | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Very Easy | One character, one dedicated action |
| **3** | Easy | Whole party in one round, or one character over 3–4 rounds (average combat length) |
| **6** | Moderate | 2–3 party members dedicating actions for 3–4 rounds |
| **10** | Hard | Whole party needs a solid strategy, everyone dedicating actions every round |

Give the stat **multiple effects at different thresholds**: immediate benefit (3), longer-term game benefit (6), plot advancement (10).

## Variations

### Negative Values
The stat can go below zero for opposite effects. Crowd's Favor at -3 = *bane* effect; at -6 = 10% price increase; at -10 = banned from the arena.

### Multiple Whatever Stats
Several stats tracking different, non-mutually-exclusive things. Used in Angry's social interaction system: an NPC has conflicting reasons to help/hinder; when help > hinder, they give in.

### Nested/Linked Stats
One stat's thresholds feed into another. Crowd's Favor at 6 → Party's Reputation +1. Reputation thresholds then gate bigger rewards.

### Inertia
The stat changes on its own if no one interacts with it. Crowd's Favor slides toward 0 each round if nothing happens (flagging interest). Angry's **Impatience** stat in social encounters: ticks up over time, and when it hits a threshold, the NPC stops listening entirely. Players can take actions to reduce it (bribes, flattery, apologies).

### Threshold Behavior
- **Standard:** Effect active while stat exceeds threshold, removed when it drops below
- **One-time trigger:** Once the stat hits the threshold, the effect is permanent even if the stat drops later
- **Conditional persistence:** Effect triggers at one threshold but is revoked if the stat drops past a different (lower) threshold

## What NOT to Do

- Don't add complex dice mechanics (no "damage dice" for stat changes)
- Don't create lists of specific actions players can take to influence it
- Don't explain the system to the players — describe the world, let them figure out how to interact with it
- Don't overcomplicate the basic mechanic — the simplicity IS the power

## Why Not Build a Full Subsystem Instead?

A dedicated gladiatorial combat subsystem with crowd excitement trackers, support scores, special actions, and handouts would:
- Take a full session to learn (and players won't read the handout)
- Feel like a board game, not exciting combat
- Get used once and forgotten
- Steal time from the stuff that actually makes games fun (cool opponents, unique arenas, memorable moments)

The Whatever Stat replaces all of that with one page of notes and the GM's judgment.

## Lightning Round: Example Uses

- **Haunted castle** — Haunting Level decreases as party solves mysteries; increases when they screw up (angry spirits attack)
- **Town invasion** — Balance of Power shifts as party bolsters defenses or handles emergencies
- **Mass combat** — Tide of Battle swings as party takes strategic locations or kills champions
- **Spiked ceiling trap** — Ceiling Descent tracks doom while party fights scorpions, jams gears, picks locks
- **Rescue mission** — Sacrifice Progress tracks how close the victim is to the lava pit
- **Zombie siege** — Barricade Strength tracks how well the cottage is fortified against the undead
- **Damaged airship** — Ship Stability tracks whether the crew can keep it flying
- **Stealth infiltration** — Base Security tracks how alert/weakened the garrison is
- **Political campaign** — Five faction Influence stats interact throughout the campaign
