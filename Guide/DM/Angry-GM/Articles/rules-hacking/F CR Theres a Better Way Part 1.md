# F CR, There's a Better Way (Part 1)

**Source:** [The Angry GM – F$&% CR, There's a Better Way (Part 1)](https://theangrygm.com/f-cr-theres-a-better-way-part-1/)
**Date:** October 25, 2019
**Series:** Rules Hacking / Encounter & Monster Building

---

## The Problem with Encounter Building in 5E

D&D 5E has two core systems for building combat encounters:

1. **Encounter Building** (DMG 81) — how many monsters of what CR for a given party
2. **Custom Monster Creation** (DMG 273) — how to stat up your own monsters and determine their CR

Both systems are a mess. They demand too much precision for results that are inherently fuzzy. The encounter math pretends to be exact when it absolutely isn't.

## Monster Design Should Reflect Group Size

Not all monsters are designed the same way or intended to be used the same way:

- **Dragons/Beholders** — show up alone, have complex stat blocks with multiple attacks, mobility options, legendary actions. Designed to hold their own solo.
- **Goblins/Kobolds** — show up in groups, have simple stat blocks. Designed to be run in numbers. Features like Pack Tactics and Martial Advantage assume allies are present.

The number of creatures in an encounter is a **property of the creature itself**, not just a GM choice. The encounter system should reflect this.

## The Statistical Treadmill Problem

Monster HP scales with PC damage output, and monster damage scales with PC HP. In theory, combats last roughly the same number of rounds and drain roughly the same percentage of resources regardless of level. This is the "statistical treadmill."

Problems with the treadmill:

- **Players never feel more powerful** — 15th-level fights feel the same as 5th-level fights, just with bigger numbers
- **Tension doesn't rise** — the game never feels like difficulty is increasing
- **Monsters have narrow windows** — each creature is only useful for a couple of levels before it's outclassed
- **No power fantasy** — players never get to stomp creatures that used to challenge them

## The Fuzziness of Difficulty Labels

The Easy/Medium/Hard/Deadly labels are mostly useless:

- The XP thresholds rarely align with actual monster XP values
- One extra goblin doesn't really shift an encounter from "easy" to "hard"
- Difficulty is affected by too many factors beyond raw stats (party composition, magic items, tactics, terrain)
- Most GMs just check if an encounter falls "somewhere between easy and hard" and call it good
- The real difficulty curve comes from **attrition** — every fight is harder than the last because resources deplete

## What Angry Wants Instead

A simpler system that:

- Doesn't require a bunch of math
- Embraces fuzziness instead of pretending to be precise
- Recognizes that different monsters need different group sizes
- Builds a natural difficulty wobble into the game (some levels feel hard, some feel easy)
- Lets GMs quickly assemble encounters

The actual system is in Part 2 — this article lays out the problems and design goals.

## Key Takeaways

- CR is based on just two real statistics: **HP** (how long the monster survives) and **damage output** (how much it hurts before dying). AC and attack bonus just modify the effective values of those two.
- The encounter building rules work best when you **don't sweat them too much** — imprecision within a reasonable range is fine.
- 4E was on the right track with its "one monster per PC" baseline and its solo/elite/minion classifications, but it still tried to be too precise.
- The best encounters involve **multiple creatures** in roughly equal numbers to the party. Solo boss fights are iconic set pieces, but the workaday encounters need groups.
