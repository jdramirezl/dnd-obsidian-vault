## Key Advice

Game balance in TTRPGs is not a single point — it's a broad, fuzzy cloud, and that fuzziness is a feature, not a bug. There are three distinct types of balance in D&D that most people (including game designers) conflate: Spotlight Balance, Statistical Balance, and Scenario Balance. Understanding the difference lets you stop worrying about perfect balance and start designing better experiences.

## Summary

Angry argues that "game balance" is one of the most misused and least understood concepts in RPGs. He defines three distinct types:

1. **Spotlight Balance** — Ensures every player feels like an equal contributor. In D&D 5e, combat has strong Spotlight Balance (all classes are roughly viable), but exploration and social interaction have almost none. 4e pushed Spotlight Balance so tight that classes felt samey — proving too much balance kills differentiation. The goal isn't equal participation in every scene, but that everyone gets time to shine over the course of the game.

2. **Statistical Balance** — The math that lets a DM predict outcomes. It sets success probabilities for individual actions based on assumptions about character ability and player skill. It's built on top of Spotlight Balance (which is already fuzzy), so it's inherently fuzzy too. D&D designers aren't transparent about their assumptions (e.g., ~65% base success rate, ±20% for player choices). The fuzziness is what makes the game exciting — neither pure strategy nor pure luck.

3. **Scenario Balance** — The secret third type that nobody talks about. It governs the structure of entire adventures: how many encounters per day, what mix of difficulties, how resources deplete over time. D&D tries to bake this into hard rules ("6-8 encounters per adventuring day") but it should be a GM-controlled knob. Understanding Scenario Balance lets you break the "adventuring day" rules intentionally and design better-paced adventures.

The balance cloud is also deliberately skewed in the players' favor — RPGs aren't competitive games, so fairness means "players usually win," not "50/50 odds."

## Actionable Steps

1. **Stop chasing perfect balance**: Accept that balance is a fuzzy cloud, not a point. A wobble in either direction is fine and makes the game more fun.
2. **Distinguish the three types when evaluating homebrew**: When designing a new spell or class, ask: Does this break Spotlight Balance (one player dominates)? Statistical Balance (the math is off)? Or Scenario Balance (it warps adventure structure)?
3. **Ensure Spotlight Balance over time, not per scene**: It's okay for the rogue to shine in stealth scenes and the cleric against undead. Just make sure no one is *consistently* marginalized across sessions.
4. **Don't over-tighten Spotlight Balance**: If every class feels the same, you've gone too far. Differentiation is part of the fun.
5. **Recognize Statistical Balance is fuzzy by design**: The encounter math gives you a ballpark, not a guarantee. Adjust on the fly based on how your specific players handle things.
6. **Take control of Scenario Balance**: The "6-8 encounters per adventuring day" is a guideline, not a law. Understand the resource-depletion model behind it, then adjust encounter count and difficulty mix to create the pacing you want.
7. **Remember the game favors the players**: RPGs aren't competitive. The balance cloud should be skewed toward player success. Even bad choices should usually lead to survival.
8. **Fix balance problems in real time**: If something is broken, you're the GM — adjust on the fly or retcon. Balance mistakes aren't catastrophic in a live game.

> [!tip] The Core Insight
> There are three types of game balance — Spotlight (player contribution equality), Statistical (action-resolution math), and Scenario (adventure-level structure). Most GMs only think about Statistical Balance. The one that actually has the biggest impact on fun is Scenario Balance — and it's the one the designers give you the least control over. Take that control back.
