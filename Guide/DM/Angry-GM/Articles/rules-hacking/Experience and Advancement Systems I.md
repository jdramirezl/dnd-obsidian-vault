# Experience and Advancement Systems I

**Source:** [True Mechanical Managery: Experience and Advancement Systems I – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/true-mechanical-managery-experience-and-advancement-systems/)
**Date:** September 25, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery — True Mechanical Managery Module

---

## Summary

This is the theoretical foundation for making Experience and Advancement policy decisions. Before deciding *when* to award XP or *whether* to use milestone leveling, you need to understand what Experience and Advancement Systems actually do for the gameplay experience. They serve four major purposes: providing extrinsic progress/accomplishment, structuring gameplay loops, maintaining game balance, and controlling complexity delivery. Understanding these purposes is what separates True Campaign Managers from people who just pick whatever's easiest.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Extrinsic Growth, Progress, and Accomplishment
- The **primary purpose** of Experience and Advancement Systems.
- Players need both intrinsic accomplishment (the feeling of overcoming challenges) and extrinsic rewards (measurable, tangible markers of progress).
- Good systems provide **two** extrinsic elements: rewards (looking backward at what you've done) and progress (looking forward at what's coming).
- The system should create a strong connection between in-game activities and advancement. Diluting that connection weakens the sense of progress.
- Really good systems tie advancement specifically to **Core Gameplay Activities** — e.g., "overcoming encounters" in modern D&D, "returning treasure to civilization" in older D&D.

### 2. Gameplay Loops and Campaign Structure
- Experience and Advancement Systems represent a **cycle**: earn XP by playing → stop playing to advance → resume playing with new capabilities.
- This cycle works best when **synchronized with the campaign's Core Gameplay Loop**.
- Example: Megadungeon campaign with a Delve-Return-Resupply loop. Sync it by making XP earnable only in the dungeon and advancement only possible in town.
- When the advancement loop clashes with the campaign structure, pacing suffers. When they harmonize, the game feels tight and purposeful.

### 3. Game Balance (It's Not What You Think)
- Game Balance is about how **players' skills and abilities** match up with challenges — not just stat comparisons.
- Experience and Advancement Systems come **first**; then the game's other math is balanced against them. Not the other way around.
- The designers found the right amount of player skill to swing outcomes, then built encounter design systems to maintain that feel at every level.
- Calling advancement a "treadmill" misunderstands the design. The point is maintaining a fun challenge level as characters grow.
- Practical implications: What happens if party members advance at different rates? Will leveling up before a boss fight wreck the encounter? What level should replacement characters start at?

#### Trust the Designers
- Unless you want to rewrite the whole game, trust that the designers built a system that provides the experience they promised.
- If you can't trust a game's designers, run a different game. If the game doesn't promise the experience you want, run a different game.
- Problems aren't problems until your players are complaining.

### 4. Complexity Delivery
- Well-designed systems drip-feed complexity over time. Characters gain new *kinds* of abilities as they advance, not just bigger numbers.
- Monsters at higher levels have more options and different ability types, not just higher stats.
- Your advancement implementation choices affect how quickly players must absorb new mechanics.
- This matters for: mid-adventure level-ups, boss fight timing, and what level replacement/new characters start at.

---

## Actionable Takeaways

- Before making any policy decisions, understand what your system's Experience and Advancement System is actually *for*.
- Evaluate whether your planned implementation preserves the connection between gameplay activities and character growth.
- Look at your campaign's structure and identify where the advancement cycle fits naturally.
- Recognize that balance is designed around the advancement system — changing advancement has ripple effects.
- Trust the system's math unless you have a specific, identified reason not to.
- Consider complexity delivery when deciding things like mid-session level-ups or starting levels for new characters.
