# Supplement Bulls: The Second Best Way to Make Characters (Curated Character Generation)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/supplement-bulls-ccg/)
**Date:** April 13, 2022

---

Curated Character Generation (CCG) is pretty much the second-best way to handle character generation that exists. Even if you're not running a Simple Homebrew Campaign. Even if you're not running D&D.

## Key Features of CCG

1. The GM provides a list of available character options before play
2. Each player comes up with **two different character ideas** by selecting from the listed options
3. The GM approves **one** of each player's two character ideas for play
4. The GM **withholds all information** about character generation including approved options until it is time to generate characters
5. The GM oversees character generation
6. Character generation is **purely mechanical** — filling out character sheets and nothing more
7. Players are **not given the opportunity** to develop their characters further before the first play session

The key is to **minimize the work players put into their characters before session one** — especially backstory, personality, and character development. And to **minimize the players' investment in and attachment to their characters.**

If you do so, you're much more likely to end up with engaged, invested players who interact with the world and end up really happy with their characters. In the long run.

## Better Roleplay Through Detachment

### The Double Standard

Every good GM knows: start small, keep it simple, build the minimum, don't plan too far ahead, leave blanks, leave room to grow. You'll end up with a better, deeper game you're more passionately connected to, with freedom to react.

**So why do you tell the players to do the opposite?** Think about how obviously messed up that is. In business, we call this a *smell test*.

### It's Called Interaction for a Reason

Roleplaying is an **interactive process**. You react to a situation, someone else reacts to you, you react to them. In doing so, you explore consequences and see things from different perspectives.

Every time you make a decision in an RPG, the situation changes. The *start small and build* advice works because it keeps the game reactive — the world and story react to players' choices, evolving based on the GM's changing perspectives and new ideas.

**But this isn't a one-way street.** When you make a choice and experience consequences, you change in response. You learn, grow, evolve. The world affects you as you affect it. It's equally important for players to leave room for change.

### Toxic Creations

If you start with the idea that your job is to build the character you want to play and then play that character, you're building a wall between your character and the world. On an unconscious level, the more invested you are in the character you've built, the more you'll insulate that character from change — because if the character changes, it might not be the character you want anymore.

Worse: if the world can't act on you, you don't have to care about the world. People don't care much about things that can't affect them.

The *create-and-portray* mindset leads to **selfish roleplaying** — not because players are inherently selfish, but because it establishes a one-way dynamic. The PC acts on the world, not vice versa. The game becomes about action, not interaction.

### Locked in Stasis

Playing the character you *want to play* and insulating it from change drastically increases the chance you'll **get bored**. Static characters — characters who undergo no substantial growth or change — are a no-no in writing because:

- The audience gets bored with them
- The audience stops caring about them
- They eventually come off as damaged or inhuman — humans change continuously

**The early MCU heroes were wonderfully dynamic.** Tony Stark grew past his flaws, suffered and overcame PTSD, his values changed. Captain America's idealism was challenged until he adopted a more practical viewpoint. By Civil War, both were on different sides from each other *and* from their past selves. That kept them interesting for a decade.

**The players who put the most work into character building are also the ones who change characters the most.**

## Teaching Players Not to Play Wrong

*Start small and build* is good for GMs and players. But people are stupid — the stuff that leads to long, satisfying experiences is almost never obvious, intuitive, or easy. Making a perfect character and playing it out feels exciting and easy compared to starting with a vague pile of mechanics and waiting for it to assert a character of its own. No amount of explanation will convince people otherwise.

### Tricksy Game Designers

Game designers know players suck at making good play choices. "Players will optimize the fun out of everything if you let them." Players almost always do whatever's easiest and wins quickest — even if it's not fun. Because human brains make almost all decisions without thought (fast monkey brain), then rationalize after the fact (slow rational brain).

Half of game design is about **tricking people into not ruining their own good time.**

### You Are the Console

You — the GM — are acting on behalf of the game designer. You're basically a PlayStation: rendering the world, parsing player inputs, determining what happens. The difference is you can adjust the game on the fly.

It's hard to do what a game designer has to do when players are making sad puppy-dog eyes asking to play a goliath. That's why I don't care about my players — I care about running great games. Which is why my players love my games and never invite me to their birthday parties.

## The Tricks Behind CCG

### "FOOLED YOU!" — The Anchoring Trick

Remember when I said the best number of races, classes, and backgrounds was four, eight, and eight? **I was messing with you.** Sort of.

There's no actual precise best number. I told you to eliminate at least one thing from each list because the best way to run a game is by carefully curating options. But that's hard — it involves taking things away from players.

So I used **anchoring** — the same trick that gets you to buy too much soup. "Limit 10 Cans" on a sale sign makes you buy 4–5 cans instead of the 1–2 you needed, because your monkey brain grabs the first number it sees and your rational brain spends all its energy arguing it down.

By saying "four, eight, and eight," I probably got you to actually think about cutting more than one thing. And even if you wanted to argue against cutting options, you were too distracted arguing why those specific numbers were wrong.

### The List of Character Options

Handing players a curated list:
- **Implies there's a reason** — players assume you had a purpose, making them less likely to ask for exceptions
- **Makes it easier for you to say no** — and that effect extends past character generation
- **Establishes you as THE source** — trumping the books from the first word
- **Implies the world is unique** — gets players excited, curious, attentive, more likely to interact

### Secretly Submit Two Concepts for Approval

- **Splits attention** — harder to get too invested in either character
- **Blocks collaboration** — harder to coordinate "optimal" party composition
- **Implies an "implied cheater" stigma** — suggests the only important stuff happens at the table
- **Wrecks the idea of "right" party composition** — no need to cover all roles
- **Establishes character creation as collaborative** — GM has veto power, so it's a player-GM collaboration, not pure player control

### Withholding Rules and Approval

- **Prevents forward work** — reinforces collaborative nature
- **Trains players to think on their feet** — the whole game is basically a string of blindsides
- **Suggests no best answers** — only good-enough answers that lead somewhere playable
- **Reinforces that what's on the sheet isn't that important**

### Rushing Through Mechanical Generation

Telling players to just fill out their sheets says in clear psychological terms: **this isn't the game, it's the prep work.** The game doesn't start until the first session. This math and paperwork are in the way.

And the real benefit: character generation sessions suck, and you just want to get through them as quickly as possible.
