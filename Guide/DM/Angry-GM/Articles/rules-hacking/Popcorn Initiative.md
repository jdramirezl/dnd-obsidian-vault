# Popcorn Initiative

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Popcorn Initiative](https://theangrygm.com/popcorn-initiative-a-great-way-to-adjust-dd-and-pathfinder-initiative-with-a-stupid-name/)
**Date:** September 13, 2013
**Series:** Hacking Rules / Initiative

---

## How It Works

1. **Start of combat:** Everyone rolls initiative normally. Highest roll goes first.
2. **After each turn:** The creature that just acted **chooses who goes next** (any PC, NPC, monster, or monster group).
3. **Everyone gets one turn per round.** Track with index cards + poker chips (chip = already acted).
4. **End of round:** The last creature to act chooses who **starts the next round** — including themselves.

## Waiting and Readying

### Readying
Works as normal — declare an action and trigger. Readied action persists until your next turn in initiative. If unused by then, it's lost.

### Waiting
You decline your turn and pass initiative to someone else. You must be nominated again to act. Your card turns sideways to show you're waiting.

**Key restriction:** If it's your turn and the only remaining creatures are all waiting, you **cannot wait**. You can take no action, but you can't wait. This prevents standoffs and gaming the end-of-round advantage.

## Handling Durations

Add an **"end of round" phase** after the last creature acts but before the next round begins. Timed effects expire here after exhausting their full duration.

**Tracking method:** Use a d10 set to the number of rounds remaining. Count down at end of round. Remove the die when it's already on 0 and a round ends (effects last through the "0th round" then expire).

## Why Use It

- **Better pacing and engagement.** No initiative tracker to watch — players stay attentive because they never know when their turn is coming.
- **Encourages teamwork.** Players talk to each other (often in character) at the end of every turn. "Bob, can you handle that ogre?" fosters cooperation.
- **Easier for the GM.** No writing down initiative order. Combat flows person to person.
- **Enables complex tactics.** Players can set up combos and follow up on assists immediately.
- **Feels more like a battle.** Players watch for openings and seize moments rather than planning in a vacuum.

## Why Not Use It

- **Favors PCs.** A party of 5 against 1 monster can theoretically get 8–9 turns before the monster goes twice. Players rarely exploit this fully, but it's possible.
- **Abstraction/metagaming concerns.** Players choosing turn order is inherently meta. If that bothers you, this system isn't for you.

## Tips for Running It

1. **Don't adjust difficulty immediately.** Play with it for a few weeks first. Build more encounters into the adventuring day instead.
2. **Game the system as GM.** Nominate the worst player to go next. If a powerful foe can take two turns in a row, do it. Play to win — it balances the PC advantage.
3. **Encourage in-character nominations.** Keep it to one sentence. "Soveliss, you've got an opening — take it!"
4. **Narrate nominations.** "While Jozan and Lidda defend against the grell's tentacles, Soveliss has an opening to act."
5. **Build encounter variety.** Use more different monster types to give enemies more initiative slots. Split 6 identical goblins into 3 skirmishers + 3 archers.
6. **Push players to act fast.** If they hesitate, remind them they can wait — but risk losing control. Zero planning time creates tension.
7. **Never let monsters wait.** Just go. Waiting slows things down and exists only as a safety valve for unprepared players.

## Experimental Extensions

### Breaking Up Monster Groups
Instead of one initiative slot for "Goblins" where all 5 act at once, each nomination of "Goblins" lets **one** goblin act. The goblins must be nominated 5 times total. Actions can be distributed however the GM wants (one goblin could theoretically take all 5 actions). This makes things more dynamic and gives the GM more tactical options.

**Tracking:** Place one card per group. Put chips equal to the number of creatures. Remove chips as they act.

### Dangerous Foes (Solo/Elite Monsters)
Upgrade a single monster by increasing CR by 2–4:

| CR Increase | HP Multiplier | Extra Actions |
|---|---|---|
| +2 | 2× | 1 extra |
| +3 | 3× | 2 extra |
| +4 | 4× | 3 extra |

Each extra action works like an additional initiative slot (extra chip on the card). This replaces the need for complex solo/elite stat blocks — one standard monster becomes functionally equivalent to 2–4 monsters for system math purposes.
