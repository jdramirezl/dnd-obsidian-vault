## Key Advice

Players don't suck at tracking inventory — they suck at tracking *anything*. The problem is a combination of cultural disinclination toward record-keeping, terrible tools (paper sheets, PDFs, and apps all suck in different ways), and zero positive incentive (tracking inventory only ever tells you what you *can't* do). The solution is a two-pronged approach: build the best possible tool that minimizes player effort, then enforce compliance with your GM authority.

## Summary

Angry (Scott Rehm) diagnoses why inventory tracking is universally terrible at TTRPG tables. The problem isn't specific to inventory — players struggle to track HP, XP, spell slots, and everything else. The trend is worsening as modern culture moves away from note-taking. Three forces combine to make inventory the worst case:

1. **People hate tracking on principle** — it's math and paperwork, and the cultural trend is toward less record-keeping, not more.
2. **Every tracking tool sucks** — paper sheets get erased through, printed PDFs can't be updated, digital apps are tedious to navigate. None are dynamic or easy to change.
3. **There's no positive incentive** — tracking inventory only reveals penalties (encumbrance, missing items, depleted resources). Players will happily *add* cool items to their sheet but won't track weights, locations, or consumption.

His solution:
- **Build a streamlined tool**: He created a shared Excel workbook with auto-calculations for encumbrance, speed penalties, worn vs. carried states, and printable single-page outputs. Players only input names, quantities, and weights.
- **Enforce compliance**: Periodically remind players between sessions to update. Check sheets before sessions. If something isn't listed, it doesn't exist. Use your "parent voice."
- **Don't track during gameplay**: Set encumbrance at session start. Only adjust mid-session for major changes. This removes the biggest friction point.
- **Slot-based systems don't solve the real problem**: Swapping "pounds" for "slots" changes what's tracked but not the fundamental issues of *when* it's tracked, *how painful* it is, and *what incentive* exists.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Ask yourself why inventory matters in your game**: Define the minimum you need from tracking. Don't track more than necessary.
2. **Build (or find) an automated tool**: Create a shared spreadsheet or use a VTT that auto-calculates encumbrance, speed, and penalties. Players should only need to enter item names, quantities, and weights.
3. **Make the tool accessible**: Put it in a shared cloud folder so all players can access and update it between sessions.
4. **Set encumbrance at session start**: Don't pause gameplay for inventory math. Lock in encumbrance levels at the beginning and only adjust for major in-game events.
5. **Enforce the "if it's not written, you don't have it" rule**: Be consistent. If a player tries to use something not on their sheet, it's not on their person.
6. **Review inventories between sessions**: Spend 15 minutes before each session checking player sheets. Print copies for your reference.
7. **Remind players between sessions**: Send a message asking them to update their inventories. Make it a routine expectation.
8. **Don't use arbitrary bonuses as incentive**: There's no good positive reward for tracking inventory. The incentive is "not getting called out by the GM." Accept that and enforce it.
9. **Minimize what players must input**: Auto-calculate everything possible. The less manual work, the more compliance you'll get.

> [!tip] The Core Insight
> The inventory problem isn't about finding a better encumbrance system — slot-based, weight-based, or otherwise. It's about reducing the pain of tracking to the absolute minimum and then enforcing compliance. Build the best tool you can, demand only the bare minimum input from players, handle the math automatically, and don't make anyone track anything during gameplay. Then use your GM authority to make it stick.
