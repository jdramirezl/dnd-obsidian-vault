# I Hate Ability Scores (In D&D 5E)

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Fanservice BS: I Hate Ability Scores (In D&D 5E)](https://theangrygm.com/i-hate-ability-scores/)
**Date:** September 27, 2017
**Series:** Rules Hacking / Core Mechanics

---

## There Are No Skill Checks in D&D 5E

This is not a semantic argument — it matters mechanically. D&D 5E has exactly **four types of die rolls**:

1. **Ability Checks** — the universal action resolution mechanic
2. **Saving Throws** — reactive resistance to incoming effects
3. **Attack Rolls** — attempts to overcome AC and deal damage
4. (Damage rolls — irrelevant to this discussion)

Skills are **not** their own category. You make a Strength (Athletics) check, not an "Athletics check." The ability score is primary; the skill is a bonus applied when proficiency is relevant.

This distinction matters because game features draw **bright lines** between these categories. Barbarian Rage gives advantage on Strength checks and Strength saving throws, but not attack rolls. Bardic Inspiration applies to "one ability check, attack roll, or saving throw." The categories exist so the rules can interact cleanly.

## The Power of the Ability Check

The ability check is the universal action resolution mechanic. For **any** action that might succeed or fail (that isn't an attack or save), the GM just needs to determine which ability score governs it. That's it. Any action is resolvable without specialized rules.

This was a revolution from pre-3E D&D, where every type of action had its own bespoke mechanic — THAC0 for attacks, d6 for secret doors, percentile for bend bars, 2d6 for reactions, etc. Ability scores only gave bonuses to their specific hard-wired mechanics, so they were irrelevant outside those narrow uses.

Now, every player knows: if my character is strong, I'm likely to succeed at things strong people do. This lets players make **logical decisions** based on their character's strengths and weaknesses.

## What's Wrong with the Six Ability Scores

The six ability scores were **not designed** to be the first step in action resolution. They were invented for a different purpose (class prerequisites, specific mechanical bonuses) and got hammered into the universal resolution role when 3E came along. The result: **fuzzy lines** between scores.

### The Bright Lines Problem

Good action resolution stats need **bright lines** — it should be obvious and intuitive which stat governs any given action. Consider a hypothetical system with just three stats: Physical, Mental, Social. The divisions are instantly clear.

D&D's six scores don't have this:

- **Intelligence vs. Wisdom** — the line has always been fuzzy. Entire sidebars have been devoted to explaining the difference. It only *seems* intuitive because we're used to it.
- **Dexterity** — covers agility, reflexes, balance, AND fine motor skills/manipulation (lockpicking, sleight of hand). These are very different things that got lumped together when thief skills needed a home.
- **Wisdom** — defined as "attunement, perception, intuition" but also governs Survival, Medicine, Animal Handling, and willpower saves (even though Charisma is supposed to cover will and confidence).

The skills underneath each ability score have become **part of the definition** of that ability score, papering over the gaps. We look at the skill list to understand what an ability covers, rather than the ability's actual definition.

### The Skill List as Button Menu

Because the ability score definitions are fuzzy, GMs and players default to looking at the **skill list first** instead of the ability scores. This trains players to treat skills as a menu of available actions — "I roll Perception," "I roll Stealth" — rather than describing what they want to do and letting the GM determine the appropriate check.

This is backwards. The intended flow is:
1. Player describes an action
2. GM determines which ability score governs it
3. GM determines if a skill proficiency applies as a bonus

But in practice: Player picks a skill → GM accepts it → the ability score is an afterthought.

## Why This Matters (and Why It Won't Change)

### The Sacred Cow Problem

The six ability scores are as iconic to D&D as fighters, wizards, and hit points. They will **never change**. Even though rejiggering them would be a relatively small mechanical change compared to the 2E→3E revolution, it would be perceived as heresy.

This conservatism has infected the entire RPG industry — even the fan-made Pokemon Tabletop RPG uses the same six D&D ability scores, despite them making no sense for Pokemon trainers.

### The Cascade Effect

Because 5E looks like a spit-polished 3E, nobody reads the books carefully. Players learn from GMs, GMs learn from older GMs, and older GMs skim. The subtle but important change — that ability checks are the core mechanic, not skill checks — gets lost. If the ability scores had visibly changed, people would actually read the rules.

## The Ideal System (That D&D Will Never Have)

If designing from scratch, ability scores should:

- Have **bright lines** between them — easy and intuitive to categorize any action
- Be difficult to fit any action into **more than one** score
- Not require a skill list to clarify their definitions
- Be designed specifically for their role as the first step in action resolution

Examples of better approaches:
- **Everway** — 4 stats based on elements, determined by two binary questions (physical/mental × inward/outward)
- **World of Darkness** — 9 attributes in a 3×3 grid (Physical/Mental/Social × Power/Finesse/Resistance)

## Key Takeaways

- D&D 5E has ability checks, not skill checks — this is mechanically important and widely misunderstood
- The ability check is a powerful universal mechanic that lets GMs resolve any action with just the six ability score definitions
- The six scores weren't designed for this role, creating fuzzy boundaries that make adjudication harder than it needs to be
- Skills have become a crutch that both clarifies the fuzzy ability definitions and limits player creativity by acting as a "menu of actions"
- This is a toothache, not a broken leg — the system works, it's just annoying once you see it
