# Does Ammunition Really Matter?

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Does Ammunition Really Matter Matter Matter?](https://theangrygm.com/does-ammunition-matter/)
**Date:** August 28, 2019
**Topic:** Why and how to track ammunition — and why most GMs do it wrong

---

## Core Idea

Ranged combat is flatly superior to melee combat in D&D 5E. Ammunition tracking is the intended cost that offsets this advantage. Most GMs either ignore it entirely or implement it inconsistently, both of which break the game's balance. There's a clean, low-bookkeeping way to do it right.

## Why Ranged Combat Is Superior

### Melee Disadvantages
- Must be adjacent to the target — always in harm's way
- Zones of control (opportunity attacks) make switching targets costly
- Closing distance against ranged enemies means eating free attacks
- Requires **Strength** for accuracy/damage, but **Dexterity** and **Constitution** for defense — three stats competing

### Ranged Advantages
- Can target anyone on the battlefield with line of sight
- Not in harm's way (melee enemies are far more common than ranged ones)
- **Dexterity** governs attack, damage, AC, initiative, AND Dex saves — one stat does everything
- Constitution is less critical because you're not getting hit as often

### Cantrips Are Even Worse
- Infinite ammo, zero cost, zero downsides
- Spellcasting with cantrips alone is the best general combat strategy in D&D

## The Common Mistakes

### The Inconsistency Problem
Most GMs evolve an ad-hoc system through table rulings:
- **Javelin thrower** — tracks each javelin, rolls to recover after combat, searches for lost ones
- **Archer** — gradually stops tracking because it's tedious and the quiver never runs out

This is **unfair**: one player is held to strict standards while another gets a free pass. And the player with the *superior* strategy (ranged) is the one getting the pass.

### The Useless Die Roll Problem
Rolling to recover thrown weapons after combat sounds right, but ask yourself:
- Would you *really* permanently destroy the barbarian's javelins? (No.)
- Does the search time cost the party anything? (No — no ticking clock, no ambush risk.)
- The die roll wastes **real player time** for zero game impact.

## The Quantum Quiver System

Divide ranged attacks into two categories:

### Recoverable Ammo (Thrown Weapons)
Javelins, spears, throwing axes, daggers — expensive, durable, carried in small quantities.
- **Track each one.** Mark it off when thrown.
- **Always recovered** after combat (they're big, durable, and don't go far).
- The cost is **running out mid-fight** and having to switch strategies or retrieve them.

### Breakable Ammo (Arrows, Bolts, Sling Stones, Darts, Shurikens)
Cheap, small, carried in large quantities (20 in a quiver).
- **A quiver always has one more piece of ammo** unless the last one is lost.
- **Mark off ammo only on a miss** (arrow flew off or splintered) **or a critical hit** (lodged in bone, snapped on impact).
- Normal hits? Don't mark anything — the arrow is recoverable.
- **After combat:** no recovery rolls needed. You already only marked the lost ones.
- **During downtime:** the quiver is empty. Buy 20 new arrows. That's the cost of being an archer.

### Why This Works
- **Minimal bookkeeping** — only mark on misses and crits
- **No useless recovery rolls** — the abstraction handles it
- **Ranged combat has a real cost** — periodic gold expenditure + occasional risk of running dry
- **Bad luck or long adventuring days** can drain the quiver, forcing a strategy change
- **Interesting decisions emerge** — conserve ammo? Switch to melee? Risk the last arrow?

## The Deeper Lesson

GMs accumulate **ad-hoc rulings** that individually seem reasonable but collectively create inconsistent, unfair, or pointless systems. The fix:
1. **Explain your systems out loud** to someone — inconsistencies become obvious
2. **Ask "what does this actually change at the table?"** for every proposed rule
3. **Beat the hell out of your proposed fix** before implementing it — make sure it actually does something

A rule change that doesn't change what happens at the table is not a fix. It's just complexity for nothing.
