# Crafting Herbcraft (Part 2): Practical Herbology

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/crafting-herbcraft-part-2/)
**Date:** September 30, 2017
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

This is the last time I talk about herbcraft. There is a latest revision of the herbcraft rules. I clarified a few things, filled in holes people noticed, cleaned up some things, and made a few minor tweaks. [Download the PDF](https://theangrygm.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/Herbalism-for-DD.pdf), extend them, use them, and have fun.

Some of that little stuff exists solely because it seemed like a fun thing to do. Why are there three different qualities even though no other die roll in D&D offers variable qualities? Because it seemed fun and it isn't terribly complicated and crafting is a completely different thing than anything else and that seemed like a fun way to say, "crafting isn't doing anything else because it's crafting."

Game design is a lot like hanging wallpaper. You spend a lot of time slogging in glue and scraping bubbles out. But you also get to pick out nice wallpaper with neat patterns in your favorite colors. And that's the fun part.

## The Fight Between Fluff and Crunch

Crunch: the numbers and mechanics that make the game work as a game system. Fluff: the story and world details that tell you what is going on in the imaginary world. Everything in the game has to serve both. A system has to provide a fun, fair, and balanced gameplay experience. A system also has to represent a part of the world that makes sense and seems like it could exist. But you can't always do both. There will always come a point where you have to add some abstract limit for the good of the game or screw the rules for the good of the story and world.

Good design involves flitting back and forth between game and story so that the two elements feed into each other without breaking each other. In the end, you WILL have to compromise between the fluff and the crunch. So, it's better to start somewhere in the middle.

## The Mechanical Tree

A game system is like a tree. It is rooted in some deep, core system. From the trunk, branches grow representing additional rules details that provide variety. Finally, growing off the branches are leaves – all of the tiny little details that vary from character to character.

What amateur game designers forget is that it's the leaves that people see the most. The leaves are bright and colorful and obvious. People who invent new classes spend so much time developing "new and interesting" deep core tree-trunk type mechanics for no good reason because those aren't the things that make the class interesting.

You never ever want to reinvent anything you don't have to when hacking a system. You want to draw, as much as possible, on the systems that already exist. First, it's less work. Second, you don't have to teach people extra stuff. Third, RPGs are complicated and adding a completely new thing can throw sand in a very complex set of gears.

## The Trunk of My Frankenstein Tree

Herbcraft is an at-the-table system. That means the tree should be rooted in the action resolution system. When a player decides to herbcraft, they roll a d20, add an ability modifier, possibly add a proficiency bonus, compare the result to a certain DC, and either succeed or fail.

Conceptually, the system is this: there's a list of effects to choose from. The herbalist chooses an effect, rolls an action check, and, if they succeed, they get the effect. Lots of things in D&D already work that way. Spellcasting, ki powers, fighter maneuvers, and so on.

The list of effects are the pretty little details that sell the system. The system is also easy to extend. All anyone has to do to add more fun is to add more effects to the list.

Does this mechanical framework actually suit herbcraft as a concept? Sure. Rolling a check represents crafting the item. The idea of lists of prescribed effects represents the idea that herbalists learn recipes for specific medicines.

## The Failure Point

Whenever you're dealing with an action resolution system and you only have one die roll to work with, it's important to think about what, conceptually, that roll represents. Finding the failure point – the point of highest tension in the action – lets you define the make-or-break moment.

For herbcraft, the failure point is at the ingredient-gathering stage. The action is about tromping through the woods, foraging and gathering, spotting the right ingredients, and intuiting where you might find what you want. Those activities align with wilderness survival and foraging. And those things are Wisdom-based.

What does keying the system off of Wisdom actually mean? It means that herbcraft is probably going to be one of those skills sought by characters who already have an above average Wisdom score. Class-wise, that means it'll be more common among clerics and druids. That makes sense. Clerics are often healers. And druids live off the natural world.

## Branching Out

We have a solid trunk and root structure. A character chooses a specific effect from a list, rolls a Wisdom (Herbalism Kit) check to gather the ingredients, and, if they are successful, they get the effect.

Fluff-wise, herbcraft is about gathering natural ingredients from the wilderness. That's not something you can do just anywhere. You can't herbcraft in the heart of the city or in an ancient crypt. You have to herbcraft where things grow.

Something else that makes herbcraft unique: the actual effect of the action is delayed. You take the action now, but you can bank the effect and use it later. You're taking an effect and putting it into a pill. That means it's perfect for right before the adventure or while resting in the middle of the adventure.

## Balancing in the Branches

Every mechanic can be abused or exploited. At the design stage, you want to focus on first-order abuses. A first-order abuse is what happens when a player – alone, in a vacuum – notices they can exploit the system to get a bigger effect than what you intended.

**Intended use:** Players will firstly use herbcraft to respond to specific dangers or problems. Secondly, players will use herbcraft to prepare for potential problems. Thirdly, players might turn to herbcraft because they have some time to kill.

**Preventing between-adventure abuse:** Remedies have a 24-hour expiration date. Ingredients, if they aren't properly stored, lose their potency. The EXISTENCE of the expiration is doing most of the work. The actual date will RARELY be a factor. Sometimes, it's okay to design a rule knowing full well people will only mostly follow it.

**Preventing stockpiling:** Gathering ingredients during a day of travel fits the rules for wilderness travel and travel tasks. But by itself, it breaks the system for emergencies. So we add another option: an herbalist can create a potion in one hour of focused time, but they and the party have to forego any traveling or progress.

The concept of "one hour" is very important in D&D 5E. It is the length of a short rest. "One hour" is the unit of time synonymous with "halting all progress in the adventure to focus on recovery and preparation for the next thing." The perfect place for potion making.

Tying the mechanic to both short rests and travel time also connects it with random encounters as a potential limiting factor. If a GM gets tired of the amateur herbalist halting the adventure for hours at a time to stockpile potions, the GM has a potential solution they can implement.

The 24-hour expiration timer is also a pressure valve. If the GM sees adventures grinding to a halt for days of flower-picking, the GM can start nitpicking the time limit.

Build systems that suggest ways that GMs can reign in potential abuses if they feel they need to.

## Branching Out More

You can use branches to intertwine your system with the rest of the game. That's why the ranger Natural Explorer feature is explicitly called out.

You can use branches to broaden the appeal of your system. Herbcraft allows the herbalist to do something nice for the rest of the group. No one complains about having extra healing and bonuses. That's why the herbal remedies can simply be purchased. Anyone can buy a remedy.

Don't leave the GM out either. The special ingredient thing – where the potion can require some sort of MacGuffin that has to be found through adventure – that's all carrot for the GM. An NPC herbalist could need a very specific ingredient to cure a particular illness.

And then there's branches that just add some fun. The variable quality thing is a perfect example. Nothing else in D&D does that. It's a little weird. But why the hell not?

Remember, it isn't all about ironing the bubbles out of the wallpaper, it's also about picking colors and patterns you like.
