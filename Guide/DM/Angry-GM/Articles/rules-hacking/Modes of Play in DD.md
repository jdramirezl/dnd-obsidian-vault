# Modes of Play in D&D

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Do We Really Need More Rules: Modes of Play in D&D](https://theangrygm.com/modes-of-play-in-dd/)
**Date:** March 29, 2017
**Topic:** Why D&D needs distinct mechanical subsystems beyond combat

---

## Core Idea

D&D has exactly two built-in modes of play: **combat** (with a full rules engine) and **everything else** (resolved with the generic d20 core mechanic). This imbalance is both D&D's greatest strength and greatest weakness. The game *can* do anything, but only combat is *easy* to design, run, and communicate.

## What Is a Mode of Play?

A **mode of play** is a distinct chunk of gameplay where players pursue particular types of goals in particular ways. Examples:
- **Combat** — resolve conflict through violence; defeat enemies without being defeated
- **Social interaction** — resolve conflict through verbal exchange; persuade, deceive, bargain, threaten
- **Stealth** — overcome a stronger foe through superior movement and terrain exploitation
- **Exploration/Survival** — navigate dangerous environments, manage resources, discover secrets
- **Investigation** — gather clues, piece together information, solve mysteries

## Why Combat Dominates

Combat has a **dedicated rules engine** layered over the core mechanic:
- Initiative and turn structure
- Predefined actions with clear resolutions
- Difficulty sliders (Challenge Rating)
- Automated running — the GM can simply *play the monsters* and try to win

This makes combat easy to **design**, **run**, **record**, and **share** in published adventures. You draw a map, drop stat blocks, and say "fight." No further explanation needed.

Everything else requires the GM to:
- Make judgment calls on every action
- Pace the scene manually
- Determine progress without structural tools
- Invent mechanics on the fly or ahead of time

## The Minecraft/Little Big Planet Analogy

Most people default to what is easy and obvious:
- In **Minecraft**, most people build houses, not redstone computers
- In **Little Big Planet**, most custom levels are simple platformers, not racing games
- In **D&D**, most sessions are combat-heavy because combat is the easiest mode to design and run

If D&D *didn't* have a robust combat engine, people would be more likely to see it as a game that does anything rather than a game that only does combat.

## The Case for More Modes of Play

### The Need Is Real
- GMs constantly ask for crafting systems, mass combat, chase rules, better exploration
- Angry has been *building* subsystems for years: Social Interaction encounters, Abstract Dungeoneering, Wilderness Exploration
- Players engage more when they can see a visible framework for what they can do (rules empower players by explicitly telling them something is an option)

### The Danger: Subsystem Overload
- Too many minigames stitched together = a mess (e.g., Hackmaster 5E, AD&D 2E)
- Any Mode of Play system should be an **extension of the underlying rules**, not a new game bolted on
- Not everything needs a subsystem — only things that come up frequently enough to matter

### The Ideal Approach
- Present subsystems as **optional overlays** — like mods in WoW
- GMs pick and choose which modes they want
- The base game still works without any of them
- This was supposedly the promise of 5E's "modular design" — which was never delivered

## Social Interaction as a Third Mode

D&D has a natural third mode: **social interaction**. RPGs are verbal games — put people in character and they naturally start talking in character. But D&D doesn't do much to support this mechanically, and Charisma/social skills arguably make it worse (a topic for another article).

## What Comes Next

Before building new modes of play, the **core mechanic itself** needs slight expansion — codifying "rules of thumb" and adding versatility to the basic action resolution system. The changes will be subtle and simple, but they'll provide the foundation for building proper subsystems.

## Key Takeaways

- Specific rules provide more depth than general rules — combat in D&D feels deeper than combat in Dungeon World *because* it has dedicated mechanics
- Without predefined rules, GMs carry enormous cognitive load for non-combat scenes
- The solution isn't to make everything as complex as combat — it's to create lightweight, extensible overlays that make other modes of play easier to design and run
- Every subsystem should feel like a natural extension of the d20 core mechanic, not a separate game
