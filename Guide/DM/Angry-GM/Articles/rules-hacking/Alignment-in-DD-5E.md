## Key Advice

Alignment in D&D 5e is a vestigial mechanic that does nothing mechanically — it's referenced everywhere but enforced nowhere. You have two valid options: **remove it entirely** (it won't be missed) or **commit to it fully** by treating alignment as an objective metaphysical property of the D&D universe that drives religion, the afterlife, and divine class obligations. There is no useful middle ground.

## Summary

Angry makes a two-part argument about alignment in 5e:

**Part 1 — Just Remove It:** Alignment in 5e is mechanically dead. Nothing requires your alignment to match your class, race, background, or deity. No spells key off alignment (Protection from Evil and Good protects against creature types, not alignments). The only mechanical interaction is with sentient magic items. The DMG barely mentions it. If you don't want to engage with alignment deeply, just delete it — the Inspiration system's Ideals and Flaws already handle character morality better.

**Part 2 — Or Commit Fully:** If you keep alignment, treat it as an objective metaphysical reality of the D&D universe — like gravity. Supernatural beings (devils, angels, gods) ARE their alignment; they lack free will regarding it. Mortal souls are blank slates that accumulate moral "temperature" through choices. The alignment on a character sheet is a *statement of intention*, not a binding contract — the GM is the ultimate moral authority and the character's true alignment is a secret known only to the universe.

**Key framework:**
- Good vs. Evil is a spectrum, not a binary. Context matters (stealing bread for starving family ≠ stealing for greed).
- People can disagree about alignment because mortals don't have perfect moral knowledge — just like we had "hot" and "cold" before thermometers.
- Divine characters (paladins, clerics) are held to a higher standard because they draw power from aligned beings. Falling should involve warnings (omens, dreams, divine agents) before consequences.
- Alignment changes should happen gradually and naturally, not as punishment. The only hard line is evil PCs (Angry bans them).
- Use alignment as an Inspiration trigger: living in accordance with your alignment can earn Inspiration.

**Angry's alignment quiz:**
- Good: Willing to suffer harm or sacrifice to benefit others.
- Evil: Willing to harm others for your own benefit.
- Lawful: Individuals should give up freedoms for society's benefit.
- Chaotic: Better off retaining individual freedoms outside society.
- Neutral: Neither extreme on either axis.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Make a binary decision**: Either remove alignment entirely or commit to making it meaningful. The 5e half-measure helps no one.
2. **If removing**: Don't ask players to pick one. Don't write it on sheets. Use Ideals, Bonds, and Flaws for character morality instead.
3. **If keeping**: Treat alignment as an objective property of the universe. Gods, planes, and supernatural beings are defined by it. Mortal souls accumulate alignment through choices.
4. **Make the character sheet alignment a statement of intention**: The player writes what they're *trying* to be. The GM tracks what they actually are based on their actions.
5. **Use Angry's two-question quiz at character creation**: Good/Evil (sacrifice for others vs. harm others for self) and Law/Chaos (give up freedoms for society vs. retain individual freedom). Don't over-explain or allow "it depends."
6. **Warn before punishing divine characters**: Use omens, dreams, visions, and divine agents to course-correct a paladin or cleric before stripping powers. Gods want to keep their agents.
7. **Handle falls outside the game**: If a divine character does fall, sit down with the player privately and work out the consequences together — atonement quest, faith change, class rebuild, or retirement.
8. **Ban evil PCs** (or don't, but decide upfront): If you ban evil, make it a Session Zero rule. If a character drifts toward evil, warn the player — cross the line and the character is retired.
9. **Use alignment as an Inspiration source**: Reward players with Inspiration when they make a costly choice to stay true to their alignment.
10. **Let alignment evolve**: Characters grow. If a major event shifts a character's moral compass, update the alignment to reflect it — no XP penalty, no punishment, just narrative truth.

> [!tip] The Core Insight
> Alignment only works if it's an objective metaphysical property of the D&D universe — not a personality descriptor on a character sheet. The written alignment is the player's intention; the true alignment is determined by the GM based on the character's actions. This makes alignment a world-building tool (driving religion, the afterlife, and divine power) rather than a behavioral straitjacket. If you're not willing to commit to that depth, just remove it — 5e already works fine without it.
