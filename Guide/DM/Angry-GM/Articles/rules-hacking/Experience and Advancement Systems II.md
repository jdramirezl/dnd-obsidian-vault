# Experience and Advancement Systems II

**Source:** [True Mechanical Managery: Experience and Advancement Systems II – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/true-mechanical-managery-experience-and-advancement-systems-ii/)
**Date:** October 4, 2024
**Series:** True Campaign Managery — True Mechanical Managery Module

---

## Summary

The practical follow-up to Part I. This lesson walks through how to actually set an Experience and Advancement Policy: read the rules completely, run a mental simulation of implementing them in your planned campaign, identify gaps and rough edges, evaluate against the criteria from Part I, and make minimal adjustments. Uses D&D 5E as a worked example. The core message: True Campaign Managers try to run by the book and only hack when absolutely necessary.

---

## Key Concepts

### Policy Evaluation Criteria (from Part I)
1. **Does the system provide an extrinsic measure of growth, progress, and accomplishment?** Is there a clear connection between gameplay activities and advancement?
2. **How does implementation impact pacing?** XP math and level-up bookkeeping are gameplay stoppages — where do you slot them?
3. **Are there balance concerns?** Character parity within the party, characters vs. designed challenges, complexity delivery rate.

### Step 1: Read the Actual Rules
- Most GMs don't actually know their system's Experience and Advancement rules. Read them completely.
- **D&D 5E rules as written (PHB 15, DMG 260-261):**
  - Characters earn XP by defeating monsters (kill, capture, rout, or other means). XP is divided among participants.
  - NPCs helping reduce each character's share proportionally.
  - Characters only earn XP for encounters they participate in. Absent players' characters may miss XP.
  - The game tolerates 2-3 levels of difference between party members.
  - GM decides when noncombat challenges warrant XP, based on significant risk.
  - **Milestones** = XP awards for completing adventures or making substantial progress. Guidelines provided for appropriate amounts.
  - **Variant: Session-Based** = level up every X sessions.
  - **Variant: Story-Based** = level up after significant campaign goals.
  - The system targets: levels 1-2 each take ~1 session, then levels come every 2-3 sessions on average.
  - If your campaign deviates from the assumed combat frequency (DMG Chapter 3), adjust the system.

### Step 2: Run a Mental Simulation
- Imagine running your actual planned campaign with the rules as written.
- Walk through scenarios: an encounter ends — what do you do? A player is absent — what happens? A character crosses a level-up threshold mid-dungeon — now what?
- Identify gaps, rough edges, and practical problems.

### Step 3: Evaluate Against Criteria
**D&D 5E analysis example:**
- **Growth/progress connection:** ✅ Works perfectly. XP tied directly to overcoming challenges. Even milestone variant preserves this.
- **Pacing:** ❌ Big rough edge. Stopping after every encounter for XP math is slow. Level-up mid-session is a drag chute. Different characters leveling at different times means repeated stoppages.
- **Balance:** ✅ Fine. Designers say 2-3 level spread is tolerable. Unlikely to exceed that in normal play.

### Step 4: Make Minimal Adjustments
- True Campaign Managers try to do everything by the book. Only use optional rules when you have no other choice.
- Only hack when the by-the-book rules fail one of the major criteria for your specific campaign.
- When you must change something, make the minimum change possible.

### What NOT to Do with XP
- Experience and Advancement Systems are **not** for driving or changing player behavior.
- They're not punishments, rewards, incentives, or disincentives.
- Stop using words like "reward" and "punishment" when talking about XP.

## Rate of Advancement

### The One Thing You Can't Screw Up
- Players need regular advancement or they feel like they're going nowhere.
- Advancement feels best when it **slows down over time** and is **slightly unpredictable** (not random — just variable by a session or so).
- D&D's target: levels 1-2 in ~1 session each, then every 2-3 sessions, with higher levels taking longer.

### Big vs. Small Advancement Systems
- **Big Advancement** (D&D, Pathfinder, class-and-level systems): Characters accumulate XP steadily but advance in periodic spurts. Lots changes at once. Target: every 1-4 sessions, shorter intervals at low levels.
- **Small Advancement** (skill-based, classless, direct-buy systems): Characters spend points as earned for incremental improvements. Can allow more frequent, smaller advancements.
- XP itself (the progress marker) should be awarded frequently — every session if possible, never more than two sessions apart.

---

## Angry's Personal Policy (D&D Example)

- Award XP at the **end of every session** — players never wait for it, gameplay never stops for it.
- Players level up **between sessions only** — no stopping the game for bookkeeping.
- Additional restriction: leveling up only happens in **safe, civilized places** (town), not mid-adventure.
- This harmonizes with a Travel → Adventure → Travel → Town gameplay cycle.
- Works for pretty much any properly structured fantasy adventure campaign.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Read your system's complete XP/advancement rules.** You'll be surprised what's actually there.
2. **Mentally simulate** running your campaign with those rules. Note every gap and rough edge.
3. **Evaluate** against the three criteria: growth connection, pacing impact, balance concerns.
4. **Adjust minimally.** Prefer by-the-book. Only change what's actually broken for your campaign.
5. **Know your campaign's structure** and sync advancement to it. If you deviate from the system's assumed play structure, you need to account for that.
6. **Set a clear policy** for when XP is awarded and when level-ups happen. Communicate it to players.
