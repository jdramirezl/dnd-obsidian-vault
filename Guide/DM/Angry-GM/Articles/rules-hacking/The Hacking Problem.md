# The Hacking Problem

**Source:** [The Angry GM – The Hacking Problem](https://theangrygm.com/the-hacking-problem/)
**Date:** December 30, 2025
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## What Is The Hacking Problem?

The Hacking Problem arises when a character must carry out a **protracted task** using **specialized knowledge** that falls under a **very small number of skills** (usually one). The result: the player rolls the same check over and over with no meaningful strategic choices beyond "keep going or give up."

Three conditions must be present:

1. **Protracted task** — Takes minutes, hours, or days (not a single instant action)
2. **Specialized/esoteric knowledge** — The player has no mental model for the task (they're not a hacker, doctor, alchemist, etc.), so they can't form plans, choose approaches, or experiment with alternatives
3. **Limited skill list** — The task is mechanically resolved by one skill, and even splitting into sub-skills doesn't help because the task dictates which sub-skill to use

## The Real Hacking Problem

The surface problem is "rolling the same check repeatedly." The deeper problem is: **how can a roleplaying game let two non-experts simulate a task requiring expertise in a way that's strategic, exciting, and engaging?** On the fly? Without so much abstraction that tasks don't feel like anything?

## Where It Hides

- **Obvious:** Computer hacking, crafting, medical treatment, magical rituals, research
- **Asymptomatic:** Social encounters (hidden because players/GMs have a mental model for conversation — they can adjust approach, say different things, get bonuses/penalties based on what they say)
- **Band-Aided:** Lock picking, trap disabling (designers made them single-roll to avoid the problem, but this prevents "pick the lock while others hold off monsters" scenes from working naturally)
- **Partially hidden:** Combat (melee can devolve into "I attack" repeatedly, but movement, positioning, multiple combatants, and environmental factors provide enough extra decision-making to mask it)

## Why Social Encounters Don't Feel Like It

Mechanically, social encounters ARE the Hacking Problem — rolling Interact/Persuasion over and over. But players and GMs **know how conversations work**, so:

- The GM modifies each check based on what the player actually says
- Players adjust strategy based on NPC reactions
- Saying the wrong thing can impose penalties; apologizing can remove them
- The possibility space is infinite because everyone understands human interaction

If two hacking experts played a hacking encounter, it would feel like a social encounter run by a great GM.

## Why Progressive Checks Are Still Worth Using

Angry previously advocated single-roll resolution to avoid the Hacking Problem. He now walks that back — Progressive Checks (accumulating progress over multiple rolls) are superior despite the problem because they:

- **Make intuitive sense** — A protracted task shouldn't resolve like a punch
- **Maintain engagement and pacing** — Give you a reason to check in with the tasking character while others act
- **Keep clocks synchronized** — Each check = a time unit, anchoring the Action Clock for parallel activities
- **Handle failure gracefully** — A wizard spending 3 days brewing a potion shouldn't have it all ride on one roll at the end
- **Model commitment and abandonment** — Players can see progress and make informed decisions about continuing, pausing, or cutting losses
- **Show variable cost** — You don't know exactly how long a task will take, which is realistic and creates tension
- **Allow pausing** — Crafting projects can be set aside and resumed; progress is already tracked

## Solutions That Don't Fully Work

### Single-Roll Resolution
Avoids the problem by eliminating it entirely, but loses all the benefits of Progressive Checks. "Avoiding filling a bathtub by drowning your baby."

### Minigame Systems
Dedicated subsystems (e.g., cyberpunk hacking rules). Problems:
- Players won't learn them (most won't even learn the base rules)
- GMs have to learn and remember them too
- One-off use means re-learning every time
- Time spent on mechanics is time not spent on fiction
- Doesn't cover tasks the designers didn't anticipate

### Encounter Design Mandates
"Just make sure hacking encounters include other skills" or "always interleave with other activities." This is forcing contrivance on adventure designers. If a skill exists in the game, using it should be interesting on its own.

## The Unsolved Core

The fundamental challenge: **gameplay is strategic choice**, and rolling dice isn't gameplay. When Darius's player chose "remote hacking," he was done making strategic choices — everything after was just seeing how the gamble played out. The only remaining decision was "keep going or stop," which isn't exciting.

The solution likely involves:
- A better toolset for constructing complex, protracted tasks (better skill challenges)
- Possibly a completely different way of thinking about action resolution at the core mechanic level

## Practical Takeaway

The Hacking Problem isn't catastrophic — it's "baked chicken over rice." Fine, even tasty, but depressing if it happens too often. Be aware of it, don't let it dominate sessions, but don't over-engineer solutions that introduce worse problems (excessive abstraction, mandatory minigames, or eliminating Progressive Checks entirely).
