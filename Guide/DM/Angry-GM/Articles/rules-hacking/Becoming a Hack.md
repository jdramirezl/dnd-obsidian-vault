# Becoming a Hack

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Becoming a Hack](https://theangrygm.com/becoming-a-hack/)
**Date:** February 28, 2017
**Series:** Hacking Theory

---

## Overview

The introductory article for Angry's series on how to hack (modify) RPG game systems. Defines key terminology (system vs. game, rules vs. game elements vs. aesthetics), establishes the two fundamental types of hacking, and lays out five essential principles for successful game design and modification.

---

## Key Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **System** | A collection of rules and game elements used to run games (e.g., D&D 5E, Pathfinder) |
| **Game** | The actual adventure, scenario, or module (e.g., Curse of Strahd) |
| **Campaign** | A series of connected games (e.g., Rise of the Runelords) |
| **Rules** | Mechanics for ability checks, initiative, magic, character definition, etc. |
| **Game Elements** | Classes, monsters, spells, magic items, feats, deities, settings |
| **Aesthetic** | The emergent feel of the game arising from player interaction with rules and game elements |

---

## The Two Types of Hacking

1. **Adding/modifying Game Elements** — new spells, monsters, classes, magic items, races
2. **Adding/modifying Rules** — new mechanics, changed procedures, new systems

### Aesthetics
- Aesthetics are emergent — they arise from the interaction between players, rules, and game elements
- When you hack either rules or game elements, you can **maintain** or **modify** the game's aesthetic
- The farther you deviate from the design aesthetic, the more work required and the more likely things break

### Example: Horror in D&D
- D&D's aesthetic favors empowerment — powerful characters, broad tools, synergistic abilities, odds favoring players
- Horror requires isolation, disempowerment, hopelessness
- Running horror in D&D requires dealing with most of those empowerment aspects
- Compare Call of Cthulhu: small repertoire, specialized/ineffective tools, obstacles far more powerful than investigators

## Five Principles of Hacking

### 1. Fail Fast, Then Iterate
- The first draft of everything is garbage — accept this
- Get your idea into a **playable form** as fast as possible and **test it**
- Game design is NOT a desk activity — it's done by testing and iterating
- Stop overthinking and tinkering before you've even tried it at the table
- "Fail fast, then iterate" — Mark Rosewater

### 2. Don't Treat Your Game Like Porcelain
- Your ongoing game IS the playtest — that's okay
- If something goes horribly wrong, **roll it back**: "I messed up inventing this ability, we have to take it out"
- Tell players upfront you're testing something new
- Don't be afraid to break your game, but stop before it gets too broken

### 3. Stick It Out
- Don't pull the plug too early — GMs tend to panic and think they broke something when they haven't
- **Let failures play out** — watching exactly how something goes wrong is more instructive than stopping it early
- Anything less than a character death is not worth rolling back mid-session
- For new rules, leave them in place for **at least 3 sessions**:
  - Session 1: People get used to it
  - Session 2: See it in action
  - Session 3: People exploit or break it
- Don't iterate immediately — you might miss other problems

### 4. Build the Smallest Thing You Need
- Big changes = more work + more potential for things to go wrong
- Always ask: can this fit into a smaller design space?
  - Instead of a new class → subclass or feat?
  - Instead of a new race → subrace?
  - Instead of a new spell → class ability?
- Challenge your assumptions about what design space your idea needs

### 5. Don't Reinvent the Wheel
- Before designing from scratch, check if something similar already exists in the system
- Tweaking and repurposing existing mechanics saves work and reveals potential problems
- Example: Need a leader monster with a battle cry? Check fighter Maneuvers for combat leadership abilities

### Prerequisite: Know Thy System
- You must deeply understand the system before modifying it
- Know the basic mechanics, options, optional rules, and the **patterns underneath**
- Every system has hidden design patterns — the designers had specific ideas even when they didn't explain them

### Example Pattern: 5E Attack vs. Damage
- Attack bonuses stay tightly constrained across levels
- Damage output rises substantially every ~4 levels
- A high-level fighter isn't much more likely to hit than a low-level one, but does far more damage
- Implication: A cantrip that scales attack bonus instead of damage would be useless at high levels in 5E (but would work in 3.5/PF)

### Pop Quiz (Know Your Monster Manual)
- Which monster has the Leadership ability?
- What about Reckless?
- Innate Spellcasting examples?
- How many monsters have poison abilities and what can poison do?

---

## Key Takeaways

1. System ≠ Game — know the difference
2. Aesthetics are emergent and fragile — respect them or plan to do extra work
3. Test early, test often — stop designing at your desk and start playing
4. Your home game is your playtest lab — embrace that
5. Small changes are safer and easier — always aim for the minimum viable hack
6. Know your system deeply — read every rule, every item, every monster; understand the hidden patterns
