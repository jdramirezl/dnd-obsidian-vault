# Crafting in the Raw

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/crafting-in-the-raw/)
**Date:** February 14, 2019
**Series:** Crafting a Crafting System

---

## The End Result of All of this Crap

When I say I want to design a crafting system for D&D, I'm designing a system whereby players can spend resources they earn in the course of their adventures to customize their characters' equipment or to acquire new equipment that would otherwise be unavailable. But that's only the surface answer.

Look at treasure and equipment acquisition in D&D. It's got the resource (money), the stuff players can buy (organized on a convenient list), treasure tables telling the GM how much to hand out, suggested magic item costs, alternate uses for money (building castles, running businesses), and cost as a soft limit on certain things (costly spell components).

I'm not just designing a simple craft system I can use with my own discretion at my own table. I promised a system that any reasonable GM and group of players can use without demanding too much extra work. The amount of discretion such a system is allowed to rely upon is pretty small.

GM discretion involves providing a set of useful tools, instructions for how to use those tools, and guidelines for how far to bend those tools in unusual circumstances. The number of situations in which a GM has to invent anything out of whole cloth is vanishingly small.

Absolutely every design decision has to keep all of this crap in mind. You can't fall back on GM discretion except in small, manageable chunks. You have to be precise. You have to integrate your system into the existing game. You can't change the intended play experience. The whole thing has to be a nearly effortless, plug-and-play system.

## Having a System for Everything

Here's what we decided: heroes would find ingredients during their adventures and they would be able to turn those ingredients into custom equipment and upgrades. Away from the table. With no die rolls.

A role-playing game exists in an odd middle ground between the fictional and the mechanical. There have to be rules because it's a game, but those rules have to fit within a narrative framework, because it's also an exercise in imagining hypothetical situations.

One of the ways to pull that off is for the game designer to use a systemic approach. A systemic approach is about coming up with ways to describe the fictional stuff in your world in a way that defines how it mechanically interacts. And then using those interactions to design the systems.

For example, the "fire" descriptor. Once upon a time, a fireball spell really did just do a certain amount of damage. But there was also a GM and players with brains. They KNOW what fire is. And so, it was only a matter of time before someone asked if they could set a pile of oily rags on fire with a fireball spell.

Systemic design is really just about seeing that crap coming and being ready for it. When a thing that is fire hits a thing that is flammable, what happens?

RPG designers are very inconsistent about systemic design. But there are actually a lot of hidden systemic designs in D&D. Take weapons: simple weapons do 1d6 damage and martial weapons do 1d8 damage. If the weapon is light or finesse, it does a smaller die. If heavy or two-handed, a larger die. Most weapons are consistent with those patterns.

And that's the key to designing ingredients for D&D crafting. First, come up with ways to define the ingredients that tell you everything you need to know mechanically. Then, use those descriptors to actually define the ingredients.

## …Or Don't

One of the complaints with ingredient systems is that they have too many damned ingredients. And the problem with assigning each one a bunch of arbitrary qualities is that every craftsperson's equipment list becomes unmanageably long and confusing.

We could go the abstract route – Grade X Art Object style. But there are two POTENTIAL problems: you make it so abstract that it stops feeling like crafting, and with too many possible keyword combinations, the odds of finding two identical items are vanishingly small.

## But Systemic!

With the right combination of descriptors, you can avoid those pitfalls and use them as the basis to define a whole system. For example, the recipe for a martial bladed weapon might be five metal ingots. To make a magical sword, add one magical metal ingot. To make a magical flaming sword, add one fiery ingot. Notice how there's a logic to it. The recipes build off of each other.

This makes it easier for players to understand, remember, or guess at recipes. And it makes it easier for the GM to invent new items or adjudicate experiments.

## What Do Ingredients Have to Do?

We need to figure out how we're going to define ingredients in mechanical terms.

**First:** If we want to avoid abstraction ruining the feeling of crafting, we need a simple way to say exactly what the ingredient is in the fiction.

**Second:** The descriptors have to hook into the adventure design tools – they have to say how the players might acquire the ingredient. Is it found in the environment? In a treasure horde? Harvested from monsters?

**Third:** The descriptors have to tell us how the ingredient interacts with the other systems in the game. Specifically, we have to be able to decide how much the ingredient is worth.

**Finally:** The descriptors have to define limits and restrictions for their uses. Specific ingredients must be used to produce specific things.

## Drumroll Please…

Every ingredient can be described by a minimum of two and a maximum of three descriptors. Each does a specific job.

### Descriptor 1: Rarity

The DMG defines magic items as being common, uncommon, rare, very rare, and legendary. It prescribes a specific cost range and a range of levels at which each becomes available. Since we're hooking our ingredient system into all of that, we use that same descriptor.

Common bone might be skeleton bones or deer antlers – any bone from a CR 1 to CR 3 creature. Rare ephemera might come from a CR 5 fire elemental. The eyeball fluid from a beholder would be very rare fluid.

### Descriptor 2: Type

The type describes exactly what the ingredient is in the world. It classifies the ingredient, defines where it can be found and how it can be harvested. Ten total classifications:

- **Metal** – base metals for weapons and armor
- **Precious Metal** – gold, silver, etc.
- **Wood** – timber and wooden materials
- **Herb** – plants and botanical ingredients
- **Hide** – skins, leather, pelts
- **Bone** – bones, shells, horns, antlers
- **Fluid** – blood, vital fluids, any liquid
- **Mineral** – stone, ore, earth materials
- **Gem** – precious and semi-precious stones
- **Essence/Ephemera** – ectoplasmic residue, soul, fairy dust, magical energy

### Descriptor 3: Special Quality (Optional)

Any ingredient might have a special quality keyed to various magical effects. So your magical flaming sword might be mostly rare metal, but it needs at least some fiery ingredient.

Working list of possible qualities: the damage types from D&D 5E along with healing, blessing, corrupting, and summoning. Those seem to be the basic sorts of magical "energies" at play in the world.

## Summary

Three dimensions:
- **Rarity** – hooked directly into the rarity, value, and CR guidelines already in place
- **Type** – defines what the ingredient is and therefore explains where it should be placed in the game world
- **Special Quality** – provides the differentiation needed so that items with different qualities require different ingredients

The party might stumble on common healing flowers, or harvest rare acidic fluid from an ooze, or discover some very rare icy gemstone in a vein of minerals in a frozen mine.

And that's it. That's ingredients. Simple. Right?
