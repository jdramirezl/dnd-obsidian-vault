# Innovation: Worthless or Priceless?

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Fanservice BS: Innovation: Worthless or Priceless?](https://theangrygm.com/innovation-worthless-or-priceless/)
**Date:** January 30, 2018
**Series:** Rules Hacking / Design Philosophy

---

## The Paradox of Innovation

Everyone claims to want innovation, but familiar things consistently outsell new things. This is true in video games, movies, and RPGs.

- **D&D 4E** was the most innovative D&D since 2000 — and it was left bleeding in a ditch after five years
- **D&D 5E** is a perfectly adequate rehash that imitates 3E, takes no chances, and adds nothing new of substance
- Players who say they want innovation will, given the choice, order the chicken alfredo (the familiar thing they know they like)

## Innovation Has Zero Intrinsic Value

Innovation and originality are **not inherently better** than derivative work. A remade movie has just as much *potential* to be good as the original. A tavern-start rescue-the-princess adventure has as much potential to be fun as a sky-pirates-in-the-ruins-of-Ragnarok adventure.

**Where innovation's value kicks in:** When two things are **equally great**, the innovative one will be remembered as the favorite. Innovation makes great things *better*. People don't want innovative things — they want **great things that are also innovative**.

## The Risk/Reward Calculus

| Approach | Risk | Reward |
|---|---|---|
| **Derivative** | Low — people give familiar things more leeway | Medium — people will probably like it |
| **Innovative** | High — people are suspicious of new things and hold them to a higher standard | High — potential for something truly amazing |

The restaurant analogy: You have a $50 gift card. One special is chicken alfredo (you know you like it). The other is an unpronounceable Italian dish you've never heard of. Most people pick the familiar dish. The more something costs to try, the more likely you stick with what's known.

## D&D Edition History as Case Study

- **3E** innovated massively from AD&D 2E. People complained it "didn't feel like D&D." But it worked, became the definitive D&D, and literally brought the brand back from extinction.
- **4E** innovated massively from 3E. People complained it "didn't feel like D&D." But this time, the OGL kept 3E alive via Pathfinder — people had a *choice* to stick with the familiar. 4E brought in new players but alienated hardcore fans.
- **5E** played it safe, imitating 3E closely. It's adequate. It's the Force Awakens of D&D.

The difference between 3E and 4E wasn't quality — it was that 3E's audience had no alternative, while 4E's audience could retreat to Pathfinder.

## What This Means for GMs

1. **To run great games, you must risk running bad ones.** You have to be willing to innovate, to be the first person to try something.

2. **Innovation alone doesn't ensure greatness.** Innovating just to innovate has no payoff. New things are held to a higher standard — they have to work twice as hard to get a fair chance.

3. **Derivative isn't as bad as people think.** Most people like familiar things and give them more leeway. Don't let anyone shame you for starting in a tavern.

4. **You can't evaluate RPG ideas by reading them.** The medium is interactive — mechanics don't mean anything until they interact with GMs, players, and each other. You have to actually **try** the thing.

5. **Don't ask for validation — just try it.** If you have a great idea for something new in your game, don't email Angry to ask if it's good. Don't poll social media. Most people will give you good-sounding reasons why it probably isn't a good idea — but they don't know what they're saying. Until an idea is executed, tested, and tweaked, it can't be evaluated.

## Key Takeaway

> If you have some great idea for doing a new thing in your game, JUST TRY IT. You want the glory, you have to have the guts.

Over time, the more risks you take on innovation, the more successes you have. You get better at it. Your flops come less often, and your successes get better and more frequent. But you have to start by being willing to fail.
