## Key Advice

D&D 5e's Inspiration system is a vestigial mechanic that almost everyone forgets. The bonus is disconnected from the action that earned it, the DM has too much to track, and Flaws are never incentivized because positive traits earn the same reward with no risk. However, hidden underneath is a powerful role-playing tool — if you're willing to hack it.

## Summary

Angry dissects why the RAW Inspiration system fails: the reward is disconnected from the triggering action, it can be passed to others (further severing the link), Flaws are never worth engaging with when safe Traits earn the same bonus, and DMs routinely forget to award it. He then presents **11 options** for fixing it, anchored by his core replacement — **Angry's Awesome Inspiration System** — where players start each session with Inspiration, spend it on **Inspired Actions** tied to their Personal Characteristics, and regain it by **Claiming Setbacks** (voluntarily taking disadvantage or creating story complications tied to their Flaws). This shifts control from the DM to the players, aligns player incentives with character behavior, and makes Flaws mechanically rewarding. Additional options include racial characteristic tables, campaign-specific Bonds, the Tabula Rasa game (blank traits filled in during play), Compulsions from external forces, and an Insanity system where trauma replaces Characteristics with uncontrollable traits.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Adopt Angry's Core System**: Players start each session with Inspiration. They spend it on Inspired Actions (advantage/disadvantage on a roll) that must tie to a Personal Characteristic. They regain it by Claiming Setbacks (voluntary disadvantage or story complications tied to Flaws).
2. **Make Inspiration cards**: Write each PC's 5 Characteristics on index cards hung on your DM screen. When a player earns/spends Inspiration, physically hand/take the card.
3. **Pick ONE purpose for Inspiration**: Don't use it as a catch-all reward. Decide if it's for Personal Characteristics, heroics, or clever play — then stick to it.
4. **Give it early and often**: Ignore the "once per session" advice. Most players should have Inspiration most of the time.
5. **Collect unused Inspiration at session end**: Make a show of it — "You still have this? Should've used it on that dragon." Players will learn to spend it.
6. **Allow Characteristics to evolve**: After major events, invite players to swap out a played-out Characteristic for a new one reflecting what happened.
7. **Create racial Characteristic tables**: Write sample Traits, Bonds, Ideals, and Flaws for each race to deepen world-building and give players more options.
8. **Tie Bonds to your campaign**: Require all Bonds connect to a faction, organization, or theme central to the campaign.
9. **Swap Characteristic types for genre**: Replace Ideals with Motivations for dungeon-crawls, or Fears for horror games.
10. **Consider Compulsions and Insanity**: For horror or dark campaigns, add externally imposed Characteristics (cursed items, possession) that can only create Setbacks, never Inspired Actions.

> [!tip] The Core Insight
> The RAW system fails because the DM controls the reward and the bonus is disconnected from the triggering action. Shift control to the players: let them spend Inspiration on actions tied to their traits, and earn it back by voluntarily suffering for their Flaws. This aligns player incentive with character behavior — even "gaming the system" produces better role-playing.
