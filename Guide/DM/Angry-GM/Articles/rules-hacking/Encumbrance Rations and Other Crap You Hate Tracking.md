# Encumbrance, Rations, and Other Crap You Hate Tracking

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Encumbrance, Rations, and Other Crap You Hate Tracking](https://theangrygm.com/encumbrance-rations-and-other-crap-you-hate-tracking/)
**Date:** September 11, 2019
**Topic:** Why resource tracking (encumbrance, food, light, ammo) matters for exploration — and why 5E systematically undermines it

---

## Core Idea

Restrictions and limitations drive meaningful choices, create a sense of progression, and make exploration actually matter. D&D 5E includes systems for encumbrance, rations, light sources, and travel — but then trivializes every single one of them, destroying the exploration pillar it claims to support.

## The "World Leveling with the PCs" Problem

### The Real Debate
- **Open-worlders** want a pre-mapped world where a lich crypt exists regardless of PC level — go there at level 1 and die
- **Tailor-made campaigners** want challenges matched to the party's current level
- Both sides are right: some verisimilitude is needed for role-playing to work, AND satisfying difficulty curves are real psychology

### The Missing Middle Ground
The real question: **what makes a location inaccessible to low-level parties besides a big monster guarding the door?**

Answer: **logistics.** The Lost Pyramid is a 15th-level dungeon because only 15th-level heroes can *afford the expedition* — three weeks of desert travel requires food, water, camels, drivers, navigation, shelter, and a massive pool of gold. The pyramid levels itself naturally through resource requirements, not arbitrary monster placement.

## Progression Is Born of Restriction

### The Vehicle Progression Fantasy
In video games, progression *feels* powerful beyond just bigger numbers:
- **WoW:** walking → mount → flying mount (each opens more of the world)
- **Final Fantasy:** walking → raft → ship → airship → spacecraft
- **Hollow Knight:** new abilities unlock previously visible but inaccessible areas

Each new capability makes you feel like you're **conquering the world**, not just fighting slightly bigger numbers.

### D&D Used to Have This
- **Bag of Holding** was beloved because it meant encumbrance was no longer a problem
- **Torches and rations** limited how deep you could delve and how far you could travel
- **Mounts** were expensive and meaningful
- **Navigation** was a real challenge
- Gradually overcoming these obstacles *was* the exploration progression

### 5E Destroyed It
- Encumbrance limits are so high most characters never hit them
- Encumbrance penalties (−10 ft speed) are trivial and don't affect overland travel
- Foraging is stupidly easy — an untrained character rarely fails
- *Goodberry* and *Create Food and Water* are low-level spells with trivial costs
- *Light* is a free cantrip (torches are irrelevant)
- Mounts are cheap and can be magically conjured at low levels
- Rangers auto-succeed at navigation and foraging
- Ammunition is trivially recovered

The result: **every party starts with the equivalent of a Bag of Holding, infinite torches, and unlimited rations.** There's nothing to grow beyond.

## Why This Matters

### Choices Require Restrictions
- Meaningful choices come from **risk and conflict** — you can't have everything
- If you remove restrictions, you remove choices
- If you remove choices, you remove the sense of growth and progression
- What's left is a treadmill of bigger numbers and flashier special effects

### The Exploration Pillar Is Hollow
5E claims three pillars: Combat, Exploration, Interaction. But:
- Combat has a full rules engine with progression (levels, abilities, gear)
- Exploration has... nothing. All constraints are trivially bypassed.
- Interaction has... Charisma scores and some vague guidelines

### The Opt-In vs. Opt-Out Problem
- Resource tracking is **easy to opt out of** if it's in the game (just ignore it)
- Resource tracking is **hard to opt into** if it's missing or undermined
- 5E's encumbrance system exists but is so permissive it might as well not
- Even if a GM tries to enforce it, the game's spells and abilities immediately undermine the effort

## The Ideal System (If Writing an RPG)

### 1. Reduce Bookkeeping
Tracking individual pounds isn't fun. Design systems that work for human beings — abstract where possible, make tracking simple.

### 2. Build Progression Tiers
Decide when each exploration obstacle should stop mattering, then align ALL abilities to those tiers:

| Obstacle | Stops Mattering At | How |
|---|---|---|
| Navigation | Level 5 | Ranger's "Never Lost" ability, Expert Navigator feat, *Know Direction* spell, NPC guides, Sextant of Accuracy magic item |
| Encumbrance | Level 10 | *Lenny's Mystical Chest*, Bags of Holding, Convenient Knapsack |
| Light | Level 3 | *Light* cantrip, Darkvision, Everburning Torch |
| Food/Water | Level 7 | *Create Food and Water*, Foraging mastery, Ring of Sustenance |
| Travel Speed | Level 9 | Affordable mounts, Paladin's summoned warhorse, *Phantom Steed* |

### 3. Provide Multiple Solutions Per Tier
Each obstacle should have several ways to overcome it — class abilities, feats, spells, items, hirelings — so no single class is "required."

### 4. Tell the GM About the Progressions
Explicitly document:
- What the tiers are and when obstacles fall away
- Why they matter for exploration-based campaigns
- How to remove specific obstacles if the GM doesn't want them
- That the GM should tell players up front so they don't waste resources on abilities that won't matter

### 5. Support Opt-Out Cleanly
> "Our game is about combat, exploration, and interaction. If you want to remove exploration, ignore the supplies and encumbrance systems. Tell your players so they won't choose abilities that interact with those systems."

## Key Takeaways

- **Restrictions create progression** — overcoming obstacles feels better than never having them
- **5E's exploration systems exist but are neutered** — they affect nothing and are trivially bypassed
- **The encumbrance system penalizes the wrong things** — tactical speed (which you can avoid by dropping your pack) instead of overland travel endurance
- **Spells and abilities undermine GM attempts to enforce resource tracking** — the game actively fights against its own exploration pillar
- **The ideal system builds exploration progression into the game's DNA** — with clear tiers, multiple solutions, and explicit GM guidance on what to keep or remove
