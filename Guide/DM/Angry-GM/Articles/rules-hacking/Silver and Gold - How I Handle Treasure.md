# Silver and Gold: How I Handle Treasure at the Table

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-i-handle-treasure/)
**Date:** December 15, 2021

---

This article's about how **I** handle treasure in D&D. The follow-up's about how **you** should handle treasure. When I say treasure, I don't mean magical items or useful equipment. I mean coins, gems, art objects, trade goods — cash and cash-equivalents.

## Part 1: How I Roll… Treasure

### Where Treasure Comes From

The treasure life cycle starts with me building an adventure and putting some treasure in it. I use the core rules as guidelines. In D&D 5E, there's generally about 200–300 gp worth of coinage and about 100–400 gp worth of gems and art objects scattered around the average adventure (DMG 137). A casual inspection is **good enough!**

I start with a budget (250–750 gp) and break it down into coins, gems, art objects, trade goods, and other valuables. The amount and denomination of coins depends on where they're coming from. Quest giver payments are gold, silver, copper — coins of the realm. Bandits have mostly copper and silver. Ancient ruins have beaten electrum coins. Dwarven redoubts or draconian ruins have platinum.

Likewise, valuable trinkets in bandit camps are trade goods or cheap tchotchkes. Ancient ruins have ancient artifacts. I put thought into this — I try to fill adventures with the sorts of treasures those adventures should be filled with.

**Example adventure:** Villagers hire the party to deal with bandits camped in a ruined abbey.

Supply tent:
- Three jars of common spices worth 10 gp each
- A bone statuette of a knight worth 30 gp
- A crate of iron ingots worth 50 gp
- A padlocked wooden chest containing 70 cp, 20 sp, and a small garnet worth 30 gp

Hidden vault beneath the ruins:
- 150 electrum coins in a moldering old sack
- A ceremonial silver cup worth 150 gp
- A moonstone worth 50 gp

That's roughly 415 gp worth of treasure, plus the 30 gp the villagers scrounged to pay the party.

### Looting and Pillaging

When the party finds treasure, I describe it narratively — earthenware jars sealed with wax and merchants' marks, a six-inch bone statuette of an armored knight, a small iron-banded wooden chest with a padlock. Then the party decides what to do.

Smart parties collect everything in a sack and move on: "We can tally and divide this later." When they explicitly declare an action to gather and stow treasure, I tell them how to record it — because I've got my own list and they need to match.

> You never count your money when you're delving in the dungeon; there'll be time enough for countin' when the adventurin's done.

### Taking Stock

When the party has time — back at camp, back at town — they sit down *as a group* and sort through their treasure pile. This is a time-consuming group action.

For each object, I consider the characters' proficiencies and make appropriate checks **in secret**. I'm wishy-washy, vague, obfuscating. I never give a precise value — I give ranges or say things in weird ways like "half a hundred gold or thereabouts."

If players fail a check or no one has the right proficiencies, I say flat out "you've got no idea what this is worth." If they fail bad, I lie. Which is why I roll in secret.

**Key detail:** When I designed the adventure, I placed 70 cp, 20 sp, and 150 ep. But the numbers get fuzzier when counted. I never use even numbers — even numbers feel fake. I just spit out a random number near what I wrote down.

**My list vs. the players' list:**

| My Notes | Players' Notes |
|---|---|
| Knight statuette, aged bone, generic, 30 gp | Knight statue made of bone ??? |
| Garnet (not ruby), small, uncut, flawed, 30 gp | Crappy ruby 250 gp maybe!!! |
| Silver cup, engraved with symbol of Zathena, 150 gp | Silver cup with symbol of Zufeena, 100–200 gp |

### Liquidating Your Assets Off

Selling non-cash treasure: one character takes all the treasure and spends one day of downtime in town wandering from market to shop to collector getting the best price for every item.

I narrate the selling process — offered prices at different shops, NPC interactions. Players can accept prices or push their luck:

- **Haggling:** Charisma (Persuasion) check. Won't get a lot more, but if the check goes poorly, they accept a lower price or keep the item.
- **Scamming:** Charisma (Deception) check, possibly with advantage from relevant proficiencies. Can drive the price up significantly but risks exposure.
- **Leveraged sales:** Selling to the right buyer (e.g., a religious relic to a temple) can yield better prices or non-cash rewards like blessings, healing, or potions.

## That Sounds Awful/Awesome

Evaluating treasure takes a few minutes at the table — ten minutes tops, once or twice an adventure. Selling the loot takes fifteen minutes of town time between adventures.

**Mechanics. Don't. Matter.** It's all about the subjective experience at the table. The feel of play.

Treasure's worthless in D&D 5E mechanically — once a player buys their best mundane armor (usually by 3rd level), there's nothing else to spend gold on. But players still care about it. They like finding money, tallying it up, negotiating prices. My system leans into that.

### The Power of Random

Random rewards feel really good. The best rewards are random rewards. It doesn't matter whether you use random treasure tables — what matters is there's a lot of variation in what the players find. Sometimes a cheap gemstone, sometimes an expensive silver chalice. That *feels* random.

**Why the D&D 5E treasure tables suck:** No variation. Each hoard contains **either** gems **or** arts, never both. All gems/arts in a hoard are worth precisely the same amount. The critical random-seeming variation just isn't there.

Players can also push their luck — accept a safe price or gamble for more via haggling/scamming.

### The Power of Delayed Gratification

When you find 1,284 gold pieces, you know exactly what you've got. But when you find a gem or art object, you know you've got something valuable but not how valuable. That's why we wrap presents — anticipation amplifies revelation.

My system giftwraps the money. Players find stuff that could be worth anything → examine it and get a vague idea → only when they sell do they know the actual value. A psychological one-two punch of randomness and mystery.

### The Power of Detail

When treasure is placed with thought and care — bandits have stolen trade goods, ancient ruins have ancient coinage — it increases the sense of world that makes players care. When players can increase their take by selling to the right people, it encourages them to interact with the world, think about NPCs as real people with motives and preferences.

## The Minimum is Enough

**The minimum:** Scatter a half-dozen valuable objects of varying worth around your dungeons with care and forethought. Let players sell off treasures in one downtime action. Use narration to bring it to life.

**The extra mile:** Add fuzzy appraisals and fuzzy final values. Don't let players evaluate treasure without spending in-game downtime. Let them push their luck gambling on value. Reward players who think about treasures as real objects and NPCs as real people.
