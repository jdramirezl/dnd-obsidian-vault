# Character Replacement Policies I

**Source:** [True Mechanical Managery: Character Replacement Policies I – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/true-mechanical-managery-character-replacement-policies-i/)
**Date:** January 16, 2025
**Series:** True Campaign Managery — True Mechanical Managery Module

---

## Summary

Replacement characters are inevitable — from character death, voluntary retirement, or new players joining. True Campaign Managers have a policy ready before any of this happens. This lesson (Part I) covers how to develop your own Character Replacement Policy based on your system and campaign. It addresses three issues: how advanced the replacement should be, what character options are available, and how to introduce the character into the ongoing game. Part II covers the objectively correct answers.

---

## Key Concepts

### One Policy for All Situations
- Your Character Replacement Policy should be a **single set of rules** covering all cases: death, retirement, new players.
- There's no good reason to have different rules for different causes. Character Parity is about the new character vs. the surviving party, not vs. the dead character.

### Issue 1: How Advanced Should the Replacement Be?

#### Know What Advancement Means
- List everything that changes on a character sheet over time in your system. That's all potentially "advancement."
- In D&D: mostly covered by the level-up system (make a 1st-level character, level it up X times).
- In Pathfinder/older D&D: also includes expected magical equipment at each level (a parallel advancement track).
- In Savage Worlds: spend Advances one at a time from a starting character.
- Some systems also track Glory, Reputation, Prestige, Sanity loss, permanent Injuries — all of these may apply to replacement characters too.

#### Character Parity
- Replacement character advancement is measured against **the party they're joining**, not the character they're replacing.
- Your system likely has tolerances for how much level variance it can handle before breaking. Know them.
- **D&D 5E:** The DMG states the game breaks outside a ~2 level spread. Magical items don't factor into balance.
- Whatever your system's tolerance, your policy must stay within it. You can't start every replacement at level 1 if the party is level 12 — the game will break.
- Decide where within the tolerance you want replacements to land. Examples:
  - "Same level as the party (since everyone's always the same level)."
  - "One level below the lowest party member, or two below the highest, whichever is higher."

#### Don't Consider Players' Feelings
- Players will never be happy to lose anything. That doesn't mean you remove costs and consequences.
- If the best thing for your game is replacements at half the party's level, do it.
- **But:** Tell players the policy up front. No surprises.

### Issue 2: What Options Are Available?

#### Campaign Continuity May Limit Choices
- If the party has traveled to an isolated cultural region, replacement characters may need to come from that region.
- You decide how much world continuity overrides player freedom. Do what's best for the campaign.

#### Party Composition Naturally Limits Choices
- Players self-limit based on existing party roles ("too many melee already, I'll play something else"). This is fine and healthy.
- Correct players who feel obligated to replicate the dead character's role — they shouldn't.

#### Campaign Structure May Limit Choices
- If you built the campaign around starting characters' choices (personal quests, backstories, etc.), you may not have the same flexibility for replacements.
- Replacements might need to tie into existing plotlines rather than generating new ones.

#### Replacement Characters Must Be Complete
- Whatever "complete" means in your campaign (backstory, motivation, land of origin, personal quest, etc.), replacements need it too.
- Some elements may be moot for replacements — identify those.
- If you can't predict exact limitations, your policy should at least warn players: "Options will be more limited than at startup. We'll work it out together when the time comes."

### Issue 3: How Does the Character Enter the Game?

#### Urgency Varies by Situation
- **Dead character's player:** Most urgent. They're sitting out with nothing to do. Minimize time out of play.
- **Retiring a character:** Can wait for a natural break between adventures.
- **New player joining:** Can wait a session or two, but don't make them wait so long they lose interest.

#### Set Expectations
- Tell players how long a replacement might take.
- Warn them that the party may need to make in-game concessions (e.g., return to town) to facilitate introductions.
- This is normal metagame accommodation. Players never reject other players' characters from the party — facilitating a meet-up is no different.

#### Angry's Personal Approach
- Dead character: player may sit out no more than one full session (beyond the session they died and the one where they're waiting to enter).
- Retirements: wait until between adventures.
- New players: generally no more than 2-3 sessions wait.
- The player and GM work out introduction details together. The player should expect compromises to preserve game integrity and party agency.

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Set one policy** that covers death, retirement, and new players equally.
2. **Know your system's advancement components** — everything that changes on a character sheet over time.
3. **Know your system's parity tolerances** — how much level/power variance it can handle before breaking.
4. **Decide replacement advancement level** relative to the party, within system tolerances.
5. **Anticipate option limitations** — campaign continuity, party composition, and campaign structure all constrain replacement choices.
6. **Define what "complete" means** for a character in your campaign and ensure replacements meet that bar.
7. **Set time expectations** for how long a player might be without a character.
8. **Communicate everything up front.** Players can't claim you didn't warn them if you did.
