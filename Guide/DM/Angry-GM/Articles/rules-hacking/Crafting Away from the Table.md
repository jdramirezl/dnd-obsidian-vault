# Crafting Away from the Table

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Crafting Away from the Table](https://theangrygm.com/crafting-away-from-the-table/)
**Date:** January 14, 2019
**Series:** Crafting a Crafting System

---

## Overview

Establishes the core design requirements for a D&D/Pathfinder crafting system. Analyzes why existing crafting systems (3.5, Pathfinder, 5E) fail, identifies three player motivations for crafting, and concludes that crafting must be an out-of-game, away-from-table system with no die rolls and no GM oversight — analogous to buying equipment or leveling up.

---

## What Crafting Is For

Crafting is a system for players to **create and customize gear**. Three motivations drive engagement:

| Motivation | Description |
|---|---|
| **Expression** | Outfit characters to match a creative vision |
| **Advancement** | Push character abilities in specific directions (mechanical expression) |
| **Preparation** | Craft items to overcome specific upcoming challenges (frosty arrows for a fire dragon) |

All three are forms of **empowerment**.

---

## Core Design Requirements

### Opt-In, Not Required
- Crafting involves work (choices, resource management) — too much to demand of every player
- Must be optional at the individual level
- Players who don't craft can't have their time wasted by those who do

### Group Benefits, Group Resources
- Benefits are group-wide (a brewmeister makes potions for everyone)
- Resources must be divisible as both group and individual resources
- **Money works well** for this — pool for group items, individual shares for personal items

### Why 3.5 Failed
- Required the crafter to spend **personal XP** — a powerful, non-shareable resource
- The crafter ended up weaker than the rest of the party despite benefiting everyone
- XP is the most valuable resource in the game; trading it for anything is almost always terrible

### Why Pathfinder Failed
- Fixed the XP problem by making it money-only
- But: if the only resource is money, **you're just buying equipment** — not crafting
- Also required a die roll + multipliers + tracking partial progress over long periods
- Die rolls require GM oversight → crafting demands table time → one player's opt-in forces everyone to participate

---

## The Trust Problem

- Die rolls require a GM to adjudicate
- If crafting involves rolls, it uses table time and GM time
- Alternatively, trust players to roll at home — but this creates an uncomfortable trust issue
- The game design should **avoid forcing the trust question** entirely
- Solution: **no die rolls in crafting**

---

## Three Analogous Systems

Crafting should work like these existing out-of-game systems:

### 1. Buying Equipment
- Players have all the info they need
- Simple process: mark off gold, add item
- GMs let players do this between sessions
- Abstractly: **creating equipment by destroying money** (there's no real economy)

### 2. Leveling Up
- Gather resources (XP) through play → convert to mechanical benefits
- More constrained and complex than shopping
- Choices are limited by prior decisions (class, subclass)
- Happens infrequently at specific intervals

### 3. Character Creation
- Like leveling up but amped to eleven
- Most complex, least constrained, happens only once
- Sets constraints for all future level gains

### Common Thread
- All three are done **outside the game, away from the table, without GM involvement**
- All three have moved away from die rolling over time (point buy replacing rolled stats, etc.)
- This avoids the trust issue entirely

## What Crafting Must Look Like

Based on the analysis:

1. **Out-of-game, away-from-table** — like buying equipment or leveling up
2. **No die rolls, no GM oversight** — comes down to exchanging resources
3. **Uses its own unique resource** — not gold (that's just shopping), not XP (too valuable, too personal)
4. **Resources gained during play** — found during normal adventuring, seekable by motivated players, usable as GM rewards for optional content
5. **More complex and constrained than shopping** — but not too much more
6. **Optional/opt-in** — groups and players who don't want it aren't affected

### Why Time Is a Terrible Crafting Resource
- Time is gained by **not playing the game** — creates a tradeoff between crafting and adventuring
- "Earning downtime" by adventuring is stupidly abstract
- Players should control the crafting system, not the GM
- Crafting resources should be tied to player actions, not GM-dictated time allotments

### Why Money Alone Isn't Enough
- If the only cost is money, crafting is just shopping with extra steps
- Pathfinder's system was exactly this — money in, item out, with a complicated bargaining roll in front
- Crafting needs to **feel different** from buying equipment

## What's Next

The article sets up two remaining questions:
1. What does crafting look like from the **GM's side**?
2. What does it look like to players and GMs who **don't want to use it**?

Answering these will determine what the spendable crafting resource actually looks like.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Crafting must be opt-in, out-of-game, and roll-free
2. Existing systems fail because they either cost the wrong resource (XP), are just shopping (gold-only), or demand table time (die rolls)
3. The crafting resource must be earned through play, seekable by players, and distinct from gold and XP
4. Time is the worst possible crafting resource
5. Crafting should be more complex than shopping but less complex than leveling up
6. The trust issue (rolling dice at home) should be designed around, not confronted
