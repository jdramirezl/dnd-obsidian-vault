# How to Dole Out Magical Items

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Ask Angry Mailbag Fakeout: How to Dole Out Magical Items](https://theangrygm.com/ask-angry-mailbag-fakeout-how-to-dole-out-magical-items/)
**Date:** October 21, 2020
**Series:** Ask Angry / Hacking Rules

---

## Overview

A deep dive into the philosophy and practice of distributing magical items in D&D 5E. Covers the official guidelines (DMG hoards, XGE ratios), argues that the exact number of items matters less than people think, introduces the concept of the "Magical Item Triad," and explains Angry's personal approach of planting twice the recommended items behind varying levels of difficulty and randomness.

---

## Official Guidelines

### DMG Hoard Tables (DMG 133+)
- Four tiers of hoard tables aligned with D&D's hidden tier structure: CR 0–4, 5–10, 11–16, 17+
- Suggested hoards per tier: **7** (tier 1), **18** (tier 2), **12** (tier 3), **8** (tier 4)
- Can double/triple hoards, break them up, spread them however you want

### XGE Ratios (XGE 135–145)
- A party finds ~**100 magical items** over 20 levels
- **80 minor**, **20 major**
- Distribution: 20 common, 20 uncommon, 20 rare, 15 very rare, ~5 legendary
- Simplified: ~**1 minor item per level per character**, ~**1 major item per player per tier**

## Balance Isn't as Fragile as You Think

### Built-in Limits
- Can only wear one suit of armor, one pair of gloves, etc.
- Can only take one action per turn (can't swing two magic swords simultaneously)
- **Attunement** limits stackable items to 3 per character
- Attrition isn't meant to be a real challenge — players always have enough resources

### Consumables Are the Real Danger
- Permanent items are self-limiting (equipment slots, attunement)
- Consumables (potions of heroism, invisibility, etc.) used before/outside combat are more dangerous to overload
- 50 suits of magic armor < 50 potions of invisibility in terms of game-breaking potential

### Bottom Line
- The game won't break with 80 or 150 items instead of 100
- As long as rarity matches character level and other restrictions are enforced, it's fine

## Scratch-Off Tickets vs. Gift Registries

### Three Approaches to Item Selection

| Approach | Description | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| **Random tables** (scratch-off tickets) | Roll for items — might get a jackpot, might get junk | Can produce nonsensical results |
| **Hand-picked gifts** | GM selects each item | Can veer toward all-jackpots or all-junk |
| **Gift registries** (wishlists) | Players request items, GM provides | Removes meaningful choices; every item is optimal |

### Why Gift Registries Are Bad
- D&D already has too many "gift registries" — class advancement tables give you exactly what you want at every level
- Equipment advancement should provide what class advancement doesn't: **tough choices, tradeoffs, dilemmas**
- Suboptimal items create interesting decisions about what players value

### The Mace of Disruption Example
- Party bought a mace of disruption for an undead-heavy adventure
- The polearm fighter refused to use it — he'd built his character around polearms
- The cleric took it but only used it situationally
- If it had been a glaive of disruption, the fighter would have used it identically to every other weapon — no interesting choice
- The mace changed the strategic game precisely because it wasn't perfect for anyone

### The Spectrum of Usefulness
- Not every item should be random junk, and not every item should be a perfect fit
- Balance between: the awesome, the useful, the useful-but-costly, and the weird thing you have to think to use
- Circumstantial utility matters — items that help with specific adventure challenges (hidden behind optional paths)

## The Magical Item Triad

Three questions to balance when distributing items:

| Question | Description |
|---|---|
| **How many?** | Compared to the ~100 baseline, how many items do you want? |
| **How useful?** | How tailor-made are items to specific characters/adventures? |
| **How hard to acquire?** | Do players have to hunt, pay, search, or do extra work? |

### Balancing the Triad
- **Lots of items + easy to find** → usefulness must be a crapshoot (some jackpots, some junk)
- **Lots of useful items** → hide them well, make acquisition difficult/optional
- **Few items + hard to find** → they should be very useful when found
- You can't max all three without breaking the game

## Angry's Personal Approach

### Philosophy
- Equipment advancement should contrast with level advancement: levels are reliable and sure; equipment is a lottery
- Plant roughly **twice as many items** as the DMG recommends
- Lots of optional content — missable side-quests, mutually exclusive paths
- The more optional something is, the more unbalanced the reward can be

### Item Selection Process
1. **Reserve 1–2 items** specifically useful (not necessarily optimal) for a specific character
2. **Reserve 1–2 items** that help with a specific adventure situation, hidden along optional paths
3. **Generate the rest randomly** using hoard tables
4. **Veto/swap** nonsensical results (scrolls in volcanic tunnels) or completely unusable items (rod of the pact keeper with no warlock)
5. **Occasionally cherry-pick** fun items spotted on tables ("the party definitely needs an immovable rod")

### Buying and Selling Magic Items
- Players can find a buyer for any magic item in any town/city — buyer pays item cost minus a random discount
- Players can commission specific items — item cost plus a random premium
- Players can browse what's available for sale in a town — randomly generated list at fair prices
- **No magic item shops** — just people occasionally buying/selling

### One-Shots
- Almost every item is hand-selected and useful
- Random generation is almost never used
- Reason (via the triad): limited time means items must be immediately useful, easy to find, and few in number

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Magic items ARE character advancement — treat them that way
2. The exact number matters less than the rarity-to-level alignment and built-in limits
3. Not every item should be perfect — suboptimal items create the best gameplay moments
4. Use the triad (quantity, usefulness, difficulty) to find your balance
5. Random tables are fun and add healthy unpredictability — use them as a starting point, then curate
6. One-shots need curated, immediately useful items; campaigns can afford more randomness
