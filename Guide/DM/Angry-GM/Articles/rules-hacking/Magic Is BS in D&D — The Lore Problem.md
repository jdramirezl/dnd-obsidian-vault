# Magic Is BS in D&D — The Lore Problem

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Magic is Bulls$&% (in D&D)](https://theangrygm.com/magic-is-bulls-in-dd/)
**Date:** January 31, 2022
**Series:** Hacking Rules / Magic System Critique

---

## The Problem: Magic Is Mechanics-Only

D&D 5E doesn't explain what magic *is* from a setting perspective — where it comes from, how it works, or what distinguishes different kinds of magic. The designers assumed magic just works "the way it's always worked" and could be changed however they wanted because it's magic.

This matters because RPGs are **open-ended systems**. Players and GMs need to infer how things work beyond what the rules explicitly cover. Gravity doesn't have rules, but everyone knows dropped things fall. Magic has no underlying logic, so nobody can reason about it outside the explicit spell list.

**Result:** The supposedly versatile, reality-altering force of magic is actually the *least* versatile part of the game. It can only do exactly what the rules say. Players can't form cunning plans with magic because there's no conceptual framework to reason from.

## Spells vs. Not-Spells

5E is full of magical abilities that aren't spells — paladin's Divine Smite, warlock invocations, Channel Divinity, etc. There's no coherent explanation for why some magic is "spells" and some isn't.

**Practical problems:**
- Players forget their non-spell abilities because there's no intuitive category for them.
- When fighting spellcasting enemies, players can't strategize about shutting down magic (silence, counterspell, grappling) because non-spell magic ignores those counters for no explained reason.
- In older editions (3.5), abilities were explicitly classified as spells, spell-like, supernatural, or extraordinary — each with defined interactions. 5E dropped all of that.

## Divine vs. Arcane Magic — A Distinction Without a Difference

The PHB claims two kinds of magic exist: divine (from gods) and arcane (learned/innate). But the mechanics are nearly identical:

- Wizards prepare spells from a known list, cast with slots, use gestures/words/focus.
- Clerics prepare spells from a known list, cast with slots, use gestures/words/focus.
- The only difference is clerics say "prayers" and use a "holy symbol" instead of an "arcane focus."

If you watch someone play a wizard and someone play a cleric, you can't tell they're supposed to be fundamentally different. The mechanics do nothing to differentiate divine and arcane magic.

## The Sorcerer/Warlock/Wizard Problem

If anyone can learn wizardry (no ability score minimums to cast, easy multiclassing), then:

- **What even is a sorcerer?** If magic is trivially learnable, why does "innate magical prodigy" need its own class? That's like having separate classes for "musician" and "musical prodigy."
- **Why would anyone be a warlock?** If magic isn't hard to learn, selling your soul for it is like making a deal with the devil for a McDonald's meal. Some people are dumb enough, but does it warrant a whole class with unique mechanics?
- **How rare are spellcasters?** The mechanics imply magic is easy and common. But if magic is common, it should transform the world (Eberron). If you don't want spellpunk, magic can't be that accessible.

## The Angryverse Solution (Pre-5E)

In older editions, magic could be coherently explained:

- **Magic** = a chaotic, transformative energy field suffusing the world.
- **Magical creatures** (dragons, fey) are part of that field — they do magic by nature.
- **Mortals** can only interact with magic in limited ways → **spells** = "pidgin magic," limited tricks passed down through generations.
- This explained why monsters could do crazy magical things that didn't match the spell list, and why spellcasting was rare and special.

5E's explosion of non-spell magical abilities for every class destroyed this framework.

## Magic Items — Who Makes Them?

- If anyone can craft magic items with no special prerequisites, they should be far more common and buyable. But D&D treats buying/selling magic items as forbidden (except healing potions at 50 gp).
- Healing potions being universally available at 50 gp has massive world implications — injury is a non-problem for anyone above the lower economic class.
- The designers clearly *intended* magic items to be rare and special, but the optional crafting rules in the DMG are inconsistent with that intent.

## The Root Cause

5E is a "best of" compilation from every edition of D&D. The designers piled up favorite elements without ensuring they fit together into a coherent reality. Each class has detailed lore, but that lore contradicts other classes' lore and mechanics.

**If you choose to include detailed worldlore, you have a duty to ensure it fits together with the mechanics.** Otherwise, just make a generic system and call it a day.

For worldbuilder GMs, this means you have to do the work yourself — invent the underlying logic that 5E never provided, and make your rulings consistent with it.
