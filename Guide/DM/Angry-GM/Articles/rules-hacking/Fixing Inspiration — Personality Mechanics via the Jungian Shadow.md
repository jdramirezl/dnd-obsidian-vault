# Let's Fix Inspiration Again — Personality Mechanics via the Jungian Shadow

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Let's Fix Inspiration AGAIN! … But Not Really](https://theangrygm.com/lets-fix-inspiration-again/)
**Date:** December 19, 2018
**Series:** Hacking Rules / Inspiration & Personality

---

## The Core Insight: The Jungian Shadow

Every personality virtue has a corresponding flaw — they are two sides of the same coin. You can't excise the bad without losing the good.

- **Assertiveness** → unchecked becomes **Bad Temper**
- **Humility** → unchecked becomes **Lack of Self-Confidence**
- **Cooperation** → unchecked becomes **Fear of Confrontation**

This maps directly to good character arcs. Luke Skywalker's selfless humility was also his crippling self-doubt. His arc was integrating the shadow — not destroying it, but learning to control it.

D&D's Inspiration system (and most personality mechanics) fail because they treat virtues and flaws as unrelated items on a checklist. They should be paired expressions of the same trait.

## Problems with RAW Inspiration

1. **No connection** between the trait that earns Inspiration and the action that spends it — it's just a stored battery.
2. **GM burden** — the GM must remember 5–6 traits per player and constantly watch for them.
3. **Easily forgotten** by both players and GMs.
4. **Overly specific traits** — long sentences nobody memorizes.
5. **No mechanism for change** — traits are locked at character creation, so no character growth or arcs.
6. **Flaws have no distinct mechanical impact** — they're just more traits.

## Design Goals for a Better System

- **Small and simple.** D&D isn't a character drama indie game.
- **Player-driven.** Minimal GM involvement.
- **Easy to ignore.** If someone doesn't use it, nothing breaks.
- **Flag-waving.** The real value is the player announcing "I'm the assertive guy — I've got this!" The mechanic exists to spotlight personality.
- **Allows growth.** Traits should emerge and change during play.
- **Virtues and flaws are linked.** Two sides of one coin.

## The Stress-Based Personality Mechanic

### Trait Pairs
Each trait is a **virtue/flaw pair** — just two words:

> Assertiveness / Bad Temper
> Courage / Foolhardiness
> Commanding / Stubborn
> Practical / Greedy
> Idealistic / Ends Justify the Means

### Using a Trait (Gaining Stress)
- **Invoke your virtue** on any relevant action to gain a bonus (+2, +4, or Advantage — whatever fits the system).
- Each use adds **one bubble of stress** to that trait on your character sheet.

### Clearing Stress (Paying the Price)
- **Voluntarily** trigger your flaw in an appropriate situation. Take a penalty equal to your accrued stress. Stress clears after the situation resolves. You deal with the consequences (blew your stack in combat, ruined a negotiation, etc.).
- **Involuntarily** — if stress reaches the cap (e.g., 3 or 5 bubbles), you're **overstressed**. Write the trait on an index card and hand it to the GM. You can no longer use that trait. At some future awful moment, the GM hands it back and you must take the full penalty. Then stress clears.

### Progression
- Start with **one trait pair** at level 1.
- Add a new pair at each **Ability Score Increase** (~every 4 levels).
- Maximum of **three pairs**. To add a fourth, discard one.
- New traits should be inspired by events in the game.

### Optional Extensions

**Coping Mechanisms:** A bad habit (e.g., Alcoholism) that clears stress from any trait with a prescribed penalty instead of triggering the flaw. Example: "Start the next day with one level of exhaustion — clear all non-overstressed stress."

**Character Arcs:** Near the end of a campaign, the GM designs situations around a player's **primary trait** (the one chosen at level 1). Resolving the arc erases the flaw side permanently — the trait can be used without ever building stress again.

## Are Personality Mechanics Worth It?

- People who want to roleplay personality will do so with or without mechanics. People who don't, won't.
- Inspiration-as-written is a waste of page space — it rewards something players already do, burdens the GM, and is easily forgotten.
- The real value of a personality mechanic is **flag-waving** and, if done well, enabling **character arcs** that tie into the narrative.
- A mechanic only justifies its page space if it hooks into something bigger — arcs, karma, reputation, honor systems — not just a forgettable bonus.
