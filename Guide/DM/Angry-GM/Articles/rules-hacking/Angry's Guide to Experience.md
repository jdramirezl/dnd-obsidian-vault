# Angry's Guide to Experience

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Angry's Guide to Experience](https://theangrygm.com/angrys-guide-to-experience/)
**Date:** July 3, 2019
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## Overview

A complete guide to handling XP in D&D 5E. Argues strongly for using XP over milestone leveling, explains the psychology behind why XP works, and lays out a simplified system for calculating and awarding XP that ties advancement to player victories and story progress.

---

## Why XP Matters

- XP accumulation triggers reward pathways in the brain — watching numbers tick up is inherently satisfying
- XP connects victories in the game to character progression, creating a psychological bundle: story progress = winning = earning points = new powers
- Milestone leveling severs the connection between player actions and advancement — it turns leveling into survival, not achievement
- Different players are motivated by different things (story, winning, numbers, powers); XP bundles all of those together so everyone shares the same sense of accomplishment

---

## End Every Session with XP

- Recap the session's victories and assign XP to each one out loud
- This serves as a **review** (fixes events in memory), a **victory list** (makes players feel good), and a **psychological bundle** (ties XP to accomplishments)
- Example: "You defeated the mercenaries — 50 XP each. You evaded the patrols — 50 XP. You delivered Stedd safely — 25 XP. Total: 125 XP each."

---

## Levels Only Come Between Adventures

- Leveling requires a few days of downtime in a safe environment
- Players can only level up **between sessions**, not during
- Levels should follow **substantial victories** — never just hand out a level arbitrarily
- Players should advance every **2–4 sessions**
- Spending a few sessions at the same level is good: session 1 = learn new abilities, session 2 = experiment, session 3 = use with confidence and form team strategies
- If an adventure runs long, insert a subgoal that allows a natural stopping point for leveling

---

## What Earns XP

### Fixed Encounters
- Any scene involving **conflict, risk, and progress toward a goal**
- Doesn't have to be combat — sneaking past guards, resolving a hostage situation, bluffing through a checkpoint all count
- Must be **fixed** (planned or would-have-been-planned) — not random encounters or wandering monsters
- Random encounters earn **0 XP** — they are drains, consequences for bad choices, and should feel that way

### XP Modifiers
| Outcome | Award |
|---|---|
| Permanent victory | Full award |
| Pyrrhic victory (barely won, heavy resource cost, unconscious PCs) | Half award |
| Bypassed but not permanently dealt with | Half award (first time only) |
| Later permanently defeated after bypass | Additional half award (totaling full) |
| Failure | 0 XP |

- Tell players when they missed XP due to failure — reinforce the connection between victory and reward
- Bypassing the same encounter multiple times earns no additional XP

### Goals
| Goal Type | Award |
|---|---|
| Adventure goal (the big one) | Double award |
| Major subgoal | Full award |
| Minor subgoal | Half award |
| Side goal | Half award |

- Goal XP can be penalized if conditions aren't fully met (e.g., only 3 of 6 monks survived the escort)

## Simplified XP Calculation

**The core idea:** It doesn't matter exactly how much XP you give out, as long as it's consistent and tied to victories.

### How to Calculate an "Award"
- Look up the party's average level on the **XP Threshold by Character Level** table (DMG 82)
- Read the **Medium Difficulty** column — that number is one award per character
- Alternatively: find a creature whose CR equals the party's average level, divide its XP by 4

### Award Sizes
| Type | Amount |
|---|---|
| Full award | Medium difficulty threshold (per character) |
| Double award | 2× the above |
| Half award | ½ the above |

### Example (Level 1)
- Full award = 50 XP per character
- Half award = 25 XP per character
- Double award = 100 XP per character

---

## Key Principles

1. **XP is always divided evenly** among all party members — no exceptions, no individual tracking
2. **XP is given per character**, not as a lump sum to divide
3. **Big numbers matter** — hundreds and thousands feel better than fives and tens (psychology)
4. **Efficient victory matters** — barely winning is not the same as winning well; half XP for Pyrrhic victories encourages players to actually get good
5. **Participation awards decrease drive** — full XP for any victory, even a terrible one, is a participation award
6. **Random encounters are punishments** — 0 XP reinforces that they are consequences, not opportunities
