# Two Game Mechanics — Fiction-First vs. Mechanics-First

**Source:** [The Angry GM — Two Game Mechanics and Learned Essays Upon Them](https://theangrygm.com/two-game-mechanics/)
**Date:** December 23, 2020
**Series:** Hacking Rules

---

## Two Approaches to RPG System Design

### Fiction-First Mechanics
- The imaginary world comes first. Rules appear only as needed, in response to the fiction.
- Players imagine their characters doing whatever they want. Rules step in only when the outcome isn't self-evident.
- The **GM** is the ultimate fiction-first mechanic — evaluates whatever players come up with and determines outcomes.
- Examples: the d20 core mechanic, Fate's core system.

### Mechanics-First Mechanics
- Rules define and constrain possible actions with precise resolutions. You check the rules *before* you know what your character can do.
- Examples: D&D combat (initiative, action economy, spell lists), most board games, all video games.
- Creates the "combat swoosh" — the clunky transition from fiction-first exploration to mechanics-first combat.

### These Are NOT a Spectrum
- You can't have a middle ground between two things going first. Either fiction or mechanics comes first.
- Most RPGs use a **layered approach**: fiction-first core (GM + basic action resolution) with mechanics-first subsystems on top.
- Fiction-first is the **defining aspect** of TTRPGs — what sets them apart from board games and video games.

### The Homebrewing Problem
- Hackers and designers increasingly default to mechanics-first. Every mechanics-first system **weakens** a TTRPG. Include them deliberately and sparingly.
- Board/video games innovate faster but their solutions usually won't work in TTRPGs without heavy modification — they're inherently mechanics-first.

## Inspiration 1: Lords of Waterdeep — Location-Based City Design

LoW uses **worker placement**: locations in a city each offer specific resources. Players assign agents to locations to gather what they need for quests. New locations get built as the game progresses.

**TTRPG application — Town/Downtime:**
- City = list of locations, each tied to specific downtime activities with specific payoffs.
- As players explore and build contacts, GM **adds newly discovered locations**.
- Player looks for an unplanned service? Pull a location out and add it.
- Players can **build their own locations** and customize benefits.
- Differentiates settlements: small village = small list; trade city = commerce/crime; theocratic city = healing/divine items.
- No more writing pages of prose for streets and NPCs players will never see.

## Inspiration 2: Persona 5 — Time Management as Gameplay

P5 offers two time slots per in-game day for activities (work, stat-building, friendship, crafting).

**What makes it feel real:**
1. **One task, one slot, one payoff.** No uncertainty. Clean and fast.
2. **Complications mess up plans.** Some activities only available certain days. Weather changes things. Random events steal time.
3. **You never know how much time you have.** Deadlines exist but events can eat days. Forces prioritization.
4. **Always more to do than time allows.** Everything feels important. Everything hurts to skip.

**TTRPG application:**
- "What do you want to do today?" beats "pick 3 things between adventures."
- One day = one task = one payoff. GM burns through days quickly, drops to adventure time-scale when needed.
- Random events and calendar complications make the world feel alive.
- Location-based: performing an activity = going to a specific place (combines with LoW approach).

## The Synthesis

Stitch together:
1. **Location-based city design** (LoW) — cities as discoverable location/service lists
2. **Time-slot downtime** (P5) — day-by-day activity selection with complications

Creates a system for **everything between adventures**: town exploration, downtime, preparation, resource gathering — while maintaining fiction-first design.
