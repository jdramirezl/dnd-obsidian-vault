## What if You Let Your Players Decide What's Worth XP?

I've been thinking about this motivation thing. A lot. I've been thinking about it from a narrative perspective where motivation is the single, most important, and most humanizing aspect of every narrative ever. I've been thinking about it from a character creation standpoint, where most players have zero clue about it and most GMs have zero clue how to help them with it. And I've been thinking about it from a game design standpoint, where open-world, player-driven games literally do not function without properly implemented character motivations.

When you've got something vitally important to the gameplay experience, there's three approaches you can take as a designer:

1. You can ignore the vitally important thing and just let the game flounder. (The *WotC Approach*.)
2. You can explain how important it is for every GM to understand the vitally important thing and then just trust them to work out how to actually do that. (The *Online Content Creator* approach.)
3. You can build the vitally important thing directly into the game's mechanics so that players and GMs literally can't ignore it but also don't actually have to understand it or even acknowledge it. (*Actual, Good Game Design*.)

Because I prefer that last approach, I'm always looking for ways to use the rules to make the important stuff happen.

Let's pretend I'm actually right and that whole character motivation thing is so central to both an RPG's narrative structure and its gameplay that you can't really have a good, long-term, satisfying RPG experience without it. Given that, is there a simple, elegant, approachable way to get the players to just, you know, do motivations?

First, ask each player to state their character's motivation when they make the damned thing. Then, whenever the player actually takes that motivation into account, applaud their good work and give them a treat.

The obvious things to cram down your players' throats for doing a good job are experience points. Because, not only are they the best carrot in the game all around, but also my particular way of handling experience point awards perfectly meshes with the more detailed version of this whole motivational XP system thing.

## Experience Awards à la Angry

I actually use XP and not milestones or other stupid GM fiat leveling. Whenever the PCs overcome an encounter, I give them a nice, pre-packaged, pre-calculated, fixed amount of XP.

In the Lexicon of the Angrican Church, the words "encounter" and "combat" are not synonyms. Neither are "kill" and "overcome." An encounter occurs whenever the players come into conflict with an outside force over whether they get what they want or not. Further, conflicts only count as encounters if the players have to make an effort or pay a cost or take a risk to resolve it.

It's also *Core* that I give out XP at the end of each session. When I remember to. And while I'm usually mumbling and half-asleep when I do it, I **try** to explain precisely why I'm awarding the XP that I am. Basically, I tally up all the encounters the PCs encountered and give them XP for each.

Once I pile up all the XP, I divide it amongst the player-characters evenly without a whole lot of worry over who did what and who missed which encounter.

As a side note, the PCs cannot gain levels while in the middle of an adventure. Even if we're between sessions. They can only gain levels by stopping the adventure. Usually, that means they have to spend some downtime in a civilized locale practicing, praying, researching, and whatnot.

Every encounter is worth the same, baseline amount of XP. Usually, whatever the rules say I should give out for a balanced encounter of average difficulty and the appropriate level. All that stuff about easy encounters and hard encounters? That granularity gets you nothing.

But before I hand them their prize, I will trim it down sometimes based on four criteria:

### 1. Win, Lose, or Draw
If the party didn't actually overcome the encounter or they didn't really overcome it well — if they barely scraped by, had a major setback, had to retreat or limp away or surrender — I don't give them the full XP prize.

### 2. Half Up Front, Half When You Actually Finish the Job
If the party only bypassed the encounter — if they didn't really resolve it permanently — I withhold some of the XP award. When they come back and finish the job, they can have the rest of the XP.

### 3. No Grinding
A fixed encounter is one the party has to deal with because I put it there to deal with. Random encounters are encounters that I didn't specifically put in the players' ways — wandering monsters, complications from stupid player decisions, setbacks, consequences, and anything some idiot player wrote into their backstory. Fixed encounters are worth however much XP they're worth. But random encounters are worth a really small amount of XP. A pittance. You can't efficiently level up your party by wandering the woods killing boars.

### 4. If I Hurt My Back Carrying You Guys, You Can Pay My Chiropractor
If the party had a substantial number of useful NPC allies along, I shave off some of the XP award on the assumption that they have to share it with their buddies.

I collectively refer to these as my **Tainted Victory** rules. All I have to do to keep track of this is put a little tick mark on a piece of paper for every encounter and then add some asterisks whenever I feel like the party maybe doesn't really deserve a blue ribbon for it.

## Why This Matters for Motivational XP

Every one of my sessions ends with this little wrap-up where I review the players' victories and defeats and score them and give them a prize. Which, structurally, is a great way to end a session. It provides the players with both a recap and a denouement even if the adventure is still ongoing. The end-of-session wrap-up is the perfect place to add some other system for tricking people into playing right.

I hand out XP based — at least partly — on arbitrary, subjective whims. In truth, my subjectivity is anything but arbitrary. There are rules. They're just not quantifiable, math rules. They're criteria. The basis for judgment calls. Considering motivation is a narrative thing and narrative things are highly subjective and not quantifiable, it's appropriate to add them to a system that's already based on judgment calls.

There's two kinds of XP advancement in my game. There's a steady, predictable instream of experience points that come from just playing the game as it is. Any deviation from that comes from what the players actually do during the game. The *Tainted Victory* rules ensure the players are taking risks, paying costs, and pushing themselves to actually win the game.

In past games, I've given out additional XP awards for accomplishing objectives, making discoveries, undertaking side quests. But that stuff's not just there to give the players extra advancement. Instead, it's there to change the way I design the game and to change the way the players play the game. And if the stuff doesn't change the game, it's not worth bonus XP.

So a system that's designed to change the way the players play their characters fits perfectly into my existing Angry XP Framework.

## Motivational Experience Awards à la Angry

The whole point of this system's just to get the players to actually think about their characters' motivations. To decide what motivates their characters and then to make gameplay choices based on those decisions.

### What Motivates You?

To use this system, the first thing you've got to do is get each player to assign their character a **primary motivation**. And you're going to have to help them. Because players seriously overcomplicate this motivation stuff.

Motivations represent the values, desires, drives, and needs that explain why a character does what they do. A motivation is not a goal. It's not a specific thing a character wants to accomplish. Instead, it explains why that goal — and every goal the character undertakes — is important to the character.

A character might want to kill the person who murdered their father. That's a goal. But the motivation might be a desire for revenge, a desire for justice, a sense of duty to the family, or a need to quench an inner rage.

Motivations explain why a character does the things they do. While a goal sets the character on one path with one finish line, a motivation opens up lots of paths and lots of finish lines.

Batman wasn't just avenging his parents' deaths — he hated crime and criminals and wanted to punish them all. He didn't have a goal; he had a motivation.

A good primary motivation is short. Two words short. A verb and a noun. A good motivation takes the form of "get thing" or "chase idea."

Here's a non-exhaustive list of good RPG motivations:

- Amass wealth
- Earn glory
- Gain respect
- Accrue power
- Achieve status
- Earn recognition
- Obey duty
- Discover truth
- Do good
- Help others
- Perfect self
- Gain knowledge
- Indulge pleasure
- Fulfill destiny
- Stave off boredom
- Satisfy wanderlust
- Instill chaos
- Slake anger
- Mete out justice
- Spread the faith
- Take revenge
- Climb the ranks
- Build a following
- Prove mettle
- Master skill

Feel free to just fling that list at your players and say, "each of you just pick one, okay?"

### Help Me Help You… Earn Motivational XP

Once everyone's got a nice, solid, primary motivation you approve — don't allow any motivation that doesn't fit or will utterly mess up your game — once the PCs are properly motivated, the hard part's done. Because now, it's entirely on the players to pursue those motivations. And to let you know whenever they do. You don't have to give this motivation stuff another thought.

Instead, at the end of each session — during your end-of-session XP roundup — invite each player to remind you what their character's motivation is and to then cite a specific example from the session's action of how they pursued their motivation. What specific action did they undertake in pursuit of their motivation? What specific choice did they make?

Each player gets one chance per session to cite a single, specific example of how their character's motivation changed the way they played the game. In one sentence tops.

### Grading their Efforts

Once a player's provided you a single, specific instance that proves they give a damn about their character's motivation, it's on you — the GM — to determine just how much of a damn they really gave. And you do so by rating the example on a four-point scale:

1. **Nothing Special** — The character's motivation didn't change the course of play — it didn't involve any risk or cost or effort, the character didn't accomplish anything, nothing happened that wasn't already going to happen anyway.

2. **Pursued their Motivation** — The character did something they wouldn't have done but for the fact that they wrote down the specific motivation they did, and the character's action required some amount of effort or involved some kind of cost or carried some level of risk, but they didn't actually satisfy their desire. Making contacts, acquiring resources, or otherwise setting up the pieces on the board.

3. **Indulged their Motivation** — The character took a risk or paid a cost or expended an effort in the pursuit of their motivation and came away with something — some achievement, something more than they had before. If as a GM, you can say they have more of whatever it is they're after than when they started the session, you've got to give them credit for it.

4. **Quenched their Motivation** — The really big moments when a player risks almost everything or pays a huge cost and accomplishes something really amazing. Rare. Like once per player per campaign rare.

As for the actual experience awards:

- **Indulging** a motivation is the equivalent of an encounter because of the whole risk, cost, and effort stipulation.
- **Pursuing** a motivation is the equivalent of circumventing an encounter or coming out at a draw because, in the end, the PC has nothing to show for it.
- **Nothing Special** is the equivalent of resolving a random encounter. It's worth a pittance. A nominal amount of XP. Why's it worth anything at all? Because the act of stating your motivation at the end of each session and then struggling to think of a way it affected the game is still adding something to the game.

I use group experience — I pile up all the XP at the end of the session and split it evenly amongst all the PCs regardless of who actually did what. I see no reason to treat this motivational XP any differently. That way, it helps the players recognize that when the characters help each other pursue what's important to them, they all benefit.

And that's it. Pretty simple, right? Make the players choose a motivation and then challenge them to prove the motivation matters. And then decide for yourself how much the motivation actually affected the game and reward the players accordingly.
