# How to Stat Friends and Influence Their Actions During Gameplay (Part 2)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-stat-friends-part-2/)
**Date:** November 11, 2020

---

This is the second part. The one in which I actually show you my system. Because I'm picking up where I left off, I don't need to ramble.

## Angry's System for Having Companion NPCs Adventure with the PC Party

Companions are NPCs that join the player characters on their adventures — however briefly and for whatever reason — and contribute skills, abilities, and resources. They include hired help, purchased pets and mounts, loyal followers and retainers, familiars, bonded animal companions, and temporarily summoned or animated creatures. If there's an NPC near the party rolling dice to help the party, they're a companion.

The system has two parts:

1. **Stat up the companion** — mostly the same way you stat up custom monsters, but more narrowly focused while being numerically more flexible. Companions are roughly as effective as player characters at overcoming individual challenges while being less versatile. Companions have narrow roles and specialized skillsets.
2. **Manage companions in play** — focused on letting the players use their companions effectively without bogging down the game too much, while ensuring companions feel like independent characters in the world.

Companions make the game more complicated. There's no way around that. I can reduce the complexity, but I can't change the laws of game design physics. The payoff is that the players get new ways to customize their party, new ways to deal with challenges, ways to cover their weaknesses at a cost, and the game gets some new narrative possibilities.

## Prerequisite Angry House Rules

### Tiers of Play

While PCs have levels, everything else — including monsters, non-combat challenges, magical items, and companions — comes in broader power bands called **tiers**. There's six tiers of play and each, except for the first, comprises three experience levels.

| Tier | Midpoint Level |
|---|---|
| Apprentice | 2nd |
| Journeyman | 4th |
| Adventurer | 7th |
| Veteran | 10th |
| Champion | 13th |
| Heroic | 16th |
| Legendary | 19th |

### Creature Status

Health-wise, every creature is either **uninjured**, **injured**, **staggered**, **critically injured**, or **disabled**.

- **Uninjured** — haven't lost any hit points
- **Injured** — lost at least one hit point
- **Staggered** — lost half or more of their hit points
- **Critical** — lost three-quarters of their hit points
- **Disabled** — no hit points left

Whenever a creature crosses a hit point threshold, the player or GM must announce it. These statuses are visible in the world.

Hit points are listed with three values: Normal maximum, staggered threshold, and critical threshold.

> **Hit points** 37|18|9

Each threshold is determined by taking the one before it, dividing by two, and rounding down.

### Morale

Every NPC has a morale threshold that describes the status at which it must make a morale saving throw and what happens when it fails.

> **Morale** critical (retreat)

Whenever a creature takes damage that brings its hit points down to the specified threshold or below, or starts its turn with hit points below its stated threshold, it has to make a morale save — a **Wisdom saving throw**. The DC is usually the highest passive Intimidate score from among all the creature's visible foes. If the creature fails the save, it responds accordingly.

Morale responses:
- **Panicked** — drop what they're holding and flee recklessly. If they can't flee, they cower and beg.
- **Routed** — keep their arms and flee as safely as possible, or fight frantically if unable to flee.
- **Retreating** — keep their arms and withdraw if able, surrender if they believe they won't be slaughtered, or otherwise defend themselves and wait for an opportunity.
- **Berserk** — can't tell friend from foe, overtaken with bloodlust, lay into the nearest creature.
- **Enraged** — turn on their former allies, treating them as enemies.

If a creature's morale has broken, an ally can try to rally it by making a **Charisma (Persuasion) check** whose DC is determined by the highest passive Intimidate score from among all the creature's visible foes.

## The Fourfold Goals

1. **Variety** — build companions of every sort (hirelings, pets, familiars, summoned creatures, temporary allies) with specific roles or skillsets. Companions are either combatants or non-combatants and they're either hirelings, followers, pets, thralls, or allies.
2. **Easy to manage** — can't be as complex or versatile as PCs, can't require too many round-by-round decisions, shouldn't involve any resource tracking beyond hit points. Companions don't have spell slots or hit dice. They don't make repeated death saves. A new resource called **Focus** handles limited-use abilities.
3. **Feel like independent creatures** — players **play** companions while the GM **portrays** companions. The GM speaks for the companion and can override player decisions. A **personality and quirk system** reminds players that companions can think for themselves.
4. **Fit into the game's balance** — as effective as player characters but with limits and costs. The quirk system means every companion causes a minor annoyance. The loyalty system dissuades players from treating allies like cannon fodder. Companions are more fragile than PCs — when they go down, they're much more likely to die.

## Companion Types

- **Hirelings** — intelligent creatures the party hires. Expect shares of treasure. Always have Personality, Quirk, Loyalty, and Loyalty Traits.
- **Allies** — intelligent creatures who follow the party for narrative reasons. Have Personality, Quirk, Loyalty, and Loyalty Traits. Usually expect a share of treasure.
- **Followers** — allies loyal to a specific member of the party.
- **Pets** — unintelligent creatures loyal to specific party members. Have Quirks but no Personality. Usually no Loyalty Traits. Don't expect treasure.
- **Thralls** — creatures bonded to or magically compelled to obey a specific PC. Do not have Personalities, Loyalty, or Loyalty Traits. Only have Quirks in exceptional cases.

Some companions are loyal to the adventuring party as a whole. Others are loyal to specific members — the PC to whom an NPC owes its loyalty is called its **Master**.

Players make **Charisma (Persuasion)** checks to interact with intelligent companions and **Wisdom (Animal Handling)** checks to interact with unintelligent creatures. If a companion has a Master, anyone trying to interact with it other than its Master has **disadvantage**.

## Hit Points, Recovery, Morale, and Death

- Companions don't have hit dice. Instead, whenever a companion participates in a short rest, they recover a fixed number of hit points — **three-quarters of their max**. That's their **Recovery** value.
- Companions don't make death saves the way PCs do. When a companion starts their turn with 0 hit points and isn't already stable, the GM rolls a **single death save** in secret. On a success, the companion becomes stable. On a failure, the companion becomes dead.

## Focus and Focus Actions

**Focus** is generic inner power used to power specific abilities that have a limited number of uses per day. Companions with such abilities have a single pool of Focus to spend on their Focus abilities. Every Focus ability has a cost, usually 1 Focus. At the end of a long rest, companions recover all their Focus.

Focus is a great way to make a spellcaster companion or monster without wasting time with spell lists and spell slots. But when making companions, you should try your damndest to **never use Focus**. There's almost always a better way to make a companion feel like a specific kind of character without tracking a resource pool.

## Interaction Stuff

The stuff in the Interaction section keeps an NPC from being a robot stat block. In this system, the players **play** companions while the GM **portrays** companions.

When the players acquire a companion, you hand them a stat block. But you keep the **Interaction section** for yourself. The players get to make all the round-by-round decisions and die rolls, but the GM speaks for the companion. If the controlling player ever tries to make the companion do something the companion wouldn't do, the GM overrides the action.

### Personality
Tells you — the GM — what motivates the companion. Why they're adventuring. Gives you a general sense of what the NPC will and won't do.

### Quirks
Specific game effects for you to activate whenever you want just to remind the players the NPC has its own ideas. Quirks always make the players' lives a little harder. They're never beneficial. Players can't use them. Players have to infer a companion's Quirk based on what they do.

### Loyalty
Measures on a **ten-point scale** how the companion feels about the party or about the companion's Master.

- **0** — Done with the party. They abandon the party in a way that causes a major problem.
- **1-3 (Disgruntled)** — Annoyance is affecting behavior. PCs have **disadvantage** on social interaction checks with them.
- **4-6 (Neutral)** — Normal behavior.
- **7-10 (Devoted)** — Willing to go out on a limb. PCs have **advantage** on social interaction checks with them.

### Loyalty Traits
Intelligent companions have two Loyalty Traits — one for Disgruntled and one for Devoted. These work just like the companion's Quirk. The GM can impose them whenever and however often he wants. Loyalty Traits are always related to a companion's Quirk. They don't override the Quirk — they're something extra connected to it.

It's important to always have the Loyalty Traits play off the Quirk. Whether the companion's Loyalty is high, neutral, or low, the GM is **always** looking for an opportunity related to the same game situation. The trigger never changes. Just the effect.

Loyalty is a "Whatever Stat" — increase it, decrease it, whatever. Generally, evaluate the companion's loyalty at the end of each adventure. After a successful adventure with fair treasure share and nothing to gripe about, bump Loyalty up a point. If things went sour or the party stiffed the companion, bump it down.

**Who gets what interaction sections:**
- Allies, followers, and hirelings — always get full interaction sections
- Thralls — only have Personalities and Quirks in exceptional cases, never have Loyalty
- Unintelligent creatures — have Quirks and Loyalties, but no Personalities or Loyalty Traits

## Treasure, Equipment, and XP

- Generally, you don't have to track a companion's stuff. They have whatever they have.
- Intelligent companions expect a **half-share** of any monetary treasure the party finds. They expect to be treated like a member of the party when divvying up magical items.
- The money the players pay their companions is just gone. Don't keep track of it.
- Companions don't gain levels and don't share in the party's experience points. When the PCs hit a new tier of play, you just level the companions up.
- **XP penalty for large parties:** If the total number of PCs and non-temporary companions participating in an encounter or adventure is greater than five, the party's XP gain is reduced by **10% for each additional member**. Intelligent noncombatant allies count as half members. Unintelligent noncombatant allies don't count at all.

**Key rules:**
- Non-temporary companions should be in the same tier as the PCs. Temporary companions should be one tier below.
- Decide if the companion is a **combatant** or **non-combatant**, and what role they fill.
- Non-combatant companions are **not proficient** with their attacks or combat spells.
- Every companion has **one saving throw proficiency** and **two skill or tool proficiencies**.
- Hit points are calculated like a PC's using effective level. Non-combatants use d4, back-line combatants d6, middle-rank d8, front-line d10.
- **Recovery** is 75% of maximum hit points.
- Non-combatants test morale when **injured**. Front-line and middle-rank combatants test when **critical**. Back-line combatants test when **staggered**.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Apprentice | 1 | +1d | 1d | 2d | 1 |
| Journeyman | 1 | +1d | 1d | 4d | 2 |
| Adventurer | 2 | +2d | 2d | 6d | 4 |
| Veteran | 2 | +4d | 2d | 8d | 5 |
| Champion | 3 | +6d | 3d | 10d | 7 |
| Heroic | 3 | +8d | 3d | 12d | 8 |
| Legendary | 4 | +10d | 4d | 14d | 9 |

**Damage die sizes:**
- **d6** — light weapon attacks, attacks with significant special effects, or multi-target attacks
- **d8** — medium weapon attacks or single-target spells with significant special effects
- **d10** — heavy weapon attacks (especially two-handed) and single-target magic attacks with only minor additional effects

**Scaling damage — pick ONE approach:**
- **Multiattack** — give the companion more attacks per turn as they tier up
- **Bonus Damage** — give a bonus damage feature (like sneak attack) that adds dice once per turn
- **Cantrip/At-Will scaling** — just increase the number of damage dice on the attack itself

**Do not combine these options.**

If a companion has a Focus Action or limited-use action, the Focus/Limited Damage column shows how many dice of damage it should do.

The Best Spell Level column tells you what spell level to look at when creating any ability that does anything other than damage.

## Building the Interaction Block

- Most thralls don't need any interaction stuff.
- Pets and unintelligent critters only need a Quirk.
- Intelligent companions need Personality, Quirk, and Loyalty Traits.

**Quirks** are annoyances. They remind the players they're not in control. They should be inconvenient.

**Quirks and Loyalty Traits are always related** — specifically, they're related in terms of what game situations they come up in. If a companion's Quirk is something that bugs the party when they make camp, their Loyalty Traits should also apply when the party makes camp. Don't ask the GM to watch more than one game situation for more than one trigger.
