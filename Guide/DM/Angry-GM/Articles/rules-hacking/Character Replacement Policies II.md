# Character Replacement Policies II

**Source:** [True Mechanical Managery: Character Replacement Policies II – The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/true-mechanical-managery-character-replacement-policies-ii/)
**Date:** January 27, 2025
**Series:** True Campaign Managery — True Mechanical Managery Module

---

## Summary

The "objectively correct" answers to Character Replacement — and why you can never fully implement them. This article is really about the intersection of ideals and practical compromise in game mastering. The correct answer for replacement character level is always 1st level. The correct answer for dead characters' equipment is "it dies with them." Neither is implementable in most systems. The lesson: identify the ideal, then get as close as your system allows. RPGs are giant piles of compromise, and that's okay.

---

## Key Concepts

### The Ideal: Replacement Characters Start at Level 1
- Experience and Advancement Systems measure a character's gameplay progress under the player's control.
- A new character has made **zero gameplay progress**. Logically, they can only be level 1.
- This is true whether it's the start of a campaign or a mid-campaign replacement. The reasoning is the same.
- To say otherwise is to deny what Experience and Advancement Systems are for.

### Why You Can't Do It
- Most systems' balance math breaks down with too much level variance in the party.
- **D&D 5E:** The DMG states the game breaks outside a ~2 level spread. A 1st-level character in a 12th-level party is unplayable — they'll die instantly, contribute nothing, and the party lacks expected resources.
- This isn't bad design. The designers correctly prioritized encounter balance systems (which come up constantly) over replacement character purity (which comes up rarely).
- Any system that *would* allow a level 1 character alongside level 10s is probably sacrificing game design elements you value more.

### The Practical Answer
- Start replacement characters at the **minimum viable level** your system can tolerate without breaking.
- **In D&D 5E:** Two levels below the highest party member, with no magical gear. That's the weakest viable character per the system's own tolerances.

### Don't Use XP Catch-Up Mechanics
- Some GMs add accelerated advancement so replacements can "catch up quickly."
- Don't. It's stupid. It undermines the advancement system and overwhelms the player with rapid complexity gains.

## The Equipment Problem

### In Systems with Equipment Advancement Tracks
- Some systems (Pathfinder, older D&D editions) expect characters to have level-appropriate magical gear as part of balance.
- When a character dies, their gear remains in the world. The party can loot it.
- A replacement character also gets level-appropriate starting gear.
- Result: every death puts the party further ahead on the Wealth-by-Level curve.

### Current D&D 5E
- Magical items explicitly don't affect game balance per the rules. GMs can do whatever they want with them.
- This problem is less acute in 5E but still exists in systems with rigorous equipment tracks.

### The Ideal: Equipment Dies with the Character
- Equipment is part of advancement in such systems. You wouldn't let survivors share a dead ally's XP, so you shouldn't let them share the gear.
- Makes perfect logical sense.

### Why You Can't Do It
- A "your gear dies with you" rule breaks the fundamental underpinning of the RPG medium: **the game world must behave like a real world.**
- Boots are boots. Anyone can pick them up. If you stop treating boots like boots, they're just an arbitrary mechanic with "boots" scribbled on them.
- You can't make arbitrary limitations on player choices and world behavior for purely mechanical reasons. That destroys the fiction.
- Any in-world justification ("the gods curse you if you don't bury gear with the dead") falls apart under scrutiny and player agency.
- **You're stuck with this problem.** Every death bumps the party slightly ahead on the equipment curve. Accept it.

## The Bigger Lesson: Ideals vs. Reality

### The Process
1. **Identify the ideal, correct answer** to any GM problem.
2. **Aim in that direction** before looking at obstacles.
3. **Recognize the obstacles** — system limitations, world integrity, player experience.
4. **Get as close as reality allows.** Sometimes that's "pretty close." Sometimes it's "not even close."
5. **Accept the compromise.** Don't hunt for a righter answer. Don't tear down your system to force the ideal. Don't give up because the ideal is unreachable.

### The Perfect Solution Fallacy
- Just because you can't implement the perfect answer doesn't mean you should do nothing or that the ideal is useless.
- Ideals give you direction. You'll never reach them, but your reach should exceed your grasp.
- Every RPG system is a pile of compromises. No system is a set of perfect answers working in harmony. That's impossible.

### Correct Answers Are Often Immaterial
- The correct answer to many GM problems is technically useless because you can't implement it without breaking something more important.
- Don't get twisted in knots hunting for The Right Answer. Find it, aim at it, then figure out how close you can get with the system you have.
- Sometimes the practical answer is just: deal with it.

---

## Actionable Takeaways

1. **Replacement characters:** Start at the minimum viable level your system tolerates. In D&D 5E, that's 2 levels below the highest party member with no magic items.
2. **Don't add catch-up XP mechanics.** They cause more problems than they solve.
3. **Dead characters' equipment:** You can't reasonably prevent the party from looting it. Accept the slight wealth inflation.
4. **For any GM problem:** Identify the ideal → aim at it → get as close as your system allows → accept the gap.
5. **Don't break the fiction** for mechanical convenience. The world must behave like a world.
6. **Don't break the game** for theoretical purity. Balance and playability trump ideological correctness.
