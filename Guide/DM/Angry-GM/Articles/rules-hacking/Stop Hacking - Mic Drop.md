# Stop Hacking… Mic Drop

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/stop-hacking-mic-drop/)
**Date:** June 27, 2023

---

*Monthly Random Bullshit column. Stream-of-consciousness rant about hacking and why you need to stop hacking the hell out of every game you run.*

## The Conclusion (Up Front)

Stop hacking your games. That's it. If you take Angry's advice on faith, you're done.

## Three Arguments That Piss Me Off

### 1. "Angry says you should hack everything that doesn't align with True Game Mastery"

No. He doesn't. I said at the start of the True Game Mastery series that if game mechanics got in the way, you should ignore the rules and run things the True Game Master way — but I wasn't telling you to rip out or rewrite huge chunks of your gaming systems or Frankenstein in compatible bits from other systems.

**True Game Masters don't waste their time rewriting their systems.**

### 2. "Game designers should justify their designs and provide hacking instructions"

The professional game designers built what is, in their professional experience, the best system they could under their constraints. They have every right to expect you to play the game they designed. They absolutely should **not** bloat the page count with design essays about why they're right and how to ruin their work.

If it's worth putting in the rulebook, it's important. It makes the game work. If you take it out, the game won't work. The optional parts are labeled as such.

Besides, most of you don't even read the rulebooks. That's why I keep surprising you by revealing the brilliant stuff I'm saying is mostly already in there.

### 3. "So, I'm thinking about hacking my game…"

Two problems:
- **Thinking and talking about a hack is the worst way to design it.** Writers say there's two kinds of people: people who tell you about the book they're going to write, and authors.
- **People who claim to be Game Masters spend way more time tinkering in the mechanical weeds than prepping and running games.** Most can't even run a simple homebrewed encounter without some bespoke mechanical minigame.

## Hacking Defined and Redefined

For Game Masters and Scenario Designers, **Hacking is a desperate last resort** — the last step in a protracted attempt to solve a problem when all else has failed.

### The Life Cycle of a Game Hack

Cast your mind back to before the Internet. Game content was hard to get, answers were weeks away, and writing anything yourself took real work.

Imagine you're running AD&D 2E and the Group Initiative Variant Rule fails in a specific encounter. You **overrule** it — "Sure, you can skip your turn and take it later." Two weeks later, another player asks for the same thing. You allow it. Then you realize the spellcaster could benefit too.

So at the next session: "New houserule — after you declare your action and initiative is rolled, you can hold the action until later in the initiative count."

Questions arise, you tweak the rule, eventually write it down. One day you realize it opens up other possibilities — receiving charges, readied actions, etc. You scribble notes, break out the typewriter...

**And that's how a hack is born. How it's supposed to be born.**

## The Angrican Lexicon

### Ruling vs. Overruling
- **Ruling:** GM judgment when rules are incomplete, contradictory, or don't cover a corner case
- **Overruling:** GM deliberately modifies or ignores an existing rule because it'd give undesirable results

### Houserules
Informal, usually small, standing modifications. Discovered organically — your game just works better with them. Often start as repeated overrules. Eventually written down.

**Example:** The entire *Pathfinder Core Rulebook* (1st edition) was Jason Bulhman's heavily houseruled personal D&D 3.5 game.

### Hacks
Extensive, formalized modifications to a game's mechanics. Major modifications of existing subsystems or entirely new subsystems. Attempts at actual, honest-to-the-gods Game Design. Examples: *Dungeon World* (Apocalypse World hack), *Pathfinder* (D&D 3.5 hack), *The Black Hack* (AD&D hack).

### Homebrew
New game content mostly compatible with published rules — new races, classes, monsters, spells, items, feats. Mostly modular. Falls into the category of **Content** rather than **Mechanics**.

## Game Masters Aren't Hackers

I'm not telling you personally not to hack. If you think you're up to it, build that shit. But **Hacking is not Game Mastering.** It's not part of the job. A Game Master can go through their whole career without ever Hacking anything. And should.

### Hacking Sucks
Hacking — formal design of game mechanics — isn't running games. It's not what Game Masters sign up for. It ain't easy and it ain't fun for most people. The current glut of Hacks and Hackers leaves GMs thinking Hacking is part of the job.

> "Don't like the game you're playing? Just redesign the thing; it's that simple."

That's an asinine response.

### Hackers Make Bad Game Masters
When Hacking becomes your go-to solution, you waste time over-solving simple problems and lose your ability to handle wonky situations on the fly.

Hackers overvalue mechanics. Overruling and Houseruling are **reactive** — responses to in-game events, putting feel and flow over mechanics. Hackers think any problem represents a failure of mechanics that must be fixed.

**In the heart of every Hacker lurks the dark belief that if they could just design the perfect set of game mechanics, they'd never again need to make a judgment call.** But in the RPG space, there is no more toxic belief than the idea that Game Mastering judgment is a bug and not a feature.

### The Discipline Problem (Ian Malcolm on Karate)

Acquiring power should be hard so that, as people acquire it, they also acquire discipline and respect for it. Game Masters who stumble into Hacking by turning Overrules into Houserules and expanding them have acquired crucial skills:

- They consider the play experience first and foremost
- They design the smallest, most elegant solutions possible
- They watch their designs in play and tweak, test, and iterate

Meanwhile, people who just start full-on Hacking tend toward:
- Elaborate designs with heavy mechanical abstraction
- Most work done away from the table
- Testing treated as something to do when finished, not when starting
- Never quite finishing because they don't know when to stop

**The hierarchy:** Ruling → Overruling → Houseruling → Hacking (desperate last resort)

The end result of everyone hacking: a bunch of really shitty wannabe Game Designers flooding DriveThruRPG with crap while there's no one left running actual games.
