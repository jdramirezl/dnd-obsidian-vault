# Silver and Gold: How You Should Handle Treasure (Part 1)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-you-should-handle-treasure-1/)
**Date:** January 5, 2022

---

This is the mechanical, systematic, table-intensive way to get the Angry Treasure Experience. Part 1 covers redefining treasure — fixing the DMG's crappy treasure breakdowns and classifications, plus solid mechanics for appraising and selling treasure. Part 2 covers seeding your game with treasure.

**Important:** These rules are not meant for the players' eyes. The system is designed to make everything seem more random and variable than it really is. Don't let the players see behind the curtain.

## Treasure Redefined

Treasure is all the stuff adventurers find that isn't useful for anything other than trading. It's not equipment or supplies — it's stuff that converts into cash.

### Cash

Coins of precious metals: copper, silver, electrum, gold, platinum. **Trade bars** weigh either 2 or 5 pounds — a 2-pound bar is worth 100 coins of the same metal, a 5-pound bar is worth 250 coins (based on 50 coins = 1 pound).

### Valuables

Three types of valuables: **trade goods**, **gemstones**, and **art objects**. Valuables don't lose value when sold (unlike equipment which sells for half).

- **Trade goods:** Things useful to normal people — sacks of grain, casks of spirits, bundles of wood, hides, bolts of cloth, ingots, jars of spices, etc.
- **Gemstones:** Individual mineral specimens prized for aesthetic/intrinsic properties. Usually cut and polished, ranging from fingernail to robin's egg sized.
- **Art objects:** Crafted items prized for aesthetic/intrinsic value — cutlery, tableware, instruments, clothing, jewelry, containers, weapons, armor, manuscripts, sculptures, etc. Even mundane items can qualify if they have historical/religious/archaeological significance.

All else being equal: art objects > gemstones > trade goods in value.

### Rarity

All valuables are classified: **Common, Uncommon, Rare, Very Rare, or Legendary.** Rarity determines how readily the item can be sold, how difficult it is to appraise, and how much it sells for.

### Quality (Optional)

Items can be **inferior** (worth half) or **superior** (worth double). Inferior items might be spoiled, damaged, flawed, uncut, poorly cut, worn, or of low-quality materials. Superior items are exceptionally valuable — large, intricately cut, lustrous, flawless, etc.

## Three Treasure Valuation Systems

Valuables do not have set-in-stone values. Each is classified by kind, rarity, and optional quality. You record the classification, not the value.

Example notation:
- bolt of wool [common trade good]
- large amber [superior rare gemstone]
- tarnished emerald-studded gold scepter [inferior legendary art object]

**Spell Component Note:** If players buy a pearl for *identify*, it costs 100 gp — that's all the rules are saying. If they find a pearl in a dungeon, don't be a jerk about its "value." Do not allow inferior quality valuables to power spells — magic requires good-quality ingredients.

## Selling Treasure

A single character can spend **one downtime day** selling any number of valuables. Determine selling price using your chosen valuation method. Inferior items sell for half, superior for double.

If the character doesn't like a price, they can keep the item — but that price stands until something significant changes (leave town, complete an adventure).

### Quick Sales
Sell a single item in 1d4 hours. Generate two selling prices and use the worse one (or reduce by 25% for Simple system).

### Leveraged Sales
Player leverages world knowledge or contacts to sell to the right buyer. Generate two selling prices and use the better one (or increase by 25% for Simple system).

### Driving a Hard Bargain
If unhappy with a price: success = +50%, failure = −50%. Player can still keep the item instead.

**Haggling DC by Rarity:**

| Rarity | DC |
|---|---|
| Common | 10 |
| Uncommon | 15 |
| Rare | 20 |
| Very Rare | 25 |
| Legendary | 30 |

Roll Charisma (Persuasion). Clever alternatives (different abilities, advantage) should be adjudicated as appropriate.

### Market Limits (Optional)

| Settlement | Trade Good | Gemstone | Art Object |
|---|---|---|---|
| Outpost | Uncommon | None | None |
| Village | Rare | Uncommon | Common |
| Town | Very Rare | Rare | Uncommon |
| City | Any | Any | Any |

## Evaluating Treasure

A party can evaluate any number of valuables with **ten minutes of examination** (or as part of a short rest). One chance per item. Use the best character's stats — don't use helping rules. Roll appraisals **in secret** so you can lie on bad failures.

**Appraisals don't actually matter.** They don't change selling prices. They create the illusion of unique values and complex market forces.

**Appraisal DC by Rarity:**

| Rarity | DC |
|---|---|
| Common | 5 |
| Uncommon | 10 |
| Rare | 15 |
| Very Rare | 20 |
| Legendary | 25 |

Intelligence check. Proficiency with relevant tools grants advantage. For the "Way Better" system: characters need appropriate skill/tool proficiency to appraise anything above common.

**Assayer's Tools:** Artisan's tools for evaluating valuables. Includes chain balance, touchstones, magnifying lens, testing liquids/powders. Cost 25 gp, weigh 6 lbs. Proficiency allows appraising any gem or art object.

### Appraisal Results by System

- **Simple:** Just tell them the fixed value.
- **Variable:** Roll 2d6, tell them the range between the two results. Same number = "probably worth about X."
- **Dicey:** Mix up average value, tight range, and broad range. Roll 1d6: 1–2 = average, 3–4 = tight, 5–6 = broad. On bad failures, lie — misidentify the item, give wrong rarity, falsely claim superior/inferior.
