# How to Stat Friends and Influence Their Actions During Gameplay (Part 1)

**Source:** [The Angry GM](https://theangrygm.com/how-to-stat-friends-part-1/)
**Date:** November 4, 2020

---

I built a pretty cool, universal-ish system for companion NPCs in D&D. That's what I'm talking about today.

## The More, The Merrier

Welcome to the first in a two-part series about companion characters. About how your players can hire henchpeople, attract followers, lead apprentices and squires, buy pets, summon magical thralls, fail escort quests, and even own slaves. If that's something you and your players are cool with in your game.

Once upon a time, leading followers was a big thing in D&D. You knew your PC had made it when he had an entourage following him around. But that's just another feature that used to exist in older editions and didn't make the cut for 5E.

Except that stuff ain't really gone from D&D. First, while PCs no longer start attracting a following once they hit name level, a few PC classes do get to pal around with buddies. Wizards can have familiars. Rangers can have animal companions. The equipment chapter of the PHB does have prices for hirelings, pets, and mounts. And some spells let PCs summon or animate all sorts of magical critters and creatures. Funny thing though? People don't see most of that stuff the same way they see henchmen, hirelings, followers, and sidekicks. Remember that. It's important.

Recently, I ended up wishing D&D had a good, core way to handle hirelings and sidekicks because my home group ended up down a player and needed a meatshield to accompany them on their adventures.

So, I decided I'd just figure it out for myself. I'd come up with my own system for henches and hirelings. And I really do mean that. I wanted to go beyond just henches and hirelings.

See, I found myself wondering just why such a system didn't already exist. Because, it seems to me that if you built a good, solid system for adding NPC allies to a PC party, then you could skip all the special rules for familiars and animal companions and summoning spells and just refer to the NPC ally system. Familiars and animal companions and mounts are just special kinds of followers. Summoned critters and animates are just temporary contractors. Even NPCs that the party escorts or specialists they bring along to deal with specific problems? Those are just followers.

So why not just come up with a universal treatment for every NPC ally that might join a PC party for any length of time and for any reason? Which is just what I did.

## Making Friends… From Scratch

The problem is that sometimes the PC party ends up with some extra characters in it. Characters who aren't controlled by the players, but who nonetheless contribute directly to the adventure as de facto party members. Temporarily, permanently, or until death do they part. There's no systematic way to handle these companions. No rules for statting them up and balancing them. No guidelines for managing them. Nothing. And there should be.

I'm going to call all such NPCs **companions**.

## What Good are Friends Anyway?

There's lots of reasons why the players would want to drag a companion or three along on their adventures. The main reasons:

### 1. Strategic/Mechanical Benefit
The players might want to fill out their roster with someone who can provide a specific role or skillset. Maybe the party doesn't have a healer or a strong front-line fighter. Maybe no one in the party can disarm a trap or pick a lock. Or maybe they don't have a tracker and a specific adventure calls for one.

### 2. Narrative/Identity Benefit
The players — or a specific player — might have friends along for fluffier, more narrative reasons. Rangers have pet wolves and wizards have pet ravens and knights have squires and nobles have retainers. A player might consider such an ally to be part of their character identity. The mechanical benefits are nice, but it's what they represent in the story that really matters.

Likewise, sometimes the players actually come to like NPCs. And so, they invite them along on their adventures. That's where all your Splugs and Meepos come from.

### 3. GM-Driven Companions
Some parties end up with extra members because the GM deemed it so. This is where you've got your escort quests, NPCs the party rescues who then want to join the fight, and the linguist who's the only one who can read the riddles carved in the ancient ruins.

## Imaginary Friends — The Thought Experiment

Let's pretend my players have asked me if they could have some NPC companions join them on their quest. What's the most obvious way to add some NPC buddies to the party?

### The Stat Problem
Those buddies need game stats. The easiest way to give a human or demi-human with class levels some game stats is to use the character generation rules. That'll work for hirelings and henchmen, but not for critters and creepy crawlies. There's no way to use the character creation rules to make the ranger's pet ocelot or the wizard's raven.

Using monster stat blocks is easy enough, but character generation is a slog. D&D characters are broad and versatile. They have lots of skills and abilities. But players usually seek companions to provide specific skills or to fill specific roles. And the *Monster Manual* doesn't really provide a good roster of humanoid-type adventuring companions.

### The Control Problem
Who makes the initiative rolls for the companions? And when a companion's turn comes up, who decides what the NPC does? Someone has to. Either the GM or the players.

If the GM runs them — the GM has enough to do without also running extra PCs. If the players run them — player-characters are already complex things to run.

There's also the issue of game lag. Deciding what to do and resolving it takes actual time. While one companion character ain't going to chew up enough time to wreck the game, a whole bunch of them could.

D&D has tried requiring the PC to use their action to control the companion. That's actually a pretty terrible solution. No player ever thought it was worth wasting their own PC's action so they could play as someone's pet for a turn. If the players aren't willing to use their companions, the companions might as well not exist.

### The Narrative Control Problem
There've been companion NPC systems in various RPGs in which a companion just boiled down to a set of static bonuses or specific character abilities. If all you want from a companion is a mechanical bonus or a specific role covered, that works fine. But if you're one of those players who want a companion because it's part of your character's identity, that approach sucks. They have to actually **be** companions.

### The Balance Problem
Unlike the rest of the gaming community, I don't have any illusions about how carefully balanced and brilliantly paced my gameplay experiences are. RPGs just aren't that precisely balanced. Which is okay because players don't notice balance nearly as much as they think they do.

That said, throwing a twelve-member Scooby gang at a fight balanced for three to five player-characters is going to cause some issues. D&D's action economy is broken enough without doubling the number of turns the players can spend tanking-and-spanking the dragon.

Companions have to be roughly as powerful as the PCs. Otherwise, they might as well just not show up for the game. But that means that, from a balance perspective, every companion is the mechanical equivalent of a PC in a fight. So there has to be a way to keep the players from bringing so many companions that they push the game's balance beyond the breaking point.

Not every companion's a combatant. Companions fill more narrowly defined roles. They're as effective as PCs, but not as versatile. The players might hire a meatshield or healer, but they might also hire a torchbearer or a Burglar Baggings to bring some non-combat skills to the table.

Non-combat companions can have a weird effect on the game too. If the party can just hire a non-combat burglar to follow them around, and a wilderness guide, and a linguist — it doesn't matter what non-combat stuff the PCs can do because they can easily hire the companions they need to do everything. While non-combat companions won't break mechanical game balance, their easy availability will trivialize the choices the players make during character generation.

## What Makes a Good Friend?

My companion system necessarily has two parts:

1. **Companion-building part** — tells the GM how to stat up any companion NPC they need.
2. **Companion-running part** — tells GMs and players how the companions act during gameplay and who handles what.

Those two parts have to meet specific criteria:

### 1. Variety
There's lots of different reasons why players might bring companions along and lots of ways they might acquire them. Pets and hirelings and summoned creatures and loyal followers should all play the same, but feel different. And non-combatants should feel like non-combatants even if they can take actions in combat.

### 2. Simplicity
Companion NPCs have to be relatively simple. They can't bog down the gameplay or burden the GM or the players. Companions should require minimal effort to play and track. But they also can't be weaksauce or pointless.

### 3. Independence
Companions have to feel like independent entities. A companion has to feel like it has a mind of its own. Even if it's under the direct control of a player at the table. Summoned creatures and animated constructs really are just an extension of the PC that conjured them up. But they're the exception, not the rule.

### 4. Balance
Companions have to play nice with the game's balance. They have to impact the game or they're pointless. Players who bring companions along have to be better off than players who don't. But there have to be limits in place so they don't trivialize the game's challenge or render the choices made during character generation meaningless. Companions can't be instant win buttons.

As a final condition: I want to let the players have as many companions as they think they can manage. Of course, I'll give the GM veto power if the players want more companions than the **GM** can manage.
