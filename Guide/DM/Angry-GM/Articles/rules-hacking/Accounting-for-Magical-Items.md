## Key Advice

Before you can build a crafting system, you must understand the economic constraints already baked into D&D 5e. By reverse-engineering the DMG and XGE crafting rules, treasure tables, and magic item distribution, you can establish a baseline of how many magic items and how much gold a party is expected to have at each level — and then design your system to stay within those bounds.

## Summary

This article is part of Angry's crafting system series. He methodically analyzes every existing D&D 5e rule related to magic item creation and treasure to establish the constraints his crafting system must respect.

**Crafting rules analysis (PHB, DMG, XGE):**
- PHB crafting is trivial: proficiency + half-cost in materials + meaningless downtime = mundane items.
- DMG magic item crafting is just a GP-to-item conversion with level gates. Most "requirements" (formulae, spellcaster restriction, time) are functionally meaningless because the DMG gives no guidance on limiting them.
- XGE revised the costs downward (roughly half the DMG prices), introduced CR-based ingredient sourcing, made consumables cheaper, and gave healing potions special low pricing.
- The real takeaway: **safe GP values for magic items by rarity** form a range from XGE's healing potion prices (floor) through XGE crafting costs (midrange) to DMG costs (ceiling).

**Magic item distribution (XGE 135):**
- XGE reveals the expected number of magic items by rarity and a hidden Minor/Major split across a 20-level campaign.
- A party is expected to find ~100 magic items total over 20 levels.
- Items only come from treasure hoards, not individual monster loot.

**Treasure analysis:**
- Over 20 levels, a party earns approximately **2.5 million GP** in coins, gems, and art objects.
- This is the pool from which optional crafting/buying draws.
- Combined with the ~100 magic items, this establishes the baseline the crafting system must not exceed.

**Key design insight:** The crafting system should *replace* some found magic items with raw materials, not add items on top. Instead of finding 5 items in a dungeon, the party finds 3 items + enough materials to craft 2 more — giving players agency over what they get without inflating power.

## Actionable Steps

1. **Know the magic item price ranges by rarity**:
   - Common: 25–100 GP
   - Uncommon: 100–500 GP
   - Rare: 500–5,000 GP
   - Very Rare: 5,000–50,000 GP
   - Legendary: 25,000–500,000 GP
2. **Use XGE 135 as your magic item budget**: Track how many Minor/Major items of each rarity your party has found vs. the expected totals for their level tier.
3. **Understand the ~2.5M GP baseline**: Over a full 1–20 campaign, the party earns roughly 2.5 million GP in non-magic treasure. Use this to gauge whether your economy is on track.
4. **Replace, don't add**: If introducing crafting, reduce found magic items and substitute raw materials. The total number of magic items available should stay roughly the same.
5. **Use the encounter-to-item ratio**: On average, the party finds roughly 1 magic item per 5 encounters (or 1/10 per monster killed). Use this to pace material drops.
6. **Treat consumables differently**: Consumable items should cost less than permanent ones (roughly half). Healing potions should be the cheapest — the game wants them freely available.
7. **Respect level gates**: Common/Uncommon items at level 1–3, Rare at 6+, Very Rare at 11+, Legendary at 17+. These align with the CRs of creatures that would yield appropriate ingredients.
8. **Don't let crafting generate profit**: The sell-back rate (half value) exists to prevent players from using crafting as a money-printing machine.

> [!tip] The Core Insight
> The D&D 5e rules contain a hidden but consistent economic framework: ~100 magic items and ~2.5M GP over 20 levels. Any crafting or magic item system you build must stay within these bounds. The trick is to *replace* some found items with crafting materials, giving players choice without inflating power. XGE 135 is the single most useful page for understanding magic item distribution.
