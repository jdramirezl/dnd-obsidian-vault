# Speed Factor Initiative in D&D 5E

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Fine! I Wrote About Speed Factor Initiative in D&D 5E!](https://theangrygm.com/fine-i-wrote-about-speed-factor-initiative-in-dd-5e/)
**Date:** February 17, 2015
**Series:** Rules Hacking / Combat Initiative

---

## How It Works

Uses the optional Speed Factor rules from DMG 270–271, slightly modified:

1. **DM declares** what monsters are doing (with hints about their actions)
2. **Each player declares** their action and any bonus actions (not specific targets or movement)
3. **Everyone rolls initiative** (d20 + Dex mod), modified by the speed factor of their declared action
4. **Count down from highest** — on your turn, execute your declared action plus movement
5. If you don't want your declared action anymore, you can **abort and hustle** (take a second move instead)

### Speed Factor Modifiers

Modifiers come from the DMG table — examples:
- Small creature: +2
- Two-handed melee weapon: −2
- Loading ranged weapon: −5
- Casting a spell: −(spell level)
- Swapping equipment: −2

**Only the slowest modifier applies** when taking multiple actions (action + bonus action). Size modifier always applies on top.

### Special Rules

- **Readied actions:** Apply the modifier of the action you want to ready. It's not "readied" until your turn comes up, but remains readied until your next turn.
- **Weird actions:** DM assigns a speed (Fast, Slow, etc.)
- Players get a printed card with the speed factor chart for reference.

## Does It Slow Down Combat?

**Yes and no.** Rolling initiative every round adds time, but:

- **No initiative tracking** — no cards, no lists, no futzing. Just count down from 25.
- **Declarations are fast** — shotgunning around the table for declarations creates urgency. Players pick "a good option quickly" instead of agonizing over "the perfect option."
- **Turns are faster** — players are locked into their action. They only decide movement and targeting, which is a simpler puzzle than "where do I go AND what do I do."

Net result: combat doesn't run faster or slower, but **boring waiting** is replaced by **tense anticipation**.

## Why Use It

### Strategic Depth

- **Speed as a factor in weapon/spell choice** — a wizard might forgo a powerful spell for a faster one to beat a slow ogre to the punch
- **Situations change between declaration and action** — if the wizard declares fireball but goes late, monsters may have scattered. Do you waste the slot or abort?
- **Reactive tactics become possible** — scatter when the enemy wizard starts warming up a spell; the healer and fighter have to meet in the middle for a heal
- **Readying actions** becomes more valuable for helping allies, not just reacting to enemies

### Combat Feel

- **Haphazard and frantic** — initiative changes every round, players can't learn the order
- **Less like slow chess, more like speed chess** — slamming the clock, adapting, building strategy from reactions
- **Actions can be rendered useless** — you might not reach your target, or your target might already be dead
- **Weapons are differentiated** beyond just damage dice — speed matters, like in Dark Souls

### The Dark Souls + Delay Connection

Two motivations converged:

1. **5E removed Delay** — in 4E, there were too many off-turn actions (reactions, interrupts, delays). 5E overcorrected by removing almost all of them. Without delay, initiative is essentially random and fixed after round 1 — no strategic decisions affect it.

2. **Dark Souls weapon philosophy** — weapons differentiated by reach, speed, and balance, not just damage. Angry wanted D&D weapons to matter beyond the damage die.

Speed Factor Initiative solves both: it **mitigates randomness** by re-rolling every round with meaningful modifiers, and it **differentiates actions** by their speed.

## Complexity as Currency

> Complexity is what game designers and game masters use to buy depth.

The system is more complex — more rules, one more thing to track. But the depth it buys is worth the cost:

- Tense anticipation instead of patient waiting
- Urgency in declarations
- Strategic weapon/spell speed tradeoffs
- Haphazard, frantic combat feel
- Actions that can be disrupted by changing circumstances

## Key Takeaways

- Speed Factor Initiative re-rolls initiative every round with action-based modifiers
- It doesn't speed up or slow down combat — it changes *what* the time is spent on (dice rolling and anticipation vs. waiting)
- It adds strategic depth: speed vs. power tradeoffs, reactive tactics, disrupted plans
- It makes combat feel more frantic and less like a predictable chess game
- The decision was partly analytical, partly "I like Dark Souls and I hate fixed initiative, so let's try this"
