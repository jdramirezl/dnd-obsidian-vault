# The Tension Pool — Complications and Design

**Source:** [The Angry GM – Why'd You Have to Go and Make Things So Complicated?](https://theangrygm.com/making-things-complicated/)
**Date:** September 18, 2019
**Series:** Hacking Rules / Tension Pool

---

## What the Tension Pool Actually Is

The Tension Pool is a **visible dice pool** (6d6) that grows as players take actions or spend time. It makes players think twice about wasting time and forces them to choose between **fast, risky actions** and **slow, careful actions**. It pushes back against exhaustive searching, repeated skill checks, and aimless exploration.

**Setup:** Six d6s and a transparent container (glass bowl/tumbler) in the middle of the table. Dice make a satisfying, dread-inducing noise when dropped in.

## When to Use It

Use the Tension Pool during **extended scenes** involving many actions or encounters:
- Dungeon exploration
- Wilderness travel
- Town investigations (questioning witnesses, chasing suspects)
- Social interactions
- Any scene where players take lots of actions and need to act efficiently

## Time vs. Actions

Each die represents either **time spent** or **actions taken**:

| Mode | Each Die = | Everyone Acts Per Die? | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time** | A chunk of time | Yes — whole party acts per die | Dungeon exploration, wilderness travel |
| **Actions** | One player action | No — each player action adds a die | Social interaction, interrogation |

**Time scales:**
- Seconds → each die ≈ 10 sec, pool clears ≈ 1 minute
- Minutes → each die ≈ 10 min, pool clears ≈ 1 hour
- Hours → each die ≈ 4 hours, pool clears ≈ 1 day
- Days → each die ≈ 1 day, pool clears ≈ 1 week

## The Four Operations

There are only four things you can do with the Tension Pool:

1. **Add a Die** — When the party spends time or a player takes a careful/deliberate action
2. **Roll the Pool** — When someone does something reckless, crazy, or dangerous. If pool is empty, add one die then put it aside. Always roll at least one die
3. **Add a Die, Then Roll** — Reckless action that also chews up time
4. **Roll and Clear** — When the 6th die is added, always roll all dice then clear the pool

**Complication trigger:** If any die shows a **1** when rolled, a Complication occurs.

## What the Tension Pool IS and ISN'T

The Tension Pool is **half a dilemma** — it only provides the **downside/cost** of taking actions. The adventure itself must provide the **upside** (treasure, discoveries, progress). Together they create meaningful choices.

**It represents inefficiency.** Time and actions are limited resources. If you don't respect their value, your life gets worse.

### Inviolable Rules
- **Never** remove a die from the pool (except when clearing)
- **Never** clear the pool without putting 6 dice in first
- **Always** roll when the 6th die is added
- **Never** change the dice type or add special interactions
- The pool is simple, universal, and unrelenting — **don't mess with the guts**

### GM Discretion Is Fine
- Add multiple dice for actions that take longer (30 min action = 3 dice)
- Roll the pool when players argue loudly in a dungeon
- Add 6 dice and roll/clear to cover 4 hours of safe travel
- Use other mechanics alongside it (countdown timers, etc.)

## Building Complication Lists

This is where the complexity goes — **not** in the pool mechanics.

### Probability Math
- 6d6 has a **66.5%** chance of showing at least one 1
- Every 3 pool fills → roughly 2 Complications
- A reasonable party adds ~2 dice per room (1 for moving, 1 for searching)
- 6-room critical path ≈ 2 pool fills ≈ ~1.3 Complications

### Adjusting Severity via Ratios
To get ~50% chance of a wandering monster per hour:
- Make the table **75% monsters / 25% non-monsters**
- 66.5% × 75% = ~50% monster chance per pool fill

For most situations, **split evenly** between monsters and non-monsters and adjust from there.

## Types of Complications (Escalating Severity)

### 1. Flavor Effects (Lowest)
Atmospheric events with **no mechanical impact** — spook the party, bring the environment to life.
- Walls start bleeding in a haunted catacomb
- Distant moaning sounds down the hallway
- Minor hallucinations in a mind flayer cave
- A flock of bats takes wing, covering the party in guano

> Don't overuse — players lose fear of Complications if they're all jump scares.

### 2. Hazard Cards (Medium)
Events that **actually hurt or hinder** the party:
- Icy wind extinguishes all light sources (costs resources)
- Psychic shockwave forces saves vs. fear or deals psychic damage
- Gout of superhot steam from a vent (damage + save)
- Sulfurous smoke fills rooms (ruins visibility, makes combat harder)

### 3. Global Effects (High)
Make the **whole scene/adventure harder** — reflects antagonists actively opposing the PCs:
- Necromantic miasma imposes penalty on Wisdom saves for living creatures
- Undead saving throws are bolstered (harder to turn)
- Villain spreads rumors about PCs (interaction checks harder)
- Villagers' morale falters (harder to train/command)

> Works especially well in **Complication Ladders** (see below).

### 4. Plot Developments (Highest)
Major changes to the adventure itself:
- The villain teleports to the party's location and messes with them (à la Strahd)
- Alert Status escalates — changes NPC positions, patrol routes, and responses
- The ritual progresses to its next phase

## List Formats

| Format | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **List** | Pick one when needed | General use, GM discretion |
| **Random Table** | Roll to determine which Complication | Replayable dungeons, fairness |
| **Ladder** | Work through in order, escalating | Worsening situations (portal spewing elementals, rising alert) |

Cross items off as used to avoid repeats (unless repeats make sense, like kobold skirmishers).

## Design Principles

- Build **twice as many** Complications as you think you'll need
- Don't put important plot moments too high on the list (party may not reach them)
- Complications should feel **organic** to the story, not random screwjobs
- Complications should yield only **trivial rewards** (nominal XP, pocket-change treasure) — the pool is about cost, not opportunity
- When a scene ends (location change or time gap), the pool **resets** — start fresh for the new scene

## The Muscle Metaphor

The Tension Pool is a **muscle** — and muscles come in pairs. As a GM, your job is to force meaningful choices by creating **internal dilemmas** (wanting two things but only being able to have one).

- The Tension Pool provides the **discouragement** (cost of acting)
- The adventure provides the **encouragement** (rewards for acting)
- Together they create the push-pull that makes exploration, interaction, and investigation actually interesting

Without the Tension Pool, there's no downside to searching every room, trying every check, and exploring every corner. Nothing is satisfying about finding treasure if you never had to give up anything to find it.
