# AngryCraft — What's in an Item

**Source:** [The Angry GM — AngryCraft: What's in an Item](https://theangrygm.com/angrycraft-whats-in-an-item/)
**Date:** August 19, 2020
**Series:** Crafting a Crafting System

---

## Overview

Defines the material types needed to craft every mundane item in the D&D 5E PHB. Introduces a spreadsheet mapping all ~200 PHB items to their component materials, broken into raw and refined categories. Establishes the physical building blocks of the AngryCraft system and explores how material rarity and traits transfer through crafting chains.

---

## Material Types

### Raw Materials
- **Bone** — skeletal remains
- **Flesh** — meat, organs
- **Fluid** — liquids, blood, ichor
- **Hide** — skin, scales, fur
- **Mineral** — stone, ore, clay, raw gems
- **Plant** — herbs, wood pulp, fibers
- **Wood** — timber, branches

### Refined Materials (crafted from raw materials)
- **Dye** — pigments, enamels, paints (also a workaround for adding organic/volatile traits to items that normally can't accept them)
- **Earthenware** — pottery, ceramics (from mineral)
- **Foodstuff** — prepared food
- **Gem** — cut/polished gems (from mineral)
- **Glass** — refined from mineral
- **Grain** — processed plant matter
- **Leather** — tanned hide
- **Metal** — smelted from mineral
- **Spice** — processed plant/animal products
- **Textile** — woven from plant or hide fibers
- **Wax** — rendered from various sources

## Partwise Materials

Each item's materials are divided into three classes:

| Class | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** | Needed in large quantities | Wood for a spear shaft |
| **Secondary** | Needed in smaller quantities | Metal for a spear tip |
| **Add-On** | Optional, for customization | Gems in a sword hilt, dye on a backpack |

- Items can have multiple primary/secondary materials
- Some items can be made multiple ways (wand = bone OR metal OR wood)
- Add-ons are where you inject magical traits — e.g., fiery gems in a hilt, red dragon hide for a grip

### Design Uses
- **Formulae:** Primary = lots of materials, secondary = few, add-on = optional extras
- **Salvaging:** Breaking down items yields some primary materials
- **Tool proficiency:** Primary material helps determine which tool kit is needed

## Refined Materials as Craftable Items

- Refined materials (glass, textile, metal, etc.) have their own crafting formulae — they're made from raw materials
- This means they appear both as treasure (bolts of textile, metal ingots) and as craftable intermediaries
- **Items can be materials too:** A spellbook is a book with special traits; a flask of oil uses a flask you already have
- 90% of DMG magic items are magical versions of PHB mundane items — so PHB items provide base formulae, DMG items just add rarity and trait requirements

## The Transitive Property of Thaumaturgical Craftsmanship

### Rarity and Trait Transfer
- When you craft an item using materials of a specific rarity, the item inherits that rarity
- A longsword made of rare metal = a rare longsword (same stats, but usable as a crafting ingredient for magic items)
- Material traits also transfer — rare fiery metal → rare fiery longsword
- These intermediate items get made and used immediately in crafting chains, so you rarely need to track their rarity

### Separating Enchanting from Crafting
- A **smith** with the right materials can make a flametongue from scratch
- A **wizard** can commission a quality sword and enchant it with the right materials
- Enchanting could be its own tool proficiency or a learnable spell
- Both paths are valid — smiths work magic the hard way (blood, sweat, shaping), wizards work it the quick way (vision, energy, mental flexing)

### Brilliant Streamlining
- A rare longsword made of rare materials could simply **be** a +2 longsword — no separate enchantment needed for basic +X weapons/armor
- This was suggested by a commenter and endorsed by Angry as "a brilliant bit of streamlining"

## Dye as a Rule-Breaker

- Many items accept dye as an optional add-on material
- Dye lets crafters add traits from organic/volatile materials to items that normally can't accept them
- Example: Can't add plants to a sword directly, but you can refine pyreweed into a dye/enamel and apply it to the blade
- It's an inefficient multi-step process, but it opens up more crafting options

---

## Ad-Hoc Traits (Tags)

- Some items require very specific materials (silk needs [bug] fluid, dragon scale mail needs dragon hide)
- Handled via **tags/descriptors** borrowed from card games like Magic: The Gathering
- [bug] fluid works as regular fluid everywhere, but silk specifically requires the [bug] tag
- Keep the tag list very short — only for edge cases
- GMs can use ad-hoc descriptors for their own story-driven magical creations

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Materials are defined by **Type** (physical form) + **Trait** (magical property) + **Rarity**
2. The PHB item list defines types; the DMG magic item list defines traits
3. Crafting chains allow items to be ingredients for other items
4. Rarity and traits transfer through crafting — enabling flexible paths to magic items
5. Dye is the universal workaround for material restrictions
6. Tags handle edge cases without bloating the core system
