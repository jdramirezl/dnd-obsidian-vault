# Merrow Tactics

> **Source**: [Original Article](https://www.themonstersknow.com/merrow-tactics/) | **Date**: August 8, 2017
> **Related Bestiary**: [[Merrow]]

---

## Overview

- Larger-than-humanoid monstrosities descended from merfolk warped by demonic influence. Coastal predators drawn to marine traffic.
- Amphibious: 10 ft land speed vs 40 ft swim. Exceptional STR, very high CON — brutes. Not bright but can tell when losing. Darkvision → twilight to dawn most dangerous.

## Primary Goal: Pull Opponents into Water

- **Harpoon**: on hit vs Huge or smaller → opposing STR checks. Merrow (STR 18) likely wins → yanks target up to 20 ft toward itself (into water).
- Harpoon: 5-ft melee reach, 20/60 ft thrown.

## Why Water is Devastating

- Non-swimmers: **disadvantage** on melee unless thrusting weapons (dagger, shortsword, javelin, spear, trident). Ranged at normal range: disadvantage. Long range: auto-miss.
- CON ≤11: hold breath only 5 rounds.
- **Grappling**: merrow STR likely wins. Grappled speed = 0. Free but no swim speed = half movement; rough water requires Athletics check.

## Visibility Advantage

- Choppy water = one level more obscured than air above. Daytime underwater = lightly obscured (−5 passive Perception). Other times = heavily obscured (unseen).

## Combat Flow

1. Stalk boat, harpoon in hand. Token Stealth for surprise. Multiple merrows from **multiple directions** to split targets.
2. Pop up at 15–20 ft, hurl harpoon as **unseen attacker** (advantage). Hit → haul into water → bite.
3. Harpoon missed → switch to **claw-bite** Multiattack.
4. Harpoon hit, target adjacent → stab with shaft + bite.
5. Target swims ≥15 ft away → forgo Multiattack → **grapple** → haul 20 ft back (40 ft if Small). Next turn: Multiattack while grappling.
6. Opportunity attacks: harpoon if in hand, claws otherwise.

## Retreat

- Seriously injured (≤18 hp) → **Disengage** (instinct), submerge, swim away. Releases grappled opponents.
- Dragged out of water at any HP → **Dash** back to water. Will not fight on land.
